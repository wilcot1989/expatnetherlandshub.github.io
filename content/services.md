## Tax Advisors and the 30% Ruling

Dutch tax is manageable once you understand the system, but there are areas where professional advice genuinely pays for itself — particularly if you qualify for the 30% ruling or have income from multiple countries.

What a good expat tax advisor does:
- Files your Dutch income tax return (aangifte)
- Applies for and maximizes the 30% ruling
- Handles cross-border income and dual-tax treaty questions
- Advises on Box 3 savings and investment declarations
- Deals with DigiD issues and correspondence with the Belastingdienst

Most expat tax advisors in the Netherlands charge EUR 200-500 for an annual return, depending on complexity. For simple situations, filing yourself via the Belastingdienst portal is genuinely feasible after your first year. ## Health Insurance

Every resident in the Netherlands must have Dutch health insurance within four months of registering. This is non-negotiable — and it's one of the first things to arrange after you get your BSN number.

The main things to understand:
- Basic coverage (basisverzekering) is standardized across insurers — the price and service quality differ, not the coverage
- The annual deductible (eigen risico) is EUR 385 in 2026 — you pay this before your insurer covers most costs
- Supplemental packages (aanvullende verzekering) cover dental, physio, glasses, and more
- Low-income residents may qualify for a zorgtoeslag (healthcare benefit) from the government

## Banking for Expats

Opening a Dutch bank account is one of the first practical tasks after arrival. Most require a BSN, which means you need to register at the municipality first.

Banks popular with expats:
- **Bunq** — expat-friendly, fast online setup, English app, no BSN required initially
- **ING** and **Rabobank** — established Dutch banks with full-service branches
- **Wise** — not a Dutch bank account, but important for sending money abroad at real exchange rates

For most expats, a combination of a Dutch current account and Wise for international transfers covers all bases. Our [banking guide](/guides/finance/) covers setup steps, what documents you need, and which banks are currently most accessible for new arrivals.

## Legal and Immigration Support

Most skilled migrants on a kennismigrant visa won't need a separate immigration lawyer — the employer's HR handles most of the process with the IND. But legal support is worth considering if:

- You're self-employed or freelancing (zzp)
- You're dealing with a visa extension or residency permit renewal
- You're sponsoring a family member
- You're facing an employment dispute

Dutch employment law is strongly protective of employees. If you're in a conflict with an employer, a brief consultation with an employment lawyer (arbeidsrechtadvocaat) often clarifies your position quickly.

## Language Schools

Learning Dutch isn't mandatory for most expats in the shorter term — the Netherlands has exceptional English proficiency and most professional environments operate in English. But it matters for:

- Government interactions (the Belastingdienst, municipalities)
- Day-to-day integration and social life
- Career advancement in Dutch organizations
- Formal inburgering (civic integration) if required by your residency category

Good options for Dutch language courses:
- **Regina Coeli** — intensive residential and online courses, highly regarded
- **Taalhuis** (language houses) — often free or subsidized for residents
- **NT2 courses** — formal Dutch as a second language, required for some residency categories
- **Duolingo / Language Transfer** — free self-study, good for building a base
