<p>Finding the right Dutch health insurance as an expat can feel overwhelming. There are over 20 health insurers in the Netherlands, each offering multiple packages with different deductibles, supplementary options, and English-language support. <h2>How Dutch Health Insurance Works</h2>

<p>The Netherlands operates a regulated health insurance system where every resident must take out <strong>basiszorgverzekering</strong> (basic health insurance). This is not a public system — you buy a private policy, but the government sets the minimum coverage every insurer must provide. All basic policies cover:</p>

<ul>
<li>GP visits (huisarts) — free of charge, not subject to the deductible</li>
<li>Hospital care and specialist treatment</li>
<li>Prescription medicines (formulary-based)</li>
<li>Mental health care (basic GGZ)</li>
<li>Maternity care</li>
<li>Emergency ambulance and transport</li>
</ul>

<p>On top of the basic policy, you can add <strong>aanvullende verzekering</strong> (supplementary insurance) for things like dental care, physiotherapy beyond the covered sessions, glasses, and alternative medicine. These add-ons vary widely in price and scope between insurers.</p>

<h2>Understanding the Eigen Risico (Deductible)</h2>

<p>Every adult with Dutch health insurance has a mandatory own-risk amount called the <strong>eigen risico</strong>. In 2026 the minimum is <strong>€385 per year</strong>. This means you pay the first €385 of most healthcare costs yourself each calendar year. GP visits do not count toward this deductible.</p>

<p>You can voluntarily increase your deductible up to <strong>€885 per year</strong> in exchange for a lower monthly premium. The savings range from roughly €100 to €330 per year depending on how much you raise it.</p>

<div class="info-box">
<p><strong>When does the eigen risico make sense to raise?</strong> If you are young, healthy, and rarely see specialists or use hospitals, raising your own risk can save you money. If you use regular medication or have a chronic condition, keep the minimum of €385.</p>
</div>

<h2>When Must Expats Sign Up?</h2>

<p>The registration deadline is strict: you must take out Dutch health insurance <strong>within 4 months of registering as a resident</strong> in the Netherlands (i.e. getting your BSN number). If you miss the deadline, the Dutch Tax Authority (CAK) will assign you an insurer and fine you for every month you were uninsured.</p>

<div class="warning-box">
<p><strong>Important:</strong> Some expats on short-term assignments may be covered by their employer's international insurance and exempt from Dutch health insurance. Always check your employment contract and residency registration status. When in doubt, register — backdated premiums are expensive.</p>
</div>

<h2>Zorgtoeslag — Government Healthcare Allowance</h2>

<p>If your income is below a certain threshold, you may qualify for <strong>zorgtoeslag</strong> — a monthly contribution from the Dutch government towards your health insurance premium. In 2026 you may be eligible if your taxable income is approximately:</p>

<ul>
<li>Below €38,000 as a single person</li>
<li>Below €48,000 as a couple (combined income)</li>
</ul>

<p>The allowance can be up to €150–€160 per month, significantly reducing your net premium. Apply through <strong>Mijn Toeslagen</strong> on the Belastingdienst website (toeslagen.nl). You can apply retroactively up to the start of the calendar year.</p>

<h2>Tips for Expats Choosing Health Insurance in the Netherlands</h2>

<h3>1. Check English-language support</h3>
<p>Not all Dutch insurers cater to English speakers. ONVZ and Zilveren Kruis offer English websites and documentation. CZ has partial English support. If navigating Dutch bureaucracy already stresses you out, prioritise an English-friendly insurer.</p>

<h3>2. Naturapolis vs. restitutiepolis</h3>
<p>Dutch policies come in two main types. A <strong>naturapolis</strong> (in-kind) policy means your insurer has contracts with specific care providers — treatment outside the network may only be partially covered. A <strong>restitutiepolis</strong> reimburses you for any licensed healthcare provider in the Netherlands. If you want maximum flexibility, choose a restitution policy — it typically costs slightly more.</p>

<h3>3. Consider dental from day one</h3>
<p>Basic Dutch health insurance does not cover dental care for adults (only for children under 18). If you want dental coverage, you must add it via a supplementary package. Waiting lists for dentists can be long in major cities, so it is smart to arrange this when you first register.</p>

<h3>4. Switching is only once a year</h3>
<p>The Dutch health insurance open enrollment period runs from 1 November to 31 December each year. Your new policy starts 1 January. Outside this window you can only switch if you move, lose your existing coverage, or have other qualifying life events. Choose carefully.</p>

<h3>5. Compare on Independer (in English)</h3>
<p>Independer is the Netherlands' largest independent insurance comparison platform and offers an English-language interface. You can compare premiums, coverage, and user reviews side by side and sign up directly. It is the recommended starting point for most expats.</p>
