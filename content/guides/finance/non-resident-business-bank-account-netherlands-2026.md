Every few weeks, someone messages me with a version of the same situation: they have decided to start a Dutch company. They have done the research, maybe even spoken to a Dutch notary. They are based in Germany, the UK, the US, or somewhere in Southeast Asia. And then they hit the same wall.

"I can't seem to open a bank account. Every Dutch bank I contact either ignores me or says I need to be a resident."

This is one of the most common practical obstacles for people incorporating a Dutch BV from abroad — and it is one that Dutch business resources rarely address honestly. Most banking guides assume you are already living here. This one doesn't.

What follows is what I tell people in those conversations: which banks will actually talk to you, what the legal structure requirements look like, and how to get a functioning Dutch business account open even if you won't be moving to the Netherlands for months.

## Why Non-Residents Want Dutch Business Accounts

The Netherlands is one of Europe's most popular jurisdictions for foreign entrepreneurs. The reasons are well-documented: a favourable holding company structure (deelnemingsvrijstelling), a well-developed network of tax treaties, an English-speaking professional ecosystem, and EU market access.

But starting a Dutch BV from abroad creates a specific problem: Dutch banks are among the most compliance-focused in Europe. Know-your-customer (KYC) checks are strict, money-laundering regulations are tightly enforced, and most banks have quietly adopted internal policies that make onboarding non-resident directors extremely difficult — not because the law prohibits it, but because it creates more work for them.

Understanding this upfront will save you a lot of frustration.

## Step One: The Legal Structure Comes First

Before you approach any bank, your Dutch legal structure needs to be in order. No bank — not even the most foreigner-friendly fintech — will open a business account without a valid KvK number.

### KvK Registration

The **KvK** (Kamer van Koophandel) is the Dutch Chamber of Commerce. Every Dutch business entity must be registered with the KvK before it can legally operate. Your KvK number is the identifier that banks, tax authorities, and clients use to verify your company.

For non-residents, the KvK registration process depends on your entity type. If you are registering a **BV (Besloten Vennootschap)** — the Dutch private limited company — you must go through a notary first. The notary prepares and signs the deed of incorporation (oprichtingsakte), which is then used to register the BV with the KvK.

For a detailed walkthrough of the registration process, including document requirements and costs, read the [KvK registration expat guide](/guides/work/kvk-registration-expat-guide-2026/).

### Incorporating a BV as a Non-Resident

The BV is the structure most foreign entrepreneurs choose because it limits personal liability and is the form that Dutch banks and investors expect for any serious business operation.

To incorporate a BV from abroad, you typically need:

- A Dutch civil-law notary (notaris) — who prepares the articles of association and signing deed
- A minimum share capital of €0.01 (since 2012, there is no longer a minimum paid-up capital requirement)
- A Dutch registered office address — many incorporation service providers offer this
- Identification documents for all founders and directors (apostilled if from outside the EU in some cases)
- A completed UBO declaration (see below)

Many Dutch notary firms now offer fully remote incorporation via a **power of attorney**. You sign a POA before a local notary or at a Dutch consulate, which authorises a Dutch representative to sign the incorporation deed on your behalf. Costs for a standard BV incorporation typically run €800–€1,500 in notary fees, plus any address service fees.

### The UBO Register

Since September 2020, all Dutch BVs are required to register their **Ultimate Beneficial Owners** (UBOs) in the KvK's UBO register. A UBO is any person who ultimately owns or controls 25% or more of the company's shares, voting rights, or ownership interest.

As a foreign founder setting up your own Dutch BV, you will almost certainly be a UBO. Registration is not optional — failure to register is a criminal offence under Dutch law and will trigger rejection from every bank you approach.

The UBO registration is done through the KvK, and requires a copy of your passport and details of your ownership stake. This information is partially public.

## Step Two: Which Banks Will Actually Open Accounts for Non-Residents

Here is the honest picture of the Dutch banking market for non-resident BV directors.

### ING: Usually No

ING is the Netherlands' largest retail bank and has a significant business banking division. Their online processes look accessible, but in practice, ING routinely declines applications where the director does not hold a Dutch home address. If you contact ING's business banking team as a non-resident, you will typically receive a form letter saying they cannot proceed.

There are occasional exceptions — usually for larger companies with existing ING relationships or Dutch co-founders who are residents — but this is not a reliable path.

### ABN AMRO: Rarely, and With Conditions

ABN AMRO has a better reputation for international business than ING, and they do handle some non-resident cases through their international corporate banking division. However, this is typically reserved for larger entities with established revenue or cross-border structures involving multiple countries.

For a small startup BV with a single non-resident founder, ABN AMRO will almost always require Dutch residency before opening an account. Their private banking team occasionally makes exceptions for high-net-worth clients.

For expats who do live in the Netherlands, the [ING vs ABN AMRO comparison](/guides/finance/ing-vs-abn-amro-expats-2026/) covers both banks in detail.

### Rabobank: No

Rabobank has the strictest non-resident policy of the major Dutch banks. They will not open business accounts for non-residents. Full stop.

### bunq Business: Most Accessible Traditional-Style Option

Bunq is the Dutch challenger bank with the most open policy toward non-residents and foreign founders. They are fully digital, which eliminates the in-person appointment requirement that blocks most traditional bank applications.

Bunq Business requires:
- A valid KvK registration
- A valid passport or EU ID
- Proof of the BV's business activities (website, client contracts, or business plan)
- UBO registration completed

The key limitation is that bunq will still assess each application individually, and they reserve the right to request additional documentation or decline applications where the business purpose is unclear. Non-resident applicants should prepare a clear explanation of why their business is Dutch-registered and what activities it will conduct.

The [bunq review for expats in the Netherlands](/guides/finance/bunq-review-expats-netherlands-2026/) covers the full range of bunq's accounts and features.

## Step Three: The Application Process in Practice

### Documents You Will Need

Regardless of which bank you approach, prepare the following before you start any application:

| Document | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| Valid passport (all directors/UBOs) | Identity verification |
| KvK extract (uittreksel) | Proof of legal registration |
| UBO registration confirmation | AML compliance |
| Articles of association (statuten) | Ownership structure |
| Proof of registered address | Verified business address |
| Business description / website | KYC — explaining what the company does |
| Source of funds declaration | Anti-money-laundering requirement |

Some banks will also request recent bank statements from your personal account, a business plan, or evidence of existing client contracts. Non-resident applicants should expect a more thorough KYC process than resident applicants.

### The Timeline

Once your BV is incorporated and KvK-registered:

- **Wise Business:** typically 2–5 business days from application submission
- **bunq Business:** typically 5–10 business days, sometimes longer if additional documentation is requested
- **ABN AMRO (if applicable):** 2–6 weeks, with possible in-person meeting required
- **ING:** not recommended for non-residents

### Virtual vs Physical Address

Your BV's registered address does not need to be a physical office — and for most small or remote BVs, it isn't. Many notaries and incorporation service providers offer a registered address service for €20–80/month. This is the address that appears on your KvK registration and that banks will verify.

Note that banks distinguish between a registered address and an operating address. If your BV's registered address is a virtual office but all your actual work happens from another country, some banks will ask for clarification about the BV's economic substance in the Netherlands — a question that has become more important post-ATAD2 for holding structures.

## Costs to Budget For

Opening a Dutch BV as a non-resident involves several one-off and recurring costs:

**One-off costs:**
- BV incorporation (notary fees): €800–€1,500
- KvK registration: €50 (one-time)
- Apostille or legalisation of documents (if required): €50–€200 depending on country

**Recurring costs:**
- Registered address service: €20–€80/month
- Business bank account: €0–€25/month depending on provider
- Accountant / boekhouder: €100–€300/month for basic bookkeeping + annual accounts
- Annual KvK contribution: €0 (scrapped in 2023)

**Tax obligations:**
- Corporate income tax (vennootschapsbelasting): 19% on profits up to €200,000, 25.8% above
- VAT (BTW) if turnover exceeds €20,000/year or you choose to register voluntarily
- Payroll tax if you employ staff in the Netherlands

## The 30% Ruling and Non-Resident BV Directors

One thing many non-resident founders ask about is the **30% ruling** — the Dutch tax facility that allows qualifying expat employees to receive 30% of their salary tax-free for up to five years.

The 30% ruling is available for employees, including director-employees of a Dutch BV. But there is a timing consideration: you need to apply within four months of starting your Dutch employment contract, and the ruling requires that you were recruited from abroad and meet a salary threshold.

If your plan is to eventually move to the Netherlands and draw a salary from your BV, start planning for the 30% ruling application before you make the move — not after.

## Common Mistakes Non-Resident Founders Make

**Trying banks before incorporating.** Without a KvK number, no bank will even start the process. Complete the legal structure first.

**Registering the BV at a personal address outside the Netherlands.** Your registered address must be in the Netherlands. A virtual address service is fine, but it must be a Dutch address.

**Skipping the UBO register.** This is a mandatory legal requirement, not an optional extra. Missing it will block all banking applications.

**Applying to ING or Rabobank as a non-resident.** These banks do not have a workable process for non-resident directors of small BVs. Save the time and go directly to bunq or Wise.

**Not preparing a business description.** Banks — especially Wise and bunq — want to understand what the company actually does. A clear two-paragraph explanation of your business activities, target clients, and expected transaction volumes will smooth the process significantly.

## When You Move to the Netherlands: Upgrading Your Setup

If your long-term plan involves relocating to the Netherlands, your banking setup will change once you have Dutch residency. As a resident with a BSN, the full range of Dutch business banking opens to you: ING, ABN AMRO, and Rabobank all become standard options.

At that point, your Wise Business account becomes a useful supplement rather than your primary solution — particularly for receiving international payments, paying foreign suppliers, and holding foreign currency. Most Dutch resident BV owners end up with both a traditional Dutch business account (for iDEAL and local transactions) and a Wise Business account (for international flows). The total cost is manageable: ABN AMRO business accounts start at approximately EUR 15-20 per month; Wise Business charges per transaction.

The [highly skilled migrant visa guide](/guides/legal/highly-skilled-migrant-visa-netherlands-2026/) covers the residence permit path if you are considering relocating to work from your Dutch BV.

### Economic Substance and the Dutch Tax Authorities

Since the ATAD2 directive (adopted in Dutch law from 2020), the Dutch tax authorities pay more attention to whether Dutch-registered companies have genuine economic substance in the Netherlands. For large holding structures, this matters significantly. For a small operational BV where the owner does actual work — consulting, software development, creative services — substance is generally not an issue.

If your BV is purely a holding structure with no active operations in the Netherlands, get professional tax advice before relying on Dutch tax treaty benefits. The substance requirements for holding companies are specific and have tightened in recent years. A Dutch tax advisor or corporate lawyer can assess your structure quickly.

### Multi-Currency Operations: A Practical Note

One area where non-resident BV founders have a clear advantage over traditional Dutch businesses is multi-currency operations. Dutch companies historically operated in EUR almost exclusively. But if your BV has clients in the UK, US, Australia, or elsewhere, you may invoice and receive payment in GBP, USD, or AUD.

With Wise Business, you can receive client payments in their local currency (using UK, US, or Australian account details), convert when the rate is right, and send EUR to Dutch suppliers — all from one interface. This eliminates the double conversion cost that traditional Dutch bank transfers impose. For a BV billing EUR 100,000 annually in mixed currencies, the saving on conversion fees alone can be EUR 1,000-3,000 per year.

Keep conversion records for your Dutch bookkeeper — each currency conversion is a taxable event for corporate tax purposes, and your accountant will need the documentation at year-end. Most accounting software that integrates with Wise (Xero, QuickBooks) handles this automatically, pulling transaction data including exchange rates directly into your books. Your Dutch accountant will appreciate receiving clean transaction data rather than having to reconcile manually from bank statements — it saves them time and you money on bookkeeping hours.
