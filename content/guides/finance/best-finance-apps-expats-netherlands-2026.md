## Banking Apps

Your banking app is the foundation of everything else. In the Netherlands, the bar is actually quite high — Dutch people have been paying with apps and QR codes since before it was fashionable in Britain. Here is what each major option looks like in practice.

### ING App — Best Traditional Dutch Bank for Expats

ING has the best English-language experience of any traditional Dutch bank, and their mobile app reflects that. The interface is clean, card controls are easy to find, and you can set up iDEAL payments, Tikkie, and automatic transfers without needing to dig through Dutch menus.

What I appreciate about ING: the app shows your spending categories automatically, which gives you a basic budget overview without needing a separate app. It is not as detailed as YNAB, but it is useful for a quick reality check at the end of the month.

For a full comparison of ING against ABN AMRO, see [ING vs ABN AMRO for expats](/guides/finance/ing-vs-abn-amro-expats-2026/). Short version: ING wins on English support, ABN AMRO wins on Tikkie integration (which makes sense — ABN AMRO built Tikkie).

**ING app rating:** 4.3/5 on both App Store and Google Play. Solid, reliable, occasionally slow to update after large transactions.

### ABN AMRO App — The Tikkie Bank

ABN AMRO built Tikkie, and it shows. The integration between ABN AMRO's app and Tikkie is the smoothest of any Dutch bank. If you have ABN AMRO as your primary account, bill-splitting with Dutch colleagues feels genuinely easy.

The ABN AMRO app itself is well-designed, with a strong overview screen and decent spending insights. Customer service is good in English. The mortgage and savings products are competitive if you reach that stage of expat life.

My only gripe: the app requires more frequent re-authentication than ING, which gets tedious. Minor point, but it comes up.

### bunq — Best for Budgeting and Sub-Accounts

bunq is the Dutch neobank, and it is genuinely impressive if you want to do more than basic banking. The feature that makes it stand out for expats is **sub-accounts** (bunq calls them "pockets"). You can create separate IBAN accounts for different spending categories — one for rent, one for groceries, one for holidays — and route your salary accordingly.

This is not just a feature; it is a budgeting system built into your bank. If you are the type of person who likes to separate money the moment it arrives, bunq removes the need for a separate budgeting app entirely.

bunq also has multi-user accounts for couples, which is useful if you and a partner are both expats managing shared household costs. Their "Together" feature lets you split and track joint expenses directly in the app.

The main downside: bunq starts at €2.99/month for the basic plan and €9.99/month for the version with all the good features. That is reasonable if you use it properly, but it does add up.

For a full head-to-head, see [bunq vs N26 for expats](/guides/finance/bunq-vs-n26-expats-netherlands-2026/).

### Wise — Best for International Transfers and Multi-Currency

If you have any money moving between the Netherlands and another country — a salary, rent income from a property back home, supporting family, or just sending your savings somewhere sensible — Wise is the single most impactful app you can have.

I use Wise every month to transfer a portion of my Dutch income to my UK account. Over the years, this has saved me a meaningful amount of money compared to using a traditional bank transfer. The exchange rate is the mid-market rate (the rate you see on Google), and the fee is transparent and small.

Wise also gives you a "balance" in multiple currencies that you can hold. I keep euros and pounds, which means when I visit the UK I am spending pounds I already converted when the rate was good, not on the day I land at Heathrow.

Beyond transfers, Wise gives you a European IBAN that works for salary deposits in the Netherlands — useful before you have a Dutch bank account, and useful as a permanent international account alongside one.

For a detailed comparison of Wise and Revolut in the Dutch context, see [Wise vs Revolut Netherlands](/guides/finance/wise-vs-revolut-netherlands-2026/).

### Revolut — Good Spending Categories and Budgeting

Revolut is popular among younger expats and digital nomads, and I understand why: the app is beautiful, the spending analytics are detailed, and the free plan is genuinely functional. You can see exactly where your money went, broken down by category, merchant, and even time of day.

For budgeting purposes, Revolut's category view is better than Wise's. You can set monthly budgets per category and get a notification when you are approaching the limit. The app also has a good "vault" feature for setting money aside towards a goal.

The limitation for Dutch life: Revolut gives you a Lithuanian IBAN (or sometimes a British one), not a Dutch NL IBAN. This matters for some Dutch employers and for Tikkie compatibility. You can use it as a day-to-day spending card, but you likely still need a Dutch bank account alongside it.

### N26 — Clean Interface, European Coverage

N26 is German (Berlin-based), operates across Europe, and has a beautifully minimal app. The free plan is genuinely free, with a German IBAN. The premium plans offer metal cards, travel insurance, and higher ATM limits.

For pure app experience, N26 is among the best. The transaction categorisation is clean, the design is clear, and the English support is excellent.

The Dutch caveat: like Revolut, N26 gives you a European IBAN but not an NL one. This works for most purposes, but some Dutch services and employers specifically require an NL IBAN. Confirm with your employer before making N26 your primary account.

See [bunq vs N26 for expats](/guides/finance/bunq-vs-n26-expats-netherlands-2026/) for the full comparison.

## Budgeting Apps

Once you have your banking sorted, you may find you want something more systematic for actually tracking where your money goes. Dutch salaries are decent, but Amsterdam and Utrecht are expensive. A budgeting app pays for itself quickly.

### YNAB (You Need A Budget) — Best If You Are Serious About This

YNAB is not an app — it is a method. The whole philosophy is "give every euro a job": before you spend anything, you allocate your income to specific categories. Rent, groceries, transport, going-out fund, holiday savings. When a category is empty, you either stop spending or consciously move money from somewhere else.

This sounds rigid. In practice, it is liberating. You stop wondering whether you can afford something, because the budget tells you immediately. After using YNAB for three years, I know exactly how much I spend on everything, which is immensely useful when negotiating a salary or trying to figure out whether you can afford a bigger flat.

The Dutch banking limitation: YNAB does not automatically sync with Dutch banks (most Dutch banks do not support Open Banking connections to third-party apps). You have to enter transactions manually or import them from a CSV export. This is less convenient than in the UK or US, but it is also fine — many YNAB users prefer manual entry because it makes you more aware of what you are spending.

YNAB costs €109 per year. There is a 34-day free trial. For anyone earning a Dutch salary and spending in two countries, it earns back its cost within months.

### Buddy — Simple and Free

Buddy is a simple expense-tracking app aimed at people who want something simpler than YNAB. You add expenses, assign them to categories, and see where your money went. There are shared budgets for couples or housemates.

It does what it says. The interface is friendly. There is a free version that covers most needs, and a premium version for €2.49/month.

I used Buddy in my first year in the Netherlands before I switched to YNAB. For someone who wants basic visibility without committing to a full budgeting system, Buddy is a solid starting point.

### Money Manager — For Detailed Record-Keepers

Money Manager (available on Android and iOS) is a more detailed expense tracker with balance sheets, reports, and the ability to track multiple accounts. It is particularly good if you want a complete picture of assets and liabilities — investments, savings, and loans alongside day-to-day spending.

The interface is more functional than beautiful, but the free version is generous. If you are self-employed or freelance in the Netherlands and need to track business versus personal spending, Money Manager handles the complexity well.

## Tax and Admin Apps — The Ones You Cannot Skip

### Belastingdienst App — Dutch Tax Returns on Your Phone

The Belastingdienst (Dutch Tax Authority) app allows you to file your annual tax return and check the status of any refund. The Dutch tax system pre-fills most of your return automatically — your employer reports your salary, your bank reports interest, and the system pulls it together. For a standard employee, filing takes 20 minutes.

For expats on the [30% ruling](/guides/finance/30-percent-ruling-netherlands-2026/) or with income from multiple countries, it is more complicated and you may want a tax advisor. But for the basic return, the app works well and the Dutch interface is manageable even with limited language skills.

See my full guide to [understanding the Dutch tax system as an expat](/guides/finance/dutch-tax-system-expats-guide-2026/) for context before you file.

### DigiD App — Mandatory for Everything

DigiD is the Dutch government's digital identity system. Without it, you cannot:
- File a tax return
- Access your pension overview (Mijnpensioenoverzicht)
- Check your healthcare registration
- Use the Belastingdienst online portal
- Apply for benefits like zorgtoeslag or huurtoeslag
- Access countless other government services

The DigiD app replaces the old SMS-code system with face or fingerprint authentication. Setting it up requires an in-person identity verification (at a post office or government service point), which is slightly annoying but you only do it once. After that, everything works from your phone.

Do not leave the DigiD setup until you need it for something urgent. Get it done in your first month. Full instructions in my [DigiD guide for expats](/guides/legal/digid-guide-expats-2026/).

### Tikkie — Non-Negotiable for Dutch Social Life

I saved this one for last in the admin section because it deserves a proper introduction.

Tikkie is a payment request app built on iDEAL. You send someone a link, they click it, and they pay you instantly. No bank account numbers exchanged. No "I'll get you back later." Just: immediate, frictionless money transfer between Dutch bank accounts.

Dutch people use Tikkie for everything:
- Splitting dinner bills (and I mean immediately — you may still be at the table)
- Paying back small amounts ("oh I got your coffee, Tikkie me")
- Collecting money for group gifts, shared holidays, football pools
- Landlords requesting deposits
- Small businesses and market stalls

The social pressure around Tikkie is real. Dutch culture is famously direct about money — nobody lets a debt sit. If someone buys you a round, expect a Tikkie within the hour. This is not rudeness; it is efficiency. Once you embrace it, you will wonder how you ever managed without it.

**Important:** Tikkie requires a Dutch bank account connected to iDEAL. You cannot send or receive Tikkies with Wise, N26, Revolut, or a foreign bank account. This is the most practical argument for opening an ING or ABN AMRO account as soon as you have your BSN.

You can also use it without the app by just clicking links that others send you — but having the app means you can send requests too.

## Common Money Mistakes Expats Make in the Netherlands

I have coached hundreds of expats through their first year in the Netherlands. Here are the money mistakes that come up most often:

**1. Waiting too long to open a Dutch bank account**
The most common one. Without a Dutch account, you cannot pay rent via iDEAL, receive Tikkies, or set up direct debits for utilities and insurance. Some people wait for their BSN, which makes sense — but open Wise the day you arrive so you have something to work with in the interim.

**2. Using a foreign credit card for everything**
The Netherlands is largely debit-based. Credit cards are accepted, but iDEAL (bank transfer via app) is the dominant online payment method. Many Dutch webshops do not accept foreign credit cards at all. Get a Dutch debit card and iDEAL access early.

**3. Underestimating the cost of international transfers**
Your Dutch bank charges €5–15 per international transfer plus an exchange rate margin. Over a year, this adds up to hundreds of euros if you regularly send money abroad. Switching international transfers to Wise typically saves expats €200–400 per year. See [best international money transfer options from the Netherlands](/guides/finance/best-international-money-transfer-netherlands-2026/) for the numbers.

**4. Not setting up zorgtoeslag and huurtoeslag immediately**
If your income falls below the threshold, you may be entitled to healthcare allowance (zorgtoeslag) and/or rent allowance (huurtoeslag). These are paid monthly by the Belastingdienst. Many expats do not realise they qualify, or they apply late and miss months of payments. You need DigiD to apply.

**5. Ignoring the Belastingdienst app**
The Dutch tax system is reasonably generous to expats — particularly through the 30% ruling, pension contributions, and various deductions. But you only get what you claim. Filing your tax return on time, and checking it carefully, is worth real money. Use the [salary checker tool](/tools/salary-checker/) to estimate your net income and potential tax back.

**6. Not tracking spending in the first 6 months**
The first six months are when expat finances get away from people. Setup costs (furniture, deposits, insurance), social spending while you are new and saying yes to everything, and a salary that looks large until you see Dutch prices. A budgeting app in the first six months helps you understand your actual cost of living before you commit to a lifestyle you cannot sustain. Use the [cost of living calculator](/tools/cost-of-living-calculator/) to benchmark your situation.

## Getting Set Up — Your First Two Weeks

Here is a practical sequence for getting your finances in order when you arrive:

**Before you arrive (or day one):**
- Open a Wise account — you can do this from anywhere, before you have a BSN

**Week one:**
- Register with your municipality (gemeente) to get your BSN
- Apply for a Dutch bank account (ING or ABN AMRO) — you need your BSN
- Download DigiD and start the verification process

**Week two:**
- DigiD activation appointment (usually within a week)
- Set up your Dutch bank account when the card arrives
- Download Tikkie and link it to your Dutch account
- Install the Belastingdienst app once your DigiD is active
- Set up a budgeting app (Buddy for simple, YNAB for serious)

**Within the first month:**
- Check whether you qualify for zorgtoeslag or huurtoeslag
- Confirm your employer has your Dutch IBAN for salary payments
- If you send money abroad regularly, switch to Wise for international transfers

## Finance Apps for Specific Expat Situations

### For Expats Who Are Also ZZP (Freelancers)

Freelancers need to manage business and personal finances separately. My recommended stack for self-employed expats:

- **bunq Business**: separate business NL IBAN, iDEAL for business invoices, sub-accounts for tax reserves
- **YNAB**: track business income vs personal spending side by side
- **Wise**: receive payments from international clients without currency conversion fees
- **Belastingdienst app**: quarterly BTW (VAT) returns and annual income tax filing
- **DigiD**: mandatory for all government interactions

The single most practical tip for ZZP expats: as soon as you receive any business income, move 30-35% into a separate account labelled "belasting" (tax). Dutch income tax plus healthcare contributions can reach 40%+ at middle income levels. Keeping a tax reserve account prevents the quarterly BTW and annual income tax from being a shock.

### For Expats on the 30% Ruling

If you qualify for the 30% ruling, some specific finance apps help you manage the financial benefits:

- **Belastingdienst portal** (browser, not just app): the ruling interacts with your Box 3 (savings and investments) tax treatment in ways that are worth understanding before you make investment decisions
- **A good budgeting app (YNAB)**: understanding your actual post-ruling net income takes some calculation — use the [salary checker tool](/tools/salary-checker/) first, then plug the numbers into your budget
- **Wise or bunq**: if the ruling makes your Dutch salary significantly higher than you planned, it is a good moment to start regular international transfers or savings in your home currency

The 30% ruling changes your net monthly income substantially — sometimes by EUR 400-800 per month at mid-level salaries. A budget set in month one without understanding the ruling will be completely wrong from month two onwards. See the [30% ruling calculator](/tools/30-percent-ruling-calculator/) for the numbers.

### For Expats Preparing to Leave the Netherlands

When you are approaching departure, your finance app stack needs to shift:

- **Wise**: start consolidating any remaining money into your home currency gradually (spread over months to average the exchange rate)
- **Belastingdienst**: file your final M-form (departure year migration tax return) — this is the complex one; see my [guide to the departure year tax return](/guides/finance/dutch-tax-return-how-to-file-2026/)
- **DigiD**: do not deactivate this until your final tax return is fully processed — you may need it for correspondence with the Belastingdienst for up to two years after departure
- **ING or ABN AMRO**: keep the account active until any final employer payments, tax refunds, or toeslagen have landed

## The First Two Weeks: Finance App Priority Order

For the benefit of those just arriving, here is the practical sequence based on what actually matters most in the first two weeks:

**Day 1:** Open Wise if you have not already. Transfer initial living funds from your home country bank. Use the Wise card for any immediate payments until your Dutch bank is sorted.

**Days 3–7 (after gemeente registration):** Apply for ING or ABN AMRO online. Both allow digital application with your new BSN. Approval and card delivery typically takes 3–5 working days.

**Week 2:** Set up Tikkie linked to your Dutch account. Install your Dutch bank's app. Enable iDEAL in your bank app. Apply for DigiD (needed for Belastingdienst, MijnOverheid, and future toeslagen applications).

**Week 3 onward:** Check whether you qualify for zorgtoeslag (healthcare allowance) — apply through the Belastingdienst portal. If applicable, apply for huurtoeslag (rental allowance). These can be worth €100–200/month for eligible expats and the application only takes 20 minutes.

Once these basics are set up, add the budgeting layer — YNAB or Buddy — once you have a month of actual Dutch spending to categorise. Trying to set up a budget before you know what your Dutch costs actually are produces a budget that needs immediate revision.

## Finance App Security: Protecting Your Dutch Financial Life

Managing money across multiple apps and accounts creates security considerations worth addressing directly.

**Enable biometric authentication on every finance app.** ING, ABN AMRO, Rabobank, bunq, and Wise all support Face ID and fingerprint authentication. Enable it on every app. The combination of biometric entry and the app's own session management provides strong protection against unauthorised access.

**Use a separate email address for financial accounts.** The email address associated with your bank, Wise, and investment accounts should ideally be different from your everyday email — particularly your Dutch government accounts. This reduces the risk that a phishing attack on your primary email compromises your financial access simultaneously.

**Enable transaction notifications on all accounts.** Dutch banking apps allow push notifications for every transaction. Enable these. A fraudulent transaction is caught within seconds rather than on your next statement review. ING and ABN AMRO have in-app fraud reporting that allows you to flag suspicious transactions immediately.

**Monitor your BKR register entry.** The BKR (Bureau Krediet Registratie) is the Dutch credit registry. Any Dutch credit card, loan, or mortgage creates an entry. You can request a free annual overview of your BKR register at mijnbkr.nl (requires DigiD). Checking this periodically ensures no unknown credit has been taken out in your name and confirms your credit history is building as expected.

**Be alert to DigiD phishing.** DigiD fraud attempts are common in the Netherlands — fraudulent emails and SMS messages that mimic official communications. The real DigiD will never send you a link asking you to confirm personal details urgently. Always access DigiD directly by typing digid.nl in your browser. If in doubt, call the DigiD helpline (0900-1998) rather than clicking any link.

**Two-factor authentication across everything.** DigiD requires 2FA by default. Extend this discipline to every finance account that supports it. Use an authenticator app (Google Authenticator, Authy) rather than SMS where possible — SMS-based 2FA is more vulnerable to SIM-swapping attacks.

The finance apps that improve your life most in the Netherlands are not the most sophisticated ones. They are the ones that connect you to Dutch payment infrastructure (iDEAL, Tikkie, your NL IBAN) and save you money on the one transaction most expats consistently overpay: transferring money internationally. Get those right and everything else is optimisation.
