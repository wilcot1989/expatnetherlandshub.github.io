## The Single Most Important Thing to Know

Before comparing any services, you need to understand one thing: **the exchange rate markup is the hidden fee that nobody advertises, and it is almost always larger than the visible fee**.

Here is how it works. When you send money from euros to British pounds (or dollars, or dirhams), the service has to convert your money. The real exchange rate — the rate banks use between themselves — is called the **mid-market rate**. You can see it on Google, XE.com, or any financial data source.

Most banks and some transfer services do not give you that rate. They give you a worse rate and keep the difference. A bank might advertise "send money abroad for only EUR 15" while quietly applying a 2% exchange rate markup. On EUR 1,000, that markup costs you EUR 20. On EUR 5,000, it costs EUR 100. The total cost is EUR 115, not EUR 15.

The services that are genuinely cheap (Wise, Revolut) charge you the mid-market rate and add a visible, percentage-based fee. The services that appear cheap or free (traditional banks on some routes, PayPal) often make their money on the exchange rate. Once you understand this, comparing transfer services becomes much easier.

### 3. ING and ABN AMRO Bank Transfers — Expensive for International, Free for SEPA

Dutch bank transfers are a tale of two use cases: excellent for SEPA, expensive for everything else.

**SEPA transfers (within Europe, EUR to EUR):**

If you are sending euros to another euro account within the EU or EEA — paying rent to a German landlord, sending money to a Polish account, transferring to your own account at a European bank — a SEPA credit transfer from your ING or ABN AMRO account is essentially free. ING charges EUR 0 for SEPA transfers through its app. ABN AMRO is also free for standard SEPA transfers. The money typically arrives within 1 business day, often the same day.

For SEPA transfers, your Dutch bank is completely fine. There is no reason to use Wise or Revolut for a EUR-to-EUR transfer within Europe.

**SWIFT transfers (outside SEPA, or any currency conversion):**

This is where Dutch banks become expensive. ING charges EUR 5–15 per SWIFT transfer depending on the destination and amount. ABN AMRO charges EUR 8–20. On top of the transfer fee, both apply their own exchange rate, which typically has a 1.5–3% markup over the mid-market rate.

Sending EUR 1,000 to the UK through ING? Expect to pay around EUR 10–15 in fees, plus lose EUR 15–25 in exchange rate markup. Total cost: EUR 25–40. The same transfer through Wise costs around EUR 5–8, nothing more.

The banks are not doing anything illegal — this is simply how traditional banking international transfers are priced. The fee model was built decades ago when SWIFT was the only option. Today it makes very little sense to use bank SWIFT transfers when dedicated services exist.

### 4. Western Union — Best for Cash Pickup

Western Union is one of the oldest money transfer companies in the world, and its main advantage is reach: it has physical agent locations in over 200 countries and territories, including many places where bank accounts are rare or where recipients prefer to collect cash in person.

**When Western Union makes sense:**

- Sending money to a family member in a rural area who does not have a bank account
- Countries where cash pickup is culturally preferred or where banking infrastructure is limited
- Some corridors (particularly within Africa and parts of Latin America) where Western Union has competitive rates due to volume

**The cost reality:**

Western Union is generally more expensive than Wise for bank-to-bank transfers. Fees for sending EUR 1,000 to a UK bank account typically run EUR 15–25, plus a 1–2% exchange rate markup. For cash pickup transfers, fees are higher still.

However, for cash-to-cash transfers in specific corridors — particularly sending to Morocco, certain African countries, and parts of Asia — Western Union can be competitive on speed and accessibility even if it is not the cheapest per euro transferred.

Western Union transfers can be made online through the Western Union website, through the app, or in person at a WU agent location (Post NL locations in the Netherlands, plus various shops and supermarkets).

### 5. PayPal — Convenient for Small Payments, Expensive for Large Transfers

PayPal is useful for certain types of payments — particularly paying for goods and services online, splitting bills with friends, or receiving small amounts. For sending money home as a regular remittance, it is one of the more expensive options available.

**PayPal's actual cost:**

PayPal advertises "free" personal transfers, but there are significant caveats. Sending money from your PayPal balance to another person's PayPal balance using the "friends and family" option is free if no currency conversion is involved. However, whenever a currency conversion is involved, PayPal applies a 3–4% exchange rate markup above the mid-market rate. That is among the highest markups of any service on this list.

Sending EUR 1,000 to a USD PayPal account would cost you approximately EUR 30–40 in exchange rate losses alone, with no visible fee line item. This is significantly more expensive than Wise (which would cost around EUR 5–8 for the same amount).

**When PayPal is fine:**

- Paying for a one-off service where the counterparty only accepts PayPal
- Receiving payments for freelance work from international clients (where your employer or client dictates the platform)
- Small, occasional personal payments where the exchange rate loss on EUR 50 is EUR 1.50 and not worth worrying about

For anything above EUR 200–300 involving a currency conversion, a dedicated transfer service will save you meaningfully more money than PayPal.

Remitly is a specialist remittance service that has built a strong position in specific money transfer corridors, particularly to India, the Philippines, Bangladesh, Mexico, and parts of Africa. Exchange rate markups on Remitly are generally modest — typically 0.5–1.5% above mid-market — making the total cost reasonable for the corridors where it specialises. Remitly also offers cash pickup through a network of partner agents in many countries, similar to Western Union.

**India corridor specifically:** For sending money from the Netherlands to India, Remitly is worth comparing directly against Wise. ## Hidden Fees: What Nobody Tells You About

Beyond the exchange rate markup I described above, there are other hidden or unexpected costs to be aware of.

**Recipient bank fees**

Some receiving banks in certain countries charge a fee when accepting an international transfer. This is separate from the sending fee and is deducted from the amount received. Indian and some Asian banks occasionally do this. If your recipient consistently receives less than the amount Wise (or any service) shows as the payout, check whether their bank charges an incoming international transfer fee.

**Intermediary bank fees (SWIFT chains)**

SWIFT transfers do not always go directly from your bank to the recipient's bank. Sometimes one or two intermediary (correspondent) banks are involved in routing the transfer. Each intermediary may deduct a processing fee — typically USD 10–25 per leg — from the transfer amount. This is why a EUR 1,000 SWIFT transfer might arrive as USD 965 even though your bank only charged you EUR 15. Wise routes around this by operating its own local payment networks in most countries, which is one reason its transfers are cheaper and more predictable.

**Weekend and off-hours rate locks**

As mentioned under Revolut: when currency markets are closed (Friday evening through Sunday evening, European time), some transfer services either suspend transfers, apply a 1% premium, or lock in an exchange rate that includes a buffer for market movement when trading resumes. Revolut charges 1% extra on weekends. Wise shows a wider expected range on weekends but does not add a flat surcharge. Traditional banks process the transfer when markets open on Monday at whatever rate applies.

**Minimum transfer amounts**

Most services have minimum transfer amounts. Wise's minimum is typically EUR 1 (for some currencies slightly more). Western Union minimums vary by corridor. Banks rarely enforce a minimum but their fixed fees make very small transfers disproportionately expensive.

**Verification delays on first transfers**

The first transfer you make on any new platform may be delayed for identity verification, especially for larger amounts. Wise typically requires ID verification before your first transfer. Setting up and verifying your account before you actually need to send money saves a lot of frustration. Plan ahead: do not try to set up a new Wise account on the same day you urgently need to send EUR 2,000.

## When Your Dutch Bank Transfer Is Actually Fine

I have been pointing out the problems with bank international transfers, but there is a large category where using ING or ABN AMRO is the right choice: **SEPA transfers in euros within the EU and EEA**.

SEPA covers 36 countries including all EU member states plus Iceland, Norway, Liechtenstein, Switzerland, and a few others. If you are:

- Paying rent to a German, French, or Spanish landlord
- Sending money to your own account at a European bank
- Paying a supplier or freelancer based in Poland, Romania, or Hungary in euros
- Making a one-off payment to a Dutch government body or institution

...then a SEPA bank transfer from your ING or ABN AMRO account is essentially free (EUR 0–1), fast (same day to next business day), and completely reliable. No need for Wise or any other service.

The only time SEPA becomes less optimal is when you want to send Polish zloty to a Polish account rather than euros. In that case, a currency conversion is involved and the SEPA advantage disappears — use Wise.

## Sending Money from the Netherlands: Regulatory Notes

### Anti-money-laundering (AML) requirements

Dutch banks and transfer services are required by law to apply Know Your Customer (KYC) and anti-money-laundering checks. For regular personal transfers — monthly remittances, paying family expenses, buying property abroad — you will not encounter issues as long as your account is properly verified and transfer amounts are consistent with your stated purpose.

For transfers above EUR 10,000, you may be asked for documentation of the source of funds. Acceptable documentation includes a salary slip, property sale documents, inheritance documentation, or a business invoice. Keep these on hand if you are making large transfers.

### Currency export from the Netherlands to non-EU countries

The Netherlands does not restrict the amount of money you can transfer abroad. However, Dutch banks may impose their own internal limits for due diligence. If you need to transfer a very large sum (EUR 100,000+), it is worth calling your bank or transfer service in advance to confirm the process and avoid delays.

### Tax implications

Money you transfer abroad from the Netherlands is not a taxable event in itself. However, maintaining foreign bank accounts with significant balances may have implications for your Dutch box 3 wealth tax (vermogensrendementsheffing), which taxes savings and investments held on 1 January each year. If you regularly keep large amounts in foreign accounts, check with a Dutch tax advisor. Our [guide to the Dutch tax system for expats](/guides/finance/dutch-tax-system-expats-guide-2026/) is a good starting point.

## International Transfers and Your Salary

Many expats in the Netherlands send a portion of their Dutch salary home each month. The mechanics of doing this efficiently — choosing the right transfer size, timing, and service — can meaningfully affect how much value you deliver to your family over a year.

A rough example: if you earn EUR 60,000 per year (EUR 5,000/month) and send EUR 1,000/month to the UK, the difference between using your bank (EUR 35/month in total costs) and Wise (EUR 7/month) is EUR 336/year. Over five years, that is EUR 1,680 in unnecessary fees.

This is not hypothetical — it is the kind of money my clients regularly leave on the table until someone walks them through the calculation. The [salary negotiation guide for expats in the Netherlands](/guides/work/salary-negotiation-netherlands-expats-2026/) covers how to maximise what you earn in the first place, so it is worth reading alongside this guide if you are at the salary negotiation stage.

## My Recommendations Summary

**For most expats sending money from the Netherlands:**

Use **Wise** as your primary transfer service for any cross-currency international transfer. It is the most cost-effective option for the widest range of destinations, transparent about its fees, properly regulated, and easy to use alongside your existing Dutch bank account.

**For euro transfers within Europe:** Use a standard SEPA bank transfer from your Dutch account. It is free and fast. No need for Wise or any other service.

**For small, frequent transfers (under EUR 500) to someone who also has Revolut:** Revolut instant transfers are free and convenient, particularly for splitting costs with friends and colleagues.

**For cash pickup in countries with limited banking:** Western Union remains the practical choice despite higher fees, particularly for Morocco, certain African countries, and rural areas in South/Southeast Asia.

**For speed-first transfers to India or the Philippines:** Compare Remitly Express directly against Wise for your specific amount on the day you transfer. Both are worth bookmarking.

**For large one-off transfers (EUR 20,000+):** Wise handles these well, but also get a comparison quote from OFX, which has no transfer fee and occasionally offers tighter exchange rate margins for very large transfers.
