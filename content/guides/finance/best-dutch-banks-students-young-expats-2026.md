## 1. ING Studentenrekening — Free Until 28, Most Used in the Netherlands

ING is where most Dutch students bank, and there is a practical reason for that: it is free, it works everywhere, and the app is completely in English. The ING Studentenrekening (student current account) costs nothing as long as you are enrolled in an HBO or WO programme and under 28.

What I like about ING is how invisible it is. The app works, contactless payments work, iDEAL works, and if anything goes wrong you can walk into a physical branch. When I was studying and needed to sort out a payment error, I could actually talk to a person. That matters more than you think.

**For international students specifically:** ING offers full English customer support and an English-language app. You can set up the account entirely online once you have your BSN.

### What the ING Student Account Includes

- Free current account (betaalrekening) while studying and under 28
- ING Mijn Geld app (English available) with spending categories
- iDEAL for Dutch online payments
- Optional savings account linked to the main account
- Access to all ING ATMs in the Netherlands free of charge
- Optional overdraft up to EUR 1,000 (rood staan, approximately 12% annual interest)

### The Catch: Age 28

On your 28th birthday, the account automatically converts to a standard account at EUR 2.45 per month. You get a notification in advance, but if you do nothing, you start paying. Most students from outside the Netherlands graduate and leave before 28, so this is rarely an issue — but international PhD students and those who start a Master's later should be aware.

**My take:** For most students in the Netherlands, ING is the default for a reason. It is free, reliable, and fully English. Start here if you want the path of least resistance.

## 2. ABN AMRO Young Banking Package — Free Until 30, More Flexibility

ABN AMRO's Young Banking package has one clear advantage over ING: it is free until age 30, not 28. For postgraduate students, PhD candidates, and young expats who arrive in their mid-twenties, those extra two years matter.

I have had clients who switched from ING to ABN AMRO specifically for this reason — if you are 27 when you arrive and plan to stay a few years, ABN AMRO gives you a longer free window before you need to reassess.

### What ABN AMRO Young Includes

- Free current account for 18-30 year olds (Studenten Betaalpakket or Jongerenpakket)
- English app and website
- iDEAL support
- Dutch NL IBAN — fully DUO compatible
- Optional Extras package for international transfers (at additional cost)
- Overdraft option up to EUR 1,500 for eligible students

### Where ABN AMRO Falls Short

The app is slightly less polished than ING's in English. Some screens and notifications default to Dutch, and the customer service wait times can be longer. If you are not in a major city, you may find fewer ABN AMRO ATMs nearby compared to ING.

For international money transfers, ABN AMRO's fees are noticeably high — typically EUR 7-15 per international transfer. This is where you use Wise instead.

**My take:** If you are between 28 and 30, ABN AMRO's free period is a real advantage. If you are under 28, the choice between ING and ABN AMRO mostly comes down to which app you prefer after downloading both and trying them.

[Open an ABN AMRO young account](https://www.abnamro.nl/nl/prive/betalen/betaalpakketten/studentenpakket.html)

## 3. bunq — The Best Choice If You Want Control Over Your Money

bunq is not free. At EUR 2.99 per month for the Easy Money plan (the minimum you need for sub-accounts), it costs more than ING or ABN AMRO. But for students who actually want to budget seriously, it is worth considering.

The feature that makes bunq genuinely useful for students is the Money tabs system. You can create up to 25 separate accounts under one subscription. Not virtual categories like most banking apps — actual separate accounts, each with their own balance. I set mine up like this when I was testing it:

- **Rent account**: my share of rent sits here, paid by direct debit
- **Groceries**: I move EUR 150 here at the start of each week
- **Going out**: EUR 80 per week, and when it is gone, it is gone
- **Emergency fund**: untouched unless something breaks
- **Travel savings**: building up for trips home

The psychological effect of physically separated money is underrated. It is much harder to raid your rent account when it literally is a different account with a different card.

### bunq's Other Advantages for Young Expats

- Full Dutch NL IBAN from day one (after BSN verification)
- iDEAL support
- DUO compatible
- Free international bank transfers using the mid-market rate (up to EUR 10,000/month on Easy Money)
- 24/7 in-app support that actually responds quickly
- Carbon footprint tracking (they plant trees based on spending — genuinely interesting, not just marketing)

### The Honest Downsides

EUR 2.99/month might seem small, but over a three-year degree that is EUR 107. If you are on a tight student budget, ING's free account does the job. bunq also does not offer an overdraft. And the sign-up process requires your BSN — you cannot use it as your first account before you register in the Netherlands.

**My take:** If you are the type who actually budgets and wants control, bunq's sub-account system is genuinely the best tool available in the Netherlands. If you just want a free account that works, go ING.

## 4. N26 — Free but With a Significant Limitation

N26 is popular among young Europeans because the Standard account is free and sign-up takes about eight minutes. I understand the appeal. But for anyone living in the Netherlands specifically, there is a problem you need to know about upfront.

**N26 gives you a German IBAN, not a Dutch one.** This matters in three specific situations:

1. **DUO payments**: DUO requires an NL IBAN. You cannot receive student finance into N26.
2. **iDEAL**: N26 does not support iDEAL. This means you cannot pay through the most common Dutch online checkout system.
3. **Some employers**: A minority of Dutch employers still specifically request an NL IBAN for salary payments. Most accept any EU IBAN, but not all.

If you already have a Dutch IBAN from ING or ABN AMRO, N26 as a free secondary card works reasonably well — especially for travel and spending in other EU countries. The Metal plan (EUR 16.90/month) includes travel insurance and other perks if you travel frequently.

### When N26 Makes Sense for Students

- You already have a Dutch IBAN elsewhere and want a free backup card
- You are in the Netherlands for only a semester (Erasmus student) and do not need DUO
- You travel frequently across Europe and want easy multi-currency spending

For a full comparison of your digital banking options, see [bunq vs N26 for expats](/guides/finance/bunq-vs-n26-expats-netherlands-2026/).

**My take:** N26 is not suitable as your primary account in the Netherlands. Use it as a secondary card if you want, but you need a Dutch IBAN elsewhere.

- iDEAL: limited support (some merchants accept it via direct debit, many do not)

Use Wise for international transfers and as a bridge account before your Dutch account is ready. Do not use it as your only account in the Netherlands.

## The DUO Question: Which Banks Can Receive Dutch Student Finance

This comes up constantly with international students, so I want to give it its own section.

**DUO (Dienst Uitvoering Onderwijs)** is the Dutch government body that administers student loans and grants. They pay out monthly to a bank account you register with them.

**DUO requires a Dutch NL IBAN.** Full stop.

| Bank | DUO Compatible? | IBAN Format |
|------|-----------------|-------------|
| ING | Yes | NL |
| ABN AMRO | Yes | NL |
| bunq | Yes | NL |
| Rabobank | Yes | NL |
| N26 | No | DE |
| Wise | No | BE or other EU |
| Revolut | No | LT |

If you are an EU/EEA student eligible for Dutch student finance (studiefinanciering), registering a non-NL IBAN with DUO will result in your payments being rejected or delayed. Set up your ING or ABN AMRO account as soon as you have your BSN and register that IBAN with DUO before the first payment date.

If you are a non-EU international student paying tuition from abroad and not relying on DUO, this is less of an issue — though you will still need a Dutch IBAN eventually for rent, utilities, and Dutch subscriptions.

## Part-Time Work and Student Finances: What You Need to Know

Most students in the Netherlands work part-time alongside their studies (bijbaan). Here is what this means for your banking:

**Receiving wages:** Dutch employers pay salary once or twice a month via bank transfer. Most will accept any EU IBAN, but having an NL IBAN makes admin simpler. Some smaller employers (cafes, retail jobs) still specifically request an NL IBAN.

**Tax and toeslagen:** Once you start earning, you may be eligible for toeslagen (government benefits like zorgtoeslag for healthcare contributions). See the [guide to zorgtoeslag and huurtoeslag for expats](/guides/finance/zorgtoeslag-huurtoeslag-expats-2026/) — these require an NL IBAN too.

**Budgeting with a student income:** The bunq sub-account approach I described earlier works particularly well when you have a predictable but small income. Set up automatic transfers on payday: rent first, then groceries, then discretionary spending. Whatever remains goes into savings.

**Building a credit history:** Dutch banks do not use a traditional credit score system like in the US or UK. However, having a Dutch bank account, being registered at a Dutch address, and maintaining consistent financial behaviour does influence your ability to get a mortgage or overdraft facility later. Start establishing your Dutch financial footprint early.

## Common Mistakes Young Expats Make With Dutch Banking

**Relying only on a foreign card:** Your home bank's contactless card might work, but iDEAL will not, and many Dutch online payments require it. You will hit this wall within your first week.

**Waiting to open an account until everything is "sorted":** Open Wise before you fly. Open ING or ABN AMRO the same week you get your BSN. Do not wait until you need the account urgently.

**Ignoring the age cutoff on free accounts:** If you turn 28 while banking with ING, your account starts charging automatically. Set a calendar reminder to review your banking options before your birthday.

**Using N26 for DUO:** This is a surprisingly common mistake among students who already had N26 from studying elsewhere in Europe. N26 looks like it works — until DUO rejects the IBAN and your student finance payment bounces.

**Not using Wise for money from home:** If your parents send you EUR 500 from the UK or US every month, the difference between a bank transfer and Wise over a year can be EUR 100-200. That is two months of groceries.
