## The Core Problem: IBAN Acceptance in the Netherlands

Before getting into fees and features, there is one practical reality that shapes everything else in this comparison.

The Netherlands has a higher-than-average rate of IBAN discrimination in practice. Despite EU regulations requiring businesses to accept any valid SEPA IBAN, many Dutch landlords, utilities, and employers continue to reject non-Dutch IBANs.

Here is what each app gives you:

| App | IBAN Country | IBAN Prefix |
|-----|-------------|-------------|
| Wise | Belgium | BE |
| Revolut | Lithuania | LT |
| bunq | Netherlands | NL |

This single fact — bunq gives you a Dutch NL IBAN — is the most important thing in this entire article for anyone who wants to use one of these apps as their primary bank account in the Netherlands.

Let me walk through exactly what this means in practice.

### Where IBAN Acceptance Causes Real Problems

**Rent payments:** Many Dutch landlords and housing corporations (woningcorporaties) only accept payments from Dutch NL IBANs. This is illegal under EU SEPA regulations, but enforcement is weak and fighting your landlord over it is not a battle most expats want during their first weeks in the country.

**Utilities and telecom:** Vattenfall, Eneco, Nuon, KPN, T-Mobile NL — many of these process direct debits by automatic incasso. Some will reject foreign IBANs outright. Others will accept them but flag them as irregular, leading to payment delays.

**Employer payroll:** Some Dutch employers' payroll software (especially smaller companies using Dutch-specific payroll tools) only processes NL IBANs for salary transfers. Your HR department may tell you this is not possible to change.

**Gym memberships, subscriptions:** Less critical, but the same pattern applies to recurring direct debits across the Netherlands.

Revolut's Lithuanian IBAN is the most problematic of the three. Belgium is a neighbouring country with extensive cross-border economic ties; a Belgian IBAN raises fewer eyebrows in practice than a Lithuanian one. But neither is a Dutch IBAN.

### Revolut's Strengths for Netherlands Expats

**Budgeting features:** Revolut's spending analytics are the most detailed of the three. Automatic categorisation, weekly spending summaries, and savings pockets make it genuinely useful for expats tracking their Dutch cost of living.

**App polish:** The Revolut app is the most intuitive and feature-rich of the three. If you spend time in the app, you will appreciate the interface.

**Travel:** For expats who travel frequently — within Europe or to home countries — Revolut is hard to beat. Multi-currency holdings, good exchange rates on weekdays, and broad global acceptance make it excellent for trips.

**Crypto and investments:** If you want to buy ETFs or crypto within your fintech app, Revolut has this built in (with caveats about regulatory status).

### Revolut's Weaknesses for Netherlands Expats

**Lithuanian IBAN is a genuine daily problem:** Of the three apps, Revolut's LT IBAN causes the most friction in the Netherlands. Many Dutch landlords, woningcorporaties, utilities and employers will reject it. I have spoken with multiple expats who have missed rent payment deadlines because their landlord's bank rejected the Revolut transfer.

**Weekend markup:** Revolut adds a markup to exchange rates on weekends. If you travel or transfer money on a Saturday or Sunday, you lose some of the rate advantage over Wise.

**Not a bank:** Like Wise, Revolut is not a licensed bank in most countries. Revolut Bank UAB (Lithuania) does hold a banking licence in the EU, so there is some deposit protection — but this is a relatively new development and the coverage for Dutch residents is not as simple as a Dutch bank account.

**Customer service reputation:** Revolut has faced persistent criticism for slow and bot-heavy customer support. For everyday transactions this matters little, but if your account is frozen or you have a dispute, the response can be slow.

### Bottom Line for Revolut

**Best for:** Travel spending, budgeting and spending analytics, crypto and investments as a side feature, weekday currency exchange.

**Not suitable as:** Your primary bank account for Dutch rent, utilities, or salary receipt.

### bunq's Strengths for Netherlands Expats

**Dutch NL IBAN:** This solves the IBAN discrimination problem completely. Your landlord, utility provider, employer, and gym will all accept it without question.

**Sub-accounts:** bunq's signature feature. You can create up to 25 separate IBANs, each for a different purpose — rent, groceries, savings, utilities. This makes it extremely useful for budgeting in the Dutch system where many bills are direct debit.

**Full banking licence:** Your funds are covered by the Dutch DGS up to EUR 100,000. This is genuine bank-level security.

**Cost:** €10.99 per month for the main plan is the highest monthly fee of the three. Compared to free accounts at ING or ABN AMRO (if you can open one), this is a real consideration.

**Customer support:** Like many neobanks, bunq's customer support is app-based and can be slow. However, it has improved significantly from its earlier reputation.

### Bottom Line for bunq

**Best for:** Primary Dutch banking, rent payments, direct debits, salary receipt, sub-account budgeting, anyone who wants a genuine Dutch bank experience without the bureaucracy of ING or ABN AMRO.

**Not suitable for:** Cheap international transfers, use before you have a BSN, travel if cost is a concern.

**Choose Wise** for the first weeks. Open it before you arrive, use it for your initial expenses, and receive any transfers or salary while you wait for your BSN.

## Frequently Asked Questions: Real-World Scenarios

### "I just got my BSN — what should I open first?"

Open bunq immediately. The Dutch NL IBAN is the bottleneck that blocks everything else — rent payments, salary receipt, utility direct debits. Get it sorted within the first week of having your BSN.

Keep your Wise account open (if you already have one) for international transfers, but prioritise bunq for Dutch daily life.

### "My HR department told me they can only pay salary to a Dutch NL IBAN. What do I do?"

This is bunq's territory. Your HR department is technically wrong under EU SEPA law — they must accept any valid SEPA IBAN. But picking a fight with payroll during your first month at a new job is rarely worth it.

Open a bunq account (Dutch NL IBAN), give that to HR, and your salary problem is resolved immediately. If you want to keep Wise for transfers, receive your salary into bunq and move funds to Wise when needed.

### "I have a Revolut account already. Should I close it when I move to the Netherlands?"

Keep it, but demote it to secondary status. Revolut's budgeting tools and multi-currency features remain useful for travel and tracking spending. But do not rely on it for anything requiring a Dutch IBAN — rent, utilities, Dutch employer salary, or government payments.

The total cost of keeping Revolut Standard is zero. There is no reason to close it. Just add bunq (and optionally Wise) to cover the Dutch-specific gaps.

### "I work remotely for a UK company and get paid in GBP. What is the best setup?"

This is Wise's strongest use case. Give your UK employer your Wise GBP account details (sort code and account number). Your salary lands in your Wise GBP balance. Convert to EUR as needed at mid-market rate. Fund your bunq NL account from Wise for Dutch bills.

This setup avoids both the international wire fees that ING/ABN AMRO charge for incoming GBP transfers and the currency conversion markup. On a GBP 40,000 annual salary, the saving versus using a Dutch bank for GBP receipt and conversion is EUR 600–1,200 per year.

### "Which of the three apps is best for a partner who is new to banking apps?"

Wise has the simplest, most focused interface. Revolut is more feature-rich but can feel overwhelming. Bunq's interface has improved significantly but is still more complex than the others.

If a partner has limited experience with digital banking and primarily needs a Dutch account for daily life, bunq's interface is functional enough. If they will be doing international transfers regularly, a Wise tutorial upfront is time well spent — the interface is genuinely intuitive once you understand what it does.

## Understanding the Dutch Banking Ecosystem: Context That Helps

To get the most from any of these three apps, it helps to understand the Dutch payment landscape these tools are operating within.

**iDEAL 2.0 (launched 2024):** The Netherlands upgraded its iDEAL system in 2024 to a European standard that theoretically accepts more banks. In practice, bunq has full iDEAL 2.0 support. Wise and Revolut still do not integrate as iDEAL sources — this is a structural limitation, not a temporary oversight.

**Instant Payments (SCT Inst):** The Netherlands moved to near-universal instant SEPA payments in 2024. Transfers between Dutch banks now complete in seconds rather than hours. Bunq was an early adopter of instant payments. Wise SEPA transfers are instant for many transactions within the eurozone. Revolut SEPA transfers are also fast. For routine euro transfers within Europe, all three perform similarly on speed.

**Belastingdienst (Tax Authority) IBAN requirements:** When you receive a Dutch tax refund or need to pay outstanding tax, the Belastingdienst allows any valid SEPA IBAN. Bunq, Wise, and theoretically Revolut all work for this purpose. However, in practice some older Belastingdienst systems have shown sensitivity to non-NL IBANs for certain payment flows. If you ever encounter a tax payment issue, bunq's NL IBAN removes the variable.

**DigiD and banking integration:** DigiD (the Dutch government's digital identity system) sometimes links to your banking app for identity verification. ING and ABN AMRO are fully integrated. Bunq has a DigiD integration. Wise and Revolut do not support DigiD authentication — you will need a traditional Dutch bank or bunq for any process requiring bank-linked DigiD verification.
