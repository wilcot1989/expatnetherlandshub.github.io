## How Dutch Savings Accounts Work

Before jumping into the comparison, let me explain two things that confuse almost every expat I work with.

### Spaarrekening vs. Deposito

In the Netherlands, you will encounter two main types of savings products:

**Spaarrekening (savings account):** A flexible account where you can deposit and withdraw money at any time. Interest rates are variable, meaning the bank can change them. This is what most people need for their emergency fund and general savings.

**Deposito (fixed-term deposit):** You lock your money away for a set period — typically 1, 2, 3, 5, or 10 years. In return, you get a higher, fixed interest rate. If you withdraw early, you pay a penalty (often losing several months of interest). This works for money you are absolutely certain you will not need.

Most expats should start with a spaarrekening. If you have savings beyond your emergency fund that you will not touch for at least a year, a deposito can earn you more — but only if you are confident you are staying in the Netherlands for the full term.

### Why Dutch Interest Rates Feel Low

If you come from the UK, US, or Australia, Dutch savings rates may look underwhelming. There is a reason for this. The big three Dutch banks — ING, ABN AMRO, and Rabobank — dominate the market with a combined 70 percent-plus share. With that much concentration, there is limited competitive pressure to raise rates. Online-only banks like NIBC Direct and platforms like Raisin exist partly to fill this gap.

The European Central Bank (ECB) base rate sits at 2.65 percent as of mid-2026, and Dutch banks pass on less of this to savers than banks in many other EU countries. This is frustrating, but it is the reality.

## Tax on Savings: Box 3 Vermogensbelasting Explained

This is the section every expat needs to read carefully, because Dutch savings taxation works nothing like what you are used to.

### How Box 3 Works

The Netherlands does **not** tax your actual interest income. Instead, under box 3 of the income tax system, the Belastingdienst (Dutch tax authority) calculates a **fictional return** on your total assets — savings, investments, second property — and taxes that fictional return at approximately 36 percent.

Here is how it works in 2026:

**Step 1: Determine your assets.** Add up everything in box 3 — savings accounts, investment accounts, second homes, crypto, anything that is not your primary residence or pension.

**Step 2: Subtract the tax-free threshold.** In 2026, the first EUR 57,000 per person is exempt (EUR 114,000 for fiscal partners). This means if you and your partner have combined savings under EUR 114,000, you pay zero box 3 tax.

**Step 3: Calculate the fictional return.** The Belastingdienst splits your remaining assets into categories:
- Savings: fictional return of approximately 1.03 percent (2026 rate, based on actual average savings rates)
- Investments and other assets: fictional return of approximately 6.04 percent

**Step 4: Pay 36 percent tax on the fictional return.** Not on your actual earnings — on what the government assumes you earned.

### A Practical Example

You have EUR 80,000 in savings accounts and no other box 3 assets.

- Threshold: EUR 57,000
- Taxable amount: EUR 23,000
- Fictional return on savings: 1.03 percent = EUR 237
- Tax at 36 percent: EUR 85

So your total box 3 tax on EUR 80,000 in savings is EUR 85 per year. That is manageable — and much less than the EUR 1,400-2,120 you would earn in interest at ING or NIBC Direct.

**The key takeaway:** For most expats with savings under EUR 200,000, the tax burden is modest enough that earning interest is still very much worthwhile. Do not let box 3 scare you out of saving.

For a deeper explanation of the Dutch tax system, read my [full expat tax guide](/guides/finance/dutch-tax-system-expats-2026/).

### The Box 3 Legal Saga

You may have heard that box 3 has been controversial. In 2021, the Dutch Supreme Court ruled that the old fictional return system was unlawful because it taxed people on returns they never actually earned. The government has been working on a new system that taxes actual returns, but this has been repeatedly delayed. In 2026, we are still operating under a transitional system that uses category-based fictional returns. The government has announced that a system based on actual returns will be introduced in 2027 — but I will believe it when I see it, given the repeated delays.

## The Deposit Guarantee Scheme (DGS)

The Dutch Deposit Guarantee Scheme (Depositogarantiestelsel, DGS) protects your savings up to **EUR 100,000 per person per banking licence**. This is one of the most important things to understand when choosing where to put your money.

### What the DGS Covers

- Savings accounts (spaarrekeningen)
- Current accounts (betaalrekeningen)
- Fixed-term deposits (deposito's)
- Up to EUR 100,000 per person per banking licence

### What the DGS Does Not Cover

- Investments (stocks, bonds, funds)
- Crypto assets
- Money held at institutions without a banking licence (like Wise)
- Amounts above EUR 100,000 at a single bank

### Important Detail for Expats

Some banks in the Netherlands share a banking licence. For example, KNAB and ASN Bank both operate under the de Volksbank licence. If you have EUR 80,000 at ASN Bank and EUR 80,000 at KNAB, you are only protected up to EUR 100,000 in total across both, not EUR 100,000 at each.

The big three — ING, ABN AMRO, and Rabobank — each have their own separate licence, so EUR 100,000 at each of them means EUR 300,000 in total protection.

**For Raisin users:** Your deposits are placed with banks in other EU countries, which means the guarantee falls under that country's national scheme. The EUR 100,000 limit is standardised across the EU, but the guaranteeing entity is the foreign country's central bank, not De Nederlandsche Bank. This has never been a practical issue — EU deposit guarantee schemes have always paid out when needed — but it is worth being aware of.

## Smart Savings Strategies for Expats

### 1. The Ladder Approach

Instead of putting all your savings in one product, spread them:

- **Instant access (3-6 months expenses):** ING or bunq spaarrekening — you need this money available immediately in a real emergency
- **Medium-term (6-12 months):** NIBC Direct flexible savings — higher rate, 1-2 day access is fine since it is not your emergency fund
- **Long-term (1+ years):** Raisin 1-year deposito — lock it up for the best rate

This way you earn more on money you do not need immediately while keeping enough accessible for emergencies.

### 2. Spread Across Multiple Banks

If your savings exceed EUR 100,000, you must spread them across banks with different banking licences to maintain full DGS protection. Even below that threshold, having accounts at two or three banks means you are never locked out entirely if one bank has a technical issue.

### 3. Automate Your Savings

Set up an automatic transfer on salary day. I transfer money to three places on the 25th of every month:

- EUR 500 to NIBC Direct (long-term savings)
- EUR 200 to bunq sub-account (holiday fund)
- EUR 300 to ING Oranje Spaarrekening (emergency fund top-up)

The money moves before I can spend it, which is the entire point.

### 4. Emergency Fund First

Before chasing the highest rates, build an emergency fund of 3-6 months of living expenses in a flexible, instantly accessible account. For the Netherlands, I recommend at least EUR 5,000-8,000 for a single person or EUR 10,000-15,000 for a family. The [cost of living in the Netherlands](/guides/finance/cost-of-living-netherlands-2026/) guide can help you calculate your specific number.

### 5. Consider Your Currency Mix

If you still receive income in GBP, USD, or another currency, do not automatically convert everything to euros. Wise pays 3.85 percent on GBP and 4.35 percent on USD — more than double what ING pays on euros. If you have GBP savings you are not converting soon, letting them earn interest at Wise is smarter than converting to euros and earning 1.75 percent.

## How the 30% Ruling Affects Your Savings

If you have the [30% ruling](/guides/work/30-percent-ruling-guide-2026/), you have a significant advantage when it comes to savings tax.

### Partial Non-Resident Taxpayer Status

Under the 30% ruling, you can opt for partial non-resident taxpayer status (partieel buitenlands belastingplichtige). This means:

- Your box 3 assets (savings, investments, property outside the Netherlands) are **exempt** from Dutch taxation
- You only pay box 3 tax on Dutch real estate (if you own property in the Netherlands other than your primary residence)
- This applies for the duration of your 30% ruling (currently up to 5 years, with the rate stepping down after 20 months)

### The Catch: Plan for the End

When your 30% ruling ends, all your savings suddenly fall into box 3 and become taxable. If you have accumulated significant savings during the ruling period, the transition can be a shock. I have seen clients go from zero box 3 tax to EUR 2,000+ overnight.

**My advice:** Start treating your savings as if they were taxable before the ruling ends. Set aside money for the tax bill, or consider restructuring your assets (moving money into pension products, paying down your mortgage, or investing in your primary residence) before the ruling expires.

For more on the 30% ruling, including the latest 2026 changes, read my [detailed guide](/guides/work/30-percent-ruling-guide-2026/).

## Opening a Dutch Savings Account: Step by Step

3. Once approved, add a savings account (spaarrekening) through the app

1. Go to nibcdirect.nl and click "Spaarrekening openen" (Open savings account)
2. You need: BSN, Dutch address, valid ID (passport or EU ID card), existing Dutch bank account
3. Complete the online application — fully digital, no branch visit
4. NIBC will verify your identity via iDIN (a secure identity check through your existing Dutch bank)
5. Transfer at least EUR 250 to activate your account

### For Raisin

1. Register at raisin.nl with your email
2. Complete identity verification (BSN, ID, proof of address)

## Common Mistakes Expats Make with Dutch Savings

After helping hundreds of expats with their finances, these are the mistakes I see repeatedly:

**1. Leaving money in the current account.** Most Dutch current accounts pay zero or near-zero interest. Moving EUR 20,000 from your betaalrekening to a spaarrekening takes two minutes and earns you EUR 350-530 per year, depending on the bank. There is no reason not to.

**2. Only using the big three.** Brand recognition is comforting, but the interest rate gap is real. ING's 1.75 percent versus NIBC Direct's 2.65 percent on EUR 50,000 is a EUR 450 per year difference. Would you turn down EUR 450 for 15 minutes of account setup?

**3. Ignoring box 3 thresholds.** If you and your partner together have savings just above EUR 114,000, restructuring (contributing to a pension, paying down mortgage) could bring you below the threshold and eliminate box 3 tax entirely.

**4. Not using the 30% ruling advantage.** If you have the 30% ruling and are not using the partial non-resident status for box 3 exemption, you are leaving money on the table. Check with your tax advisor.

**5. Keeping all savings in one bank.** Beyond the DGS protection argument, having savings at multiple banks gives you access to different rates and products. It takes minimal effort and provides real diversification.

**6. Converting currency unnecessarily.** If you have GBP or USD savings, check whether the interest rate in that currency (especially via Wise) beats what you would earn converting to euros. Often it does.

**7. Not having an emergency fund.** I cannot count the number of expats who have come to me after an unexpected job loss or medical bill with zero accessible savings. Before optimising interest rates, make sure you have 3-6 months of expenses in a flexible account. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I open a Dutch savings account without a BSN?

Traditional Dutch banks require a BSN for any savings product. However, Wise allows you to earn interest on your balance without a BSN or Dutch residency. If you have not yet received your BSN, open a Wise account first and move to a Dutch spaarrekening once your BSN arrives.

### Is my savings account in the Netherlands guaranteed?

Yes. The Dutch Deposit Guarantee Scheme protects up to EUR 100,000 per person per banking licence. This applies to all banks with a Dutch licence — ING, ABN AMRO, Rabobank, bunq, and NIBC Direct included. For Raisin deposits placed with EU banks, the guarantee falls under that country's national scheme (same EUR 100,000 limit).

### Do I pay tax on savings interest in the Netherlands?

The Netherlands does not tax actual interest earned. Under box 3, the tax office assumes a fictional return on your total assets above the tax-free threshold (EUR 57,000 per person in 2026). You pay approximately 36 percent on this fictional return. For most expats, the actual tax burden is modest — see the worked example in the box 3 section above.

### What is the difference between a spaarrekening and a deposito?

A spaarrekening is a flexible savings account with instant or near-instant access. A deposito locks your money for a fixed period (1-10 years) at a higher rate. Early withdrawal from a deposito usually costs you several months of interest as a penalty.

### How does the 30% ruling affect savings tax?

With the 30% ruling, you can opt for partial non-resident taxpayer status, which exempts your box 3 assets from Dutch tax. This is a significant benefit that disappears when the ruling ends — plan your transition carefully.

### Which savings account has the highest interest rate in the Netherlands?

For flexible savings, NIBC Direct leads among Dutch banks at 2.65 percent. Raisin offers access to EU banks paying up to 3.10 percent on flexible savings and 3.50 percent on 1-year deposits. For non-EUR currencies, Wise pays 3.85 percent on GBP and 4.35 percent on USD.

### Can I have savings accounts at multiple banks?

Yes, and I recommend it. Each bank covers up to EUR 100,000 per person under the DGS. Multiple accounts also let you take advantage of different interest rates and keep your emergency fund separate from your long-term savings.

### Is Raisin safe for expats?

Raisin is a German fintech regulated by BaFin that has managed over EUR 50 billion in deposits since 2013. It is an intermediary — your deposits are held by the underlying banks, each with their own deposit guarantee. The platform has a strong track record and operates fully in English.

### Do Dutch savings accounts have minimum deposit requirements?

ING, ABN AMRO, and Rabobank allow you to start with EUR 1. NIBC Direct requires EUR 250. Raisin minimums vary by bank but typically start at EUR 1,000. bunq and Wise have no minimum.

### Should I keep my emergency fund in a Dutch savings account?

Absolutely. Keep 3-6 months of expenses in a flexible Dutch savings account with instant access — ING or bunq works well for this. Do not use a deposito or Raisin for your emergency fund, as access is not immediate.

### What happens to my savings if I leave the Netherlands?

Most banks allow you to keep existing accounts after moving abroad, though terms may change. Check your specific bank's policy before relocating. Consider moving funds to Wise or your home country bank if the bank plans to close your account.

### Is it worth opening a savings account for small amounts?

Yes. Even on EUR 5,000, the difference between zero interest (current account) and 1.75 percent (ING spaarrekening) is EUR 87.50 per year. It takes two minutes to set up. There is no minimum where it stops being worthwhile — any amount earning interest is better than any amount earning nothing.
