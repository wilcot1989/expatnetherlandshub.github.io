## The Story That Made Me Take Business Banking Seriously

A client of mine — I'll call her Priya — moved to Amsterdam from London in 2023. She registered her KvK within a month of arriving, started picking up consultancy work almost immediately, and felt rightly proud of how smoothly her freelance career was taking off in the Netherlands.

Then, about eighteen months in, she got a letter from the Belastingdienst.

They had noticed that her BTW (VAT) records didn't quite line up with the transactions flowing through her personal ING account. She'd been using her personal account for everything — client invoices coming in, Adobe subscriptions going out, the occasional coffee with a client. She thought it would be fine "until she got more established."

It was not fine. The audit took four months, involved reconstructing two years of bank statements, and cost her roughly €800 in accountant fees — not because she had done anything dishonest, but because the Belastingdienst couldn't easily distinguish her business transactions from her personal ones.

The moment Priya told me this story, I started including business bank accounts in every single onboarding conversation I have with new freelancers. It's the kind of thing that feels optional until it suddenly isn't.

This guide is what I wish Priya had read before she started.

## Why a Separate Business Account Matters for ZZP Freelancers

If you're operating as a **eenmanszaak** (sole trader) in the Netherlands, you are not legally required to have a separate business bank account. The law treats you and your business as one entity. But that legal fact creates a practical trap that catches a surprising number of expat freelancers.

Here's the reality: the Belastingdienst expects clean, auditable records. When your quarterly BTW declaration references transactions mixed in with your Spotify subscription and grocery shopping, you're making the tax office's job harder — and they will, at some point, make yours harder in return.

Beyond tax compliance, there are practical reasons to separate your accounts:

**Professional credibility.** Sending invoices from a personal account with your first and last name looks unprofessional to larger Dutch clients and corporates. A dedicated business account — especially one with your company name on it — signals that you take your work seriously.

**Expense tracking.** When all your business income and outgoings are in one place, calculating your actual profit (and your provisional income tax instalments) becomes much less painful. Your bookkeeper will charge you less, too.

**Cash flow clarity.** Knowing at a glance how much money is genuinely "business money" versus your personal savings prevents the common freelance mistake of accidentally spending your tax reserve.

## What to Look for in a Dutch Business Bank Account

After helping dozens of expat freelancers set up their finances in the Netherlands, I've narrowed down the key criteria that matter in practice:

### 1. KvK Registration Requirement
Almost every Dutch business bank account requires a KvK (Kamer van Koophandel) number. If you haven't registered yet, that's your first step. My [KvK registration guide for expats](/guides/work/kvk-registration-expat-guide-2026/) walks you through the whole process — it's simpler than you might expect.

### 2. Monthly Fees
Business accounts always cost more than personal ones. Monthly fees range from free (Wise, N26) to €15–20 per month for traditional banks. Whether the extra features justify the cost depends entirely on your transaction volume and needs.

### 3. English Language Support
For expat freelancers, this is often the deciding factor. Some of the traditional Dutch banks are almost entirely Dutch-language — which is manageable once you know the system, but exhausting when you're just starting out.

### 4. Multi-Currency Capabilities
If you have clients in the UK, US, or elsewhere in Europe outside the eurozone, the ability to receive and hold payments in multiple currencies without paying punishing conversion fees is genuinely valuable. This is where the fintech options pull far ahead of the traditional banks.

### 5. Accounting Integration
Connecting your bank directly to your bookkeeping software (Moneybird, Exact Online, Twinfield, QuickBooks) saves hours every month. Most modern banks offer this; the traditional banks vary in how smoothly it actually works.

### 6. Invoicing Features
Some business accounts include built-in invoicing tools. Useful if you're just starting out and not yet using dedicated invoicing software.

### 7. Opening Requirements and Speed
Can you open the account online or do you need to visit a branch? How long does verification take? For a newly registered ZZP freelancer eager to start invoicing, waiting three weeks for a bank account to open is a real problem.

## The KvK Requirement — What You Actually Need

Every single business bank on this list requires your KvK registration. This is non-negotiable in the Netherlands — banks are legally required to verify your business registration as part of their KYC (Know Your Customer) checks.

The good news: registering as a ZZP eenmanszaak (sole trader) at the KvK is fast, costs around €90, and can often be done with an appointment at your local KvK office in less than a week. I've written a detailed walkthrough in my [KvK registration guide for expats](/guides/work/kvk-registration-expat-guide-2026/).

What you'll need for the bank application after KvK registration:

- Your **KvK uittreksel** (KvK extract) — downloadable from kvk.nl
- Your **BSN** (Burgerservicenummer) — your Dutch citizen service number
- A valid **passport or EU ID card**
- **Proof of Dutch address** — gemeente registration confirmation (BRP uittreksel) or a recent utility bill works for most banks
- Some banks also request **DigiD** for identity verification

A common question I get from recent arrivals: "Can I open a business account before I have a BSN?" The answer is no — you need your BSN first. This is often the bottleneck for newly arrived expats, so register at your gemeente as soon as you arrive.

## BTW (VAT) Considerations for ZZP Freelancers

If your annual revenue exceeds €20.000, you'll need to register for BTW and charge VAT to Dutch clients. (There are exceptions — certain professions like healthcare, education, and some creative services may be exempt.)

Having a business bank account makes BTW administration significantly easier because:

1. You can see clearly which income is subject to BTW and which isn't
2. Accounting software can automatically calculate your BTW position based on real bank transactions
3. During a Belastingdienst audit (like Priya's), you can produce clean, business-only records

One practical tip: most accountants recommend setting up a separate sub-account or savings pot specifically for your BTW reserves. bunq makes this very easy with their multiple sub-accounts feature — you can automatically sweep 21% of every incoming payment into a "BTW pot" so you're never caught short at quarter-end.

## Common Mistakes Expat Freelancers Make with Business Banking

**Using a personal account for business transactions.** You know this one by now — Priya's story at the top of this guide covers why.

**Waiting until they're "more established."** I hear this constantly. The right time to open a business account is the week you register your KvK — not three months later when you've already mixed six months of transactions.

**Choosing only on monthly fee.** The cheapest account isn't always the best value. If a €10/month account saves your accountant two hours a year, it's paid for itself many times over.

**Not checking IBAN compatibility.** Opening a German or Lithuanian IBAN account (N26, some Revolut plans) can create friction with Dutch payment systems. Ask specifically about IBAN country when opening an account.

**Ignoring the accounting integration.** Opening a great account and then manually exporting CSV files every month defeats the point. Make sure your bank connects properly to your bookkeeping setup before committing.

## Opening a Business Account Step by Step

Here's the practical process I walk my clients through:

**Step 1: Register at the KvK**
If you haven't already, register your eenmanszaak at kvk.nl. Book an appointment online — typical wait times are 3–10 days. Bring your passport and proof of Dutch address. Cost: approximately €90.

**Step 2: Download your KvK uittreksel**
Immediately after registration, log in to kvk.nl and download your KvK extract. You'll need this for every bank application.

**Step 3: Gather your documents**
Passport, BSN, KvK uittreksel, proof of Dutch address. Have them in digital format (clear PDF scans or photos) ready to upload.

**Step 4: Choose your bank and apply online**
For bunq or Wise, go directly to their websites and complete the online application. For traditional banks, you may need to book an appointment or visit a branch.

**Step 5: Complete identity verification**
Most banks use video verification or NFC scanning of your passport chip. Have your phone charged and your documents to hand.

**Step 6: Set up accounting integration**
As soon as your account is open and you receive your login details, connect it to your bookkeeping software. Don't wait — this is the step most people put off and then regret.

**Step 7: Notify existing clients**
Send a short, professional email to any existing clients with your new business IBAN for future invoices.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I legally need a separate business bank account as a ZZP freelancer in the Netherlands?**
Legally, sole traders (eenmanszaak) are not obliged to keep a separate business account. However, the Belastingdienst strongly recommends it, and mixing accounts frequently triggers extra scrutiny during tax audits. Most accountants consider it non-negotiable in practice.

**Can I open a Dutch business bank account as a non-EU expat?**
Yes, but it depends on the bank. Bunq Business and Wise Business are the most foreigner-friendly — they accept non-EU residents provided you hold a valid Dutch BSN and KvK registration. Traditional banks like ING and ABN AMRO may require proof of Dutch residency and an in-person appointment.

**What documents do I need to open a ZZP business bank account in the Netherlands?**
Generally: valid passport or EU ID, your KvK registration number (or extract), Dutch BSN, proof of Dutch address (such as a utility bill or gemeente registration), and in some cases a DigiD. Online-first banks typically require fewer documents than the traditional ones.

**How long does it take to open a business bank account in the Netherlands?**
Bunq Business and Wise Business can be open and functional within 1–3 business days. Traditional banks — ING, ABN AMRO, Rabobank — often take 5–15 business days due to manual KYC checks and sometimes a required branch visit.

**Do Dutch business banks offer English-language support?**
Bunq, Wise, and Revolut all offer fully English interfaces and English customer support. ING has an English website and app, though some support agents switch to Dutch under pressure. ABN AMRO and Rabobank are primarily Dutch-language for day-to-day banking.

**Which business bank account is best for receiving payments in multiple currencies?**
Wise Business is the clear frontrunner for multi-currency — you get local account details in 10+ currencies including EUR, GBP, USD, and AUD. Revolut Business is a strong second. Traditional Dutch banks charge significant conversion fees and are poorly suited for multi-currency freelancers.

**Can I use Wise Business as my only business account for ZZP purposes in the Netherlands?**
Many expat ZZP freelancers do exactly this. Wise Business gives you a Dutch IBAN, multi-currency accounts, invoicing tools, and accounting integrations. The one caveat: some Dutch clients still expect a traditional IBAN starting with NL from a recognised bank. In practice this is rarely a problem, but worth knowing.
