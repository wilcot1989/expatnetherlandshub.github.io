## The Story That Made Me Write This Review

A client of mine — I will call her Priya — moved from London to Amsterdam in late 2024 for a tech job. Smart woman, organised. She had read about Revolut before she arrived, set up an account during her flight, and gave her new employer the Revolut IBAN for salary payments.

Her first payslip arrived on time. Her second did not. Her payroll department sent her an apologetic email: their system had automatically flagged her IBAN as non-Dutch and refused to process it. Getting it fixed took three weeks of back-and-forth between Priya, the Dutch HR team, and the payroll processor. During those three weeks, she was living off savings, embarrassed to raise it again, and increasingly stressed.

Priya had done everything right by most people's standards. She had a functioning European bank account. She had provided the correct details. The IBAN was legally valid. And yet there she was, three weeks without a salary, in a city with EUR 1,800-a-month rent to pay.

This happens constantly. And it is entirely predictable once you understand the Lithuanian IBAN problem.

## The Big Issue: Revolut's Lithuanian IBAN

Revolut is a UK-founded company that obtained its EU banking licence via Lithuania. As a result, when you open a Revolut account in the Netherlands, your IBAN starts with LT — the country code for Lithuania.

This is legal. Under EU Regulation 260/2012, any SEPA-compliant IBAN must be accepted for payments anywhere in the EU. Dutch businesses are not allowed to reject a Lithuanian, Belgian, or German IBAN and demand a Dutch one. This practice is called IBAN discrimination, and it is prohibited.

The problem is that "prohibited" and "doesn't happen" are very different things.

In practice, a meaningful number of Dutch employers, landlords, and utility companies either:

- Have payroll or billing systems that were set up years ago and only accept NL IBANs
- Have staff who simply do not know the regulation and refuse out of habit
- Have systems that flag non-NL IBANs automatically as potential fraud

I have seen all three happen. The worst cases are when an automated payroll system rejects the IBAN without any human decision being made. You cannot argue with a computer. You have to escalate to an IT department that often has no idea how to fix it quickly.

### Why It Matters More Than You Might Think

When you first arrive in the Netherlands, you need to sort a lot of things at once: registration at the gemeente, your BSN, health insurance, a rental contract, and your salary setup. The last thing you need is a banking problem adding to that list.

Dutch employers expect things to work. Dutch payroll systems are not especially flexible. And Dutch landlords, particularly in the private rental market, often set up direct debits through their own bank and have zero tolerance for complications.

If you give a Dutch landlord your Revolut LT IBAN for the monthly rent direct debit, there is a reasonable chance they will come back to you saying it does not work. You then have to sort an alternative urgently, while also juggling every other administrative task that comes with a new country.

Contrast this with [Wise](/guides/finance/best-international-money-transfer-netherlands-2026/), which issues a Belgian IBAN (BE prefix). This does not solve the IBAN discrimination problem in theory — Belgian IBANs should be rejected just as rarely as Lithuanian ones — but in practice, Belgian IBANs seem to be better known and more widely accepted in Dutch systems. I have seen significantly fewer salary payment failures with Wise's Belgian IBAN than with Revolut's Lithuanian one.

### What You Can Do If You Get Rejected

If a Dutch employer or landlord refuses your Revolut IBAN, you have several options:

1. **Point to EU Regulation 260/2012.** Some employers will fix it once they realise they are breaking the law. Put this in writing.
2. **Escalate to the ACM (Autoriteit Consument & Markt).** The Dutch consumer authority handles IBAN discrimination complaints. This takes time, but logging a complaint creates a paper trail.
3. **Just open a Dutch bank account.** Honestly, for most people, this is the fastest solution. See my guide on [how to open a bank account in the Netherlands](/guides/finance/how-to-open-bank-account-netherlands-2026/).

The third option is what most expats end up doing, which rather undermines the idea of using Revolut as your primary bank. Which brings me to the honest verdict on Revolut's role in your banking setup.

## What Revolut Does Well

Having spent two sections on the problems, it is only fair to spend some time on the genuine strengths — because there are several.

### Currency Exchange

This is where Revolut started, and it is still one of its strongest suits. On weekdays, Revolut exchanges currency at the interbank rate with no markup. That is the rate you see on Google, the rate that banks use when trading between themselves, and the rate that is almost always better than what your high-street bank offers.

If you are exchanging EUR 500 into GBP, for example, ING might give you a rate with a 2–3% spread built in. Revolut gives you the mid-market rate. Over the course of a year, if you regularly move money between currencies, this difference is real money.

The catch: at weekends, Revolut adds a markup of around 0.5–1% because the foreign exchange markets are closed and they are taking on exchange rate risk. It is still usually cheaper than your bank, but it is not the same deal as on a weekday.

On the free Standard plan, you can exchange up to EUR 1,000 per month at the interbank rate. Above that threshold, there is a 0.5% fee. Premium and Metal plans have higher or unlimited exchange.

### Multi-Currency Accounts

Revolut lets you hold balances in over 25 currencies simultaneously. You can keep GBP, EUR, USD, and PLN in the same account, exchange between them when the rate is good, and spend from whichever currency makes sense at the time.

For expats who travel regularly or have financial ties to their home country, this is genuinely useful. I keep a small GBP balance in Revolut so that when I visit family in England, I am spending from pounds rather than paying exchange fees on every transaction.

This multi-currency feature is one area where Revolut clearly beats most traditional Dutch banks, which treat foreign currency as an afterthought.

### Budgeting Tools

Revolut's in-app budgeting features are better than most banking apps I have used. The main tools include:

**Vaults:** A savings pot within your account where you can stash money for a specific purpose — holiday fund, emergency fund, new laptop. You can set automatic round-ups so that every purchase gets rounded up to the nearest euro, with the difference going into a vault.

**Spending categories:** Revolut automatically categorises your spending (groceries, transport, restaurants) and shows you weekly and monthly breakdowns. These are more granular than ING's app and more accurate in my experience.

**Analytics:** You can set monthly spending limits by category and get alerts when you are approaching them. This sounds basic but it is surprisingly effective for people who are adjusting to the cost of living in Amsterdam or Rotterdam.

None of these features help you deal with the IBAN problem, but they make Revolut a useful secondary spending account. For tracking your lifestyle costs in the Netherlands alongside your Dutch bank account, the budgeting tools are worth having.

### Premium and Metal Plan Perks

On the Premium plan (EUR 8.99/month) and Metal plan (EUR 15.99/month), Revolut adds:

- Travel insurance (medical, delayed baggage, flight delay, car hire excess)
- Unlimited currency exchange at the interbank rate
- Higher ATM withdrawal limits
- Priority customer support
- Cashback on spending (Metal only)
- Airport lounge access through a discounted LoungeKey membership

The travel insurance is the most genuinely useful perk for expats who visit home regularly or travel for work. It is not a full-coverage policy — read the terms carefully before relying on it for something major — but for the usual expat trip (flight delays, minor medical incidents abroad), it provides reasonable coverage at a price that undercuts standalone travel insurance.

Whether the premium plans are worth the money depends on how much you travel. If you are taking two or three international trips a year and regularly exchanging more than EUR 1,000 per month, Premium probably pays for itself. Metal is harder to justify unless the cashback and lounge access genuinely fit your lifestyle.

### Disposable Virtual Cards

One Revolut feature that I think is genuinely underused: disposable virtual card numbers. You can generate a new virtual card number for each online purchase, and the number is automatically cancelled after one transaction.

This dramatically reduces the risk from data breaches. If an online shop gets hacked and your card details are stolen, there is nothing to steal — the number has already been retired. For someone doing a lot of online shopping or using unfamiliar services, this is a meaningful security improvement over a regular card.

## Getting Started with Revolut

If you have decided to use Revolut — even as a secondary account — the setup process is genuinely fast.

You need: a smartphone, a valid passport or ID card, and about ten minutes.

1. Download the Revolut app ([revolut.com](https://www.revolut.com/))
2. Enter your mobile number and verify it
3. Complete identity verification (photo of ID + selfie — usually processed within minutes)
4. Order your physical card (delivered in 3–5 business days to a Netherlands address)
5. Fund your account via bank transfer or card

You do not need a BSN to open a Revolut account. This means you can set it up before you even arrive in the Netherlands, which makes it useful as a holding account during your first few weeks while you sort your Dutch bank account and BSN.

## My Verdict: Who Should Use Revolut, and Who Shouldn't

After ten years of watching expats in the Netherlands figure out their banking, here is my honest assessment.

### Use Revolut If:

**You travel frequently.** If you are flying back to the UK, visiting family in India, or travelling for work around Europe, Revolut's travel features — multi-currency card, free ATM withdrawals up to the limit, travel insurance on paid plans — are genuinely worth having. The currency exchange savings alone will cover most of the Premium fee within a few trips.

**You want better budgeting tools.** If you are adjusting to the cost of living in the Netherlands and want to track your spending carefully, Revolut's categorisation and vault features are better than most Dutch bank apps.

**You manage multiple currencies regularly.** Receiving income in GBP, paying rent in EUR, sending money home in USD? Revolut handles this well and at lower cost than a traditional bank. You might also want to look at my guide to the [best finance apps for expats in the Netherlands](/guides/finance/best-finance-apps-expats-netherlands-2026/) for other tools that pair well with it.

**You want a secondary spending card.** Keep a small EUR balance on Revolut for day-to-day spending and use it as your "going out" card. You get better exchange rates than your Dutch bank and better spending analytics.

**You care about card security online.** The disposable virtual card feature alone is worth something if you do a lot of online shopping.

### Do Not Use Revolut As Your Only Bank If:

**You are receiving a Dutch salary.** The LT IBAN causes too many problems with Dutch payroll systems. Use ING, ABN AMRO, or Wise for salary. You can redirect money to Revolut afterwards if you want to use its budgeting features.

**You are renting through a Dutch agency or landlord.** Some agencies require NL IBAN for direct debit rent payments. Do not risk a failed payment on your first month.

**You pay Dutch government services or taxes.** The Belastingdienst, health insurance payments, and municipality fees all work better through iDEAL or NL IBAN direct debit. Revolut cannot help here.

**You ever plan to apply for a Dutch mortgage.** Mortgage lenders want to see your salary history landing in a Dutch account. A Revolut LT IBAN in your bank statements raises questions. See my [guide to buying a house in the Netherlands as an expat](/guides/finance/buying-house-netherlands-expat-guide-2026/) for more on this.

**You are on a tight timeline.** If you have just arrived and need to get paid within two weeks, do not gamble on whether your employer accepts an LT IBAN. Open a Dutch account or Wise account first, and add Revolut once the important things are sorted.

The setup that works for most expats I know — including me — looks like this:

**Layer 1: A Dutch bank account (ING or ABN AMRO)**
This is your administrative anchor. Salary goes here. Rent direct debit comes from here. Health insurance payment goes from here. Dutch taxes, utility bills, all of it. This is non-negotiable for life in the Netherlands.

Monthly cost: EUR 2–6.

**Layer 2: Wise**
Your international transfer layer. When you need to send money to the UK, India, the US, or anywhere else, you send it via <a href="https://go.expatnetherlandshub.com/wise?ref=/guides/finance/revolut-review-expats-netherlands-2026/" target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow sponsored" class="cta-affiliate">Use Wise for international transfers →</a> rather than through your Dutch bank. The rates are dramatically better and the fees are transparent. Wise also serves as a backup account with a European IBAN if your Dutch bank has issues.

Monthly cost: Free (pay per transfer).

**Layer 3: Revolut (optional)**
Your spending and travel optimiser. Keep a working balance here, use it when you are abroad, use the budgeting tools to track spending categories, and exchange currency when the weekday rate is good. The Standard free plan is fine for most people at this layer.

Monthly cost: Free–EUR 8.99 depending on your travel frequency.

This three-layer approach is more than most people feel they need when they first arrive. But within six months, most expats I work with end up here naturally, because each layer solves a different problem.

## Concrete Steps to Get Started

If you are ready to add Revolut to your setup:

1. **Sort your Dutch bank account first.** Read my guide on [how to open a bank account in the Netherlands](/guides/finance/how-to-open-bank-account-netherlands-2026/) and get your ING or ABN AMRO account in place. Do not use Revolut while waiting for a Dutch account — sort the Dutch account first.

2. **Open a Wise account if you send money internationally.** Do this before Revolut. It will serve more of your banking needs and causes fewer headaches with Dutch systems.

3. **Download Revolut and complete verification.** Use [revolut.com](https://www.revolut.com/) and have your passport or ID ready. Takes about ten minutes.

4. **Start with the free Standard plan.** There is no reason to pay for Premium until you have actually tested whether Revolut fits your usage patterns. Give it three months and then decide.

5. **Do not give Revolut to your employer.** Use your Dutch bank or Wise IBAN for salary. The Revolut account is for spending, not for receiving.

6. **Enable notifications and set a budget category.** Turn on instant spending notifications in the Revolut app. Set a monthly spending limit for at least one category — restaurants, for example. These small things make the budgeting features useful rather than decorative.

7. **Use the [salary checker tool](/tools/salary-checker/) to understand your take-home pay.** Once you know what you are earning net of Dutch taxes, you can plan your budgets across your Dutch bank, Wise, and Revolut accounts more sensibly.

## Final Thoughts

Revolut is a well-built product. The app is fast, the exchange rates are among the best available, the travel features are genuinely useful, and the budgeting tools are better than what most Dutch banks offer. I am not writing a negative review.

But for expats in the Netherlands specifically, Revolut has one significant limitation — the Lithuanian IBAN — that can cause serious practical problems if you rely on it as your primary account. I have seen this derail people's first weeks in a new country, and I would rather you read about it here than discover it when your salary does not arrive.

Use Revolut for what it is good at. Pair it with a Dutch bank account and Wise. That combination covers everything you need.
