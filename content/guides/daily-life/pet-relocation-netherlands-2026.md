When I first moved to the Netherlands, I brought my cat Noor with me from the UK. I will not pretend it was simple. There were vet appointments, paperwork, a stressful check-in process at the airport, and a very unhappy cat for about 48 hours. But she settled in beautifully, and nearly a decade later she still rules our Amsterdam apartment.

If you are moving to the Netherlands with a dog, cat, or other pet, this guide covers everything you need to know — the legal requirements, the airline rules, what happens when you arrive, and how life with a pet works here day to day.

---

## The Netherlands Is a Pet-Friendly Country

The Dutch take their pets seriously. The Netherlands consistently ranks among the most pet-friendly countries in Europe. Dogs are welcome in most shops, restaurants, and public spaces. There are dedicated dog parks, off-leash areas, and a genuine culture of treating animals well.

That said, bringing a pet from abroad involves real bureaucracy — especially from non-EU countries. The requirements depend on where you are coming from, what animal you have, and how you are travelling. Get the paperwork wrong and your pet could be turned away at the border or placed in quarantine at your expense.

Start planning at least two to three months before your move.

---

## EU vs Non-EU: The Core Distinction

The single most important factor in planning your pet relocation is whether you are moving from inside the EU or from a non-EU country.

### Moving from Within the EU

If your pet is already in an EU member state, the process is relatively simple. Your pet needs three things:

1. **A microchip** — ISO standard 15-digit chip implanted before or at the same time as the rabies vaccination
2. **A valid rabies vaccination** — given at least 21 days before travel (this waiting period only applies the first time; boosters given on time count immediately)
3. **An EU pet passport** — issued by an authorised vet in your country, recording the microchip number, vaccination history, and owner details

The EU pet passport is a small blue booklet. Your vet in any EU country can issue it. Keep it with you throughout the journey — it is the document Dutch border authorities will check.

### Moving from the UK

Post-Brexit, the UK is treated as a third country. This matters a lot for British expats moving to the Netherlands.

Your pet needs:
- A microchip
- A valid rabies vaccination (at least 21 days before travel, for first-time vaccination)
- An **Animal Health Certificate (AHC)** issued by an Official Veterinarian (OV) in the UK — not an EU pet passport
- The AHC must be issued **within 10 days of travel**

Book your OV appointment well in advance. Not all vets are authorised to issue AHCs. The certificate costs around £150-250 depending on your vet, and the timing window is strict — if your travel date changes by more than 10 days, you may need a new certificate.

One important detail: once your pet is in the Netherlands and you get a Dutch vet registration, you can then obtain an EU pet passport for future travel within the EU. I did this for Noor after we arrived — it makes life much simpler for any travel within the Schengen area.

### Moving from Other Non-EU Countries

The Netherlands accepts pets from countries on the EU's "listed" countries — those with equivalent rabies disease control standards. These currently include the US, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Japan, and many others.

For listed countries, requirements are similar to the UK:
- Microchip
- Rabies vaccination (at least 21 days before first travel)
- Official veterinary health certificate issued within 10 days of entry

For **unlisted countries**, the process is considerably more demanding. Your pet may need a rabies antibody titre test (a blood test confirming vaccination efficacy), which must be done at least 3 months before travel. This adds significant time and cost to your planning. Check the current list on the European Commission website and contact the Dutch Food and Consumer Product Safety Authority (NVWA) if your country is not listed.

## Microchipping: The Non-Negotiable First Step

All dogs, cats, and ferrets entering the Netherlands must have an ISO 11784/11785 microchip (15-digit). This is the international standard and most chips implanted after 2010 comply with it.

If your pet already has a chip, verify the format. Some older American chips use a different standard. If yours does not comply, a new chip can be implanted — but it must happen before or on the same day as any rabies vaccination that you want to count toward the entry requirements. A vaccination given before the chip was implanted does not count.

In the Netherlands, microchipping dogs became compulsory in 2013. All dogs must be registered in a national database (Dierenpaspoort or I-base). Once you register with a Dutch vet, they will help you transfer your pet's details to the Dutch registry.

---

## Rabies Vaccination Timeline

The 21-day rule catches a lot of people off guard. Your pet's rabies vaccination must have been given at least 21 calendar days before the date of entry into the Netherlands — and this applies to the first vaccination only.

If your pet received a booster vaccination on time (before the previous vaccination expired), there is no waiting period. The booster counts immediately.

Practical checklist:
- Confirm your pet has a valid rabies vaccination in their records
- Check the expiry date — Dutch border staff will
- If you need a new or first vaccination, book it at least 4-5 weeks before travel to give yourself buffer
- Make sure the vet records the microchip number alongside the vaccination in the passport or certificate

## EU Pet Passport: What It Contains and Where to Get One

The EU pet passport is the gold standard document for pet travel within Europe. It contains:
- Owner details
- Animal description and photo (optional)
- Microchip number and implantation date
- Vaccination records (rabies and optionally other vaccines)
- Antiparasitic treatments
- Official vet signatures and stamps

Any authorised vet in an EU member state can issue one. If you are moving from within the EU, get this sorted before you leave. If you are moving from the UK or another non-EU country, you will get it issued in the Netherlands after arrival.

Cost in the Netherlands: approximately €25-50 depending on the veterinary practice.

## Airline Rules: Cabin, Cargo, and What to Expect

How your pet travels depends on the airline and your pet's size. There is no single European standard — each carrier sets its own rules.

### Cabin Travel

Most airlines allow small dogs and cats in the cabin if the pet plus carrier does not exceed 8-10 kg total weight. The carrier must fit under the seat in front of you.

Typical cabin pet fees: €50-150 per flight, depending on the airline and route. KLM charges €65 within Europe. Transavia, Ryanair, and easyJet have varying policies — always check directly before booking.

Your pet travels in a soft-sided carrier. They must stay in the carrier for the entire flight. Book the pet spot at the same time as your own ticket — airlines limit the number of animals per flight and spaces fill up.

### Cargo (Checked Baggage or Freight)

Larger dogs that do not fit in the cabin travel in the hold, in an IATA-approved hard carrier. This is called excess baggage (if on the same flight as you) or manifest cargo (shipped separately).

The requirements for cargo travel are more demanding:
- IATA-compliant crate with sufficient space for the animal to stand, turn, and lie down
- Food and water containers attached to the crate door
- Absorbent bedding
- "Live Animal" labels on all sides

Cost for cargo varies significantly: €100-400 for short-haul flights, potentially much more for long-haul. Some airlines restrict certain dog breeds in cargo due to respiratory risk in pressurised holds.

### Breed Restrictions

Several airlines (KLM included) restrict brachycephalic (flat-faced) breeds — bulldogs, pugs, French bulldogs, Boston terriers, and similar — from travelling as cargo due to the risk of respiratory distress. Some carriers have banned them entirely. Check with your airline well in advance if you have one of these breeds.

In terms of entry to the Netherlands itself: the Netherlands has banned certain dog breeds under its animal welfare legislation, including some fighting and guard breeds. The specifics change periodically — check with the NVWA before travelling if you have a breed that might fall under these rules.

## Quarantine in the Netherlands

Good news: quarantine is **not required** for pets entering from EU member states or from listed third countries (UK, US, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, and others) provided all documentation is in order.

Quarantine is only imposed if:
- Your pet is arriving from an unlisted country without the required titre test documentation
- Documents are missing, expired, or incorrect at the border
- The microchip cannot be read or does not match the documentation

Quarantine is at the owner's expense and can last from a few days to several months depending on the circumstances. This is why getting the paperwork right the first time is so important.

If in any doubt about your specific situation, contact the NVWA (Nederlandse Voedsel en Warenautoriteit) before you travel.

## Professional Pet Relocation Services

For complex moves — especially from non-EU countries, or for large animals — professional pet relocation agencies handle all the logistics. Services include documentation preparation, airline booking, crate procurement, and airport collection at the Dutch end.

Costs:
- Simple EU moves: €300-600
- UK or listed third country: €800-1,500
- Complex or unlisted country: €1,500-3,000+

Agencies I have heard recommended by expats include IPATA-member companies (International Pet and Animal Transportation Association). Always verify membership and check reviews before booking.

Whether or not you use a professional service, I recommend downloading the NVWA pet import checklist from their website and going through it point by point with your vet at least 6-8 weeks before travel.

## Arriving in the Netherlands: What Happens at the Border

For EU travellers entering the Netherlands without crossing a formal external border checkpoint, pet checks are not routine. Your documents should still be correct and available, but you are unlikely to be stopped.

For travellers arriving from non-EU countries — especially at Amsterdam Schiphol — there are designated arrival points for animals. Your pet's carrier will go through customs, and an official may check documentation. Schiphol has an animal hotel facility (KLM Animal Hotel) for animals that need to stay overnight or require processing.

If arriving by Eurostar or ferry from the UK, border checks are thorough. Pets must go through the designated pet check lane. Have all documents ready: AHC, microchip record, vaccination proof. Expect it to take 20-30 minutes.

## Registering with a Dutch Vet

Once you have arrived and settled in, registering your pet with a local vet (dierenarts) is one of your first practical tasks. There is no formal compulsory registration system in the same way as with the GP (huisarts), but most practices ask you to register in advance.

Finding a vet: Most Dutch cities have multiple practices. Ask neighbours, expat Facebook groups, or your building manager for recommendations. In Amsterdam, Rotterdam, The Hague, and Utrecht there are English-speaking practices familiar with expat pets.

What to bring to your first appointment:
- Your pet's existing health records, vaccination history, and passport
- Your own ID and Dutch address
- Any previous test results or treatment history

At first registration, the vet will typically:
- Scan and verify the microchip
- Update or create the Dutch registration record
- Issue or update the EU pet passport if you do not already have one
- Give you a schedule for annual check-ups and booster vaccinations

One thing that surprised me: Dutch vets are generally excellent and costs are reasonable by Western European standards. A standard check-up runs €40-70. Keep receipts — if you take out pet insurance, you will need them.

You will need your BSN number for some administrative paperwork with vets and insurance companies. Use my [BSN Planner](/tools/bsn-planner/) to make sure you have everything in order before your first vet appointment.

## Pet Insurance in the Netherlands

Pet insurance is not mandatory but I strongly recommend it. Veterinary costs for serious illness or surgery can reach €3,000-8,000, and without insurance, those costs fall entirely on you.

Dutch pet insurance options include:
- **Reaal Dierenverzekering**
- **FBTO Huisdierenverzekering**
- **Interpolis**
- **Aegon**

Cover levels vary from basic accident-only to full policies covering illness, dental care, and alternative treatments. Monthly premiums typically run:
- Basic dog cover: €15-30/month
- Full dog cover: €40-80/month
- Cat cover: €10-25/month

Pre-existing conditions are generally excluded, so take out insurance as soon as you arrive — ideally within the first few weeks — before any Dutch vet visits create a treatment history.

When comparing policies, check the deductible (eigen risico), the annual limit, and whether there are breed exclusions. Some policies exclude brachycephalic breeds or charge higher premiums for them.

## Dog Tax: Hondenbelasting

The Netherlands has a dog tax called **hondenbelasting**. Not every municipality charges it, but many do — including Amsterdam, Rotterdam, and Utrecht.

You are required to register your dog with your municipality within 14 days of acquiring or moving in with the dog. You do this at the gemeente (municipal office) or via their online portal.

Typical rates in 2025/2026:
- First dog: €80-140/year
- Second dog: €100-180/year

Amsterdam charges €140.16 for the first dog and €210.24 for each additional dog per year (2025 rates). Some municipalities offer exemptions for assistance dogs.

Failure to register can result in a fine. When you register your address at the gemeente — which you must do as an expat to get your BSN — you can often register your dog at the same appointment or online shortly afterward.

## Dog Walking Rules and Daily Life

Walking your dog in the Netherlands is generally a pleasure. There are plenty of parks, canals, and off-leash areas. But there are rules you need to know.

**On-leash requirement:** In most urban areas and public spaces, dogs must be kept on a leash. Look for signs indicating off-leash zones (losloopgebieden). Amsterdam has over 40 designated off-leash areas. The municipality's website lists them.

**Picking up after your dog (opruimplicht):** Mandatory throughout the Netherlands. Fines for not picking up after your dog can reach €90. Nearly all Dutch parks and streets have dispensers with free bags — this is a genuine cultural norm here, not just a theoretical rule.

**Beaches:** Many beaches have seasonal restrictions on dogs. Outside the main summer season (roughly September to April), dogs are usually welcome on most beaches. In summer, dog-friendly sections are designated. Check local signage.

**Public transport:** Dogs are allowed on NS trains with a day ticket purchased for them (€3.50 for a day pass). Small dogs in carriers travel free. Buses and trams in major cities vary by operator — in Amsterdam, small dogs in carriers are generally allowed.

**Restaurants and cafes:** Many Dutch restaurants and cafes welcome dogs, particularly on terraces (terrassen). A "honden welkom" sign is a good indicator. It is always polite to ask first.

## Pet-Friendly Housing

Finding a rental that allows pets is one of the bigger practical challenges. The Dutch rental market is tight and landlords can legally prohibit pets in their lease conditions.

When searching for housing, filter specifically for "huisdieren toegestaan" (pets allowed) or ask landlords directly before applying. Having a well-trained, microchipped, insured pet and being upfront about it tends to go down better than hiding it.

Some landlords are flexible if you offer a larger deposit or guarantee to restore any damage. Ground-floor apartments or houses with gardens are easier to obtain with pets, particularly larger dogs.

## Other Pets: Birds, Rabbits, and Exotic Animals

This guide has focused on dogs and cats, which cover the majority of expat pet relocations. But a few notes on other animals:

**Rabbits and guinea pigs:** No specific EU import requirements for personal pet travel, but they cannot enter via the standard pet passport system. Rules vary by origin country — check NVWA guidance.

**Birds:** Strict import controls apply due to avian influenza risk. Import permits are often required, and rules change with disease outbreaks. Plan 3-6 months ahead.

**Reptiles and exotic animals:** Many reptile and exotic species are protected under CITES (Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species). You may need import and export permits, and some species cannot be brought in at all. Check the CITES database and Dutch customs regulations before making any plans.

**Fish:** Generally low requirements for personal pet fish in small tanks. Very large aquaria may attract customs attention.

## Summary Checklist

**At least 8 weeks before moving:**
- Verify microchip is ISO compliant
- Check rabies vaccination status and book booster if needed (remember the 21-day rule)
- If from a non-EU country, contact an Official Veterinarian or professional relocation service
- Book your pet's place on the flight

**2-4 weeks before moving:**
- Obtain EU pet passport (EU) or official veterinary health certificate (non-EU)
- Confirm airline requirements and carrier dimensions
- Research vets in your new area

**On arrival:**
- Register at the gemeente and register your dog for hondenbelasting if applicable
- Register with a local vet within the first few weeks
- Take out pet insurance before your first Dutch vet appointment
- Update Dutch pet registry
