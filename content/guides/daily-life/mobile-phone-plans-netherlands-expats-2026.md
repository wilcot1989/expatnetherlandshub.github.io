When I arrived in the Netherlands from the UK, I bought a Lebara prepaid SIM from the Albert Heijn near my flat before I had even unpacked my suitcase. It was the right call at the time — no BSN, no Dutch bank account, no hassle. I just handed over a tenner, showed my passport, and had a Dutch number within ten minutes.

Two years later, once I had settled in properly, I moved to KPN. I was working with clients across the country, driving to rural Gelderland and Zeeland for consultations, and I needed a network I could actually rely on when I was in the middle of nowhere. KPN was worth the extra cost for that period of my life.

Now? I'm on T-Mobile. I live in Amsterdam, I work mostly remotely, and I realised I was paying a significant premium for rural coverage I no longer needed. T-Mobile's mid-tier unlimited plan gives me everything I need at roughly €12 less per month than I was paying KPN. That's €144 per year back in my pocket for identical service in my daily life.

I tell you this not to be self-indulgent, but because it illustrates the key insight: **the "best" Dutch phone plan depends entirely on your situation, and it will probably change as your life here settles**. There is no single right answer. What there is, however, is a wrong answer — and that is signing a two-year phone contract within your first month of arrival. More on that in a moment.

## Understanding Dutch Mobile Networks

Before comparing providers, it helps to understand how the infrastructure actually works. There are only three physical networks in the Netherlands. Every provider you see — whether it's a big brand or a budget operator — runs on one of these three.

### KPN — The Former State Network

KPN was the Dutch state telephone company before privatisation, and it still owns the most extensive network infrastructure in the country. This history matters: KPN has masts in places where the others simply do not bother, because it was legally required to provide universal coverage for decades.

In practice, this means KPN is consistently the best network for:
- Rural areas and smaller towns
- Indoor coverage in older (pre-1980s) buildings with thick walls
- Remote areas like the Veluwe, Zeeland islands, and parts of Friesland

KPN also runs its own 5G network and has strong coverage in all major cities.

Budget operators **Simyo**, **Lebara**, and **Youfone** all piggyback on the KPN network. You get the same coverage as KPN customers at a fraction of the price. The trade-off is that during peak congestion, KPN's own subscribers may get priority. In practice, I have rarely noticed a difference.

### T-Mobile / Odido — The Second Network

T-Mobile is the second-largest physical network in the Netherlands. In 2021, T-Mobile merged with Tele2 Netherlands and acquired their frequency licences, which significantly expanded their network. The Odido brand was created as a sub-brand targeting budget and younger customers.

T-Mobile has invested aggressively in 5G, and in major urban areas their speeds are genuinely impressive — often faster than KPN in my experience. The gap in rural coverage has narrowed over the past few years, but it still exists if you venture well outside the Randstad.

Budget operator **Ben** uses the T-Mobile network.

### Vodafone — Third Place

Vodafone is the third physical network, smaller than the other two but still solid in urban areas. They have a stronger international presence, which means their roaming arrangements can be favourable, and their international calling rates have historically been competitive.

Vodafone has fewer budget sub-brands using its network, which means if you want Vodafone coverage you are largely paying Vodafone prices.

## SIM-Only vs. Phone Contract: The Expat Trap

I want to spend a moment on this because I have seen expats get genuinely burned by it.

Dutch mobile providers sell two types of plans: **SIM-only** (just the SIM card, no handset) and **combined phone + plan packages** (you get a new handset bundled with your monthly plan). The combined packages are always advertised prominently, often with attractive monthly prices that feel reasonable.

The problem is the contract length and what comes with it.

Combined phone contracts typically run for **24 months**. In the Netherlands, phone contracts are linked to a **BKR registration** — this is the Dutch credit reference system. A 24-month phone contract appears on your BKR file. This might not sound like a big deal, but when you later apply for a mortgage, a private rental property, a car lease, or any form of credit in the Netherlands, lenders can see all your BKR commitments. A phone contract counts towards your total financial commitments in some lender calculations.

More practically: in your first year in the Netherlands, your circumstances may change significantly. You might move cities, change employers, or simply find a better deal. Being locked into a 24-month phone contract with an expensive cancellation fee is the last thing you need.

**My strong advice for expats:** always buy your handset outright and take a SIM-only plan. SIM-only plans in the Netherlands are genuinely cheap and flexible. You can get excellent plans for €10–20 per month with no lock-in beyond a rolling monthly term.

If you are thinking about the [cost of living in the Netherlands](/guides/housing/cost-of-living-netherlands-2026/) and trying to keep your monthly commitments manageable, SIM-only is the way to go.

## Prepaid vs. Postpaid: What Expats Should Know

This is the other key decision, and the answer changes depending on where you are in your Dutch life journey.

### Prepaid: Your Starting Point

If you have just arrived and do not yet have a [BSN registration](/guides/legal/bsn-registration-guide-2026/) or a Dutch bank account, **prepaid is your only real option for a postpaid contract** — so prepaid it is. This is not a bad thing.

You can buy a Lebara, KPN, or T-Mobile prepaid SIM at the supermarket, top it up with €10–20, and have a working Dutch number immediately. Most prepaid plans now offer monthly bundles (rather than pure pay-as-you-go), so you essentially pay for a month's worth of data and calls upfront.

Once you have your BSN and have opened a Dutch bank account (if you need help with this, try the [bank account chooser tool](/tools/bank-account-chooser/)), you can switch to a postpaid plan. The process is simple — your number ports across within one business day.

### Postpaid: For Settled Expats

A monthly postpaid contract (abonnement) gives you better value per gigabyte, often includes more features, and is how most Dutch residents manage their mobile usage. You need:

1. A valid BSN number
2. A Dutch bank account (for direct debit)
3. A valid passport or ID

Some providers also check your BKR credit file, though this is more common for combined phone contracts than for SIM-only plans.

Most SIM-only postpaid plans in the Netherlands are now **month-to-month** with no minimum term beyond the first month. This is genuinely flexible and means you are never locked in.

## International Calling: Which Provider Is Cheapest?

The EU roaming rules mean that calling and texting within the EU is included in all Dutch plans at no extra cost. If you are calling friends or family elsewhere in Europe, any Dutch SIM will work fine.

The story changes for calls outside the EU. Here is a rough guide by region:

**South Asia (India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka)**
Lebara and Lyca Mobile are far ahead of everyone else. Lebara bundles include significant India minutes as standard. Per-minute rates are a fraction of what KPN or Vodafone charge for the same calls.

**Middle East and North Africa (Morocco, Turkey, Egypt, UAE)**
Again, Lebara wins. They built their whole brand around affordable international calling to these regions, and it shows in the pricing.

**West Africa (Nigeria, Ghana, Senegal)**
Lebara and Lyca Mobile both perform well here. Check the per-minute rates for your specific destination when you sign up.

**Eastern Europe (Poland, Romania, Bulgaria)**
EU/EEA destinations are covered by standard roaming rules, so all Dutch plans work. For countries like Ukraine or Russia, Lebara has competitive rates.

**USA and Canada**
No Dutch provider is exceptionally cheap here. KPN, Vodafone, and T-Mobile all offer international call add-ons. Alternatively, WhatsApp, FaceTime, or Signal calls over data are essentially free.

**UK (post-Brexit)**
The UK is no longer covered by EU roaming rules. Most Dutch providers now charge for calls to UK numbers. Check the specific rates — they vary significantly. Vodafone's historical UK connections can sometimes give it an edge here.

## eSIM in the Netherlands

eSIM (embedded SIM) has been mainstream in the Netherlands since 2023, and by 2026 the major providers all support it. Here is the current state:

- **KPN:** Full eSIM support, both prepaid and contract
- **T-Mobile:** Full eSIM support, both prepaid and contract
- **Vodafone:** Full eSIM support, both prepaid and contract
- **Simyo:** eSIM introduced in 2026
- **Odido:** eSIM support available
- **Lebara, Ben, Youfone:** Physical SIM only (as of early 2026)

For expats with modern dual-SIM smartphones, eSIM is genuinely useful. You can keep your home country physical SIM in your phone for banking SMS codes or staying connected to your home number, while running a Dutch eSIM for local use. I did this for nearly a year after arriving — my UK SIM stayed in the phone for Barclays verification texts, while the Dutch eSIM handled everything local.

One practical note: eSIM activation requires online registration and sometimes a video verification process. Make sure you have a working internet connection (hotel WiFi, a friend's hotspot) before you try to activate an eSIM on arrival.

## What You Need to Sign Up

The documentation requirements vary by plan type:

**Prepaid SIM**
- Valid passport or national ID card (required for identity verification under Dutch law)
- That is it — no BSN, no bank account, no credit check

**Postpaid SIM-only contract**
- Valid passport or national ID card
- BSN (Burgerservicenummer) — you can read more about getting this in my [BSN registration guide](/guides/legal/bsn-registration-guide-2026/)
- Dutch bank account (IBAN) for direct debit
- Some providers run a BKR credit check (primarily for phone contracts, less common for SIM-only)

**Combined phone + plan contract**
- All of the above, plus
- BKR credit check is standard
- 24-month commitment is typical

If you have not yet registered with the municipality and do not have a BSN, start there first. You will need it for almost everything in the Netherlands — banking, healthcare, tax, and yes, phone contracts. My [guide to getting a DigiD](/guides/legal/digid-guide-expats-2026/) also covers the sequence of registrations you need to do.

## How to Switch Providers (and Port Your Number)

Switching Dutch mobile providers is one of the easier administrative tasks you will face here. The process is:

**Step 1: Sign up with your new provider**
During sign-up, tell them you want to keep your existing number (nummer meenemen/porteren). You will need to provide your current phone number and, sometimes, your current provider's customer number.

**Step 2: Your new provider contacts your old one**
You do not need to call your old provider to cancel. Your new provider initiates the transfer. Under Dutch law, your old provider must release your number promptly.

**Step 3: Your number transfers within one working day**
In practice, number transfers in the Netherlands happen fast — often the same business day. You will receive a confirmation when the transfer is complete. Your old SIM stops working at that point.

**Step 4: Your old contract ends automatically**
Once your number has ported, your old subscription terminates. If you were on a monthly rolling plan, there is nothing more to do. If you were mid-contract, check whether any cancellation fees apply (for most SIM-only monthly plans, there should be none).

A few things to note:
- You cannot port a non-Dutch number to a Dutch provider. You need a Dutch number to port.
- Porting requires the number to be active — you cannot port a number you have already cancelled.
- If you have an outstanding balance with your old provider, they can delay the port. Make sure you are up to date.

Once you have sorted your phone plan, you might also want to look at your [home internet options](/guides/daily-life/best-internet-providers-netherlands-expats-2026/) — some providers offer discounts if you bundle mobile and home internet together, though in my experience the savings are rarely as impressive as they look in the brochure.
