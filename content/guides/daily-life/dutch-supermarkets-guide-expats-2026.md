The first time I walked into a Dutch supermarket on my own, I came out with two bags of groceries and a mild identity crisis. The bonuskaart lady at the checkout asked me something I did not understand. I smiled and nodded. She asked again, more slowly, still in Dutch. I handed her my debit card. She sighed gently and moved on. I later discovered she was asking whether I had a loyalty card. In hindsight, not a catastrophe. At the time, it felt like failing some kind of citizenship test.

That was ten years ago. Now I do my weekly shop with the confidence of someone who actually grew up here — which I half did, my mother being Dutch — and I have tried every major chain at some point. I have also made all the classic expat mistakes: paying for bags I did not need, standing blankly in front of a statiegeld machine with an armful of empty bottles, buying the wrong kind of milk because the packaging looked similar. This guide is meant to save you that learning curve.

The Dutch supermarket sector is genuinely competitive and, by European standards, quite affordable. You have real choices here, and the right choice depends on where you live, your budget, and how important things like organic produce or English-language apps are to you.

## The Dutch Supermarket Market at a Glance

The Netherlands has a concentrated grocery market. Albert Heijn dominates with around 35% market share, followed by Jumbo with around 22%. Together they account for well over half of all grocery spending. But the remaining market is contested by budget chains, neighbourhood stores, and regional players — and some of them are genuinely excellent value.

Here is a quick overview before I go into detail on each chain:

| Supermarket | Type | Price Level | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Albert Heijn | Full-service, market leader | Mid to high | Convenience, range, online delivery |
| Jumbo | Full-service, challenger | Mid | Value, large stores, price guarantee |
| Lidl | Hard discounter | Low | Budget shopping, fresh produce |
| Aldi | Hard discounter | Low | Budget basics |
| PLUS | Full-service, independent | Mid | Neighbourhood feel, quality meat/fish |
| Dirk van den Broek | Full-service, Randstad | Low-mid | Cheapest full-service, Randstad only |
| Coop | Full-service, regional | Mid | Rural and smaller towns |
| Spar | Convenience | High | Evening and Sunday top-ups |

## Albert Heijn

Albert Heijn is everywhere. With over 1,000 stores across the Netherlands — and smaller AH to go outlets in train stations, universities, petrol stations, and even hospitals — it is the supermarket you will almost certainly use most often simply because it is there.

**Price level:** Mid to high. AH is not the cheapest option, but it runs weekly bonus deals that can bring prices down significantly. The trick is to structure your shopping around what is on offer, which their app makes fairly easy.

**Quality:** Consistently good across fresh produce, meat, fish, and bakery. The AH Biologisch (organic) range is extensive. Their own-brand products — sold under the "AH" label — are reliable and competitively priced against equivalent branded items.

**International products:** This is where AH has a genuine edge. Larger branches stock Heinz beans, Marmite, Bovril, Branston pickle, Tetley tea bags, Cadbury products, American-style breakfast cereals, a growing range of Asian sauces and pastes, and various halal-certified products. The "world food" aisle varies by store size — an AH XL in Amsterdam Oost will have far more than a neighbourhood AH in a small town. If you cannot find something in store, the online shop often carries it.

**English-friendliness:** The best in the sector. The AH app is fully available in English and shows your bonus deals, digital receipts, shopping lists, and allergy filters. Self-scan machines have an English option. Staff in urban branches almost universally speak English.

**Online delivery:** AH's online shop (ah.nl) is polished and reliable. Same-day delivery is available in most cities, and you can also collect from a Pickup Point if delivery windows do not suit you. The website and app both work in English.

**The bonuskaart:** Get one immediately. It is free, takes two minutes to set up online, and unlocks the weekly bonus deals. Without it, you pay full price for everything that is discounted — which in a typical weekly shop can mean paying 15-20% more than a regular shopper. You can use a digital version in the app; no physical card needed.

**AH to go:** These compact convenience stores in transport hubs are open long hours and stock sandwiches, salads, hot meals, drinks, snacks, and a limited grocery range. The prices are higher than a regular AH, but for a quick lunch or forgotten item they are hard to beat. Most train stations in the Netherlands have one.

**Locations:** Everywhere. If you live in any Dutch town of reasonable size, you are within cycling distance of an AH. See my guide to [cycling in the Netherlands](/guides/daily-life/cycling-netherlands-expat-guide-2026/) for getting around once you have done your shop.

## Jumbo

Jumbo is Albert Heijn's most credible rival, and for good reason. It has grown aggressively over the past decade and now offers a genuinely compelling alternative — often at lower prices.

**Price level:** Mid, slightly below AH in most categories. Jumbo operates a "Geen Gedoe" (no fuss) price guarantee: if you find a comparable product cheaper elsewhere, they will refund the difference. In practice, their shelf prices are already competitive enough that most shoppers never need to invoke this.

**Quality:** High, comparable to Albert Heijn. Jumbo's own-brand range is solid. Their fresh produce section tends to be larger in the bigger stores, and the bakery counter (in full-format stores) is excellent — proper fresh bread, which you do not always get in a smaller AH.

**International products:** Good, though slightly behind AH in variety. You will find most British staples and a reasonable world food section. For expats from outside Europe, AH XL stores will typically have a wider range.

**English-friendliness:** The Jumbo app is available in English. In-store signage is Dutch only, but the layout is logical. Staff are helpful, and English is widely spoken.

**Online delivery:** Jumbo online (jumbo.com) works well and is comparable to AH. They offer delivery and collection options across the country.

**Store size:** Jumbo stores are generally larger than AH stores, with wider aisles and a more spacious feel. If you do a big weekly shop with a lot of items, this matters. Jumbo Foodmarkt — their flagship format — is enormous and excellent, with in-store butchers, fishmongers, and cheese counters.

**Locations:** Widespread, though slightly less ubiquitous than AH in city centres. Strong presence in suburban areas.

## Lidl

Lidl is a German hard discounter, and it has improved dramatically over the past five years. If price is your primary concern, Lidl deserves serious consideration.

**Price level:** Low. Significantly cheaper than AH or Jumbo on most categories.

**Quality:** Better than many people expect. Fresh produce quality is good, particularly the fruit, and Lidl's bakery section (fresh rolls and bread baked in store) is genuinely excellent. Their own-brand products have won multiple independent taste tests. The "Lidl Plus" app offers additional discounts.

**International products:** Limited. Lidl runs themed international weeks — "Italiaanse week," "Japanse week," and so on — during which you can find interesting specialist products. But for regular expat staples, the range is thin compared to AH or Jumbo.

**English-friendliness:** Lower than AH or Jumbo. The app works in English, but the in-store experience is primarily Dutch. Staff helpfulness varies.

**Online delivery:** Lidl does not offer home delivery in the Netherlands. Shop in-store only.

**Best use case:** Weekly staples where brand does not matter — fruit and veg, dairy, bread, cleaning products, pasta, tinned goods. Top up with AH or Jumbo for specific items.

## Aldi

Aldi is Lidl's main competitor in the discounter segment and operates a similar model. The two are closely matched on price.

**Price level:** Low. Comparable to Lidl, occasionally cheaper on specific items.

**Quality:** Decent, though I personally find Lidl's fresh produce slightly better. Aldi's own-brand products are reliable.

**International products:** Similarly limited. Aldi has its own themed weeks but no regular international aisle to speak of.

**English-friendliness:** Limited. Primarily Dutch in-store experience.

**Online delivery:** Not available.

**Best use case:** Same as Lidl — budget staples. Worth checking both if you are near one of each, as prices on specific products can differ.

## PLUS

PLUS is a Dutch cooperative chain that positions itself around quality, freshness, and local sourcing. It sits in the upper-mid tier on price and tends to attract a slightly older, quality-conscious customer base.

**Price level:** Mid, comparable to AH. Regularly runs good bonus deals.

**Quality:** High, particularly on meat, fish, and fresh produce. PLUS stores often have a more premium feel than AH, with better deli counters in larger branches.

**International products:** Good for a mid-sized chain, though not as extensive as AH. Expect British and German staples; specialist Asian or Middle Eastern products are less consistent.

**English-friendliness:** Moderate. The PLUS app and website are Dutch-first, though staff are typically helpful.

**Online delivery:** Available through the PLUS website, though less widely used than AH or Jumbo's systems.

**Locations:** Roughly 270 stores, predominantly in mid-sized Dutch towns and suburbs. Less common in major city centres.

**Best use case:** If you live near a PLUS and quality matters to you, it is worth using regularly. The neighbourhood cooperative feel is genuine — this is a chain that actually knows its local customers.

## Dirk van den Broek

Dirk is a bit of an institution, and one that many expats overlook. It is not fancy. The stores are large, slightly chaotic, and entirely uninterested in atmosphere. But for sheer value in the Randstad, nothing beats Dirk.

**Price level:** Low-mid. Consistently the cheapest full-service supermarket in the Randstad, regularly beating both Lidl and Aldi on branded goods. This is remarkable for a full-assortment store.

**Quality:** Reasonable. Fresh produce is not as consistently good as AH or Jumbo, but branded products are the same as everywhere else — just cheaper.

**International products:** Surprisingly decent. Dirk stores in Amsterdam, Rotterdam, and The Hague often have good ethnic food sections due to the diverse neighbourhoods they serve.

**English-friendliness:** Low on paper, but Dirk stores are typically in multicultural areas where staff are well used to non-Dutch speakers.

**Online delivery:** Not available.

**Locations:** Randstad only (Amsterdam, Rotterdam, The Hague, Utrecht and surrounding areas). If you live outside this zone, Dirk is not an option.

**Best use case:** Main weekly shop if you are on a budget and live in the Randstad. The savings versus AH over a year are significant. Pair with a trip to a toko for specialist ingredients.

## Coop

Coop is a cooperative chain with about 300 stores, mostly outside the major cities in smaller towns, villages, and rural areas. If you move to a smaller Dutch city or a more rural area, Coop may be your local supermarket.

**Price level:** Mid, comparable to AH.

**Quality:** Good. Coop has a reputation for quality and community focus. Their meat and dairy sections are typically strong.

**International products:** Limited. Coop is very much a local Dutch chain with a Dutch product range.

**English-friendliness:** Variable, and lower in rural areas. In university towns or international community locations, you may find more English-speaking staff.

**Online delivery:** Available through jumbo.com since Coop and Jumbo share a fulfilment arrangement in many areas.

---

## Spar

Spar operates differently from all the above. It is a convenience chain, not a full supermarket, and prices reflect that. You will typically find Spar in locations where space is limited: petrol stations, tourist areas, small town centres, and student housing areas.

**Price level:** High — typically 20-30% above full supermarket prices.

**Quality:** Decent for a convenience format. Fresh sandwiches and snacks are reliable.

**International products:** Very limited.

**English-friendliness:** Variable. Tourist-area Spar stores are often English-friendly.

**Best use case:** Emergency top-ups and late-night purchases when nothing else is open. Not for regular shopping.

## Practical Tips for Expat Shoppers

### Get the Bonuskaart Immediately

I mentioned this already but it is worth repeating. Albert Heijn's bonuskaart is free, requires no commitment, and without it you simply pay more than everyone else for no reason. Set it up at ah.nl or in the app before your first shop. You can scan from your phone at the checkout — no physical card needed.

Jumbo's equivalent is the Jumbo app with the "Extra's" loyalty feature. PLUS has the PLUS card. Lidl has Lidl Plus. All are free.

### Understand Statiegeld

Statiegeld is a bottle and can deposit scheme. When you buy a plastic bottle of water, soft drink, or juice, you pay a deposit of EUR 0.15 (for bottles under 1 litre) or EUR 0.25 (for larger bottles and glass bottles). Aluminium cans also carry a EUR 0.15 deposit since 2023.

When you finish the drink, take the empty container to the retourautomaat — a machine near the supermarket entrance, sometimes called the "fles machine" — feed in the empties, and collect a voucher for the deposit amount. You spend the voucher at that store's checkout; it is deducted from your bill.

Key points:
- You can return empties to any supermarket, not just where you bought them
- The machine prints a voucher — do not lose it before you pay
- Not all containers are eligible. The barcode is scanned; if it does not register, the bottle is not in the system
- Bags of cans or bottles will each need to be inserted individually

Once you get used to it, statiegeld becomes second nature. It keeps streets, parks, and cycle paths remarkably litter-free.

### Sunday Shopping

Most Dutch supermarkets are open on Sundays, but with shorter hours — typically 11:00 to 18:00 or 10:00 to 19:00. In large cities this is entirely reliable. In smaller towns, hours can vary and some shops may be closed entirely. If you are settling in a smaller place, check local Sunday hours in the first week. Albert Heijn to go stores in train stations tend to have the longest hours, including Sundays.

### Self-Scan (Scan & Go)

Both AH and Jumbo offer self-scan shopping: you scan items with your phone as you put them in your basket, then pay at a dedicated terminal without unloading everything onto a conveyor belt. AH calls it "Scan & Go" and it works through the AH app. Jumbo has a similar system. It is faster for medium and large shops and avoids queuing behind people with overflowing trolleys.

At self-scan checkouts, random basket checks are common — a staff member will occasionally ask to verify a few items. Just scan the items they point to. It is routine and not an accusation.

### Bags and Packaging

Plastic carrier bags are not free in Dutch supermarkets. Bring your own reusable bags (a "tas" — most chain checkouts sell sturdy reusable ones for EUR 1-2) or pay EUR 0.25-0.50 per plastic bag. This catches out many new arrivals on their first shop.

For produce like loose fruit and vegetables, most supermarkets now have paper bags or no bags at all. In the AH app, you can get a "no bag" discount on orders if you collect them yourself.

### Online Grocery Delivery

The two main full-supermarket delivery options are Albert Heijn (ah.nl) and Jumbo (jumbo.com). Both offer reliable next-day and same-day delivery in most Dutch cities.

For faster delivery (under 30 minutes) there are specialist services. See my detailed guide to [grocery delivery in the Netherlands](/guides/daily-life/best-grocery-delivery-netherlands-2026/) for a full comparison of Gorillas (now Getir), Picnic, Flink, and the supermarket services.

## Finding International and Specialist Ingredients

No Dutch supermarket, however large, covers everything. For more specialist items, you will need to explore beyond the main chains.

**Toko:** A toko is an Indonesian or broader Asian grocery shop. Every Dutch city has several, and they are excellent for soy sauces, fish sauce, tempeh, tofu, Asian noodles, spices, coconut products, banana leaves, and much more. Prices are typically lower than supermarket equivalents. Toko De Zon, Toko Ramee, and similar small shops are worth finding in your neighbourhood.

**Moroccan and Turkish supermarkets:** Concentrated in larger cities. Excellent for halal meat, fresh herbs, bulk spices, olives, and flatbreads. Often significantly cheaper than Dutch chains for these products.

**Surinamese shops:** Amsterdam has a strong Surinamese community and various shops selling roti, cassava, specific spices, and preserved goods unavailable in mainstream chains.

**Organic and health food:** Ekoplaza is a dedicated organic supermarket with branches in major cities. It is expensive but the range is remarkable. Marqt is a premium food shop focused on local and sustainable sourcing.

**British and American products:** The British Store in Amsterdam stocks a wide range of British pantry staples. Online shops like British Corner Shop and American Food Mart deliver across the Netherlands.

## Supermarkets by City: Where to Go

Your city determines your options significantly. Here is a quick guide:

**Amsterdam:** All major chains present. Strong Dirk presence in Oost, Zuid, and Noord. Several AH XL stores with the widest range. The Markthal equivalent is Foodhallen for premium prepared food. Good toko and international shop density in the Pijp and Oost.

**Rotterdam:** Similar to Amsterdam. Dirk well represented. Strong Surinamese and Moroccan supermarkets in areas like Delfshaven. See my guide to [moving to Rotterdam](/guides/housing/moving-to-rotterdam-guide-2026/) for more on shopping and neighbourhood specifics.

**The Hague:** AH and Jumbo dominant. Strong Turkish and Moroccan supermarket presence in the Schilderswijk and Transvaal areas. See the [moving to The Hague guide](/guides/housing/moving-to-the-hague-guide-2026/) for more.

**Utrecht:** Good AH and Jumbo coverage. A well-regarded Jumbo Foodmarkt. Dirk present in some areas. See the [moving to Utrecht guide](/guides/housing/moving-to-utrecht-guide-2026/).

**Eindhoven:** AH and Jumbo dominant. Some Lidl and Aldi. International range in main AH is reasonable given the expat tech community. See the [moving to Eindhoven guide](/guides/housing/moving-to-eindhoven-guide-2026/).

## My Personal Shopping Strategy

After ten years, this is what I actually do:

I do the main weekly shop at Jumbo because the nearest branch to me is a large-format store with wide aisles and genuinely good fresh produce. I use the Jumbo app for my list and scan as I go.

For top-ups mid-week — and whenever I need something specific that Jumbo does not carry — I go to Albert Heijn. I keep the AH app on my phone and check bonus deals on Monday mornings to plan what to buy where.

For produce and staples when price matters, I occasionally do a Lidl run, particularly for fruit and their excellent bakery bread.

For anything South or Southeast Asian, I use the toko around the corner. The prices for things like kecap manis, dried shrimp paste, and fresh lemongrass are a fraction of what AH charges.

I almost never use Spar for anything beyond genuine emergencies.

This might sound like a lot of shops. But in a Dutch city, multiple supermarkets are almost always within cycling distance. Once you know your area, splitting your shop across two or three stores to optimise for price and range is genuinely easy. If that sounds like more effort than it is worth, simply use AH with the bonuskaart and you will be fine.

## Planning Your Budget

Grocery costs are one of the more controllable parts of living in the Netherlands. A single person can eat well on EUR 200–250 per month shopping primarily at AH or Jumbo with bonus deals. Switching to Jumbo as your main shop and using Lidl for staples can bring that to EUR 150–180.

## Quick Reference

**Best overall for expats:** Albert Heijn (bonuskaart a must)

**Best value:** Dirk van den Broek (Randstad only) or Lidl (nationwide)

**Best for international products:** Albert Heijn XL

**Best large-format experience:** Jumbo Foodmarkt

**Best neighbourhood feel:** PLUS

**Avoid for regular shopping:** Spar (use only in emergencies)

**Do not forget:** Statiegeld deposits, bring your own bags, bonuskaart before first shop

Shopping in the Netherlands is genuinely pleasant once you know the system. The quality of food — particularly fresh produce, dairy, and bread — is high by European standards, and the price competition between chains keeps costs reasonable. Get the loyalty apps set up, find your nearest toko, and you will be eating well within your first week.
