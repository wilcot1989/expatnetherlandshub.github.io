Within my first week of arriving in the Netherlands, long before I became a relocation coach, I was sent a Tikkie for €12.50, pointed to the 9292 app for public transport, and told to check Buienradar before leaving the house. I had no idea what any of these were. By the end of that week, I had all three open daily and had realised that Dutch daily life runs, to a surprising degree, on a specific set of apps that the whole country uses and that no one outside the Netherlands has heard of.

This guide covers the apps you will genuinely use, not a list of everything that exists. I've included the government apps that are annoying to set up but unavoidable, the transport apps that make getting around practical, the food and shopping apps that save time and money, and the one finance app I recommend for managing the security side of all this.

For the broader financial picture, the [best finance apps for expats in the Netherlands](/guides/finance/best-finance-apps-expats-netherlands-2026/) covers banking and money transfer apps in more depth.

## Government and Identity Apps

### DigiD — Your Digital Dutch Identity

**What it is:** DigiD (Digitale Identiteit) is the Dutch government's digital identity system. It is how you prove who you are when interacting with government portals, healthcare systems, and many regulated services online.

**Why you need it:** Without DigiD, you cannot file your tax return online, access the UWV benefits portal, log into Mijn Overheid, complete many municipality registrations online, or use the zorgtoeslag (healthcare allowance) portal. Almost every government interaction in the Netherlands that can be done digitally requires DigiD.

**How to get it:** You register online at digid.nl, providing your BSN (citizen service number). The initial registration requires a verification letter sent to your registered address — this takes 3-5 days. After that, you set up the DigiD app with SMS verification. See the [DigiD guide for expats](/guides/legal/digid-guide-expats-2026/) for the complete step-by-step process.

**Free/paid:** Free.

**English available:** Partial — the website has English but some linked government portals are Dutch-only.

**Priority:** Get this in your first two weeks.

---

### Mijn Overheid — Your Government Inbox

**What it is:** MijnOverheid.nl / app is the Dutch government's central citizen portal. Tax messages, municipality correspondence, and other official communications land here.

**Why you need it:** The Dutch government now sends important official messages digitally via Mijn Overheid rather than post. Tax assessments, reminders about registrations, and correspondence from the Belastingdienst (tax authority) may appear here. Some messages are sent only digitally now.

**Requires:** DigiD to log in.

**Free/paid:** Free.

---

### Belastingdienst App — Dutch Tax Authority

**What it is:** The Belastingdienst (tax authority) app lets you view your tax assessments, check status of your aangifte (tax return), and see correspondence.

**Why you need it:** The Netherlands taxes residents on worldwide income, and expats have specific obligations around the 30% ruling, Box 3 savings declarations, and annual returns. Having the app means you get notifications when things are due or when a message arrives. Read the [Dutch tax system guide](/guides/finance/dutch-tax-system-expats-guide-2026/) to understand what you are actually filing.

**Free/paid:** Free.

## Transport Apps

### 9292 — The National Public Transport Planner

**What it is:** 9292 (named after the old telephone information number) is the official Dutch national public transport journey planner. It covers every train, tram, metro, bus, and ferry in the Netherlands in one app, with real-time disruption information.

**Why you need it:** NS (the train app, covered below) only covers trains. 9292 covers the entire network — so when you need to take a train plus a bus, or a metro plus a tram, 9292 plans the full route. It is the app to have for any journey involving a mode of transport that is not purely train.

**Key feature:** The disruption alerts are genuinely useful — Dutch public transport has a good overall record, but when something goes wrong, 9292 shows you alternatives in real time.

**Free/paid:** Free. A paid version removes ads and adds some extras, but the free version is entirely functional.

The [OV-chipkaart guide](/guides/daily-life/ov-chipkaart-guide-expats-2026/) explains how to pay for all these journeys once you have planned them.

---

### NS — The National Rail App

**What it is:** The NS app is the Nederlandse Spoorwegen (Dutch Railways) official app for train travel specifically.

**Why you need it:** For buying train tickets, viewing your subscription (NS Business Card, Dal Voordeel, etc.), checking train platform information, and accessing real-time delays. It also shows the layout of specific trains so you know which carriage to board. The NS app has one useful feature that 9292 lacks: you can buy a digital train ticket directly in the app.

**Free/paid:** Free. Tickets purchased within the app are standard price — no surcharge.

**English:** Yes, fully available in English.

---

### OV-Chipkaart App (or via your bank app)

**What it is:** If you have an OV-chipkaart (the public transport chipcard), you can check your balance and transactions via the ov-chipkaart.nl portal or through the app. Alternatively, your bank app may show OV-chipkaart transactions directly.

**The trend:** Anonymous OV-chipkaart cards are being phased out in favour of contactless bank card payment directly on the turnstiles. By 2026, most major cities accept tap-to-pay via Mastercard, Visa, or Apple/Google Pay. However, some bus lines and smaller regional services still work better with a physical OV-chipkaart, so having both is sensible for the first year.

### Google Maps / Apple Maps — Still Necessary

**What it is:** You know what these are.

**Why I mention it:** In the Netherlands, Google Maps and Apple Maps are both well-calibrated for cycling routes — which is important, because cycling navigation is different from driving or walking navigation. Google Maps specifically has excellent cycling directions in Dutch cities, including dedicated cycle path routing. Apple Maps has improved significantly. Both also integrate public transport directions.

## Weather Apps

### Buienradar — Rain Radar (Non-Negotiable)

**What it is:** Buienradar is the Dutch rain radar app. It shows a real-time and 2-hour animated map of rainfall across the Netherlands, updated every 5 minutes.

**Why you need it:** Dutch weather is famously unpredictable. The difference between leaving the house now and in 15 minutes can be the difference between cycling in sunshine and arriving completely soaked. Buienradar is how every Dutch person decides whether to leave now, wait a bit, or take the bus.

The app also shows hourly forecasts and a curve showing exactly when rain will start and stop at your location. The accuracy for 0-2 hour windows is genuinely impressive — not perfect, but substantially better than a general weather forecast.

**Free/paid:** Free (with ads). Premium version removes ads and adds multi-day radar, but the free version is what most people use.

**English:** Yes.

This app alone will change your relationship with Dutch weather. You will stop being surprised by sudden rain within two weeks of installing it. Read more about [Dutch weather and what to expect throughout the year](/guides/daily-life/netherlands-climate-weather-expats-2026/).

## Shopping and Food Apps

### Albert Heijn — Supermarket App

**What it is:** The app for Albert Heijn, the Netherlands' largest supermarket chain with around 1,000 stores.

**Why you need it:** The AH app is genuinely useful beyond just shopping lists. The **Bonuskaart** (loyalty card) function in the app gives you access to the weekly discount offers — without it, you pay full price on items that are on Bonus for cardholders. In practice, weekly Bonus discounts of 25-50% on common items make the app worth having from day one.

Additional features: digital receipts, online shopping for home delivery, and the self-scan function in larger AH stores (scan items as you go, skip the checkout queue).

**Free/paid:** Free.

**English:** Available in English.

---

### Thuisbezorgd — Food Delivery

**What it is:** Thuisbezorgd (literally: delivered to your home) is the Dutch market leader in food delivery — it is the local brand under Just Eat Takeaway.

**Why you need it:** When you are too tired to cook in your first weeks and have not yet learned where the best local takeaway spots are, Thuisbezorgd maps every delivery restaurant in your area. It covers most major cities and many mid-sized towns. Delivery times in the Netherlands are generally good — 30-45 minutes in most urban areas.

**Free/paid:** Free. Delivery fees apply per order; a monthly subscription (Thuisbezorgd+) reduces or eliminates them if you order frequently.

**English:** Yes.

See also the [grocery delivery guide for the Netherlands](/guides/daily-life/best-grocery-delivery-netherlands-2026/) for grocery delivery options specifically.

---

### Marktplaats — Buy and Sell Second-Hand

**What it is:** Marktplaats is the Dutch equivalent of Gumtree or Craigslist — a large, well-maintained second-hand marketplace. Unlike Facebook Marketplace, it has a proper search function and national reach.

**Why you need it:** Moving to a new country means you are missing a lot of things. Buying second-hand furniture, a bicycle, appliances, or children's toys on Marktplaats saves you hundreds compared to buying new. It is also where Dutch households sell everything when they move, upgrade, or downsize — the quality of listings is generally high.

**Practical tip:** Bicycles on Marktplaats in €80-€150 range are the realistic starting point for a functional city bike. Do not buy the €30 ones — they are usually either stolen or broken, and often both.

**Free/paid:** Free (listing fees for some categories).

## Housing Apps

### Funda — Property Search

**What it is:** Funda is the dominant Dutch property portal, covering both buying and renting. Most Dutch real estate agents list exclusively on Funda — it is not one of several options, it is the main market.

**Why you need it:** If you are searching for rental properties in the free sector, Funda is the first place to look. Set up alerts for your target area, size, and price range — the app will notify you the moment a new listing appears.

**Key feature:** Funda's map search is well-designed. You can search by neighbourhood, see individual streets, and toggle between satellite and street view to get a real sense of an area before visiting.

**Free/paid:** Free for searching. Agents pay for listings.

For a detailed comparison of Funda vs other housing platforms, see the [finding housing in the Netherlands guide](/guides/housing/finding-housing-netherlands-expats-2026/).

---

### Kamernet — Rooms and Short-Term Rentals

**What it is:** Kamernet specialises in room rentals, short-term furnished apartments, and expat-oriented housing. It is particularly strong for people who need a furnished place quickly, or who are looking for a room in a shared house.

**Why you need it:** If you need housing in the first few weeks before your permanent flat is available, Kamernet's short-term furnished listings are the realistic option. The platform also lists many expat-oriented apartments in the €1,000-€1,800/month range for one-bedroom furnished properties.

**Free/paid:** Searching is free; subscribing to contact landlords and get priority notifications costs €15-€30/month. Worth the cost if you are actively searching.

## Finance Apps

### iDEAL — Online Payment

**What it is:** iDEAL is the Netherlands' national online payment system, used by approximately 90% of Dutch webshops and payment portals.

**How it works:** You do not download an "iDEAL app" as such — iDEAL appears as a payment option in checkout flows. When you select it, you choose your bank and are redirected to your bank's own app to authorise the payment. It is instant, free, and extremely widely accepted.

**Why you need it:** You cannot properly function in Dutch online shopping, government portals, or utility payments without iDEAL access. It requires a Dutch bank account. Opening a Dutch bank account quickly after arrival is therefore a priority — see the [Dutch bank account guide for expats](/guides/finance/best-bank-account-expats-netherlands-2026/).

---

### Tikkie — Splitting Bills

**What it is:** Tikkie is a payment request app, originally developed by ABN AMRO but now used by customers of all Dutch banks. You send someone a Tikkie (a link) for an amount you are owed, they tap it and pay instantly via iDEAL.

**Why you need it:** The Dutch social culture runs on splitting costs precisely, and Tikkie is the mechanism. Dinner with colleagues? Someone pays and sends a Tikkie. Group gift? Tikkie. Shared Airbnb? Tikkie. You will receive your first Tikkie within days of arriving and meeting Dutch people. Not having the app set up means awkward cash transfers or PayPal requests that no one uses.

**Setup requirement:** Dutch bank account + phone number.

**Free/paid:** Free for personal use.

## Security and Privacy Apps

### NordVPN — Secure Your App Usage

**What it is:** NordVPN is a leading VPN (Virtual Private Network) service that encrypts your internet connection and masks your IP address.

**Why expats specifically need it:** You have just moved countries. You are managing a Dutch bank account, possibly a home-country bank account, financial apps, and government portals — sometimes all from the same phone, sometimes on public Wi-Fi. Here is when a VPN earns its keep:

1. **Banking security on public Wi-Fi** — cafés, coworking spaces, and hotel networks are convenient but expose unencrypted traffic. A VPN encrypts everything from your phone to the VPN server.

3. **Maintaining privacy** — the Netherlands has strong privacy laws, but ISPs still log connection metadata. A VPN limits what is visible to your internet provider.

4. **Secure remote work** — if you work for an international employer and connect to company systems, many IT departments recommend or require VPN.

## A Note on Dutch Apps and Dutch Language

Many of these apps — particularly Buienradar, Marktplaats, and older versions of Thuisbezorgd — were primarily designed for Dutch users and still have Dutch as the default language in some sections. Most have English settings by now, but some system notifications and error messages may still come in Dutch.

This is genuinely not a barrier to using them. The functionality is clear enough, and a quick translation via the built-in phone translate function handles anything unclear. After a few months in the Netherlands, you will start to recognise the Dutch words for common concepts — bezorging (delivery), bestelling (order), vertraging (delay) — and the apps will feel natural.

## Additional Apps Worth Having by Category

### Healthcare Apps

**Zorgkaart Nederland:** A patient review platform for Dutch healthcare providers. If you are looking for a new GP (huisarts), specialist, or therapist and want to see genuine patient ratings, this is the place. It covers practically every registered healthcare provider in the Netherlands and the reviews are typically candid.

**Jouw Omgeving (your area) app:** Some municipalities have neighbourhood apps where residents can report issues (a broken streetlight, an abandoned bike), check local news, and connect with municipal services. Amsterdam has its own official app (Amsterdam app); other cities have similar services. Useful for practical neighbourhood matters.

**PGO (Personal Health Environment) apps:** DigiD links to Personal Health Environments where you can view your own medical records as shared by your healthcare providers. MedMij is the Dutch standard for this. The functionality is still developing, but by 2026 several health providers share data through this system.

### Finance and Banking Apps

**Bunq:** If you are using Bunq as your Dutch bank (increasingly popular with expats), their app is one of the best designed in the Netherlands — real-time notifications, sub-accounts, and multi-currency support. The premium subscription includes carbon tracking and tree planting, which the Dutch environmental community has adopted enthusiastically.

**Moneybird app:** For ZZP'ers and freelancers using Moneybird for accounting, the app lets you photograph and submit receipts on the go, create invoices from your phone, and check outstanding payments. The mobile app is functional but less powerful than the desktop version. See the [freelancer accounting guide](/guides/work/best-accounting-software-freelancers-netherlands-2026/) for full context on Dutch accounting software.

**ABN AMRO or ING banking apps:** Both major banks have well-designed apps that handle iDEAL, contactless payments, and the full range of banking functions. ING's app has consistently received strong user ratings in the Netherlands; ABN AMRO's app has better international transfer functionality.

### Cycling Apps

**Komoot:** The best route planning app for longer recreational cycling in the Netherlands. It handles the knooppunten (node) network, suggests routes by difficulty, and works offline. Invaluable for day trips to the Veluwe, Zeeland coast, or bulb fields.

**Strava:** The cycling community uses Strava extensively in the Netherlands. The segment tracking and route replay features are useful for anyone training seriously, and Strava's heatmap shows the most popular cycling routes in any Dutch city — which is genuinely useful for route planning.

**Swapfiets and Lease a Bike apps:** If you use a subscription bike service rather than owning your own (common among expats who do not want the hassle of maintaining or insuring a bike), both Swapfiets and Lease a Bike have apps for managing your subscription and reporting problems.

### Language Learning Apps

If you are working on your Dutch (which I recommend — even basic Dutch dramatically improves your experience of the country), several apps work well alongside the full language courses:

**Babbel:** The most structured app-based Dutch learning available. Focuses on practical conversational Dutch rather than vocabulary games. See the [Dutch language learning guide](/guides/integration/best-dutch-language-courses-2026/) for a fuller comparison.

**Anki (flashcard app):** For building Dutch vocabulary systematically, Anki's spaced repetition flashcard system is highly effective. The Netherlands expat community has shared Dutch vocabulary decks online that you can import for free.

## Managing Multiple Accounts Securely

One practical challenge for expats that the Dutch app ecosystem creates: you end up managing a lot of accounts simultaneously. Dutch bank account, possibly home-country bank account, DigiD for government services, tax portals, healthcare portals, rental platforms, and all the social and work apps. Each has its own login.

A few practices that make this manageable:

**Use a password manager.** 1Password and Bitwarden are both widely used in the Netherlands. Many expat employers cover the cost of 1Password as a work tool. Having strong unique passwords for every account, retrievable in one place, is the baseline for managing this complexity.

**Enable two-factor authentication on everything that supports it.** DigiD already requires it (SMS verification). Dutch banks require it by default. For everything else — email, financial apps, housing platforms — enable 2FA.

**Be aware of DigiD phishing.** The Dutch government regularly warns about phishing emails and SMS messages impersonating DigiD. The real DigiD never sends links asking you to confirm your account urgently. When in doubt, go directly to digid.nl in your browser rather than clicking any link.

The Dutch app ecosystem is, in my view, a genuine reflection of how the Netherlands works: practical, efficient, with a slight bias toward assuming you know what you are doing. Once you have the core stack set up — DigiD, your bank app, Tikkie, 9292, Buienradar — daily life in the Netherlands becomes considerably less friction. Install them in order, get the authentication sorted in the first two weeks, and you will spend a lot less time confused and a lot more time cycling through the rain at the optimal moment.

## Apps That Dutch Expats Wish They Had Found Sooner

After many years helping people settle into the Netherlands, I have noticed consistent patterns: certain apps are mentioned in retrospect by almost everyone as "I wish I had found this sooner." Here is that list.

**Too Good To Go:** Food waste app where restaurants, supermarkets, and bakeries sell end-of-day surplus at 60–70% discount. A "magic bag" from a local bakery costs €3–4 and contains €10–15 of bread, pastries, and sandwiches. Used daily by a significant portion of the Amsterdam population and expanding rapidly. It also becomes a reliable way to try local food places — the surprise element is part of the appeal. Available across all Dutch cities.

**Nextdoor (Burenapp):** A neighbourhood social network used extensively in Dutch residential areas. People post about lost cats, neighbourhood events, local recommendations, secondhand items, and mutual help requests. It is one of the fastest ways to understand and connect with the people literally around you. Many expats who have used it describe it as transforming their sense of neighbourhood belonging within weeks of joining.

**Donkey Republic / Swapfiets:** For expats who do not yet have a bike and do not want to invest in one, Donkey Republic offers dock-free bike rental by the hour or day (€2–6/hour). Swapfiets is a subscription bike service (€19–28/month) where they deliver a bike to your door, maintain it, and replace it if it breaks. Neither is as economical as owning a secondhand bike for long-term use, but both solve the first-weeks transport problem while you find your feet.

**Funda:** The dominant Dutch property platform for renting and buying. Even if you are already housed, creating a Funda account and enabling alerts is standard practice for anyone anticipating a housing move. The Dutch rental market moves fast — the most desirable listings are gone within days. Having alerts set up means you see new listings as they go live.

**Thuisbezorgd for the "meeneem" moment:** Beyond delivery, the Thuisbezorgd app is useful for preordering pickup meals from restaurants — ordering ahead to collect on your way home. This "meeneem" (takeaway pickup) use case is underappreciated and is genuinely convenient in Dutch cities where walking past excellent food on the way home from work is a daily temptation.

## App Permissions and Privacy: What to Accept

One distinctly Dutch practical note: Dutch apps and government services take data privacy more seriously than many equivalents in the US or UK. Several apps ask for minimal permissions and explain clearly what they collect.

DigiD collects very limited usage data. The Belastingdienst app is among the most privacy-respecting tax apps in Europe. Dutch banking apps comply with strong European PSD2 and GDPR requirements.

When apps ask for location permissions, microphone access, or contact list access, consider whether the functionality requires it. Buienradar genuinely requires location to give hyperlocal rain forecasts — approve it. A shopping app asking for microphone access with no clear reason — decline it.

The Netherlands has an active digital rights community (Bits of Freedom, for example) that regularly audits popular Dutch apps for privacy compliance. Their reports are public and worth checking if you are privacy-conscious.
