When I moved to the Netherlands eleven years ago, I did not yet have children. I thought about schools vaguely, the way you do when it still feels hypothetical. Then I had two kids, worked through the Dutch childcare system, sat through parent evenings at a local Dutch school, discovered kinderbijslag, argued with myself over whether to buy a bakfiets — and realised that the Netherlands is, genuinely, an outstanding place to raise a family. Not perfect, but very good. This guide covers everything I have learned and everything I tell the families I coach when they arrive here with children in tow.

---

## Why the Netherlands Works Well for Expat Families

Before we get into the mechanics, it helps to understand what makes this country such a good place for children.

UNICEF has ranked the Netherlands among the top two or three countries in the world for child wellbeing in multiple studies. Dutch children report high levels of happiness, low levels of school pressure in primary years, and strong social connections. That is not an accident — it reflects deliberate cultural and policy choices.

**What stands out from a practical perspective:**

- **Childcare subsidies** that can cover up to 96% of costs for lower-income families
- **Free dental care** for all children under 18, included in the basic health insurance
- **Kinderbijslag** — quarterly cash payments for every child, no means-test required
- **Excellent public education** that is essentially free, with strong international options in major cities
- **Safe infrastructure** — the cycling paths, the wide pavements, the 30 km/h zones in residential areas — all of it is genuinely built around children
- **Part-time work culture** that makes it possible for parents to actually spend time with their kids without career penalties

The trade-offs exist too — Dutch cities are expensive, international school fees are high, waiting lists for good daycare are brutal — and I will not pretend otherwise. But for families who do the groundwork, the Netherlands delivers.

## The Dutch Childcare System: What You Need to Know

Childcare in the Netherlands is broken into a few distinct types, and knowing which one applies to your situation saves a lot of confusion. I have a full breakdown in my [Dutch childcare guide for expats](/guides/daily-life/dutch-childcare-expat-guide-2026/), but here is the summary.

### Kinderdagverblijf (KDV) — Daycare for 0-4 Year Olds

This is the standard daycare centre, known as a KDV or kinderdagverblijf. It operates Monday to Friday during working hours, typically 7:30 or 8:00 to 18:00 or 18:30. Children are grouped by age and looked after in small groups — legally, the staff-to-child ratio is 1:3 for babies and 1:5 for toddlers.

KDV is where most expat families start, and the quality is generally high. Regulated by the GGD (municipal health service), with inspection reports publicly available at landelijkregisterkinderopvang.nl. If a centre is not on that register, it does not qualify for childcare subsidy — do not enrol your child there.

**Costs:** €8-€10 per hour before subsidy. Monthly costs for 3 days per week typically land between €1,200-€2,000. After kinderopvangtoeslag, most families pay €200-€800/month. See the subsidy section below for the full picture.

**Waiting lists:** In Amsterdam, Utrecht, Eindhoven, and The Hague, waiting lists of 6-18 months are common at popular centres. Register before your child is born. Register when you arrive. Register now.

### Gastouderopvang — Childminding

A gastouder is a registered childminder who looks after a small group of children — maximum 6 — in a home environment. The gastouder must be registered with a gastouderbureau (intermediary agency), and only registered arrangements qualify for subsidy.

Gastouder care tends to be more flexible than KDV — hours, days, and routines can be tailored. The home setting works well for some children who do not thrive in larger group environments. Rates are typically €6-€9 per hour.

For expat families, gastouder care can be a good solution if you cannot find a KDV place or if your work schedule is irregular.

### BSO — Buitenschoolse Opvang (After-School Care)

Once your child starts school at age 4, BSO covers before-school and after-school hours. It bridges the gap between the school day (which ends at 15:00 or 15:30) and a full working day.

BSO is often attached to or located near a school. Costs are similar to KDV, and kinderopvangtoeslag applies here too. Many Dutch families use BSO 2-3 days per week alongside a parent working reduced hours on other days.

### Peuterspeelzaal — Playgroup for 2-4 Year Olds

Peuterspeelzaal is a 2-4 morning-per-week group for children aged 2-4. It is not full-day childcare — it is more like a structured play and learning session. Some municipalities offer it at low or no cost, particularly for children who might benefit socially or developmentally. Worth asking your gemeente (municipality) about.

## Kinderopvangtoeslag: How the Subsidy Actually Works

This is the part that surprises most expat parents most pleasantly. The Dutch government subsidises a substantial portion of childcare costs through kinderopvangtoeslag. At the top end, families with the lowest incomes receive 96% reimbursement. Even families earning well above the average get meaningful support.

**How it is calculated:**

The subsidy is based on your combined household income (toetsingsinkomen). The lower your income, the higher the percentage covered. The government publishes tables each year showing exactly what percentage applies at each income bracket. In 2026:

- Combined income under €30,000: 96% reimbursement
- Combined income €50,000: approximately 76% reimbursement
- Combined income €80,000: approximately 61% reimbursement
- Combined income €120,000+: approximately 33% reimbursement

The subsidy applies up to a maximum hourly rate set by the government (the maximum uurtarief). If your daycare charges more than that, you pay the difference in full.

**To qualify, you need:**
1. Both parents working or studying (at least one hour a week counts)
2. A registered BSN (citizen service number)
3. Your child attending a registered childcare provider
4. A Dutch DigiD to apply online

**Apply at:** toeslagen.nl — the same portal used for zorgtoeslag and huurtoeslag. If you are new to Dutch subsidies, my [guide to zorgtoeslag and huurtoeslag for expats](/guides/finance/zorgtoeslag-huurtoeslag-expats-2026/) explains the system. Apply as soon as your child starts childcare — payments are backdated but the administration takes time.

**Common mistake:** Many expats assume that because they do not speak Dutch well, the subsidy is somehow harder for them to access. It is not. The system is open to all residents with valid documentation. If you need help, the gemeente often has expat desks or free tax advice services (belastingwinkel) that can assist.

## Kinderbijslag: Child Benefit Payments

Separate from the childcare subsidy is kinderbijslag — a quarterly cash payment for every child under 18, administered by the SVB (Sociale Verzekeringsbank). There is no means test. If you have children and you live and work in the Netherlands, you receive it.

**2026 amounts (per child, per quarter):**
- Age 0-5: approximately €264
- Age 6-11: approximately €321
- Age 12-17: approximately €378

You apply once at svb.nl after your child is born or after your family registers in the Netherlands. Once approved, payments arrive automatically every quarter (January, April, July, October).

If you have two children aged 4 and 10, you receive roughly €585 per quarter — over €2,300 per year — without any bureaucratic renewal process. For expat families managing a high cost of living, this matters.

Note: kinderbijslag is different from kindgebonden budget, which is an additional income-tested supplement for lower-income families with children. If your household income is below roughly €60,000, check whether you qualify for this extra amount too.

## International Schools vs. Dutch Schools: Making the Choice

This is the question I am asked most often, and it genuinely depends on your situation. I have explored it in depth in my [international schools guide](/guides/daily-life/international-schools-netherlands-2026/), but let me give you the honest version here.

### International Schools: When They Make Sense

International schools — offering IB, British, American, or other curricula — exist in every major Dutch city. The best-known are in Amsterdam (British School of Amsterdam, Amsterdam International Community School), The Hague (which has a large cluster around the diplomatic community), and Eindhoven.

**International school makes sense when:**
- You expect to stay under 2 years
- Your child is in secondary school and switching curricula mid-way would disrupt exam qualifications
- Your child has significant learning support needs that are better served in a smaller English-language environment
- You anticipate moving again and want curriculum continuity

**The real costs:** Fees run €5,500-€24,000 per year depending on age and school. Registration fees add €500-€3,000. Many employers cover these costs as part of relocation packages — if yours does, negotiate this before you sign.

**Waiting lists:** Popular schools in Amsterdam and The Hague have waiting lists of 1-2 years. Apply immediately upon deciding to relocate.

### Dutch Schools: Why Many Expats Choose Them

For families staying more than two years, especially those with younger children, Dutch schools are worth serious consideration — and in my experience, expat parents who choose them often say it was one of their best decisions.

**Dutch school advantages:**
- Essentially free (small voluntary parental contribution of €50-€150/year, which is genuinely voluntary)
- Excellent quality, particularly in primary (basisschool) years
- Children become fluent in Dutch, which opens doors socially and professionally in the long run
- Deep integration with local peers — your child builds real friendships, not just an expat bubble
- Less pressure and homework in primary years, which many parents find a relief

**The reality of language:** Children under 8 typically acquire Dutch language within 6-12 months in a Dutch school environment. Many municipalities offer schakelklassen (transition classes) for children who arrive with no Dutch — a short bridge program before joining the regular class. For older children (12+), the transition is harder, and this is where an international school often makes more sense.

**Finding a school:** Schools are assigned partly by postcode but also by denomination (public, Protestant, Catholic, and others all operate within the state system). Most schools welcome expat families. Contact the school directly, explain your situation, and ask about their experience with non-Dutch speaking children.

### The Third Option: Bilingual Schools

A growing number of Dutch primary and secondary schools offer tweetalig onderwijs (TTO) — bilingual Dutch-English education within the Dutch school system. These are not international schools, but they teach a significant portion of lessons in English. Fees are similar to regular Dutch schools. If you can find a place, this is often the best of both worlds for expat families.

## Healthcare for Expat Children in the Netherlands

One of the most welcome surprises for expat parents is how the Dutch health system treats children. I cover the full health insurance picture in my [Dutch health insurance guide for expats](/guides/health/dutch-health-insurance-guide-expats-2026/) and what the basic package covers in [this detailed breakdown](/guides/health/what-dutch-health-insurance-covers-2026/), but the key points for families are:

**Children are free under the basic insurance.** Children under 18 are co-insured under one parent's basisverzekering at no additional premium. You do not pay for your child's health insurance — period.

**The deductible does not apply to children.** Adults have an eigen risico (deductible) of €385 in 2026 — the first €385 of your annual medical costs is not covered. For children under 18, this deductible does not exist. Their medical costs are covered from the first euro.

**Dental care until 18 is fully covered.** This is the one that genuinely surprises most expat parents. In the Netherlands, all dental care for children under 18 is included in the basic health insurance package. No supplementary dental plan needed for your kids. Annual check-ups, fillings, orthodontics referrals — all covered.

**The GP (huisarts) is the gatekeeper.** Just as for adults, children see the huisarts for almost all health matters. The huisarts refers to specialists when needed. Paediatric specialists are available at university hospitals and general hospitals. Well-baby checks are done by the consultatiebureau (child health clinic), a free public health service that tracks development from birth to age 4.

**JGZ — Youth Health Care:** The jeugdgezondheidszorg offers regular free health checks, vaccinations, and developmental screening for all children up to age 18. Vaccinations through the national programme are free. This is a complete public health system that most expat families find reassuring.

### Travel Health Coverage for Families

The Dutch health insurance covers you in the Netherlands and within the EU for emergency treatment. For families who travel internationally — for holidays, to visit family, or for work — standard Dutch insurance leaves gaps. This is where an international travel health plan is useful.

## Dutch Parenting Culture: What to Expect

This section exists because culture shock around parenting style is real, and the Netherlands is distinctive enough that it is worth preparing for.

### "Normaal Doen" — The Dutch Approach to Normal

The Dutch have a phrase: doe maar gewoon, dan doe je al gek genoeg — roughly "just act normal, that's already crazy enough." It applies to parenting too. Dutch child-rearing tends to be relaxed, non-competitive, and focused on independence. You will notice:

**Children cycle alone from a young age.** By age 8 or 9, many Dutch children cycle to school unaccompanied. This is not neglect — it is the Dutch default, enabled by excellent cycling infrastructure and a culture of child independence. Expat parents sometimes find this confronting at first. Most come around to it.

**Less structured enrichment, more free play.** Dutch parents do not typically stack afterschool activities the way is common in some expat cultures. Children have time after school to play outside, often unsupervised. Schools actively encourage this.

**Direct communication.** Dutch adults speak to children directly and honestly, often without the softening that other cultures might use. Children are expected to have opinions and express them. This is not rudeness — it is the Dutch directness applied to child-rearing.

**Part-time work as the norm.** The "papadag" (daddy day) is a real institution — many Dutch fathers work four days a week and take care of children on the fifth. This is socially accepted and employer-supported in a way that is genuinely unusual internationally. Many expat parents find the Dutch part-time culture gives them permission they did not realise they had.

### School Culture

Dutch primary school (basisschool) runs from age 4 to 12. The first two years (groep 1 and 2) are play-based with very little formal instruction — children are not expected to read until age 6, and the emphasis is on play, creativity, and social development. This is sometimes a surprise for expat parents from systems where academic pressure starts earlier. Most find they appreciate it in retrospect.

School days end at 15:00-15:30 for most Dutch primary schools, with Wednesday often a half day (woensdagmiddag vrij). This shapes the entire family schedule and is partly why BSO and part-time work patterns are so common.

**No homework until later.** Formal homework is minimal until secondary school. Projects and reading are encouraged, but the pile-of-worksheets model common in some other systems is not the Dutch way.

## Practical Activities for Expat Families

The Netherlands has genuinely excellent options for children's activities, sports, and social connection.

### Sports and Clubs

The Dutch association sports culture (verenigingsleven) is strong. Most communities have football clubs, hockey clubs, gymnastics, swimming, tennis, and more — all operating through local associations with affordable membership fees (typically €50-€200/year). This is where Dutch children build their social world outside school.

For expat families, joining a local sports club is one of the best integration tools for children and parents alike. Your child does not need to speak Dutch to play football. Connections form on the pitch.

**Schaatsen (ice skating):** Almost every Dutch city has an ice rink. Skating lessons for children are inexpensive and ubiquitous. It is a genuine Dutch cultural experience.

**Swimming:** The Dutch take swimming seriously — there is even a cultural tradition of swimdiploma certificates (A, B, and C levels) that most Dutch children work through. If you are raising children here long-term, the diploma programme is worth knowing about.

### Cultural Activities and Days Out

- **Museums:** Many Dutch museums are free or discounted for children. The Rijksmuseum, the Science Center NEMO (Amsterdam), the Madurodam (The Hague), and Efteling (theme park) are family favourites.
- **National parks:** Hoge Veluwe, the Biesbosch, and Schoorl dunes are all accessible day trips.
- **Market days:** The Dutch love a market (markt), and they are excellent for children — food, activity, local life.

### Building a Social Network

Social integration for expat children — and for expat parents — takes time and effort. I have written about this in more depth in my [guide to making friends in the Netherlands as an expat](/guides/integration/making-friends-netherlands-expats-2026/). For families specifically:

- **School parent groups** are the fastest route. Show up at school events, volunteer for the medezeggenschapsraad (parent council) or the ouderhulp.
- **Playgroups:** Many churches and community centres run English-language playgroups for expat families. FIGT, Expatica, and local Facebook groups are good starting points.
- **Sports clubs** as mentioned above.
- **Neighbourhood life:** Dutch neighbourhoods are more social than they might first appear. Introduce yourself, be present in shared spaces, and connections develop.

## Best Cities for Expat Families

Not all Dutch cities offer the same experience for families. I cover this in full in my [best cities for expats guide](/guides/housing/best-cities-netherlands-expats-2026/), but here is the family-specific picture.

**Amsterdam** offers the most international schools and the most established expat family community, but it is the most expensive city and has significant childcare waiting list pressure. Best for families with employer support and children already in the international school pipeline.

**The Hague** is the preferred city for many diplomatic and international organisation families. Strong international school offering, slightly lower housing costs than Amsterdam, and a large, established international community. Very family-friendly.

**Eindhoven** has grown strongly as a family destination thanks to ASML and the tech sector. Good international school options (International School Eindhoven), lower cost of living than Amsterdam or The Hague, and a genuinely liveable city with good cycling infrastructure.

**Utrecht** is a beautiful, manageable city with a strong university culture. More limited international school options than Amsterdam or The Hague, but excellent Dutch schools and a very family-friendly centre. A good choice for families comfortable with Dutch schooling.

**Rotterdam** has become significantly more attractive for expat families in recent years. Lower housing costs, excellent urban infrastructure, a growing international community, and Rotterdam International Secondary School for older children.

**Smaller cities (Groningen, Maastricht, Arnhem):** If your employer is there, they can be excellent — lower costs, more space, strong community feel. International schooling options are limited, so Dutch schooling becomes the default.

Housing costs and family budget matter enormously here. ## Financial Planning for Expat Families

Raising a family in the Netherlands is expensive on one dimension (housing, international school fees) and supported on another (childcare subsidies, kinderbijslag, free healthcare for children). Getting the financial picture right from the start matters.

**Budget framework for a family of 4 in Amsterdam:**

| Item | Monthly estimate |
|------|-----------------|
| Rent, 3-bed flat | €2,200-€3,500 |
| Childcare (after subsidy, 2 children, 3 days/week each) | €600-€1,400 |
| Groceries | €600-€900 |
| Health insurance (2 adults, children free) | €240-€320 |
| Transport | €150-€250 |
| Activities and clubs | €100-€200 |
| **Total (excl. international school)** | **~€4,000-€6,500** |
| International school (if applicable) | +€700-€2,000/month |

Kinderbijslag (approximately €585/quarter for 2 children aged 4-10) offsets some costs. Kinderopvangtoeslag can save €1,000-€2,000/month depending on income.

For a realistic picture of your family budget, factor in the [cost of living in the Netherlands](/guides/housing/cost-of-living-netherlands-2026/) and use the available [tools to check your housing budget](/tools/housing-budget-checker/) before committing to a specific city or neighbourhood.

## Practical First Steps: A Checklist for Arriving Expat Families

This is the sequence I recommend to every expat family I work with.

**Before you arrive:**
- [ ] Research and apply to childcare providers — do not wait until you land
- [ ] Apply to international schools if relevant (6-12 months lead time minimum)
- [ ] Check whether your employer's relocation package covers school fees
- [ ] Research cities against your work location and family priorities

**Within the first month:**
- [ ] Register at the gemeente (municipal registration — BRP)
- [ ] Get your BSN numbers (you need these for everything)
- [ ] Register with a huisarts (GP) and dentist
- [ ] Apply for kinderopvangtoeslag at toeslagen.nl (use your DigiD)
- [ ] Apply for kinderbijslag at svb.nl
- [ ] Register children at school (Dutch) or confirm international school place
- [ ] Arrange health insurance for all adults — children are co-insured

**Within the first three months:**
- [ ] Confirm childcare subsidy payments are arriving
- [ ] Join a local sports club or parent group
- [ ] Confirm your child is registered with the consultatiebureau for health checks (0-4 years)

## The Honest Summary

The Netherlands is not the cheapest place to raise a family, and the bureaucracy of arriving with children — registrations, subsidy applications, school waiting lists — is genuinely demanding. But the system, once you are in it, works. The childcare subsidies are real and significant. The education is excellent. The healthcare for children is genuinely free and full. The physical environment is safe, child-friendly, and built for outdoor life.

I have lived here over ten years and raised my own children here. I would not choose anywhere else.

If you are planning a move or are newly arrived and feeling overwhelmed, start with the practical steps: register, apply for subsidies, get on waiting lists. The rest follows. And if you need help working through the specifics, that is exactly what I do.
