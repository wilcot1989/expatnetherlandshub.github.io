My first King's Day in the Netherlands, I walked outside in a normal outfit and was immediately swallowed by a sea of orange. Thousands of people in orange wigs, orange suits, orange face paint -- selling old junk on blankets and dancing on boats in the canals. Nobody had warned me. That is the thing about Dutch holidays: they are wonderfully bizarre, and as a half-Dutch person who grew up in the UK, even I was caught off guard. Here is your heads-up for every major holiday and tradition so you will not be surprised by a closed supermarket or an unexpected orange invasion.

## The Big Celebrations

### King's Day (Koningsdag) — April 27

The biggest, loudest, most orange holiday in the Netherlands. The entire country celebrates the King's birthday. If this is your first time experiencing it, the [King's Day guide for expats](/guides/daily-life/kings-day-guide-expats-2026/) walks you through everything — what to wear, where to go, and how to survive the vrijmarkt.

**What happens:**
- **Vrijmarkt** — Everyone sells their stuff on the street (the only day you can trade tax-free)
- **Orange everywhere** — Clothes, wigs, face paint, food, drinks — everything turns orange
- **Live music** — Stages in every city, DJs on boats
- **Boat parties** — In Amsterdam, the canals are packed with party boats
- **Tompouce** — The traditional orange-topped pastry
- **Royal family** visits a different city each year

**Survival tips for expats:**
1. Wear orange (or risk standing out badly)
2. Bring cash for the vrijmarkt
3. In Amsterdam, arrive early — it gets incredibly crowded
4. Try a tompouce and oranje bitter (orange liqueur)
5. Public transport is limited — walk or cycle
6. Vondelpark area is popular for families with kids' vrijmarkt

### Sinterklaas — November/December

The most beloved Dutch tradition, especially for children.

**Timeline:**
- **Mid-November:** Sinterklaas arrives by steamboat (intocht) — live on TV
- **Nov-Dec 5:** Children put their shoe by the fireplace with a carrot for Sinterklaas' horse, and receive small gifts overnight
- **December 5 (Pakjesavond):** The main event — families exchange gifts with creative poems (gedichten) and surprise packages (surprises)

**What to know:**
- Sinterklaas is more important than Christmas for gift-giving
- Gifts are accompanied by humorous poems about the recipient
- "Surprises" are creative packages disguised as something else
- Traditional treats: pepernoten, speculaas, chocolate letters
- The tradition is evolving — Zwarte Piet has become Roetveegpiet (soot-wiped Pete)

**As an expat:**
- You might be invited to a Sinterklaas party — join in, write a poem!
- Office Sinterklaas celebrations are common (loting/drawing names)
- Buy chocolate letters at any supermarket (get the first letter of your name)

### Carnival — February/March (Southern Netherlands)

A 3-day explosion of costumes, parades, and party culture in the south.

**Where:** Noord-Brabant (Den Bosch, Eindhoven, Breda, Tilburg) and Limburg (Maastricht)
**When:** Sunday to Tuesday before Ash Wednesday (Feb-Mar)

**What to expect:**
- Cities get temporary names (Den Bosch becomes "Oeteldonk")
- Everyone wears costumes
- Parades with elaborate floats
- Non-stop music and dancing in bars and on streets
- Traditional songs everyone knows (you'll learn fast)

**Expat tip:** If you live in the south, Carnival is unmissable. If you live in the north, plan a weekend trip — it's a completely different Netherlands.

### New Year's Eve (Oud en Nieuw)

Dutch New Year's Eve is famously... explosive.

**Traditions:**
- **Oliebollen** — Deep-fried dough balls, the quintessential NYE food
- **Fireworks** — The Netherlands spends more on consumer fireworks per capita than almost any country
- **Champagne toast** at midnight
- **Nieuwjaarsduik** — New Year's Day polar plunge at beaches across the country (Scheveningen is the largest)

**Important:** Professional firework shows are increasingly common, but individual fireworks are still widespread. If you have pets, prepare them for a very loud night.

## Seasonal Traditions

### Spring
- **Tulip season (March-May)** — Visit Keukenhof, the world's largest flower garden
- **Koningsdag** (April 27) — See above
- **Lente in the parks** — First sunny day = everyone outside in Vondelpark

### Summer
- **Terrasje pakken** — The Dutch art of sitting on a restaurant terrace at any hint of sunshine
- **Festival season** — Lowlands, Pinkpop, North Sea Jazz, Mysteryland
- **Vierdaagse** (July) — 4-day walking event in Nijmegen (50,000 walkers)
- **Pride Amsterdam** (August) — Canal parade and week of events

### Autumn
- **Sinterklaas intocht** (mid-November)
- **Sint Maarten** (November 11) — Children go door-to-door with lanterns singing for candy (mainly in the north)
- **Herfstvakantie** — Autumn school holiday (1 week in October)

### Winter
- **Sinterklaas pakjesavond** (December 5)
- **Gourmetten** — Christmas dinner with small grill at the table
- **Kersttoespraak** — King's Christmas speech on TV
- **Oud en Nieuw** — New Year's Eve (see above)
- **Elfstedentocht fever** — Everyone watches the weather hoping for the legendary ice-skating tour in Friesland (hasn't happened since 1997)

## Work and Holidays

### Standard Holiday Allowance
- Dutch employees get minimum 20 vacation days (full-time)
- Most get 25-30 days
- **Vakantiegeld** (holiday allowance): 8% of annual salary, paid in May
- Public holidays are additional (not deducted from vacation days)

### Bridge Days (Brugdagen)
Strategic days off between a public holiday and the weekend. Popular bridge days in 2026:
- May 15 (Friday after Ascension Day)
- Plan these early — everyone books them

### How Many Vacation Days Do You Actually Get?

Dutch employment contracts typically include more leave than most expats expect. Here is what to look for when you read yours:

| Leave Type | Amount | Notes |
|------------|--------|-------|
| Statutory vacation | 20 days (4× weekly hours) | Minimum by law |
| Typical employer offer | 25-30 days | Most office jobs |
| Public holidays | 8-10 days | Not deducted from vacation days |
| Vakantiegeld | 8% of gross salary | Paid in May, not extra days |
| Parental leave | 9 weeks (partially paid) | 70% of salary via UWV |

**The vakantiegeld (holiday allowance)** surprises many expats. In May you receive 8% of your annual salary as a lump sum — separate from your salary. A person earning €50,000/year gets an extra €4,000 in May. It is designed to fund your summer holiday, and most Dutch people treat it that way.

**Bridge days in practice:** Dutch workers are strategic about public holidays. When Ascension Day falls on a Thursday, the Friday becomes the most requested vacation day of the year. Submit your bridge day requests early — some employers operate on a first-come, first-served basis.

**Liberation Day (May 5):** Officially a national holiday every year, but only a *paid* day off once every five years (next: 2030). However, many employers grant it annually as a company benefit. Check your collective labor agreement (CAO) or employment contract.

## School Holidays 2026: What Expat Parents Need to Know

Dutch school holidays follow a regional stagger system — not all schools have holidays at the same time. This was introduced deliberately to spread holiday travel and reduce motorway congestion. The country is divided into three regions: **Noord** (north), **Midden** (middle/central), and **Zuid** (south).

Your school's region determines exactly when holidays fall. Here are the confirmed 2026 dates:

### Meivakantie (May Holiday) 2026

| Region | Dates |
|--------|-------|
| Noord | 27 April – 8 May |
| Midden | 27 April – 8 May |
| Zuid | 4 May – 15 May |

The meivakantie overlaps with King's Day (April 27) and Liberation Day (May 5), making it a popular period for family travel. Book early — accommodation in popular destinations (the coast, Zeeland, Drenthe) fills up months in advance.

### Zomervakantie (Summer Holiday) 2026

The longest holiday — 6 weeks. Staggered by region to avoid everyone moving at once.

| Region | Dates |
|--------|-------|
| Noord | 11 July – 23 August |
| Midden | 18 July – 30 August |
| Zuid | 25 July – 6 September |

**For expat families:** If your children attend an international school, check the school's own calendar — many follow a slightly different timetable not fully aligned with the national regional system.

### Other Holidays 2026

| Holiday | Dates | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|
| Herfstvakantie (autumn) | ~24 Oct – 1 Nov | Regional variation, typically 1 week |
| Kerstvakantie (Christmas) | 26 Dec – 9 Jan 2027 | 2 weeks, all regions similar |
| Krokusvakantie (February) | 21 Feb – 1 Mar | Not all schools; 1 week |
| Voorjaarsvakantie (spring) | ~27 Feb – 7 Mar | Some schools only |

Check your municipality's school calendar at **scholenopdekaart.nl** or your child's school directly — dates can shift by a day or two each year.

### BSO During School Holidays

BSO (after-school care) typically offers full-day care during school holidays (7:30–18:30) at no extra charge if your child has a holiday care contract. Confirm this with your BSO provider — not all contracts automatically include holiday coverage.

## Planning Around Dutch Holidays as an Expat

Dutch public holidays are more disruptive to daily life than in many other countries, partly because they are genuinely observed. Here is what actually closes and what stays open — and how to plan around the biggest days.

### What Closes on Public Holidays

**Everything meaningful closes on King's Day, Easter Monday, Ascension Day, Whit Monday, and Christmas.** I mean supermarkets, most shops, pharmacies (except emergency duty pharmacies), banks, most restaurants (some open in tourist areas), and most services.

**Government offices and municipalities** are closed on all public holidays plus Good Friday in most cities.

**Albert Heijn, Jumbo, Lidl:** Most supermarkets are closed on Christmas Day. Some open on Boxing Day (Tweede Kerstdag). On King's Day, essentially all supermarkets close. Stock up the day before.

### King's Day (April 27): Practical Survival Guide

King's Day deserves special mention because it is genuinely unlike any other day in the Netherlands. My first year, I had nothing in the house and spent the day hungry because I hadn't thought to shop the day before.

**Before Koningsdag:**
- Do your grocery shopping on April 26
- Get cash — most of the vrijmarkt is cash-only
- Plan your transport, because public transport runs to a limited schedule and roads in Amsterdam are closed to cars

**On the day:**
- Wear orange. Anything orange. You will feel deeply conspicuous if you do not
- The vrijmarkt (street market) starts early — by 10:00, the best spots in Amsterdam are already taken
- Amsterdam fills with 800,000+ people for the biggest celebrations; smaller cities like Utrecht, Groningen, or Haarlem have equally fun atmospheres with fewer crowds
- The Royal Family visits one Dutch city each year — check in advance if you want to see them
- Tompouce (the orange-cream pastry) is mandatory. Try oranje bitter (orange liqueur) if you are feeling adventurous

**Leaving the city:** Trains run but are packed. Book tickets in advance. Driving out of Amsterdam on King's Day is virtually impossible for several hours.

### Ascension Day: The Bridge Day Opportunity

Ascension Day (Hemelvaartsdag) is always a Thursday, which means the Friday becomes the most popular bridge day (brugdag) in the Netherlands. Most Dutch workers take it off, creating a 4-day weekend.

**In 2026: Ascension Day is 14 May, bridge day is 15 May.**

Book this long weekend early if you want to travel — trains and holiday parks fill up fast. If you are an employer or employee, note that bridge days are taken from personal vacation days, not public holidays.

### Christmas in the Netherlands: Lower Key than You Expect

Coming from the UK, I found Dutch Christmas surprisingly low-key the first time. Sinterklaas (December 5) is where all the gift-giving energy goes. Christmas Day and Boxing Day (both public holidays) are quieter — focused on family dinners, walks, and the television.

**What changes around Christmas:**
- Most shops close December 25 and often December 26
- Restaurants are open but booked out weeks in advance
- Supermarkets close or run limited hours December 25

**What continues:** The Christmas markets in Maastricht, Amsterdam, Rotterdam, and dozens of smaller cities run from late November through mid-December.
