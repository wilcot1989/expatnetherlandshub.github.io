My first summer in the Netherlands, I made every mistake possible. I wore a heavy jacket to an outdoor festival because "it's the Netherlands, it'll be cold." I planned a beach day two weeks in advance and booked nothing. I had no fan, no blackout curtains, and no idea what bouwvak was when my entire neighbourhood suddenly disappeared for three weeks.

By year three, I had figured it out. By year ten, summer here is genuinely one of my favourite times of year — but only because I stopped expecting it to be something it isn't, and started embracing what it actually is.

Summer in the Netherlands is not a guarantee. It is a reward. When it shows up properly, it is brilliant. When it doesn't, you make the best of it. This guide is everything I wish someone had told me before my first June here.

## What to Expect from Dutch Summer Weather

Let's start with the part that surprises most expats: the Netherlands actually gets proper summer weather.

[The Dutch climate](/guides/daily-life/netherlands-climate-weather-expats-2026/) has a reputation for being grey and wet, which is fair for autumn and winter. But June through August brings genuine warmth, long evenings, and stretches of sunshine that can last for weeks at a time. Average temperatures sit between 20 and 25°C, which is comfortable without being oppressive. The days are long — in June, sunset is after 22:00 — so there is a lot of usable daylight.

The flip side is that the Netherlands has been experiencing more intense heatwaves in recent years. Temperatures above 30°C are no longer unusual, and 35°C+ is possible during peak summer. The 2019 heatwave hit 40.7°C in some areas. These extreme periods typically last three to five days before breaking with heavy rain or thunderstorms.

Rain is still part of summer here. Sudden afternoon showers are common, especially in July. The weather can change quickly, which is why the Dutch obsession with checking Buienradar — the rain radar app — is something you will adopt within your first month. A lightweight waterproof jacket is useful even in July.

What makes Dutch summer special is the light. The evenings are extraordinary. At 9pm in late June, the sky is still this warm amber-gold, everyone is still sitting outside, and the whole country feels relaxed in a way it doesn't at any other time of year.

## The Terras: The Heart of Dutch Summer Life

If you want to understand how the Dutch experience summer, watch what happens the moment the temperature hits 18°C: every terras in every city fills up within the hour.

A terras (plural: terrassen) is simply an outdoor seating area attached to a café, restaurant, or bar. In winter, they are deserted or covered with heat lamps and blanket boxes. The second spring arrives and temperatures rise, the Dutch are outside. Rain? Fine, there are awnings. A bit chilly? That's what the blankets are for.

Sitting on a terras with a biertje or a glass of wine, watching the world go by, is not something the Dutch rush. It is the activity. You are not waiting for something else to happen. This is it.

The best terrassen in the Netherlands are not always the most famous ones. In Amsterdam, the spots on Nieuwmarkt, Spui, and along the Amstel are classic, but they fill up early. My preference is to find a neighbourhood terras in a quieter part of the Jordaan or De Pijp — less tourist foot traffic, better prices, and the people-watching is just as good.

In Rotterdam, the Witte de Withstraat and the area around the Markthal have great outdoor spots. In The Hague, the Plein is central and lively. In Utrecht, the Oudegracht canal terrassen — where you sit at water level — are as good as anything in the country.

One thing to know: on sunny weekends, terrassen in popular spots fill up by noon and you may not get a seat. Go early, or go on a weekday.

## Dealing with No Air Conditioning

This is the one thing most expats are not prepared for: the vast majority of Dutch homes, offices, and shops do not have air conditioning.

It seems strange for a country that regularly hits 30°C in summer, but the Dutch have historically built for insulation and heat retention rather than cooling. Old Dutch houses — particularly the tall, narrow canal houses — can become extremely stuffy in a heatwave. Insulated modern apartments built to the latest energy efficiency standards trap heat effectively.

Here is how to manage it.

**Blackout curtains are your friend.** Close them on the south and west-facing windows before you go to work in the morning. It makes a significant difference. The Dutch call this "de zon buitensluiten" — keeping the sun out — and it is standard practice in most households.

**Cross-ventilation works if you do it correctly.** Keep windows and doors closed during the hottest part of the day (roughly 11:00–18:00). Once the outside temperature drops below the indoor temperature in the evening, open everything up and create a through-draft.

**A portable air conditioner is worth it.** Not cheap — expect to pay €300–600 for a decent portable unit — but if you are in an upper-floor apartment and you are sensitive to heat, it can be the difference between sleeping and not sleeping. You will need to vent the exhaust hose out of a window. Alternatively, a good tower fan with ice packs in front of it is a surprisingly effective poor man's version.

**Cold showers and cold water.** Simple, obvious, effective.

**Sleep with a thin cotton sheet only.** Most Dutch people switch to a lighter dekbed (duvet) for summer — you can buy a summer-weight version or simply fold your duvet in half.

During the record-breaking heatwaves, supermarkets sell out of fans within hours. Buy yours before summer, not during the first heatwave.

## Beaches: The Best Spots for Expats

The Netherlands has about 450km of North Sea coastline, and a significant stretch of it is accessible, clean, and well-organised. Water temperatures in summer reach 17–20°C — not tropical, but perfectly swimmable if you grew up in northern Europe.

### Scheveningen

Scheveningen is the beach district of The Hague, and it is the most developed beach resort in the country. There is a proper boulevard, a long pier, dozens of beach clubs and restaurants, and easy tram access from The Hague city centre. It gets very busy in peak summer, but it handles crowds reasonably well. Good for a full day out, not ideal if you want quiet.

### Zandvoort

Zandvoort is the most popular beach for Amsterdam expats, and with good reason: there is a direct train from Amsterdam Centraal that takes about 30 minutes, and the beach is five minutes' walk from the station. On a hot Saturday, the beach fills up fast, but it is long enough that you can usually find space. The town itself has good food options and a few decent beach clubs.

### Bloemendaal aan Zee

Bloemendaal is just north of Zandvoort — a 10-minute bus ride or a 30-minute walk along the beach. It has a different character: fewer families with small children, more young adults, some of the best beach clubs in the country (Woodstock 69 and Bloomingdale are well-known), and a stretch of beach backed by dunes rather than a boulevard. It is my preference when I want a beach day that doesn't feel like a mass event.

### Texel

Texel is the largest of the Wadden Islands, reachable by ferry from Den Helder (about 20 minutes). It is a different experience entirely — a proper island, with multiple beaches on the North Sea side, cycling paths, nature reserves, and small villages. Worth doing as an overnight or weekend trip rather than a day trip, but entirely doable for expats based in the Randstad. The beaches on the western side (De Koog, De Cocksdorp) can be wild and windy; the eastern side (the Waddenzee shore) is calmer and shallower.

## Lakes and Inland Swimming

For inland water lovers, the Netherlands has excellent options that most new expats don't know about.

### Loosdrechtse Plassen

The Loosdrechtse Plassen are a series of interconnected lakes between Amsterdam and Utrecht, created by peat extraction in past centuries. They are enormously popular for sailing, windsurfing, kayaking, and swimming. The area has several beach areas, boat rentals, and waterside restaurants. Loosdrecht village itself is worth an afternoon. Reachable by car or bus from Amsterdam in about 45 minutes.

### Veluwemeer

The Veluwemeer is a long lake on the western edge of the Veluwe nature area in Gelderland. It is popular for camping, cycling, and water sports. The surrounding Veluwe forest and heath landscapes make it a great combination with outdoor activities on land and water. If you have a bike and want a proper weekend escape from the Randstad, the Veluwe area in general is underrated.

### Local Zwemplassen

Almost every Dutch city and large town has a zwemplas — a designated swimming lake — within easy cycling distance. Amsterdam has the Sloterplas, Kralingse Plas in Rotterdam, the Uitgeestermeer near Haarlem, and many more across the country. These are maintained by municipalities, water quality is monitored, and most have a beach area, toilet facilities, and somewhere to buy an ijsje (ice cream). Check zwemwater.nl for conditions before you go.

## Parks: Urban Green Space in Summer

Dutch cities invest heavily in parks, and in summer they become the living rooms of the city.

### Vondelpark, Amsterdam

Vondelpark is the most visited park in the Netherlands, and on a sunny weekend in July it absolutely earns that title. The park fills up with picnickers, sunbathers, families, students, tourists, and street musicians. There is a free outdoor theatre (Openluchttheater Vondelpark) that runs a summer programme of concerts, performances, and children's shows. It is crowded, yes, but there is an energy to it that is genuinely joyful.

### Kralingse Bos, Rotterdam

Kralingse Bos is my recommendation for Rotterdam expats who want a green escape. The park surrounds the Kralingse Plas (a swimming and sailing lake), has cycling and running paths through forested areas, and is large enough that you can find a quiet corner even on a warm weekend. Far less tourist traffic than Vondelpark and a more local atmosphere.

### Sonsbeek, Arnhem

Sonsbeek in Arnhem is one of the most beautiful parks in the Netherlands and is almost unknown to expats in the west of the country. Hilly, wooded, with a waterfall and a series of ponds — it feels more like an English country park than a Dutch one. Arnhem is worth a summer day trip from Utrecht or Amsterdam.

### Haagse Bos, The Hague

For Hague-based expats, the Haagse Bos is a genuine forest within the city — old beech trees, wide paths, and enough space to escape the city noise completely. Connect it with the Westbroekpark (with its rose garden) for a full afternoon.

## Cycling in Summer

Summer is when cycling in the Netherlands moves from a commuting habit to an actual pleasure. The [cycling infrastructure here](/guides/daily-life/cycling-netherlands-expat-guide-2026/) is the best in the world, and long-distance routes that you wouldn't consider on a grey November day become genuinely appealing in June.

Some routes worth knowing about:

**Amsterdam to Zandvoort via the dunes:** About 35km one way, through the coastal dune nature reserve (Kennemerduinen). You can take your bike on the train back. The dune landscape in summer is stunning.

**The Veluwe:** Hundreds of kilometres of marked cycling routes through heath, pine forest, and open moorland. Rent a bike in Apeldoorn or Arnhem and pick a route based on distance. The LF3 national cycle route passes through the area.

**The Kinderdijk windmill route:** A flat ride through the polder landscape southeast of Rotterdam. Combine it with a visit to the Kinderdijk windmills (a UNESCO site) in the morning before the tour groups arrive.

**Texel by bike:** The island has a dedicated cycling route that circles the entire island — about 55km in total. Rent a bike from one of the many shops near the ferry terminal and take two days to do it properly.

If you have not yet sorted your bike situation, summer is a good prompt. Read the [cycling guide](/guides/daily-life/cycling-netherlands-expat-guide-2026/) for advice on what to buy and where.

## Summer Festivals

The Dutch festival calendar in summer is packed and genuinely world-class. These are the four you should know.

If you want a full breakdown of outdoor activities to plan your summer — from SUP and kayaking to hiking the Veluwe and beach sports on the North Sea coast — the [best outdoor activities in the Netherlands for summer 2026](/guides/daily-life/best-outdoor-activities-netherlands-summer-2026/) covers all the options with practical detail on where to go and how to get started.

### Pinkpop (June, Landgraaf)

Pinkpop is the oldest pop music festival in the world (it first ran in 1970) and takes place in Landgraaf, near Maastricht in the south of the Netherlands. It is a three-day event over the Pentecost (Pinksteren) weekend in early June. The lineup typically mixes big international rock, pop, and electronic acts with Dutch talent. It is not a camping festival — most people stay in Maastricht or surrounding towns and travel in. Tickets sell out fast.

### North Sea Jazz (July, Rotterdam)

North Sea Jazz is in a different category from most festivals. It is held indoors at the Rotterdam Ahoy arena over one weekend in July, and it is one of the most prestigious jazz festivals in the world. The name is slightly misleading — the lineup extends well beyond jazz into soul, R&B, hip-hop, and world music. Artists like Kendrick Lamar, Alicia Keys, and Stevie Wonder have all played here. Tickets are expensive but the production quality is extraordinary. For Rotterdam-based expats, this is an annual highlight.

### Lowlands (August, Biddinghuizen)

Lowlands is the big one for younger expats. It is a three-day camping festival in Biddinghuizen (Flevoland province), held in the third week of August. The site is enormous — multiple stages, art installations, cinema tents, comedy, theatre — and the lineup covers indie, electronic, hip-hop, and pop. It is less about any single headliner and more about the overall experience. The camping atmosphere is excellent and the organisers have a strong reputation for good logistics and safety. Tickets sell out months in advance; keep an eye on the sale date (usually early in the year).

### Mysteryland (August, Haarlemmermeer)

Mysteryland is the world's longest-running electronic music festival, held on the grounds of the Floriade park near Hoofddorp. It is a one-day (or multi-day camping) event in August focused on electronic music — techno, house, drum and bass, trance. The setting is genuinely unusual: the former World Expo grounds, with the festival spread across different "worlds" in the landscape. Well-organised and with a passionate regular crowd.

Beyond these four, the Netherlands has a huge number of smaller summer festivals — food festivals, cultural festivals, neighbourhood parties. Amsterdam's Uitmarkt (end of August) marks the start of the cultural season with free performances across the city. If you want a broader picture of Dutch public celebrations, the [King's Day guide](/guides/daily-life/kings-day-guide-expats-2026/) is also worth reading — Koningsdag in late April is the warm-up act to summer and gives you a good sense of how the Dutch party outdoors. Rotterdam hosts numerous outdoor events throughout summer on its harbour terraces and squares.

## Outdoor Markets in Summer

Dutch outdoor markets are at their best in summer, when good weather turns them into proper social events rather than just shopping errands.

**Albert Cuyp Markt, Amsterdam (De Pijp):** The largest street market in the Netherlands, running Monday to Saturday. In summer, the street fills with stalls selling stroopwafels, fresh herring, cheese, clothing, and flowers, and the surrounding cafés spill out onto the pavement. Go on a weekday morning for the most local experience.

**Dappermarkt, Amsterdam:** A more neighbourhood-oriented market in Oost, less tourist-focused than the Albert Cuyp. Good for cheap fresh produce and a very genuine Amsterdam atmosphere.

**Markt, Rotterdam (various):** Rotterdam has a good network of weekly neighbourhood markets. The Afrikaandermarkt in Feijenoord is one of the most diverse and interesting in the country.

**Boerenmarkt, Amsterdam (Nieuwmarkt, Saturday):** A farmers' market focused on organic produce, local cheese, bread, and specialty food. Smaller and more focused than the street markets.

**Antiekmarkt de Looier, Amsterdam (indoor but connects to outdoor stalls in summer):** Antiques and vintage items, good for Saturday browsing.

## Rooftop Bars

The rooftop bar scene in the Netherlands is relatively young but growing fast, particularly in Amsterdam and Rotterdam.

**Sir Adam Hotel rooftop (A'DAM Lookout area), Amsterdam:** The views across the IJ waterway and city skyline are excellent. Go for the sunset.

**OT301 summer terras, Amsterdam:** Not a rooftop, but a good outdoor summer bar in the Oud-West neighbourhood with a creative, local crowd.

**Rooftop at Rotterdam Marriott or nhow Rotterdam:** Rotterdam's skyline actually rewards rooftop drinking in a way Amsterdam's flat cityscape doesn't always. The city's architecture is dramatic from above.

**The Rotterdam (by Rem Koolhaas) terras:** The waterfront terrace attached to this iconic building has become a summer destination in its own right.

Check for pop-up rooftop bars — these appear and disappear from season to season in both cities, and the best ones are often announced via Instagram or local event listings like iamsterdam.com or rotterdammakeithappen.nl.

## Dutch Vacation Culture and Bouwvak

Understanding bouwvak — the Dutch summer holiday window — will prevent a lot of confusion and logistical headaches.

Bouwvak (literally "construction trade holiday") originated as the mandated holiday period for the construction industry, but it has evolved into the de facto Dutch family summer holiday window. The country is divided into three regions for school holiday scheduling:

- **Noord (North):** roughly the last three weeks of July
- **Midden (Middle):** roughly the last two weeks of July and first week of August
- **Zuid (South):** roughly the last three weeks of July (overlapping with Noord in some years)

During bouwvak, several things happen simultaneously: major roads and motorways get significantly busier (especially on the first and last Saturdays), camping sites across Europe fill up with Dutch caravans, cities like Amsterdam and Rotterdam lose a noticeable portion of their local population, and many small businesses (particularly tradespeople, independent restaurants, and local shops) close for two to four weeks.

As an expat, bouwvak affects you in a few ways:

If you are trying to get work done on your home in August — plumber, electrician, carpenter — expect delays. Many tradespeople are away.

If you are planning a road trip or driving to Germany, France, or Belgium in late July, leave on a Thursday or Sunday rather than a Saturday.

If you need to get anything administrative done involving a Dutch neighbour, colleague, or service provider, assume they may be unreachable for two to four weeks in August.

On the positive side: if you stay in the city during bouwvak, it can be wonderfully quiet. Amsterdam in the first two weeks of August, with the tourists present but many locals away, has a slower, more relaxed rhythm that I actually enjoy.

For a complete picture of Dutch public holidays and cultural events that affect daily life, the [Dutch holidays and traditions guide](/guides/daily-life/dutch-holidays-traditions-expat-guide-2026/) is worth reading.

## Best Cities for Summer Life

Not all Dutch cities experience summer the same way. Here is my assessment for expats.

**Amsterdam** is the obvious answer and the most complete summer city — festivals, markets, parks, canals, rooftop bars, and beaches a short train ride away. The downside is tourist saturation in peak summer, particularly July and August. If you live here, you learn to avoid the centre on sunny weekends.

**Rotterdam** is my personal pick for summer in the Netherlands. The city is built for outdoor life — wide riverfront promenades, excellent architecture, North Sea Jazz, great terrassen culture, and significantly fewer tourists than Amsterdam. The waterfront area around Katendrecht and the Wilhelminapier is genuinely world-class in good weather.

**The Hague** has Scheveningen on its doorstep, the Haagse Bos for green space, a good restaurant scene, and a more relaxed pace than Amsterdam. For expats who work in the diplomatic or international legal community here, the social calendar in summer is particularly active.

**Utrecht** punches above its weight for summer life. The canal terrassen (sitting at water level below street level) are unique in the country, the [city has a strong social scene](/guides/integration/making-friends-netherlands-expats-2026/) for young expats, and it is centrally located for day trips to the Loosdrechtse Plassen, the Veluwe, and the coast.

**Maastricht** is the most southern city and genuinely warmer than the rest of the Netherlands — a few degrees can make a real difference. The old town, the Vrijthof square, and the café culture are all at their best in summer.

For a detailed comparison of where to live, the [best cities for expats guide](/guides/housing/best-cities-netherlands-expats-2026/) covers location, housing costs, and lifestyle in detail.

## Summer Fitness and Staying Active

The long Dutch summer evenings make it easy to build outdoor activity into daily life. Running clubs, outdoor bootcamps, and open-water swimming groups all become more active in summer.

If you use a gym regularly, be aware that some smaller gyms reduce their hours in August or close for a week during the bouwvak period. It is worth checking with yours in advance. The [best gyms and fitness guide for the Netherlands](/guides/daily-life/best-gyms-fitness-netherlands-2026/) has advice on chains that are reliably open year-round.

Outdoor yoga and fitness sessions pop up in Vondelpark, Kralingse Bos, and other large parks throughout summer — often free or cheap, run by local instructors. Check local Facebook groups or neighbourhood apps like Nextdoor for listings.

Open-water swimming is popular at the zwemplassen. Many towns also have outdoor lidos (openluchtzwembaden) that open from May to September. These are typically excellent — well-maintained, family-friendly, and much cheaper than indoor pools.

## Practical Tips for Your First Dutch Summer

A few things I learned the hard way, offered without the lecture:

**Book beach days flexibly.** The weather forecast is unreliable more than three days out. The Dutch check Buienradar obsessively for a reason. Plan loosely and confirm the day before.

**Sunscreen is not optional.** The Dutch sun in June and July is stronger than it looks, particularly near the coast where UV is reflected off sand and water. SPF 30 minimum, reapplied after swimming.

**Buy a fan before June.** See the AC section above. In a hot spell, every fan in every shop sells out within a day or two.

**Festival tickets: buy early.** Lowlands, Pinkpop, and North Sea Jazz all sell out, sometimes months in advance. If there is a festival you want to attend, buy when tickets go on sale rather than waiting to see if the weather looks good.

**Bike lights are still required in summer.** It is still light at 10pm in June, but you will need lights for any rides after 22:00 as darkness does eventually arrive. More importantly, police do issue fines for riding without lights even in summer.

**Outdoor dining reservations:** Popular terrassen and restaurants with outdoor seating book up on warm sunny evenings. If you want to eat at a specific place, call ahead on the day.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How hot does it get in the Netherlands in summer?**

Average daytime temperatures in June, July, and August sit between 20 and 25°C. Heatwaves are becoming more frequent — it is not unusual to see several days above 30°C, and occasionally even 35°C or higher. The heat usually breaks after a few days with a thunderstorm.

**Do homes in the Netherlands have air conditioning?**

The vast majority of Dutch homes do not have air conditioning. This is changing slowly, but it remains unusual. Most people rely on blackout curtains, fans, and cross-ventilation. If you are renting, a portable air conditioning unit is worth considering before the first heatwave hits.

**What is bouwvak and how does it affect expats?**

Bouwvak is the Dutch construction industry holiday, which falls in the last three weeks of July for the northern provinces and August for the rest of the country. Many Dutch families take their annual summer holiday during bouwvak, which means roads are busy, camping sites are full, and cities can feel quieter — or more tourist-heavy, depending on where you are.

**Which is the best beach near Amsterdam for a day trip?**

Zandvoort is the closest — about 30 minutes by direct train from Amsterdam Centraal. It can get very busy on hot weekends. For a quieter, more upmarket atmosphere, Bloemendaal aan Zee is a 10-minute bus ride from Zandvoort station.

**What are the biggest summer music festivals in the Netherlands?**

The four major ones are Pinkpop (June, Landgraaf), North Sea Jazz (July, Rotterdam), Lowlands (August, Biddinghuizen), and Mysteryland (August, Haarlemmermeer). Lowlands and Mysteryland are multi-day camping festivals. North Sea Jazz is an indoor arena event with a legendary lineup tradition.

**Is it possible to swim in lakes in the Netherlands?**

Yes, and it is very popular. The Loosdrechtse Plassen, Veluwemeer, and many smaller lakes and plas areas are designated swimming spots. Water quality is generally good and monitored by local authorities. Check the zwemwater.nl website for up-to-date water quality reports before you go.

## The Dutch Summer Is Worth It

The Netherlands will never be the Mediterranean. The weather is not guaranteed, the sea is not warm, and on a grey August Tuesday when you are sitting in an apartment without air conditioning listening to rain on the window, you might wonder what you are doing here.

But the Dutch summer, when it shows up, is one of the best things about living here. The evenings alone — those long, golden evenings where it stays light until 22:00 and every park and terras is full of people simply enjoying being outside — are worth a lot. The festival culture is excellent. The cycling is genuinely joyful in the sun. The beaches are accessible and clean. And there is something about the way the Dutch collectively exhale in summer, throwing open their terrassen and pulling out their bikes and filling the parks, that makes the grey winters worth pushing through.

Give Dutch summer a real chance. Buy the fan before June, learn the bouwvak dates, get on a bike, and let the country show you what it can do when the sun cooperates.

And check Buienradar before you leave the house.
