My first Dutch winter nearly broke me. I moved from Cape Town to Amsterdam in October, and by mid-November I was sitting in my apartment at 4:15pm wondering why it was already dark, why my legs were wet from cycling in the rain, and whether this had been a mistake. It was not a mistake. But nobody had told me what to expect.

Ten years later, I actually like Dutch winter. Not all of it — the grey weeks of January are hard regardless — but the cosy evenings, the oliebollen, the occasional frozen canal, and the strange togetherness that dark weather creates. Here is everything I wish someone had told me before that first November.

---

## Month-by-Month: What to Actually Expect

### October: The Warning Signs

October is the transition month. The days shorten fast — by the end of October, sunset is already around 6pm. Temperatures drop from mild (15°C) to cold (7-10°C). Rain becomes more frequent.

October is when you should be preparing for winter, not being surprised by it. This means:
- Buying waterproof cycling gear
- Getting your bike lights checked (mandatory once it gets dark early)
- Stocking up on vitamin D supplements
- Getting your boiler/heating serviced before the cold hits
- Checking your energy contract

The Dutch celebrate Sinterklaas season starting on 5 November (arrival of Sinterklaas by boat), which brings a festive atmosphere to shops and schools. It is distinctly Dutch and distinctly not Christmas, and understanding it helps you feel connected to the cultural calendar rather than confused by it.

### November: The First Shock

November is when many expats first realise they may have underestimated Dutch winter. The clocks go back in late October, and by early November it is dark by 5pm and the sun is already low even at midday.

Temperatures hover between 3°C and 10°C. Rain and wind are frequent. The Netherlands is flat, which means wind from the North Sea hits without obstruction. A 10°C day with strong wind feels significantly colder than 10°C suggests.

November is also when the Dutch start lighting candles, pulling out blankets, and making their homes gezellig. If you have not yet understood gezelligheid (see below), November is when it becomes relevant.

**What helps in November:**
- Start using a daylight lamp in the morning
- Establish a morning routine before it gets dark to preserve your sense of normal daytime
- Find one or two indoor social activities (a weekly dinner, a sports club, a language class)
- Accept the rain rather than fighting it

### December: Short Days, Festive Light

December is the darkest month. In Amsterdam, you get approximately 7.5 hours of potential daylight, and much of it is grey. Sunrise is around 8:45am and sunset around 4:20pm.

On the other side: December is also the most socially rich month. Sinterklaas celebrations happen in early December (5 December is Pakjesavond — gift evening). Christmas is celebrated on 25-26 December (both days are public holidays in the Netherlands). New Year's Eve (Oud en Nieuw) is celebrated with fireworks that are famously excessive — the Netherlands spends enormous amounts on private fireworks, and the night is loud and spectacular.

December evenings are genuinely beautiful in Dutch cities: canal reflections, market lights, and windows lit from inside. Learning to see winter aesthetics, rather than just experiencing cold, is one of the mindset shifts that makes a difference.

**Practical December note:** Dutch heating bills rise sharply. Keep an eye on your gas meter reading and compare to your contract estimates. See our [section on heating costs](#heating-costs) below.

### January: The Hard Month

January is the month most expats find genuinely difficult. The festivities are over, the weather is unchanged or worse, and there is nothing on the cultural calendar to compensate. Temperatures drop (0-5°C is typical), frost is possible, and occasional snow is likely — though heavy snowfall is not common in most of the Netherlands.

January is when SAD (Seasonal Affective Disorder) tends to peak in people who are susceptible. It is also when the Dutch cosy culture is most valuable: the contrast between the grey outside and the warm, lit inside becomes the entire point.

**What helps in January:**
- Do not cancel social plans when you feel like staying home — this is when connection matters most
- Keep exercising (even cold cycling keeps energy levels up)
- Plan something to look forward to: a trip, a weekend activity, a dinner party
- Take your vitamin D consistently

### February: The Light Returns

February is psychologically lighter than January even before the temperature changes. The days are noticeably longer by mid-February — sunset creeps back past 6pm, and the quality of light improves. Some years bring early spring signs by the end of the month: snowdrops, the first days above 10°C, cyclists wearing slightly fewer layers.

Winter is not over in February, but the sense that it is ending begins to arrive. This is meaningful if you have found the preceding months hard.

Carnival (Carnaval) is celebrated in February in the southern Netherlands — Brabant, Limburg, and Zealand have a genuine carnival culture with costumes, parades, and street parties. If you are in or near these regions, it is worth experiencing.

## The Darkness: Understanding the Daylight Impact

| Month | Sunrise (Amsterdam) | Sunset (Amsterdam) | Daylight Hours |
|-------|--------------------|--------------------|----------------|
| October | ~7:30am | ~6:30pm | ~11 hours |
| November | ~7:45am | ~4:45pm | ~9 hours |
| December | ~8:45am | ~4:20pm | ~7.5 hours |
| January | ~8:45am | ~5:00pm | ~8.25 hours |
| February | ~8:00am | ~6:00pm | ~10 hours |

For reference: Cape Town in December has over 14 hours of daylight. Mexico City has around 11 hours. Even London, just west across the North Sea, gets similar hours but with somewhat more sunshine on average.

The issue is not just the hours but the quality. Dutch winter days are frequently overcast from morning to sunset. On a grey November day, you might have 9 hours of pale grey light and feel like you never really saw the sun at all. This is not a metaphor — on many winter days, you genuinely will not see direct sunlight.

## SAD and Vitamin D: Taking It Seriously

Seasonal Affective Disorder is not just "feeling a bit down in winter." It is a recognised form of depression linked to light reduction, and it is significantly more prevalent in the Netherlands than in most expats' home countries.

**Symptoms to recognise:**
- Low energy and increased fatigue
- Sleeping more than usual but still feeling tired
- Low mood, irritability, or emotional flatness
- Increased appetite, especially for carbohydrates
- Withdrawal from social activities
- Difficulty concentrating

If you experience several of these symptoms consistently from October to March and they improve in spring, SAD is a reasonable explanation. Talk to your huisarts (GP). There are effective treatments including light therapy, talking therapy, and in some cases medication.

**Vitamin D:** The Netherlands gets insufficient sunlight for your skin to produce adequate vitamin D from October to April. The Dutch health authority (RIVM) recommends vitamin D supplementation for most people during these months. For expats from sunny climates, deficiency can happen faster than you expect. A daily supplement of 10-25 micrograms (400-1000 IU) is the standard recommendation — your huisarts can test your levels if you want to know your baseline.

**Daylight lamps:** A 10,000 lux daylight lamp used for 20-30 minutes within an hour of waking up is the most evidence-backed non-medical intervention for winter mood. You sit near it (not staring directly at it) while eating breakfast or reading. They cost EUR 50-150 from electronics stores. Many expats who use them report a meaningful improvement in energy and mood within a week or two.

## Cycling in Winter: Yes, the Dutch Really Do It

If you have not yet started cycling in the Netherlands, winter is when you discover whether you are committed or not. The Dutch cycle year-round — in rain, in cold, in occasional snow. You will see people on bikes with umbrellas, in full suits, on their phone (not recommended), and with children in cargo bikes, regardless of the weather.

**Why it works:**
The Dutch have cycling infrastructure so good that cycling remains faster and more practical than driving or public transport for most urban journeys. The cycling lanes are protected, the routes are direct, and the distances are short. You do not choose to cycle in winter because it is pleasant — you choose it because it is still the most practical option.

**What you need:**
- A waterproof jacket that is actually waterproof (not just water-resistant — there is a difference)
- Waterproof trousers or overpants
- Waterproof gloves
- A good front and rear light (legally required after dark, and it gets dark early)
- Mudguards on your bike — front and rear
- A pannier bag or backpack cover

You will arrive at your destination somewhat damp on rainy days. This is accepted. Dutch offices and workplaces understand that people arrive by bike and may need a moment to dry off. The unspoken rule is: you do not complain about the weather, and you do not use it as a reason not to cycle.

For a full guide to cycling in the Netherlands, including buying a bike, routes, and rules, see our [cycling guide for expats](/guides/daily-life/cycling-netherlands-expat-guide-2026/).

**One winter cycling tip that took me three years to learn:** get a good pair of waterproof gloves rather than normal gloves. Cold hands while cycling are far more miserable than cold feet or a wet jacket. Waterproof, windproof gloves with some thermal lining make a 10-minute cycle in November completely manageable.

## Heating Costs in Winter

Dutch homes are heated primarily by natural gas (aardgas), though district heating (stadsverwarming) is increasingly common in newer buildings. Gas prices in the Netherlands are variable and have been volatile since 2022.

**Typical additional winter heating costs:**
- Small apartment: EUR 50-80 extra per month
- Standard apartment (80-100m²): EUR 80-130 extra per month
- House: EUR 120-200+ extra per month

These are averages and depend heavily on insulation quality. Older Dutch buildings, particularly pre-1980 terraced houses, can be poorly insulated. If you are renting, ask about the energy label (energielabel) — an A or B label means decent insulation; a D or E label means expect higher bills.

**Practical tips:**
- Check that all rooms have working radiators before the cold arrives
- Draught-excluding tape for old window frames costs EUR 5 and reduces heat loss noticeably
- In a rental, report heating problems to your landlord early — you have a legal right to a properly functioning heating system
- Consider a fixed-price energy contract if you are on a variable tariff — it removes the risk of price spikes in cold months

## Winter Clothing: What You Actually Need

The Netherlands is not Arctic cold — temperatures rarely drop below -5°C in most years — but the combination of wind, rain, and dampness makes the cold feel more penetrating than a dry -10°C somewhere else.

**What you need:**

- **Waterproof outer layer:** Not a wool coat, not a down jacket without a shell — a proper waterproof jacket. This is the single most important item.
- **Thermal base layer:** For days below 5°C, a thermal layer under your normal clothes makes a significant difference.
- **Warm socks:** Wool socks in your regular shoes are warmer than thin socks in expensive boots.
- **Hat and gloves:** Cheap and genuinely effective. Many expats skip these and are cold. Do not skip them.
- **Waterproof shoes or boots:** Your feet will get wet if your shoes are not waterproof. Accept this and buy accordingly.

What you probably do not need: the heaviest arctic-weight coat. Dutch winter is cold and wet, not extreme. A good waterproof jacket with a warm mid-layer covers 90% of days.

## Gezelligheid: The Mindset That Makes Winter Bearable

Gezelligheid (pronounced approximately: heh-SELL-ikh-hite) is one of those Dutch words that does not translate cleanly. The closest English approximation is "cosiness," but that misses the social dimension. Gezelligheid is the warm, convivial atmosphere of being together indoors — candles lit, drinks in hand, no rush to leave, conversations that go in no particular direction.

Dutch winter culture is built around creating gezelligheid to counterbalance the grey. You see it in the way Dutch homes are decorated in winter — more candles than you would expect, blankets on sofas, soft lighting. You see it in the cafe culture: Dutch cafes (often called brown cafes, bruine kroegen) are deliberately dark and intimate, designed for spending two hours over one drink in good company rather than moving quickly.

For expats, understanding gezelligheid as a practice rather than just a word changes how you experience winter. Instead of tolerating the dark months, you participate in the culture built around them:
- Host a dinner that goes on for three hours longer than planned
- Go to a bruine kroeg with a friend on a rainy Tuesday evening
- Light candles on weekday evenings (the Dutch do this regularly, not just for occasions)
- Invest in soft, warm lighting for your home — overhead fluorescent lights feel wrong in Dutch winter

This is not just cultural tourism. It is a genuine coping strategy. Research on winter mood consistently shows that social connection and warm physical environments reduce the psychological impact of low light and cold. The Dutch discovered this empirically over centuries of bad winters. You can borrow their solution.

## Oliebollen: The Food of Dutch Winter

No guide to Dutch winter is complete without acknowledging oliebollen — deep-fried dough balls dusted with powdered sugar, sold from mobile stalls (oliebollenkraam) that appear across the Netherlands from late November through New Year.

They are not healthy. They are delicious. Eating one on a cold December evening while cycling home is a genuinely good experience, and the powdered sugar on your dark jacket is a badge of participation.

Other winter food traditions worth trying:
- **Erwtensoep (snert):** Thick Dutch pea soup with smoked sausage. Eaten on cold days and at outdoor skating events. If you see a stall selling erwtensoep at an ice-skating event, stop.
- **Stamppot:** Mashed potatoes combined with vegetables (kale, sauerkraut, endive) and sausage. The definitive Dutch winter comfort food.
- **Appelbeignets:** Apple fritters, also sold at oliebollen stalls.
- **Glühwein / mulled wine:** At Christmas markets and outdoor events throughout December.

## Ice Skating (Schaatsen): When It Happens

Ice skating on natural ice is one of the iconic Dutch winter experiences. When temperatures drop to -5°C or below and stay there for a week or more, canals and lakes freeze and the Netherlands transforms.

This does not happen every year. Some winters pass without sufficient frost. But when it does happen:
- Everyone goes skating — children leave school early, offices thin out in the afternoon
- Makeshift stalls appear at the edges of frozen canals selling erwtensoep and hot drinks
- The atmosphere is communal and joyful in a way that is difficult to describe and worth experiencing

If ice appears, find a pair of skates (many sports shops rent them, and the streets fill with people selling second-hand pairs), get to a frozen body of water, and participate. This is one of the experiences that makes Dutch winter worthwhile.

Safety note: always check that ice is at least 10-15cm thick before skating on it. In the Netherlands, local authorities post ice thickness reports and the media covers it extensively. Never skate on canals in cities without checking thickness — bridges, boat traffic, and warmer water around infrastructure can create weak spots. When in doubt, stick to officially approved skating areas.

The Elfstedentocht — a legendary 200km race across eleven Frisian cities on natural ice — has not been held since 1997, as winters have been too mild. If conditions ever allow it again, it will be an event of national significance.

## Long Winter Evenings: Making Them Work for You

With evenings starting at 4:30pm in December, you have a lot of indoor hours to fill. Some expats find this oppressive. Others treat it as an unexpected gift of time.

Productive uses of long Dutch winter evenings:
- Learning Dutch (Duolingo, classes, language exchange)
- Reading more than you have in years
- Cooking properly instead of grabbing food on the go
- Building a side project

## Tips by Nationality

Different nationalities find different aspects of Dutch winter most challenging. Here are the specific adjustments I have seen work best:

**From the UK or Ireland:** You are already familiar with grey and rain, which helps. The darkness may still be more extreme than you expect — even compared to Scotland, the Netherlands feels darker in December. The cycling culture is the main practical adjustment.

**From Southern Europe (Spain, Italy, Greece, Portugal):** The darkness and cold hit harder. Vitamin D supplements are genuinely important for people from sun-heavy climates. Gezelligheid translates well to Mediterranean indoor social culture — evening dinners and gatherings are something you will recognise. The cycling in rain may take longer to accept.

**From North America:** The winter is colder than much of the US South or West Coast, but less extreme than Canada or the northern US. The lack of a car and the compactness of Dutch cities means you interact with the weather more directly than you might be used to. The directness of Dutch social interactions helps with finding community quickly.

**From Asia (India, Southeast Asia, East Asia):** The cold and darkness are a significant shift. Layering properly takes adjustment — many people from warmer climates underestimate how many layers they need. The cultural isolation of not speaking Dutch can feel more acute in winter when outdoor activity decreases. Finding a community (expat groups, cultural organisations, Dutch language classes) makes a significant difference.

**From South America or Africa:** Darkness at 4:30pm is genuinely shocking if you have not experienced it. The daylight lamp investment is most valuable for people from equatorial or southern hemisphere origins. The cosy Dutch interior culture may feel unfamiliar but is worth engaging with.

## After Your First Winter: What Changes

Most expats who get through their first Dutch winter say the same thing: the second winter is much easier. Not because the weather is better, but because you know what to expect. You have the gear. You have the routine. You have the gezelligheid strategy.

The Netherlands in winter has real pleasures that take a full cycle to discover: the bruine kroeg on a Tuesday, the first oliebollen of the season, the morning your canal freezes, the moment in late February when you realise sunset is past 6pm again and something lifts. These things only happen once you have survived the difficult parts.
