A colleague of mine moved here from Mumbai with fifteen years of driving experience. She could handle a car in Indian city traffic — which, trust me, is an entirely different sport compared to Dutch motorways — and assumed that getting a Dutch licence would be a formality. A quick test, a stamp, done.

Six months and roughly €2,600 later, she had her rijbewijs. It took far longer and cost far more than she had expected. Not because she was a bad driver. She was brilliant. But because the Netherlands has a specific, structured process for granting driving licences, and no amount of prior driving experience skips you past it if your country is not on the exchange list.

This guide is for everyone in that situation. If you come from India, China, Brazil, Indonesia, Thailand, most of Africa, or any of the many countries that do not have a driving licence exchange agreement with the Netherlands — this is your roadmap. I will walk you through every step of the CBR process, what it costs, how long it takes, and how to avoid the mistakes that trip people up.

If you are not sure whether your country has an exchange agreement, read my [exchange driving licence guide](/guides/daily-life/exchange-driving-license-netherlands-2026/) first. That guide covers which countries can skip the tests entirely. If your country is not on that list, come back here.

## Who Must Get a Dutch Licence from Scratch?

Let me be clear about who this article is for, because it is a question I get wrong answers to constantly — including on expat forums.

The Netherlands has exchange agreements with over 30 non-EU countries. If your country is on that list (the UK, USA for most states, Canada, Japan, South Korea, Australia, and others), you can exchange your foreign licence without sitting any tests. The process takes a few weeks and costs around €80–€100.

If your country is **not** on the list, you must complete the full CBR process: a health declaration, a theory exam, driving lessons, and a practical test. There is no partial credit for years of driving experience. There is no simplified version for people who already drive. You do the whole thing.

**Countries commonly not on the exchange list include:**

- India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka
- China (mainland), Indonesia, Thailand, Philippines, Vietnam
- Brazil, Mexico, Colombia, Argentina, Peru
- Turkey, Egypt, Morocco (check — Morocco has a partial agreement), Nigeria, Kenya, Ghana
- Russia, Ukraine

This list is not exhaustive, and the RDW does update agreements. Always check the official [RDW website](https://www.rdw.nl) for the current list before assuming your position.

### The 185-day rule

Once you register at a Dutch municipality and get your BSN number, a 185-day window opens during which you may drive on your foreign licence. After day 185, you need a valid Dutch licence to drive legally.

185 days sounds like plenty of time. It is not. The CBR process realistically takes 3–6 months from start to finish, and that assumes you start immediately. If you wait a few weeks before getting going, you may find yourself in a situation where your 185 days expire before you have your Dutch licence in hand.

My advice: start the process on the day you register, or as close to it as possible. For a fuller picture of how driving works here once you have your licence, have a look at my [driving in the Netherlands guide for expats](/guides/daily-life/driving-in-netherlands-guide-2026/).

## Overview: The CBR Process

The Dutch driving licence process, administered by the CBR (Centraal Bureau Rijvaardigheidsbewijzen), has four stages:

1. **Health declaration (gezondheidsverklaring)** — €41.50
2. **Theory exam (theorie-examen)** — €39.50
3. **Driving lessons** — variable, typically €1,500–€2,800
4. **Practical exam (rijexamen)** — €130.50

**Estimated total: €2,000–€3,500**

You do not need to complete these in strict sequential order in all cases, but the health declaration must be submitted before CBR will let you book any exam. The theory exam must be passed before you can take the practical exam. Driving lessons can happen at any stage, though most people begin them while preparing for or shortly after passing the theory exam.

Let me go through each stage in detail.

---

## Step 1: Health Declaration (Gezondheidsverklaring)

Before you can register for any CBR exam, you must submit a health declaration. This is a self-assessment form that asks about medical conditions that could affect your ability to drive safely — things like epilepsy, heart conditions, serious vision problems, and certain mental health diagnoses.

**Cost:** €41.50 (paid directly to the CBR)

**How to submit it:**

1. Go to cbr.nl
2. Create a MijnCBR account (you will need a DigiD for this — if you do not have one yet, read my [BSN registration guide](/guides/legal/bsn-registration-guide-2026/) which explains how to get both)
3. Complete the gezondheidsverklaring online
4. Pay the fee

For most healthy adults, the declaration is approved quickly — often within a few days. If you disclose a medical condition, the CBR may request additional information from your GP or a specialist, which can add several weeks to the timeline. Be honest on the form. If a medical issue surfaces during your driving test that was not disclosed, your exam result can be invalidated.

Once your health declaration is approved, you receive a registration confirmation and can book your theory exam.

## Step 2: Theory Exam (Theorie-Examen)

The theory exam is the first formal hurdle, and it is where many expats come unstuck. The pass rate in the Netherlands sits at around 50% — which means roughly half the people sitting it fail on their first attempt. That is not a figure to scare you; it is a figure to tell you that the exam deserves serious preparation.

### What the exam covers

The theory exam consists of 65 questions in three sections:

- **Traffic knowledge (verkeerskennis):** Road signs, right-of-way rules, speed limits, legal obligations. 25 questions. You need at least 23 correct.
- **Hazard recognition (gevaarherkenning):** Video clips showing real driving situations. You must identify developing hazards as early as possible. 25 questions. You need at least 13 correct.
- **Insight into traffic situations (inzicht):** More complex scenarios testing your judgement about what to do in various traffic situations. 15 questions. You need at least 11 correct.

Overall, you need 54 out of 65 correct to pass.

### The English option

The exam is available in English. When booking at cbr.nl, you select your preferred language. This is a real help — Dutch road sign terminology in a second language is tricky, and sitting the exam in your strongest language removes one variable. The content is identical regardless of which language you choose.

### Booking the exam

1. Log into your MijnCBR account
2. Select "Theorie-examen Rijbewijs B" (B is the category for standard passenger cars)
3. Choose your preferred location and date
4. Select English as your exam language
5. Pay €39.50

Exam locations are spread across the country — major cities like Amsterdam, Rotterdam, Utrecht, Eindhoven, and The Hague all have CBR exam centres. You can usually get an appointment within 2–4 weeks of booking.

### How to prepare

This is where most people underestimate the work involved. Even if you have been driving for twenty years, Dutch traffic rules have specific quirks that will trip you up if you have not studied them.

**Things that surprise experienced drivers:**

- **Right has priority (rechts heeft voorrang):** At most unmarked intersections in the Netherlands, the vehicle coming from the right has absolute right of way. This is enforced rigorously on the exam. Many expats from countries with yield-to-main-road conventions find this counterintuitive.
- **Cyclist priority:** Cyclists have priority in many situations where cars do not in other countries. The exam tests this in multiple scenarios.
- **Tram priority:** Trams almost always have right of way. This matters more in Dutch cities than in most countries.
- **Hazard recognition timing:** The gevaarherkenning section requires you to click the moment you see a hazard developing — not when it is fully apparent. Many people click too late.

**Recommended study resources:**

- **CBR official study materials:** Available at cbr.nl and in bookshops. Slightly dry but accurate.
- **iTheorie app:** The most popular Dutch theory exam app. Has both Dutch and English modes. Highly recommended — the question bank closely mirrors actual exam questions.
- **Rijbewijs theorie practice websites:** Sites like Theorie Oefenen and Verkeerschool.nl offer free practice tests. Run through as many as you can until you are consistently scoring 60/65 or above.
- **YouTube:** Search "Dutch theory exam English" for walkthroughs of the hazard recognition format.

**Realistic study time:** 3–4 weeks of daily practice (30–60 minutes per day) is typical for someone approaching the exam seriously. If you are working full-time and studying in the evenings, allow 4–6 weeks.

### If you fail

Failing the first time is genuinely common. Do not take it as a sign you will never pass — take it as information about which sections need more work. The CBR provides a score breakdown after the exam, so you can see exactly where you lost marks. Book your resit, target the weak areas, and keep practising.

## Step 3: Driving Lessons

Dutch driving lessons are taken with a CBR-registered rijschool (driving school). You are not legally required to take lessons — you could theoretically prepare yourself and book the practical exam directly — but in practice, virtually everyone takes lessons, and for good reason.

Dutch examiners assess candidates against a specific set of criteria that reflects Dutch road conventions. Even if you have driven safely for decades, there are Dutch-specific habits that examiners look for: the way you check mirrors before every manoeuvre, the specific order of the IPSGA (Information-Position-Speed-Gear-Acceleration) routine, the way you handle cyclists at junctions. A good instructor teaches you exactly what the examiner wants to see.

### How many lessons do you need?

This is the question everyone asks, and the honest answer is: it varies enormously.

The Netherlands average for a first-time driver is around 40–45 lessons. For expats with significant driving experience who are mainly adapting to Dutch conventions, 20–30 lessons is more typical. I have seen experienced drivers pass with 15 lessons. I have also seen people need 50+.

A rough guide:

| Prior experience | Estimated lessons needed |
|-----------------|------------------------|
| Never driven before | 40–55 lessons |
| Driven but few years, different traffic system | 30–40 lessons |
| 5+ years driving in similar system | 20–30 lessons |
| 10+ years, confident driver, minor adaptation needed | 15–25 lessons |

These are estimates, not guarantees. Your driving school instructor will assess you in the first lesson and give you a realistic target.

**Cost per lesson:** €50–€70 in most cities. Amsterdam and The Hague tend to be at the higher end. Smaller cities and towns are often cheaper.

At 30 lessons at €60 per lesson, that is €1,800 in lessons alone. At 40 lessons, it is €2,400. This is the biggest variable cost in the whole process.

### How to choose a driving school

Not all rijscholen are equal, and for expats, English-speaking instruction is a practical requirement rather than a preference. Here is what to look for:

**Check for CBR accreditation.** All legitimate driving schools in the Netherlands are registered with the CBR. This is a baseline requirement, not a distinguishing feature — but verify it anyway.

**Find English-speaking instructors.** Search for "rijschool English [city name]" or ask expat community groups on Facebook, Reddit (r/Netherlands, r/Amsterdam, etc.), or Internations. Word of mouth is the most reliable guide. A school that has worked with many expats will understand the specific challenges you face — including how to bridge the gap between your existing driving habits and Dutch exam expectations.

**Ask about pass rates.** Reputable schools are willing to share their CBR pass rates. The national first-time pass rate for the practical exam is around 55–60%. Good schools often exceed this. If a school is cagey about their rates, that tells you something.

**Ask about the instructor continuity.** You want to work with the same instructor throughout your lessons if possible. Different instructors have different styles, and consistency helps.

**Check reviews on Google Maps.** Read the reviews carefully — specifically look for feedback from expats who came with foreign licences. Their experience will be most relevant to yours.

**Package deals vs. pay-per-lesson.** Some schools offer lesson packages at a slightly reduced rate. These can save money if you are confident about the number of lessons you will need, but they can be inflexible. If you only need 20 lessons but bought a 35-lesson package, you may struggle to get a refund for unused lessons. Read the terms carefully.

### What happens in lessons

In your first lesson, your instructor will typically assess your existing level and discuss a realistic lesson plan. From there, lessons build progressively: basic car control, city driving, roundabouts, motorways (snelwegen), parking manoeuvres, and finally mock exam conditions where the instructor observes without coaching.

Most instructors will also brief you on what the CBR examiner looks for during the practical test. This exam-specific preparation is part of what you are paying for.

## Step 4: Practical Exam (Rijexamen)

The practical exam is booked through your driving school. Your instructor submits the booking to the CBR on your behalf, and you sit the exam in your school's car with a CBR examiner.

**Cost:** €130.50 (paid to the CBR via the school)

### What to expect

The exam lasts approximately 45–50 minutes. The examiner will give you directions and observe how you handle Dutch roads and traffic. There is no trick to it — they want to see that you can drive safely according to Dutch standards.

**The exam covers:**

- Departure from the exam centre (your first moves are observed immediately)
- Following directions from the examiner (later in the exam, you may be asked to drive using satnav)
- Handling urban traffic, roundabouts, intersections, and priority situations
- Motorway driving (if the exam centre is near a motorway)
- Parking — parallel parking, perpendicular parking, or reversing into a space
- An emergency stop at a designated point
- Handling of cyclists, trams, and pedestrians

**Common reasons for failing:**

- Not checking mirrors frequently enough before and during manoeuvres
- Hesitation at intersections — the examiner wants confident, decisive driving
- Misjudging right-of-way (the rechts heeft voorrang rule trips many people up)
- Errors in the parking manoeuvre
- Not giving cyclists adequate priority

Your examiner will give you immediate feedback at the end of the exam. If you pass, you receive a paper certificate that acts as a temporary driving permit while your actual rijbewijs is produced. The physical licence arrives by post within a few weeks.

**First-time pass rate:** approximately 55–60% nationally. With good preparation and a quality instructor, your odds are better than average.

### If you fail the practical exam

A fail is frustrating, but it is not uncommon. The examiner's feedback sheet (beoordelingsformulier) details exactly which behaviours were marked. Share this with your instructor — a few targeted lessons addressing the specific issues, followed by a resit, is the standard approach.

A resit costs another €130.50. Budget for at least one resit possibility when you plan your finances.

One way to reduce costs: take fewer lessons by being better prepared. Study your theory thoroughly before you start, so your instructor can focus on practical Dutch-specific adjustments rather than teaching you basics. And use the practice apps consistently — the more fluent you are in Dutch traffic rules before you get behind the wheel, the faster you progress.

## Timeline: What to Expect Month by Month

Here is a realistic timeline for an expat starting the process on arrival:

**Weeks 1–2: Admin**
Register at your municipality, get your BSN number, set up DigiD. These are prerequisites for the MijnCBR account. See my [BSN registration guide](/guides/legal/bsn-registration-guide-2026/) for the full process.

**Week 2–3: Health declaration**
Submit the gezondheidsverklaring online. Approved within a few days for most healthy applicants.

**Weeks 3–7: Theory exam preparation**
Daily practice with the iTheorie app and practice websites. Book your theory exam during this period — you want an appointment 3–4 weeks into your preparation.

**Week 5–7: Theory exam**
Sit the exam. Pass rate is ~50%, so allow for a possible resit two weeks later.

**Weeks 6–20: Driving lessons**
Start lessons once your theory exam is passed (some instructors prefer you have passed theory first; others are flexible). 20–40 lessons over 8–12 weeks is typical.

**Week 14–22: Practical exam**
Your instructor will advise when you are ready. The exam itself is booked through the school with a few weeks' lead time.

**Week 18–26: Licence arrives**
After passing the practical exam, your licence is produced and arrives by post within a few weeks.

**Total realistic timeline: 3–6 months**

Starting promptly is everything. If you begin the process within your first week in the Netherlands, you should have your licence well within your 185-day window. If you delay by a month or two, you may run into a gap where your 185 days expire before you have your Dutch licence.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

After helping many expats through this process, these are the pitfalls I see most often:

**Assuming you will need fewer lessons than you do.** Over-confidence about your existing driving ability leads people to book too few lessons and fail the practical exam unnecessarily. Trust your instructor's assessment over your own gut feeling.

**Underestimating the theory exam.** A 50% pass rate among all candidates, including young Dutch people who have studied intensively, should tell you something. Study properly. Do not treat it as a quick tick-box exercise.

**Not selecting English for the theory exam.** Many expats sit the Dutch version because they assume they should, then struggle with terminology. If English is your strongest language, use it.

**Choosing a driving school based on price alone.** The cheapest school is not always the slowest — but it frequently is. A school with experienced, English-speaking instructors who know the CBR exam criteria may cost €5–€10 more per lesson but save you lessons in the long run, and a potential resit fee of €130.50.

**Losing track of the 185-day deadline.** I have met expats who simply forgot about this deadline until it was almost too late. Mark it in your calendar the day you register at the gemeente. Add a reminder 30 days before it falls.

**Not starting with DigiD.** The MijnCBR account requires DigiD. If you have not set up DigiD yet when you try to book, you lose time. Sort DigiD in your first week.

**Skipping mock exam sessions with your instructor.** The final few lessons before the practical exam should simulate exam conditions as closely as possible. Do not use them as coaching sessions where your instructor talks you through every decision. You need practice making those decisions independently.

## Life Without a Car While You Wait

One silver lining of the CBR process taking several months: it gives you a good excuse to experience Dutch life without a car, which is actually quite liberating.

The Netherlands has exceptional public transport infrastructure. The [OV-chipkaart system](/guides/daily-life/ov-chipkaart-guide-expats-2026/) covers trains, trams, buses, and metro, and the intercity train network connects almost every city in the country efficiently. For most daily commutes and weekend trips, you genuinely do not need a car.

And then there is cycling. If you have not yet discovered the Dutch cycling culture, prepare to have your mind changed about what urban transport looks like. My [cycling guide for expats](/guides/daily-life/cycling-netherlands-expat-guide-2026/) covers everything from buying a bike to using cycle paths safely. Many expats who move here expecting to need a car ultimately decide they do not.

Once you do have your licence, there is a practical next step: [registering your car in the Netherlands](/guides/daily-life/register-car-netherlands-expat-2026/) if you plan to own one. That process has its own documentation requirements and costs — worth reading before you buy.

## Is It Worth It?

This is a genuine question that some expats ask, and it deserves an honest answer.

In Dutch cities, a driving licence is genuinely optional for many people. Amsterdam, Rotterdam, Utrecht, The Hague, Eindhoven — all are highly walkable, cycle-friendly, and well-served by public transport. Plenty of expats live here for years without a car and barely notice the absence.

A driving licence becomes more valuable if you:

- Live in or frequently visit rural areas or smaller towns with limited public transport
- Have children and want the flexibility of a car for school runs, sports, and weekend trips
- Work in locations not easily reached by train or bike
- Travel regularly to neighbouring countries by road
- Simply value the freedom and convenience of being able to drive

If any of those apply to you, the €2,000–€3,500 investment is likely worth it. If you are based in central Amsterdam and work five minutes from a train station, you might reasonably decide to postpone or skip it entirely.

There is no universal answer. What I will say is this: if you do want a Dutch licence, start the process early. The 185-day clock is unforgiving, the CBR process takes longer than people expect, and the later you start, the more pressured the whole thing becomes.

## Final Thoughts

Getting a Dutch driving licence from scratch is a real commitment — in time, money, and mental energy. There is no shortcut, and the CBR does not care how many years you have been driving in another country.

But it is absolutely achievable. Thousands of expats do it every year. The process is well-organised, the theory exam is available in English, and there are good driving schools that specifically cater to international clients.

The key steps are simple enough in theory:

1. Register at the gemeente and get your BSN — this starts the 185-day clock
2. Set up DigiD and a MijnCBR account
3. Submit your health declaration (€41.50)
4. Study for and pass the theory exam (€39.50)
5. Find a good English-speaking driving school and take enough lessons
6. Pass the practical exam (€130.50)

Start as early as possible. Budget honestly — €2,500 as a working figure is realistic for most people. Choose your driving school carefully. And do not let the theory exam catch you out; it is harder than it looks.
