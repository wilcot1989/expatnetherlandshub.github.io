I moved to the Netherlands in early October. Nobody told me what was coming.

By November I was buying a second umbrella because wind had already destroyed the first. By January I was seriously questioning every choice that had brought me here. By March I was genuinely excited about a single hour of pale sunshine breaking through. And by May I was cycling through tulip fields thinking this was the most beautiful place on earth.

Dutch weather does something specific to expats. It breaks you down a little, then builds you back up. After ten years here I have made peace with the grey, and I have also learned how to work with it rather than against it. This guide is the honest version — the one I wish someone had handed me at the airport.

## What the Dutch Climate Actually Is

The Netherlands has a temperate maritime climate. That means mild temperatures year-round, significant rainfall, and — most importantly — a lot of wind. The North Sea is the dominant influence. It keeps winters from getting too cold and summers from getting too hot, but it also brings a near-constant supply of cloud and moisture.

The headline statistics: average annual temperature around 10°C, annual rainfall around 800mm, roughly 130 rain days per year, and approximately 1,700 sunshine hours annually (for comparison, London gets around 1,630, Madrid around 2,800).

The Netherlands is flat. Almost entirely flat. There are no hills to shelter cities from wind, no mountains to create rain shadows or block cold air. What comes off the North Sea arrives in full force, and it arrives horizontally.

This is the weather reality. Let me take you through it month by month.

## Spring: March, April, May

Spring is the most deceptive of the Dutch seasons. It starts cold and grey — March is essentially still winter — then gradually blooms into something genuinely spectacular by May.

**March** is an odd month. Temperatures range from 3°C to 11°C. There will be bright days that feel almost warm, followed immediately by a cold front with driving rain and near-zero temperatures. Do not be fooled. March is still winter with occasional sunshine.

**April** is when things begin to shift. The tulip fields open. Temperatures reach 10°C to 15°C on good days. The evenings are still cold — keep a coat handy — but the light starts to return in a way that feels genuinely hopeful after winter. The Keukenhof gardens near Lisse are worth visiting in April if you have not been.

The other notable April event is [King's Day](/guides/daily-life/kings-day-guide-expats-2026/), celebrated on April 27th (or the 26th if the 27th falls on a Sunday). This is one of the biggest national celebrations in the Netherlands — the entire country turns orange and takes to the streets. Except that the weather does not care about national celebrations. King's Day weather is genuinely unpredictable: I have experienced brilliant sunshine, steady rain, and one memorable year, hail. The Dutch celebrate in all conditions. They dress in orange, put on a rain jacket, and carry on.

**May** is the reward for getting through winter. Days stretch to 16 hours. Temperatures hit 15°C to 20°C. The countryside is green in a way that feels almost artificial. Terrace culture — the terrasjes that define Dutch social life — starts to come alive. By late May you can sit outside in a light jacket and feel genuinely good about where you live.

**What spring means practically:**
- Pack layers. The temperature can vary 10°C between morning and afternoon.
- Carry a rain jacket always, but you will also want sunglasses.
- Spring is a wonderful time to explore [the best cities in the Netherlands](/guides/housing/best-cities-netherlands-expats-2026/) — tourist crowds have not yet peaked and the light is excellent.

## Summer: June, July, August

Dutch summer gets undersold by expats who arrive in winter and leave before experiencing it. It is genuinely good, with a few caveats.

Average temperatures in summer run from 20°C to 25°C. That is comfortable without being overwhelming. The Netherlands does not have the dry Mediterranean heat that cooks southern Europe — it stays green and fresh through summer. Humidity is moderate.

**Heatwaves** do happen, and they have become more frequent in recent years. July 2019 saw temperatures above 40°C in parts of the country — an event that would have seemed impossible a decade earlier. When a heatwave hits, the Netherlands is not well prepared for it. Air conditioning is rare in homes and older offices. The Dutch heat up quickly and cope awkwardly. If you come from a hot country, this will seem mild. If you are used to British summers, 38°C with no air conditioning in a brick apartment will humble you.

The day length is remarkable. By late June, the sun rises before 5:30am and sets after 10pm. This sounds wonderful — and it is — but it means the Dutch summer is experienced with a kind of intensity. People are out until midnight at terrasjes, cycling along canals, sitting in parks. There is a collective urgency to enjoy the good weather while it lasts, because everyone knows what is coming.

**Terrasjes culture** is one of the genuine pleasures of Dutch life. Almost every café, bar, and restaurant puts tables outside when the weather allows. This happens even when "the weather allows" means 15°C and partly cloudy — the Dutch have low thresholds for acceptable terrace weather. You learn to dress for sitting outside, to accept that a glass of wine in a light jumper is perfectly civilised, and that a blanket on your lap is not giving up.

Rain still happens in summer. An average July sees around 80mm of rainfall, often as short sharp showers that pass within an hour. Carry a compact rain jacket and you will be fine.

**June** can be disappointing — often more like a grey extension of spring than proper summer. July and August are more reliable.

## Autumn: September, October, November

Autumn in the Netherlands is beautiful and melancholy in equal measure. The light turns golden. The trees — particularly in areas like the Veluwe national park — shift through spectacular reds and oranges. And then November arrives and takes all of it away.

**September** is often the best month of the year. Temperatures stay around 16°C to 20°C, the summer crowds have gone, and the light is softer and more interesting than in summer. Rain increases compared to August, but it tends to come in manageable showers.

**October** is genuinely lovely in its first half, then the weather turns. By mid-October the temperature drops noticeably — 10°C to 14°C — and the rain becomes more persistent. The leaves are at their best in early to mid-October. If you want to experience Dutch autumn at its most photogenic, this is the window.

**November** is the transition into winter, and it is hard. The clocks go back at the end of October, and suddenly the evenings are dark by 5pm. Rain is frequent and no longer feels refreshing. The first grey weeks settle in. For expats from sunnier countries, November can be the first serious test of whether they can manage Dutch winters emotionally as well as practically.

## Winter: December, January, February

I am going to be straight with you: Dutch winter is grey. Persistently, unrelentingly grey. Not Antarctica. Not minus thirty. Just grey, and damp, and often quite windy, for about three months.

Temperatures range from 0°C to 7°C through winter. Frost happens regularly overnight. Snow falls, but rarely settles for more than a day or two. Most Dutch winters feature perhaps two or three genuinely snowy days. The canals freeze occasionally but not reliably.

The issue is not the cold. It is the light. December in the Netherlands sees as few as 8 hours of potential daylight, and with overcast skies — which is most of the time — you can go entire weeks seeing almost no direct sunlight. This is not a minor inconvenience. It affects mood, energy levels, and sleep for a significant proportion of expats and Dutch people alike.

**The Elfstedentocht** deserves mention because every Dutch person will bring it up when discussing winter. This legendary 200km ice skating race through the canals and lakes of Friesland can only run when the ice is at least 15cm thick along the entire 200km route. The last time it ran was 1997. When conditions look promising — a serious cold snap, temperatures staying well below zero — something happens to the Dutch national mood. It is almost reverential. The country holds its breath. Usually, the ice does not cooperate, and the disappointment is communal. If you are here when conditions look possible, even if it ultimately does not happen, witnessing the anticipation is something.

**Christmas and New Year** in the Netherlands have their own character. Sinterklaas (December 5th) is more culturally significant than Christmas for many Dutch families — it is the main gift-giving event. Check the [Dutch holidays guide](/guides/daily-life/dutch-holidays-traditions-expat-guide-2026/) for a full breakdown of the calendar. New Year's Eve (Oud en Nieuw) involves fireworks on a scale that needs to be experienced to be believed — the Dutch spend more per capita on fireworks than almost any other European country, and the midnight sky is extraordinary. The following morning, the streets are covered in debris, but nobody seems bothered.

**What to do about winter:**
- Vitamin D supplements are not optional — they are genuinely medically sensible. The Dutch health system recommends them broadly.
- Get a daylight therapy lamp (lichttherapielamp). They cost €40-80 and genuinely work for many people.
- Stay active. [Cycling in winter](/guides/daily-life/cycling-netherlands-expat-guide-2026/) sounds unpleasant but the Dutch do it, and there is a reason — it forces you outside, keeps you warm, and provides daily movement that counteracts winter sluggishness.
- Plan something to look forward to. A trip in January or February to somewhere sunny can reset your mental state significantly.

## The Wind: The Feature Nobody Tells You About

If I had to identify the single defining characteristic of Dutch weather — more than the rain, more than the grey — it would be the wind.

The Netherlands is flat. The North Sea is close. The result is that wind is a near-constant presence, and it is not gentle. Average wind speeds in coastal areas run to 5-6 on the Beaufort scale regularly. Inland is somewhat more sheltered, but not dramatically so.

The wind does several things to daily life:

**It makes everything colder.** A temperature of 5°C with a force 6 wind feels closer to minus 5°C in terms of how it affects your body. Wind chill is the dominant factor in Dutch winter discomfort. Dress for it, not just for the air temperature.

**It destroys umbrellas.** If you arrive in the Netherlands with an umbrella, it will turn inside out within a week. The Dutch do not use umbrellas to any great extent — they wear waterproof jackets. This is not a fashion choice, it is pragmatic adaptation. Buy a proper waterproof with a hood.

**It makes cycling harder and more interesting.** Riding into a headwind on a Dutch dike road is an experience. Riding with a tailwind is sublime. [Cycling in the Netherlands](/guides/daily-life/cycling-netherlands-expat-guide-2026/) in wind is not optional — you will encounter it — so your technique and your bike matter more than in calmer countries.

**It also means the weather changes quickly.** A dark overcast morning can turn into brilliant sunshine by noon. Rain comes and goes fast. Unlike climates where grey means grey all day, Dutch overcast can break unpredictably. This makes waterproofing more important than always choosing to stay indoors.

## The Rain Reality

The Netherlands has a reputation as one of Europe's rainiest countries. The reality is more specific than that.

Amsterdam receives about 800mm of rainfall per year. London receives about 600mm. So yes, Amsterdam is wetter than London by total volume. However, Rotterdam gets about 800mm too, and much of the UK's west coast receives far more — Manchester averages around 800mm as well.

The more relevant figure is rain days: the Netherlands experiences around 130-140 days with measurable rainfall per year. That is roughly one in three days. But the nature of Dutch rain matters as much as the quantity. Most Dutch rain is drizzle or light rain — what the Dutch call motregen. A typical rainy day involves a few showers, some grey skies, and perhaps 3-5mm of total precipitation. It is rare to have a day of heavy, sustained downpour.

The practical implication: you will rarely be stuck indoors because of rain. You will frequently be slightly damp. The Dutch solution is simple — good waterproof clothing, no umbrellas, and an attitude that a bit of rain is not a reason to cancel plans.

## How Weather Shapes Daily Life

Dutch culture has adapted to its climate in ways that become visible once you start noticing them.

**Gezelligheid** — the Dutch concept of cosiness and togetherness — is partly a weather response. When it is cold and grey outside, you create warmth inside. Candles on tables in cafés. Curtains you actually draw. A culture of inviting people home rather than always going out. This is not just an aesthetic preference — it is centuries of adaptation to a climate that makes outdoor socialising difficult for half the year.

**Vitamin D** is a serious public health issue here. The Dutch health authority (RIVM) recommends vitamin D supplements for most adults, especially during winter months. Many expats arrive without knowing this and spend their first winter wondering why they feel inexplicably tired and flat. Start supplements in October. It genuinely helps.

**Cycling in rain** is non-negotiable if you want to live like the Dutch. The infrastructure exists, the culture supports it, and stopping cycling every time it rains would mean not cycling for a third of the year. Invest in good rain gear: waterproof trousers, a waterproof jacket with a hood, and either waterproof shoes or overshoes. You will get used to arriving slightly damp and drying out indoors. It is fine.

**Seasonal eating and social patterns** shift noticeably. Summer sees Amsterdam's parks, canal sides, and outdoor market spaces fill with people at every available opportunity. Winter sees a retreat into stroopwafels, erwtensoep (split pea soup), and evenings in brown cafés. [Making friends in the Netherlands](/guides/integration/making-friends-netherlands-expats-2026/) is actually easier in autumn and winter in some ways — there is more social cohesion around indoor activities and shared spaces.

## Tips by Nationality

**If you come from a sunny country (Spain, Italy, Greece, Middle East, South Asia, South America, Southern USA):**

You will find Dutch winter genuinely difficult, and there is no shame in acknowledging that. The grey is real. The darkness is real. People from sunny climates report the lack of sunlight as the biggest quality of life shock, bigger than the rain, bigger than the cold.

Concrete advice: do not be a hero. Get the daylight lamp, take vitamin D seriously, and do not spend winter huddled indoors — counterintuitively, getting outside even on grey days helps your body regulate mood and energy far better than staying in.

Consider building a trip south into your winter — February is an excellent time to visit somewhere with sun, because by then you will have accumulated enough grey to genuinely appreciate it.

The Dutch themselves do not pretend winter is fun. Many book holidays to the Canaries, Thailand, or Florida specifically to break the winter. You are in good company.

**If you come from a cold country (Scandinavia, Canada, Russia, Northern US):**

You will be fine with the temperatures — Dutch winters are mild compared to what you know. But you may find the grey dreariness unexpectedly wearing, even if the cold does not bother you. Cold and sunny is energising. Grey and damp can feel oppressive in a different way.

Your advantage: you probably know how to dress for cold and wet, how to cycle in winter, and how to create warm indoor environments. These skills transfer directly.

**If you come from the UK:**

You have experience with maritime grey weather. Dutch winter is not dramatically different from a wet British winter, except the wind is stronger and the architecture is flatter (no hills, no greenery climbing up to shelter you). The upside is that summer surprises you more — it can feel genuinely warmer and sunnier than British summers, and the infrastructure for outdoor life (canals, terrace culture, cycling) is far more developed. You will feel at home in Dutch rain within weeks. The [Netherlands vs UK comparison](/guides/integration/netherlands-vs-uk-expats-2026/) covers more of these cultural parallels.

## Seasonal Affective Disorder: Taking It Seriously

SAD (Seasonal Affective Disorder) is not just feeling a bit down in winter. It is a recognised mood disorder linked to reduced light exposure, and in a country with Dutch winter characteristics, it affects a meaningful proportion of the population.

Symptoms include persistent low mood, fatigue (sleeping more but feeling unrefreshed), loss of motivation, changes in appetite (often craving carbohydrates), difficulty concentrating, and social withdrawal. These symptoms follow a seasonal pattern — appearing in autumn, worsening through winter, and lifting in spring.

Expats can be more vulnerable than local residents for several reasons: the winters are a shock if you are not used to them, you may lack the established social network and routines that buffer against isolation, and you may be underestimating how much the light change is affecting you (it is easy to attribute symptoms to work stress or homesickness without recognising the seasonal component).

What actually helps:
- **Daylight therapy lamps:** 10,000 lux lamps used for 20-30 minutes each morning have solid evidence behind them. Use them consistently from October through March.
- **Vitamin D:** Get a blood test in autumn. Many expats are deficient within their first Dutch winter. Supplementation (typically 1000-2000 IU daily) is recommended by Dutch health authorities.
- **Exercise outdoors:** Even a 20-minute walk in daylight — even grey daylight — helps your circadian rhythm more than the same time spent indoors.
- **Social continuity:** Do not let the weather drive you into isolation. [Mental health support for expats in the Netherlands](/guides/health/mental-health-support-expats-netherlands-2026/) is available and covers this in more detail.
- **Huisarts:** If symptoms are serious — affecting work, relationships, or daily function — speak to your GP. This is a recognised condition with established treatments, and there is no reason to suffer through it.

## What to Wear: The Practical Guide

**Year-round essentials:**
- A waterproof jacket with a hood. This is the single most important item. Do not bring an umbrella as your primary rain protection — it does not work on a bicycle and the wind will defeat it within days. Spend €80-150 on a proper breathable waterproof. You will use it 150 days a year.
- Layers. Dutch weather changes fast and varies significantly during a single day.

**Winter (November through March):**
- Thermal underlayer (top and bottom)
- Mid-layer fleece or wool jumper
- Waterproof outer layer
- Hat that covers your ears — ear warmth matters more in Dutch wind than in still cold
- Waterproof gloves, not just warm ones
- Waterproof ankle boots or cycling overshoes if you cycle

**Spring and Autumn:**
- Light fleece or down gilet as a mid-layer
- Waterproof jacket always in your bag
- Light scarf (useful from September through May)

**Summer:**
- Light layers you can add and remove
- A compact rain jacket — heatwaves can break with sudden showers
- Sun protection — the Dutch forget about this because they are not used to needing it, but UV in summer is real

**Cycling gear specifically:** Waterproof trousers that fit over your regular clothes, mudguards on your bike (most Dutch city bikes have them built in), and lights for any cycling before 9am or after 4pm in winter.

## After Ten Years: What I Actually Think

I grew up partly in the UK, where I thought I understood grey weather. I did not. Dutch winter is a different kind of grey — flatter, more persistent, more wind-driven.

But the Dutch summers surprised me. Genuinely warm, long, golden evenings, an entire country that throws itself outdoors with almost childlike enthusiasm because everyone knows it will not last. There is something charming about a culture that has collectively decided to maximise every available ray of sunshine.

And I have come to love autumn — October especially — in a way I did not expect. The light over the polders and canals in October is extraordinary. Low, golden, photographically interesting in a way that bright summer light is not.

What I tell expats now: prepare for winter properly, both practically and mentally. Do not tough it out without the daylight lamp and the supplements. Build social structures before autumn arrives, because they are harder to build when you are already in low-mood winter mode. And do not write the Netherlands off based on January. By May, you will wonder what all the fuss was about — right before you start buying extra layers in September.

The weather is not why you move to the Netherlands. But how you handle it will determine whether you stay.
