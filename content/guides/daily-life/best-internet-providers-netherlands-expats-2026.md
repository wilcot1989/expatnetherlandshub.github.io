I had just moved into a lovely flat in an older part of Amsterdam — solid brick walls, high ceilings, the full Dutch period-building experience. A colleague recommended Ziggo. It was a bit cheaper, they said, and really fast. So I signed a two-year contract, paid the installation fee, and waited for my modem to arrive.

The connection worked fine at first. But within a few weeks I noticed something: in the evenings, especially between 7pm and 10pm, the speeds would drop noticeably. Video calls became unreliable. Streaming stuttered. I kept rebooting the router. Months later, after complaining multiple times to Ziggo's Dutch-speaking customer service (my Dutch was not great yet, and their English was limited), I learned what I should have checked before signing: the older coax cable infrastructure in my specific street was shared with a dense cluster of nearby flats, and evening congestion was a known issue. I was in an area where glasvezel was actually available — KPN had run fiber to my building already — but nobody had told me to check.

I was stuck for two years.

That experience is why I now tell every client: before you sign anything, check what technology is available at your postcode. This guide will walk you through exactly how to do that, and help you choose the right provider once you know what your options are.

## Understanding Dutch Internet Infrastructure

Before comparing providers, you need to understand what is actually physically available at your address. The Netherlands has three main types of internet infrastructure, and your choice of provider is largely determined by which one runs past your building.

### Glasvezel (Fiber Optic)

Glasvezel is the best internet technology available in the Netherlands, and the country has been rolling it out aggressively. As of 2026, roughly 75% of Dutch households can connect to fiber. KPN owns the largest fiber network, but several regional and municipal providers also run their own (Delta Fiber, Caiway, and local cooperative networks in many smaller towns).

What makes glasvezel different:

- **Symmetrical speeds**: Your upload speed equals your download speed. If you sign up for 500 Mbps, you get 500 Mbps in both directions. This matters enormously for video calls, cloud backups, and working from home.
- **Low latency**: The signal travels at close to the speed of light. Response times are typically under 5ms, which makes real-time applications like video conferencing and gaming feel noticeably better.
- **Uncontended bandwidth**: Unlike cable, your fiber connection is not shared with your neighbours at a network level. Your speeds are far more consistent across the day.
- **Future-proof**: Current fiber infrastructure supports speeds far beyond what any consumer package offers today.

If glasvezel is available at your address, I would choose it every single time.

### Kabel (Coax Cable — Ziggo's Network)

Cable internet runs over the same coaxial cable infrastructure that delivers television. Ziggo is by far the dominant provider here, covering around 7 million Dutch households. Cable can deliver impressive download speeds — up to 1200 Mbps — but has two important limitations:

1. **Upload speeds are much lower than download speeds**. A 500 Mbps download package typically gives you only 50 Mbps upload. This is fine for streaming and browsing, but is a real constraint if you upload large files or do a lot of video calling.
2. **Shared bandwidth in busy periods**. The coax network is a shared medium in your local area. In dense urban areas at peak times (evenings, weekends), speeds can drop. This is less of an issue in quieter areas.

Cable is a perfectly good option in many circumstances. It is widely available, generally reliable, and speeds have improved significantly in recent years. But it is worth knowing its limitations before you sign up.

### DSL (Old Copper Telephone Lines)

DSL is old technology running over the traditional copper telephone network. You will still see DSL packages advertised, especially in areas where fiber has not yet arrived. If you are offered a DSL connection, know this: maximum speeds are typically 40–80 Mbps download, often less, and performance degrades with distance from the exchange. I would avoid DSL unless it is genuinely your only option or you are only staying somewhere very temporarily.

**Two-year contracts carry early exit fees.** If you are unsure how long you are staying in the Netherlands, consider paying the slightly higher monthly rate for a flex (no-commitment) contract.

## Ziggo: Best Cable Internet, But Know the Trade-offs

Ziggo is everywhere. The purple branding is on vans, billboards, and television adverts across the country. It serves around 4 million internet customers and has the most extensive cable network in the Netherlands. For many Dutch households, Ziggo and internet are essentially synonymous.

For expats, Ziggo is a reasonable choice in the right circumstances — but there are things you should know before you sign.

### What I Like About Ziggo

**Very widely available.** If fiber has not reached your street yet, Ziggo cable almost certainly has. In areas where KPN glasvezel is not an option, Ziggo is often the best speed you can get.

**Download speeds are genuinely fast.** 500 Mbps or 1000 Mbps download is more than enough for any normal household. If you mainly stream, browse, and do video calls, Ziggo's download performance is excellent.

**Good for TV bundles.** If you want Dutch television channels alongside your internet, Ziggo's combined packages are well priced.

### What to Watch Out For With Ziggo

**Upload speeds are low relative to download speeds.** A 1000 Mbps download package gives you only 100 Mbps upload. For most people this is fine. But if you work from home extensively, upload large files, or do video calls in 4K, this asymmetry will frustrate you.

**Evening congestion in some areas.** As I learned the hard way, Ziggo's cable network is shared infrastructure. In densely populated areas, you may see slower speeds between 7pm and 10pm. This is not universal — many Ziggo customers never notice it — but it is a real phenomenon, and it is worth asking neighbours about their experience before signing.

**Customer service is primarily in Dutch.** Ziggo does not formally offer English-language support. Many agents are happy to switch to English, but it is inconsistent and you cannot count on it, especially via phone. Online support, bills, and the My Ziggo app are all in Dutch.

**Auto-renewal is aggressive.** Ziggo is particularly well known for rolling contracts into new fixed terms automatically. Set a calendar reminder at least two months before your contract end date.

My honest assessment: Ziggo is fine if fiber is not available at your address, if price is a factor, or if you want a TV bundle. If you have the choice between KPN glasvezel and Ziggo cable, I would take glasvezel without hesitation.

## T-Mobile Home and Odido: Good Value on KPN's Network

T-Mobile and Odido (which is the consumer brand of the same parent company in some product lines) are worth considering if price matters and you still want fiber. Both use KPN's wholesale network — meaning they are essentially reselling KPN's infrastructure at a lower price.

### T-Mobile Home

T-Mobile Home has been offering home internet products in the Netherlands for several years and has positioned itself as a value alternative to KPN. Because it uses KPN's network, the fiber quality is identical — you get the same physical infrastructure at a lower price point.

**Typical prices (2026):**
- 200/200 Mbps: ~€30–35/month
- 500/500 Mbps: ~€38–43/month
- 1000/1000 Mbps: ~€45–50/month

**What works well:** Competitive pricing. English-speaking customer support (T-Mobile's support culture is noticeably more internationally oriented than many Dutch telecoms). Good for bundling with T-Mobile mobile plans if you already use T-Mobile for your phone.

**What to watch:** You are dependent on KPN's network but supported by T-Mobile's customer service infrastructure, which can mean slower resolution of network issues because T-Mobile has to escalate to KPN for physical faults. In practice this rarely causes problems, but it is worth knowing.

If you are already a T-Mobile mobile customer, the combined packages offer real savings. I have recommended T-Mobile Home to several clients who were happy with the trade-off between price and the slightly more indirect support chain.

### Odido

Odido has been positioning itself as a budget-friendly option, also running on KPN infrastructure. Prices are competitive — often the lowest you will find for fiber in the Netherlands — but customer service is primarily in Dutch and the brand is less established.

For expats who are confident managing utility contracts in Dutch (or have a Dutch partner or friend who can help), Odido is worth comparing. For everyone else, the savings are real but the support experience is limited.

## Budget Providers: Youfone, Budget Thuis, Online.nl

If you are watching every euro — and the [cost of living in the Netherlands](/guides/housing/cost-of-living-netherlands-2026/) means many expats absolutely are — budget internet providers can save you €10–20 per month compared to KPN or Ziggo directly.

The key thing to understand: these providers do not own their own network. They are virtual operators (MVNOs in telecom terminology, though applied to fixed-line internet). Budget Thuis uses Ziggo's cable network. Online.nl and Youfone use KPN's fiber and DSL infrastructure. You get the same physical connection at a lower price.

### What You Gain

- **Lower monthly prices.** Packages typically start around €25–28/month for 50–100 Mbps, rising to €35–42 for 500 Mbps. This is meaningfully cheaper than the main providers.
- **Same underlying network quality.** The fiber signal from Online.nl is the same fiber signal as KPN. Budget Thuis's cable is Ziggo's cable.
- **Simpler product ranges.** No confusing bundle upsells.

### What You Give Up

- **English customer support.** All three operate almost entirely in Dutch. Websites, billing, and support are Dutch-language only. If something goes wrong, you are dealing with a Dutch-speaking call centre.
- **Limited flexibility.** Fewer options for flex/no-commitment contracts. Most are 1-year agreements.
- **Slower resolution of issues.** When there is a network fault, budget providers need to coordinate with their network owners (KPN or Ziggo) as intermediaries. Resolution can take longer.

My view: budget providers make sense if you have someone who can help you in Dutch (a colleague, a partner, a Dutch friend), if you are confident enough in Dutch to handle a support call, or if you are only staying for a defined period and want to keep costs down. For most newly arrived expats still finding their feet, the €10–15 monthly saving is not worth the potential headache of dealing with a Dutch-only support chain when your internet goes down.

## What Expats Specifically Need to Know

Beyond the speed and price comparisons, there are several aspects of Dutch internet contracts that catch expats off guard. I have seen these issues come up again and again.

### Contracts and Auto-Renewal (Opzeggen)

Dutch internet contracts have a fixed initial term — almost always one or two years. During this period, early cancellation typically incurs a fee (usually the remaining months of the contract, sometimes discounted). After the fixed term ends, the contract rolls automatically to a month-to-month arrangement with one month's notice required.

**The trap:** Providers are entitled to notify you that your contract is being renewed for another fixed term. If you miss the cancellation window (which varies by provider, but is typically 1–3 months before the contract end date), you can be locked in for another year.

What to do: Note your contract end date on the day you sign. Set a calendar reminder at least two months before that date. If you want to cancel or switch, act then.

### English Customer Support

I have touched on this throughout, but it bears repeating as its own point. On a normal day, you will manage your internet contract entirely online and never need to call anyone. But on the day you have a fault, a billing dispute, or a moving query, the language of customer support matters.

- **KPN**: Formally offers English-language support. I have used it. It works.
- **T-Mobile Home**: Good English support experience in practice.
- **Ziggo**: Officially Dutch-only, but many agents speak English and will help.
- **Odido, Budget Thuis, Youfone, Online.nl**: Expect Dutch only.

If you are not yet comfortable with conversational Dutch and the idea of a technical support call in Dutch while your internet is down sounds stressful, factor this into your decision.

### Installation Wait Times

Expect 2 to 4 weeks between signing your contract and having a working connection installed. Sometimes less (Ziggo is often quicker because the coax infrastructure is already in the building). Sometimes more during busy periods or if your building requires new cabling.

Practical implication: as soon as you have a confirmed move-in date and address, order your internet that same day. If you are going to be in the Netherlands for an overlap period before your main flat is ready, check whether your temporary accommodation has WiFi included. Many expat short-stay apartments do. You can always use your mobile data as a hotspot for the first week or two — see my guide on the [best SIM cards for expats in the Netherlands](/guides/daily-life/best-sim-card-netherlands-expats-2026/) for options that give you enough data to work on.

### Moving Within the Netherlands

If you move to a new address within the Netherlands during an active contract, you are not usually entitled to cancel without a fee just because you moved. Most providers will transfer your contract to your new address if the same infrastructure is available there. KPN is generally good at handling this. Ziggo can be more complicated if your new address is outside their cable coverage.

If your new address does not have the same network type as your current contract (e.g., you move from a Ziggo area to a KPN-only area), providers are typically obliged to release you from the contract without penalty. Document this situation carefully.

This is also worth thinking about when you are [finding housing in the Netherlands](/guides/housing/finding-housing-netherlands-expats-2026/): if you know you are going to move again in 12 months, a two-year contract is a risk. Either take a flex contract or stick to a 1-year term.

### BSN and Dutch Bank Account

Most providers require both a BSN (Burger Service Nummer) and a Dutch bank account to set up a direct debit (automatische incasso), which is the standard way internet bills are paid in the Netherlands. If you have just arrived and do not yet have a BSN, see my [DigiD guide for expats](/guides/legal/digid-guide-expats-2026/) — getting your BSN is step one of almost everything official here.

Some providers will start the process without a BSN but pause the contract activation until you provide one. Ask explicitly when signing up.

### Cancellation Rules in Practice

To cancel a Dutch internet contract after the fixed term, you need to give notice in writing (email or via the provider's online portal is fine — a formal letter is not required). One calendar month's notice is standard. Keep a copy of your cancellation confirmation. If you do not receive a confirmation, follow up — a cancellation that has not been acknowledged is a cancellation that may not have been processed.

## What Speeds Do You Actually Need?

There is a lot of marketing noise around internet speeds. In reality, most Dutch households do not need the most expensive gigabit packages. Here is a practical breakdown.

### Streaming

Each HD stream uses roughly 5–8 Mbps. A 4K stream uses 15–25 Mbps. If you have one television running Netflix in 4K and a laptop streaming music in the background, you are using perhaps 30 Mbps. A 100 Mbps connection handles this with ease.

### Working From Home

A standard video call on Zoom or Teams uses 3–5 Mbps per participant at HD quality. Two people on video calls simultaneously need 10–15 Mbps. However, **upload speed matters here**, not just download. This is where fiber's symmetrical speeds shine. For regular home working, I would recommend a minimum of 50 Mbps symmetrical — which means fiber rather than cable if upload speed matters to you.

If you are looking for a place to work outside the home, see my guide to the [best coworking spaces in the Netherlands](/guides/work/best-coworking-spaces-netherlands-2026/).

### Gaming

For gaming, latency (ping) is more important than raw speed. You want a ping below 30ms to Dutch servers for most online games. Fiber typically achieves 5–15ms. Cable is usually 10–25ms. Both are fine. A 50 Mbps connection is more than enough bandwidth for any game — the issue is never the download speed, it is the latency.

### A Household With Multiple Devices

If you have two adults working from home, two children streaming, smart home devices, and a gaming console all running simultaneously, I would suggest a minimum of 200 Mbps and ideally 500 Mbps fiber. This is not about anyone single device needing that much — it is about having overhead so that everyone's connection stays smooth when peak household usage coincides.

### My Speed Recommendations by Household Type

| Household | Recommended Speed | My Provider Pick |

## How to Switch Internet Providers (Step by Step)

Switching providers in the Netherlands is not complicated, but the timing matters. Here is the process I walk clients through:

**Step 1: Check your current contract end date.**
Look at your original contract confirmation email or log in to your provider's portal. Note the end date and calculate when you need to give notice to avoid automatic renewal.

**Step 2: Check what is available at your new (or current) address.**
Use the KPN and Ziggo coverage checkers, or run a search on Independer. Confirm that the provider you want to switch to can actually connect you.

**Step 3: Sign up with the new provider first.**
Counter-intuitive, but important: sign your new contract before cancelling the old one. Most new providers will coordinate the switch date so you have minimal downtime. They often handle the cancellation notification to your old provider on your behalf — confirm whether this is the case.

**Step 4: Confirm the switch date.**
Your new provider will give you an installation date. Your old contract should end on or just before that date. Confirm both sides have the date aligned.

**Step 5: Cancel the old contract in writing.**
Even if the new provider is handling it, send your own cancellation email to your old provider. Keep the confirmation.

**Step 6: Return equipment.**
Most providers require you to return their modem/router after cancellation. They will send a prepaid return box, usually within a week of your contract ending. Return it promptly — there are fees for unreturned equipment.

**Total downtime:** With a properly managed switch, you should have zero to a few hours of downtime on the switchover day. In my experience, KPN-to-KPN switches (i.e., moving between providers on the same network) are fastest.

## Final Word

Dutch internet infrastructure is genuinely excellent by European standards. The speeds are real, the fiber coverage is expanding every year, and prices are reasonable relative to what you get. Most expats I know are pleasantly surprised once they are connected.

The mistakes happen before the connection is set up — signing a long contract for the wrong technology, not checking what is available at your address, or choosing purely on price without considering English-language support.

My recommendation for almost every expat client: check your postcode first on Independer or the KPN website, choose glasvezel if it is available, go with KPN or T-Mobile Home depending on your budget, and opt for a 1-year contract over 2-year until you are sure you are staying. Do that, and Dutch internet will be one of the least stressful parts of your relocation.

Which it absolutely should be, given how good the speeds are.
