A client of mine — I'll call her Priya — arrived in Amsterdam from Mumbai with eight years of project management experience, excellent English, and the kind of CV that should have opened doors immediately. Her first Dutch job interview did not go well. Not because she was underqualified. Not because of language. It went badly because she spent fifteen minutes telling the panel how exceptional she was, listing achievements with the kind of polished confidence she had always been told to project.

The interviewers smiled politely and said they would be in touch. They were not.

When Priya came to me afterwards, frustrated and confused, it took about five minutes to identify the issue. "Priya," I told her, "you were doing everything right for a British or American interview. But Dutch interviews have different rules. Here, that level of self-promotion reads as arrogance, not confidence."

Three weeks later, with a different approach, she had two job offers.

That story is not unusual. The Dutch job market is genuinely welcoming to international talent — and with [Dutch work culture](/guides/work/dutch-work-culture-guide-expats-2026/) being far less hierarchical than in many countries, expats often thrive here once they understand the unwritten rules. The challenge is that those rules are different enough to trip people up, and nobody hands you a guide on the way in.

So here is the guide.

## Understanding Dutch Interview Culture First

Before you think about what to wear or what questions to prepare, you need to understand the cultural framework.

Dutch professional culture is built on a few core values: **directness, equality, and consensus**. These are not just buzzwords — they genuinely shape how interviews feel and what interviewers are looking for.

**Directness** means that Dutch interviewers will ask blunt questions without softening them. "Why did you leave that job?" is asked exactly like that. They expect equally direct answers. Vagueness or evasiveness makes Dutch interviewers uncomfortable in a way that would not happen in, say, a British setting where people are more accustomed to reading between the lines.

**Equality** means the hierarchy in an interview room is often flatter than you might expect. The hiring manager may well introduce themselves by their first name and expect you to do the same. Calling someone "Mr de Vries" when they have just told you their name is Kees will feel odd to them. Treat the conversation as a two-way exchange, not a formal examination.

**Consensus** (the Dutch concept of *gezelligheid* aside, the more relevant one here is *poldermodel*) means Dutch teams genuinely care whether you will fit into the group. The interview is partly about your skills, but also about whether your colleagues will actually enjoy working with you.

## What to Wear

I get this question constantly and the answer is almost always: **smart casual**.

The Netherlands is not a suit-and-tie culture for most sectors. Turning up in a full formal suit to a Dutch tech company, creative agency, or startup will make you look out of place — and being out of place is a form of poor preparation.

The exceptions are finance (particularly banking and investment), law, consulting at the Big Four, and some corporate multinationals. For these, business formal is still the norm.

When in doubt, look at the company's LinkedIn photos, their website team page, or their office photos. You will usually get a very clear read on the dress code from these. If you are genuinely unsure, smart casual — clean, pressed, well-fitting clothes in neutral tones — is a safe default for almost any Dutch interview.

One practical note: do not be thrown off if your interviewer arrives in jeans. This is not unusual in the Netherlands, even at a fairly senior level.

## The Structure of a Dutch Job Interview Process

Here is what you should expect, broadly speaking:

### Round 1: The Introductory Interview

This is usually 30 to 45 minutes, often done by video. The recruiter or HR contact wants to check a few basics: is your experience genuine, are you legally able to work here, is your salary expectation in the right range, and do you come across as reasonably sane. It is not a deep assessment — it is a filter.

Prepare a clear two-minute summary of your background and why this role interests you. Make sure you know roughly what salary you want. This will come up.

### Round 2: The Main Interview

This is typically with two or three people — often a direct manager and a future colleague. It runs 60 to 90 minutes. This is where you will face behavioural questions, discuss your actual experience in detail, and where the "culture fit" assessment happens in earnest.

Expect the interview to feel conversational. Dutch interviewers often do not follow a rigid script and may encourage discussion rather than structured Q&A. Go with it.

### Round 3: Final Round or Assessment

For more senior or specialist positions, a third round is common. This might be a presentation on a case study they give you in advance, a panel interview with senior leadership, or a formal assessment day with psychometric tests, group exercises, and competency interviews.

**Assessment days** (*assessmentcentrum*) are genuinely common in the Netherlands, more so than in the UK or US. If you are invited to one, do not be alarmed — it is standard practice, not a sign of doubt about you.

## Common Questions and How to Answer Them

Dutch interview questions fall into a few recognisable categories. Here are the ones that come up most often, and what makes a strong answer.

### "Tell me about yourself" (*Vertel eens iets over jezelf*)

Keep this to two to three minutes. Cover your professional background, your most relevant experience, and why you are interested in this role. Do not start with where you were born or your life story. The Dutch prefer efficiency.

### "What are your strengths and weaknesses?"

On strengths: be specific and factual. "I consistently deliver projects on time" is better than "I am very organised." The Dutch are sceptical of vague self-praise.

On weaknesses: give a real one. Dutch interviewers see through the "I work too hard" non-answer immediately, and it reads as evasive. Give a genuine development area and explain what you are doing about it.

### "Why do you want to work here?"

Do your research. Dutch companies expect you to know who they are, what they do, and — at a basic level — what their position in the market is. A vague answer here is a red flag.

### Behavioural questions ("Can you give an example of a time when...")

The STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result) works well here. Keep your examples specific, factual, and relatively concise. Avoid dramatic language or hyperbole — Dutch professionals are often suspicious of anyone who sounds like they are reading from a motivational poster.

### "Where do you see yourself in five years?"

This is asked sincerely. Dutch employers value stability and tend to favour candidates who have a realistic sense of their own development. You do not need to claim you want their job — you just need to show you have thought about your own growth.

### "What do you like to do outside of work?"

This is not small talk. Dutch employers genuinely value work-life balance, and this question is an honest probe into whether you are someone who will burn out in six months. Answer genuinely. Cycling, sport, family time, hobbies — all perfectly good answers.

## The Culture Fit Question

I want to spend a moment on this because it surprises many expats.

Dutch companies — particularly in the tech sector, but increasingly everywhere — place enormous weight on culture fit. This is sometimes labelled *teamfit* or just described as "seeing if you click." It sounds soft, but it has real teeth.

What are they actually assessing?

- Will you work well in a flat structure where you are expected to push back on your manager?
- Are you someone who takes ownership without waiting to be told?
- Will you handle direct feedback without taking it personally?
- Can you be honest — including when the honest answer is that you do not know?

The best way to demonstrate culture fit is not to try to perform it. It is to be genuinely yourself and to ask questions that show you understand the environment. Asking "How does your team typically handle disagreements?" or "What does a typical week look like?" signals that you are thinking about the reality of the job, not just whether you get it.

## Salary Negotiation and the 30% Ruling

Salary negotiation in the Netherlands is normal and expected. You should not be embarrassed to discuss it.

Use our [salary checker tool](/tools/salary-checker/) to benchmark what your role typically pays in the Dutch market before any conversation happens. Going in with knowledge is far better than going in blind.

A few important points:

**Gross versus net:** Dutch salaries are quoted gross. With income tax, social contributions, and other deductions, your net take-home will be considerably lower. This catches many expats off guard.

**The 30% ruling:** If you are an expat moving to the Netherlands from abroad and your salary meets the threshold, you may qualify for the [30% ruling](/guides/finance/30-percent-ruling-netherlands-2026/) — a significant tax advantage that allows your employer to pay 30% of your salary tax-free for up to five years. This can have a substantial effect on your net income, and it is worth mentioning during salary discussions. Some Dutch employers factor it into their offers; others do not and are happy to learn you are aware of it.

**Holiday allowance (*vakantiegeld*):** In the Netherlands, employees are legally entitled to 8% of their annual gross salary as holiday allowance, typically paid in May. This is separate from your monthly salary, so factor it into your total package calculation.

**Negotiating:** Make a counter-offer if the first offer is below your target. One round of negotiation is standard and expected. Two rounds is acceptable. Three begins to feel uncomfortable in most Dutch contexts.

## Video Interviews: What to Know

Video interviews are now standard at the first-round stage, and some companies — particularly those hiring internationally — run entire processes remotely.

A few things that matter more than people think:

- **Background:** Clean and neutral. Bookshelves are fine. Unmade bed or cluttered kitchen are not.
- **Lighting:** Light source in front of you, not behind. A simple ring light is a worthwhile investment if you interview frequently.
- **Internet:** Test it. Have a backup plan (mobile hotspot) if your connection is unreliable.
- **Eye contact:** Look at the camera, not at their faces on screen. It sounds counterintuitive but reads as engagement.
- **Sound:** Use headphones with a microphone if possible. Background noise is more distracting than people realise.

Dutch interviewers conducting video calls tend to be fairly relaxed about the format — they are used to it. A moment of technical difficulty will not end your candidacy. Just handle it calmly.

## Assessment Days: What to Expect

If you are invited to an *assessmentcentrum*, here is what to prepare for:

| Exercise Type | What They Assess |
|---|---|
| Cognitive ability tests | Numerical, verbal, and logical reasoning |
| Personality questionnaire | Working style and cultural fit (no right or wrong answers) |
| In-tray or inbox exercise | Prioritisation and decision-making under time pressure |
| Presentation or case study | Analytical thinking, communication, structure |
| Group exercise | Collaboration, leadership style, how you handle disagreement |
| Competency interview | Specific examples from your professional history |

The most important advice for assessment days: be consistent. The various exercises are designed to triangulate your actual working style. Trying to perform a version of yourself you think they want to see usually backfires. Show up as you are, and show up prepared.

## Common Mistakes Expats Make

In ten years of coaching internationals through the Dutch job market, I have seen the same patterns repeatedly.

**Over-selling yourself.** The Dutch do not respond well to superlatives and big statements. "I am a highly driven results-oriented leader who consistently exceeds expectations" will make Dutch interviewers raise an eyebrow. Specific, factual, modest: that is the tone that lands well.

**Being too formal.** Calling your interviewer "Mr" or "Ms" when they have introduced themselves by first name, sitting rigidly, not making small talk — these things read as socially awkward rather than professional.

**Not asking questions.** Dutch interviewers take this as a signal of low interest or lack of preparation. Prepare at least three genuine questions. "What does success look like in this role after six months?" is always a solid one.

**Ignoring the work-life balance question.** Some expats, particularly from cultures with a strong hustle mentality, try to signal that they will work extremely long hours. This often backfires. Dutch employers value efficiency and sustainability, not martyrdom.

**Being vague about salary.** If you do not know what you want, they lose confidence in you. Know your number.

**Not being honest about your Dutch language level.** If you cannot hold a conversation in Dutch, say so. Claiming fluency you do not have will create an awkward situation that is far more damaging than honesty.

## If You Are Considering Freelancing

Some expats who struggle to find the right permanent role consider going the ZZP route — working as a self-employed freelancer (*zelfstandige zonder personeel*). This is a genuinely viable path in the Netherlands, particularly in tech, design, and consulting. Our [ZZP freelancer guide](/guides/work/freelancer-zzp-guide-netherlands-2026/) covers the setup process, tax implications, and what to expect in detail.

The Dutch freelance market is competitive but active. Many companies prefer to hire experienced specialists on a project basis before considering permanent roles, so it can also serve as a route into the permanent market.

## A Note on Rejection

If you do not get the job, it is absolutely fine to ask for feedback. Dutch employers are often unusually forthcoming with it — that directness cuts both ways. You may get genuinely useful information about what to improve or refine.

Do not take rejection personally or as a statement about your broader prospects in the Dutch market. The culture fit element means that sometimes it genuinely is just a matter of team dynamics rather than your qualifications or performance.
