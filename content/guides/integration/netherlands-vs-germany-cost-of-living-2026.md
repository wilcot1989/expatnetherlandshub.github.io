When I first moved to the Netherlands, I assumed Germany would be basically the same. Neighboring countries, shared borders, both in the EU. How different could they really be? Then a German friend showed me her rent bill in Düsseldorf and I nearly spilled my coffee. She was paying EUR 850 for a two-bedroom apartment. I was paying EUR 1,650 for a one-bedroom in Rotterdam. That moment made me realize the cost gap between these two countries is far bigger than most expats expect.

I have spent over a decade helping expats relocate to the Netherlands, and Germany is always the comparison that comes up most. Both countries attract highly skilled workers. Both have strong economies and good infrastructure. But the day-to-day cost of living can differ by hundreds of euros per month depending on where you settle and whether you qualify for the Dutch [30% ruling](/guides/work/30-percent-ruling-guide-2026/).

This article is the breakdown I wish someone had given me before I signed my first Dutch lease. I am comparing real 2026 numbers — rent, groceries, healthcare, taxes, transport, childcare, energy — so you can make a clear financial decision.


## Quick Verdict: Which Country Is Cheaper?

Before I get into the detail, here is the short version.

> **Bottom line:** Germany is 10-20% cheaper than the Netherlands for daily living. Rent is the biggest difference — you will save EUR 300-800 per month on a comparable apartment outside Munich. Groceries, energy, transport, and childcare are all lower in Germany.
>
> **But there is a catch.** The Dutch 30% ruling can flip the equation completely. A skilled migrant earning EUR 70,000 in the Netherlands keeps more money after tax than the same person in Germany — enough to offset the higher costs and still come out ahead.
>
> **My advice:** If you qualify for the 30% ruling, the Netherlands is likely the better financial deal for your first five years. If you do not qualify, Germany offers more purchasing power in almost every category.

## Rent and Housing: The Biggest Cost Difference

Housing is where you feel the gap most. The Dutch rental market is one of the tightest in Europe, especially in the Randstad (Amsterdam, Rotterdam, The Hague, Utrecht). Germany has more supply, more space, and lower prices in most cities.

### Amsterdam vs Berlin

Amsterdam is the most expensive city in the Netherlands. Berlin is Germany's capital but has historically been one of Europe's more affordable capitals. That gap is narrowing — Berlin rents have climbed roughly 10% per year since 2023 — but it is still significant.

| Property type | Amsterdam | Berlin |
|---------------|-----------|--------|
| 1-bed apartment (center) | EUR 1,600-2,200 | EUR 1,000-1,500 |
| 1-bed apartment (outside center) | EUR 1,200-1,600 | EUR 750-1,100 |
| 2-bed apartment (center) | EUR 2,000-2,800 | EUR 1,300-1,900 |
| 2-bed apartment (outside center) | EUR 1,500-2,100 | EUR 1,000-1,400 |

That is a difference of EUR 500-800 per month for a comparable apartment. Over a year, you are looking at EUR 6,000-9,600 saved in Berlin.

### Rotterdam vs Munich

This comparison is more interesting because Munich breaks the German pattern. Munich is Germany's most expensive city — often comparable to Dutch prices.

| Property type | Rotterdam | Munich |
|---------------|-----------|--------|
| 1-bed apartment (center) | EUR 1,300-1,700 | EUR 1,400-1,900 |
| 1-bed apartment (outside center) | EUR 1,000-1,300 | EUR 1,000-1,400 |
| 2-bed apartment (center) | EUR 1,600-2,200 | EUR 1,800-2,400 |
| 2-bed apartment (outside center) | EUR 1,200-1,600 | EUR 1,300-1,800 |

Munich is actually slightly more expensive than Rotterdam. If you are moving to Germany for cost reasons, Munich is not the city to pick.

### Rotterdam vs Frankfurt

Frankfurt is Germany's financial hub and the fairest comparison to Rotterdam as a mid-tier business city.

| Property type | Rotterdam | Frankfurt |
|---------------|-----------|-----------|
| 1-bed apartment (center) | EUR 1,300-1,700 | EUR 1,100-1,500 |
| 1-bed apartment (outside center) | EUR 1,000-1,300 | EUR 800-1,100 |
| 2-bed apartment (center) | EUR 1,600-2,200 | EUR 1,400-1,900 |

Frankfurt is about 15-20% cheaper than Rotterdam, but not as dramatically different as Berlin vs Amsterdam.

### Buying a Home

If you plan to stay long term, buying changes the equation. German mortgage interest is not tax-deductible, while in the Netherlands your mortgage interest (hypotheekrenteaftrek) reduces your taxable income. German property transfer tax (Grunderwerbsteuer) varies by state from 3.5% to 6.5%. Dutch overdrachtsbelasting is 2% for primary residences under EUR 510,000 (first-time buyers get an exemption).

The bottom line on housing: unless you target Munich, Germany gives you more square meters for less money. For a detailed look at Dutch rents and what to expect, see my [renting guide for expats](/guides/housing/renting-netherlands-expat-guide-2026/).

### Dining Out

Restaurant meals are also cheaper in Germany. A three-course dinner for two at a mid-range restaurant costs around EUR 50-70 in German cities versus EUR 65-90 in Dutch cities. A beer at a bar is EUR 4.00-5.00 in Germany compared to EUR 5.50-7.00 in the Netherlands.

One place the Netherlands wins: Indonesian and Surinamese food. If you love rijsttafel, the Netherlands is unbeatable. Germany counters with Turkish food — Berlin has some of the best döner kebab outside Istanbul, and it costs EUR 5-7 versus EUR 8-10 in Amsterdam.

### Coffee Culture

The Dutch love their koffie, but a flat white in a good Amsterdam café will set you back EUR 4.50-5.50. In Berlin, the same drink is EUR 3.50-4.50. Not a huge difference per cup, but if you are a three-coffees-a-day person, it matters over a year.


## Healthcare: Two Different Systems, Two Different Price Tags

Both countries have excellent healthcare, but the systems are structured very differently. Understanding the costs is one of the first things I tell new expats to figure out. I have written a [full guide to Dutch health insurance](/guides/finance/dutch-health-insurance-expats-2026/) if you want the deep dive, but here is the comparison.

### The Netherlands: Mandatory Private Insurance

Every resident must buy private health insurance (basisverzekering). Here are the 2026 numbers:

- **Monthly premium:** EUR 140-170 (depending on insurer)
- **Annual deductible (eigen risico):** EUR 385
- **Employer contribution:** Your employer pays an income-dependent contribution (6.57% of salary, capped)
- **What is covered:** GP visits (free, no deductible), hospital care, specialists, prescription drugs, mental healthcare
- **What is not covered:** Dental care for adults, physiotherapy (first sessions), glasses

Your total out-of-pocket cost as an employee is roughly EUR 1,680-2,040 per year in premiums plus up to EUR 385 in deductible. Many people never hit their full deductible.

### Germany: Dual Public-Private System

Germany splits into public (gesetzliche Krankenversicherung, GKV) and private (private Krankenversicherung, PKV) insurance:

**Public insurance (most employees):**
- **Rate:** 14.6% of gross salary + ~1.7% supplementary contribution = ~16.3% total
- **Split:** Employer pays half, you pay half (~8.15% of your gross salary)
- **Income cap:** Contributions are capped at a salary of EUR 62,100 (2026)
- **Maximum monthly contribution (your share):** ~EUR 422
- **Family benefit:** Spouse and children covered for free if they do not work

**Private insurance (earnings above EUR 69,300):**
- Premiums based on health and age, typically EUR 300-600 per month
- No family coverage — each person pays separately

### Cost Comparison by Salary

| Gross salary | Netherlands (your cost/year) | Germany public (your cost/year) |
|-------------|-------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| EUR 40,000 | ~EUR 2,000 | ~EUR 3,260 |
| EUR 55,000 | ~EUR 2,000 | ~EUR 4,480 |
| EUR 70,000 | ~EUR 2,000 | ~EUR 5,064 (capped) |
| EUR 90,000 | ~EUR 2,000 | ~EUR 5,064 (capped) |

The Netherlands is cheaper for individuals at almost every salary level. The German system's percentage-based model means higher earners pay significantly more.

However, Germany has one major advantage: family coverage. If your partner does not work, they are covered under your German public insurance at no extra cost. In the Netherlands, every adult pays their own premium. A non-working spouse adds another EUR 1,680-2,040 per year.


## Transportation: Bikes vs Autobahn

### Public Transport

Germany has a clear winner here: the Deutschlandticket. For EUR 58 per month, you get unlimited access to all regional public transport in the entire country — buses, trams, U-Bahn, S-Bahn, regional trains. It is one of the best transport deals in Europe.

The Netherlands has no equivalent. Public transport runs on the OV-chipkaart system, and it is pay-per-kilometer. Some typical costs:

| Route/Pass | Netherlands | Germany |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Monthly unlimited (national) | No equivalent (NS Flex Dal Vrij: ~EUR 119 off-peak only) | EUR 58 (Deutschlandticket, all regional) |
| Amsterdam → Rotterdam (single) | EUR 17.10 | Berlin → Hamburg equivalent: EUR 0 (included in Deutschlandticket) |
| Monthly city transport pass | EUR 90-100 (GVB Amsterdam) | Included in Deutschlandticket |
| Intercity train (e.g. 100 km) | EUR 15-25 | EUR 0 (regional) or EUR 20-50 (ICE) |

For commuters, Germany saves EUR 30-60 per month on transport costs.

### Cycling

This is where the Netherlands dominates. The cycling infrastructure is the best in the world. Separated bike lanes, dedicated traffic lights, bike parking garages at every train station. I cycle everywhere — work, groceries, dinner with friends. It is not a lifestyle choice here, it is just how people move.

Germany has good cycling infrastructure in some cities (Münster, Freiburg, Cologne), but nothing approaching the Dutch standard. Most German cities are car-oriented.

### Car Costs

If you need a car, Germany is cheaper. Fuel is roughly comparable (EUR 1.75-1.90 per liter for petrol), but Dutch car ownership costs are higher due to BPM (vehicle purchase tax), higher insurance premiums, and expensive parking in cities. Annual road tax (motorrijtuigenbelasting) in the Netherlands is also higher than Germany's Kfz-Steuer.

A car that costs EUR 30,000 in Germany might cost EUR 33,000-36,000 in the Netherlands after BPM. Annual insurance and tax add another EUR 500-1,000 more in the Netherlands.


## Taxes and Net Salary: Where the Real Comparison Happens

This is the section that matters most. I have a full breakdown of the [Dutch tax system for expats](/guides/finance/dutch-tax-system-expats-2026/), but here is the head-to-head comparison.

### Income Tax Rates (2026)

**Netherlands:**
- Box 1: 36.97% on income up to EUR 75,518
- Box 1: 49.50% on income above EUR 75,518

**Germany:**
- 0% up to EUR 11,784 (tax-free allowance)
- 14-42% progressive rate up to EUR 277,826
- 45% above EUR 277,826
- Plus 5.5% solidarity surcharge on tax amount
- Plus 8-9% church tax if registered with a church (can be avoided)

Germany's tax-free allowance and progressive structure mean that on lower-to-middle incomes, the effective rate can be lower than in the Netherlands. But the solidarity surcharge and potential church tax add up.

### The 30% Ruling: The Biggest Tax Advantage

I keep coming back to this because it genuinely changes the math. The [30% ruling](/guides/work/30-percent-ruling-guide-2026/) lets qualifying skilled migrants treat a portion of their gross salary as a tax-free allowance:

- **Years 1-2:** 30% tax-free
- **Year 3:** 20% tax-free
- **Years 4-5:** 10% tax-free

### Net Salary Comparison: EUR 70,000 Gross

Let me show you what EUR 70,000 gross actually means in each country.

| | Netherlands (with 30% ruling) | Netherlands (without 30% ruling) | Germany |
|---|-------------------------------|-----------------------------------|---------|
| **Gross salary** | EUR 70,000 | EUR 70,000 | EUR 70,000 |
| **Tax-free allowance** | EUR 21,000 (30% ruling yr 1-2) | EUR 0 | EUR 11,784 |
| **Taxable income** | EUR 49,000 | EUR 70,000 | EUR 58,216 |
| **Income tax** | ~EUR 18,120 | ~EUR 25,117 | ~EUR 16,800 |
| **Solidarity surcharge** | N/A | N/A | ~EUR 924 |

| **Health insurance (your share)** | ~EUR 2,000 | ~EUR 2,000 | ~EUR 5,064 |
| **Approximate net annual** | **~EUR 49,880** | **~EUR 37,383** | **~EUR 40,012** |
| **Approximate net monthly** | **~EUR 4,157** | **~EUR 3,115** | **~EUR 3,334** |

With the 30% ruling, the Netherlands gives you EUR 823 more per month than Germany. Without it, Germany gives you EUR 219 more. That is a significant swing.

## Childcare and Education

If you have kids, this category can make or break your budget.

### Childcare Costs

**Netherlands:**
- Full-time daycare (kinderdagverblijf): EUR 1,800-2,400 per month per child
- After-school care (BSO): EUR 800-1,200 per month
- Government subsidy (kinderopvangtoeslag): covers 50-96% of costs depending on income
- **Net cost after subsidy (average household):** EUR 400-900 per month

**Germany:**
- Kita (daycare) costs vary wildly by state:
  - Berlin: free from age 1
  - Hamburg: EUR 0-200 per month (income-based)
  - Munich: EUR 100-400 per month
  - Average across Germany: EUR 0-400 per month
- After-school care (Hort): EUR 50-150 per month

Germany wins this category clearly. Even after Dutch subsidies, most families pay more than they would in Germany. Berlin's free Kita policy alone can save a family EUR 5,000-10,000 per year.

### Schools

Both countries have good public schools. The Netherlands has a wider range of international schools (especially in Amsterdam, The Hague, and Eindhoven), and instruction is often partly in English. German public schools operate in German, and international school options are more limited outside major cities.

International school fees are comparable: EUR 15,000-25,000 per year in both countries.

## Quality of Life: Beyond the Numbers

Cost of living is only half the picture. I always tell expats to think about what their money buys them in terms of daily experience.

### Work-Life Balance

The Netherlands is famous for its work-life balance. The Dutch work an average of 31 hours per week — the lowest in Europe. Part-time work is socially accepted and legally protected. Many professionals work four days a week. Wednesday afternoon is often a "papa day" or "mama day" where a parent picks up the kids.

Germany has a more traditional working culture. Full-time positions are the norm (38-40 hours per week), and while vacation time is generous (25-30 days), the daily work rhythm is more rigid. German workers value punctuality, structure, and clear boundaries between professional roles.

### Language and Social Life

If you only speak English, the Netherlands is dramatically easier. About 95% of Dutch people speak fluent English, and many workplaces in the Randstad operate entirely in English. You can live in Amsterdam for years without speaking a word of Dutch (though I would not recommend it for long-term integration).

Germany requires more German. Even in Berlin's international bubble, many everyday situations — dealing with bureaucracy, seeing a doctor, joining a sports club — require at least basic German. This is a genuine consideration for your quality of life and one that the cost-of-living numbers do not capture.

For tips on settling into the Netherlands, see my [guide to the best cities for expats](/guides/integration/best-cities-expats-netherlands-2026/).

### Safety and Infrastructure

Both countries are safe and well-organized. The Netherlands has slightly better cycling infrastructure, a more compact geography (everything is within 2-3 hours by train), and a stronger international community in major cities. Germany has more geographic diversity (mountains, forests, coastline), larger cities with more cultural offerings, and better domestic flight connections.

### Housing Quality

German apartments tend to come unfurnished to a degree that shocks most expats. I mean truly empty: no kitchen, no light fixtures, sometimes no flooring. You install your own kitchen when you move in and take it with you when you leave. Dutch rentals typically come with a basic kitchen and light fixtures, though unfurnished apartments are also common.

This cultural quirk means your initial setup costs in Germany can be EUR 5,000-15,000 higher if you need to buy and install a kitchen.

## Which Country Is Better for Expats? My Honest Assessment

After ten years of helping expats make this choice, here is how I frame it.

### Choose the Netherlands if:

- **You qualify for the 30% ruling.** This single factor can make you EUR 5,000-10,000 per year richer than you would be in Germany, even with higher rent.
- **You work in tech, finance, or international business.** The Dutch job market for English-speaking professionals is significantly larger.
- **You do not want to learn a new language immediately.** You can function fully in English from day one.
- **You value work-life balance above all.** The Dutch four-day work week culture is real and life-changing.
- **You want a compact, easy-to-navigate country.** Everything is close. Amsterdam to Rotterdam is 40 minutes by train.

### Choose Germany if:

- **You do not qualify for the 30% ruling.** Without it, Germany's lower costs give you more purchasing power.
- **You have a family.** Free or near-free childcare and family health insurance coverage save thousands per year.
- **You work in engineering, manufacturing, or automotive.** Germany's industrial base is unmatched in Europe.
- **You want more space.** Both more living space per euro and more geographic variety.
- **You plan to stay very long term (10+ years).** Germany's lower baseline costs compound over time, and the 30% ruling expires after five years anyway.
- **You want affordable homeownership.** Property prices outside Munich and Frankfurt are significantly lower than Dutch equivalents.

### The Hybrid Option

I know several expats who moved to the Netherlands first to benefit from the 30% ruling, saved aggressively for five years, and then moved to Germany when the tax benefit expired. It is not for everyone, but if you are flexible about where you live, it is a financially sound strategy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is the Netherlands more expensive than Germany?

Yes, on average. The Netherlands costs roughly 10-20% more than Germany for daily living. Rent is the biggest driver — expect to pay 20-40% more for a comparable apartment in Dutch cities (excluding the Munich anomaly). Groceries are 15-25% higher, dining out is 15-20% more, and childcare is significantly pricier before subsidies. The main exceptions are energy costs (comparable) and health insurance (cheaper per person in the Netherlands for higher earners).

### How much do I need to earn to live comfortably in the Netherlands vs Germany?

For a comfortable single life in a major city (not Amsterdam or Munich), I would say EUR 45,000 gross in Germany and EUR 50,000 in the Netherlands. For a family of four, aim for EUR 65,000 in Germany and EUR 75,000 in the Netherlands. With the 30% ruling, you can be comfortable in the Netherlands on EUR 55,000 gross. These numbers assume renting, not buying.

### Is it easier to get permanent residency in the Netherlands or Germany?

Both countries offer permanent residency after five years of legal residence. The Netherlands requires passing an integration exam (inburgeringsexamen) including a Dutch language test at A2 level. Germany requires B1-level German, which is a higher language bar. However, the Netherlands also offers the highly skilled migrant (kennismigrant) route, which is faster for initial work permits.

### Can I work in Germany and live in the Netherlands (or vice versa)?

Yes, many people do — especially in border regions like Limburg/NRW or Groningen/Niedersachsen. However, the tax situation gets complicated. You generally pay income tax in the country where you work, and cross-border commuters may face double taxation treaties. Health insurance also becomes complex. I would recommend getting professional tax advice if you are considering this.

### Which country is better for remote workers?

The Netherlands edges ahead for English-speaking remote workers. The widespread English proficiency, strong internet infrastructure (average speeds of 100+ Mbps), and the abundance of coworking spaces make it an attractive base. The Netherlands also has more developed visa options for digital nomads. Germany offers cheaper living costs but requires more German for daily life.

### Are housing deposits higher in the Netherlands or Germany?

In the Netherlands, landlords typically ask for one to two months' rent as a deposit (borg). In Germany, the maximum deposit is legally capped at three months' cold rent (Kaltmiete), but landlords often ask for the full three months. In absolute terms, a deposit in Amsterdam might be EUR 1,600-4,400, while in Berlin it would be EUR 2,400-3,600 — so they end up roughly comparable despite different rules.

### How do retirement systems compare?

The Netherlands has a strong three-pillar system: AOW (state pension), employer pension, and private savings. Germany has a similar structure with the Gesetzliche Rentenversicherung (state pension, 18.6% of salary split with employer), company pension (Betriebsrente), and private savings (Riester-Rente). Both systems are solid, but the Dutch pension system consistently ranks in the top three globally in the Mercer pension index, while Germany typically ranks around 14th.

### Is one country better for expats from specific regions?

Generally yes. Germany has larger communities from Turkey, Eastern Europe, and Southeast Asia. The Netherlands has strong communities from Indonesia, Suriname, India, and the UK. Both have significant American and Brazilian communities. Go where you will find your community — it makes the transition much easier.

### What about the cost of learning the local language?

Dutch courses cost EUR 500-1,500 for a full course at a language school. German courses are similar at EUR 500-1,200. Both countries offer government-subsidized language programs for permanent residents. German is widely considered harder to learn than Dutch for English speakers due to its more complex grammar (four cases, three genders), though both languages share Germanic roots with English.
