## 1. Duolingo Dutch — The Obvious Starting Point

I know, I know. Everyone tells you to download Duolingo. And honestly? For a free resource, it is hard to beat as a starting point.

### What Is Actually Free

Duolingo's full Dutch course is available on the free tier. You get access to all lessons, all units, and the entire curriculum from beginner to roughly A2 level. The catch is that the free tier includes ads between lessons and limits how many mistakes you can make before you need to wait for your "hearts" to refill (or watch an ad to get them back). On the desktop website, these limitations are less annoying than on mobile.

### What I Like

The gamification genuinely works for building a daily habit. The Dutch course covers practical vocabulary, basic grammar, and even some specifically Dutch cultural scenarios. The speaking exercises, while imperfect, get you used to producing Dutch sounds. And the community stories feature gives you short reading practice with dialogue.

### Limitations

Duolingo will not get you past A2 level. It does not teach you to have actual conversations, its grammar explanations are minimal, and it over-relies on translation exercises that do not mirror real-world language use. The Dutch course is also noticeably less polished than Duolingo's Spanish or French courses — you will occasionally encounter odd sentences that no Dutch person would ever say.

### Who It's Best For

Complete beginners who need to build a daily study habit. Download it, set a 15-minute daily goal, and use it as one component of your learning — not your only one.

I compare Duolingo head-to-head with a paid alternative in my [Babbel vs Duolingo for Dutch](/guides/integration/babbel-vs-duolingo-learn-dutch-2026/) comparison.


## 2. DutchPod101 — Free Sample Lessons for Listening Practice

DutchPod101 is a podcast-style platform with audio and video lessons at various levels. It is technically a paid subscription service, but there is a meaningful amount of free content available.

### What Is Actually Free

When you create a free account, you get access to sample lessons from each level (absolute beginner through advanced), a basic vocabulary tool, and new lessons posted weekly. The free content rotates, so what is available today might differ from next month. There is also a 7-day free trial of the full platform.

### What I Like

The audio quality is excellent, and the lessons are taught by native Dutch speakers who explain things clearly. The podcast format means you can learn while commuting, walking, or doing household chores. The cultural notes woven into lessons give useful context about Dutch life and customs that pure language apps miss.

### Limitations

The free tier is genuinely limited. You get a taste but not a full course. The platform pushes you hard toward a paid subscription, and the email marketing after signing up is relentless. Grammar instruction in the free lessons is surface-level.

### Who It's Best For

Commuters and busy professionals who want to supplement app-based learning with listening practice. Create a free account, download the sample lessons that match your level, and use them during your commute. If you find the format helpful, the paid subscription (roughly EUR 8-25 per month depending on the plan) is reasonable.


## 3. Goethe Verlag / 50Languages — Simple and Effective Phrase Learning

This is one of those resources that nobody talks about but that quietly works very well for beginners.

### What Is Actually Free

Everything. The entire 50Languages platform is free, both on the web and in the app. There are 100 lessons covering common phrases and vocabulary, each with audio from native speakers. The Dutch course is available from English and dozens of other source languages, which is particularly useful if English is not your first language either.

### What I Like

Each lesson takes just 5-10 minutes and focuses on a specific real-world situation: at the restaurant, at the doctor, asking for directions, numbers, days of the week. The audio recordings are natural-sounding, and you can slow them down. There are no ads, no premium upsells, no accounts required. It just works.

### Limitations

The approach is entirely phrase-based with no grammar instruction whatsoever. You will learn to say things but not understand why they are structured that way. The interface looks like it was designed in 2005. And 100 lessons, while substantial, only cover A1-A2 vocabulary.

### Who It's Best For

Absolute beginners who want a no-nonsense, free resource to learn survival Dutch. Also excellent for expats whose first language is not English, since courses are available from over 50 source languages.


## 4. Clozemaster — Build Vocabulary Through Context

Clozemaster is the resource I wish I had found years earlier. It uses a fill-in-the-blank (cloze test) approach to teach vocabulary in the context of full sentences.

### What Is Actually Free

The core gameplay is free. You get sentences with a missing word, four multiple-choice options, and audio of the complete sentence. The Dutch course has thousands of sentences organized by frequency, so you learn the most common words first. There are also grammar challenges and listening-only exercises in the free tier.

### What I Like

This is the single best free tool for expanding your Dutch vocabulary beyond beginner level. Instead of learning isolated words (like most apps do), you learn words in context, which means you also absorb sentence structure, word order, and natural Dutch phrasing. The sentences are taken from real sources like Tatoeba, so they sound natural. The spaced repetition system is effective, and the stats tracking is motivating.

### Limitations

Clozemaster assumes you already have a basic foundation. If you start as a complete beginner, you will be lost. The interface is functional but not pretty. There is no pronunciation practice, no speaking exercises, and no grammar explanations. The premium tier (Clozemaster Pro, roughly EUR 8 per month) adds useful features like text input instead of multiple choice.

### Who It's Best For

Intermediate learners (A2+) who want to rapidly expand their vocabulary. I recommend starting with Duolingo or 50Languages, then switching to Clozemaster once you have a basic foundation. The two complement each other perfectly.


## 5. Language Transfer — The Hidden Gem for Grammar Understanding

Language Transfer is a free audio course created by Mihalis Eleftheriou that teaches languages through a thinking method rather than memorization. The Dutch course has about 40 lessons.

### What Is Actually Free

Everything. Language Transfer is entirely free, funded by donations. All lessons are available on the website, YouTube, SoundCloud, and the Language Transfer app. There is no premium tier, no ads, and no catch.

### What I Like

This is the best free resource I have found for understanding how Dutch grammar works. Instead of having you memorize rules, the teacher guides you through building Dutch sentences based on patterns you already know from English. Within the first few lessons, you are constructing sentences you would not have thought possible. The approach builds real understanding rather than rote memorization, and the lightbulb moments are frequent.

### Limitations

The Dutch course is shorter than Language Transfer's Spanish or German courses — about 40 lessons compared to 90+ for those languages. It covers A1-A2 level grammar and some vocabulary, but you will need other resources for listening comprehension, reading practice, and vocabulary building. The audio-only format means no visual aids for spelling or written Dutch.

### Who It's Best For

Learners who want to understand the logic of Dutch rather than just memorize phrases. Excellent for analytically-minded people who get frustrated by apps that never explain the "why" behind grammar rules. Use it alongside Duolingo or 50Languages for the best results.


## 6. NOS Journaal in Makkelijke Taal — News in Simple Dutch

NOS (the Dutch public broadcasting news service) publishes a daily news bulletin written in simplified Dutch, aimed at language learners and people with reading difficulties.

### What Is Actually Free

Everything. The NOS Makkelijke Taal section on the NOS website and app is completely free. Articles are published daily covering the same news topics as the main NOS Journaal but using shorter sentences, simpler vocabulary, and clearer explanations of complex topics.

### What I Like

This is real Dutch about real current events, not textbook exercises. Reading about things you actually care about (politics, weather, football, the housing market) keeps you motivated in a way that fictional textbook scenarios never can. The simplified language hits a sweet spot for A2-B1 learners: challenging enough to learn new words, simple enough not to be overwhelming. It is also a brilliant way to learn about Dutch society and culture, which ties directly into the KNM (Knowledge of Dutch Society) component of the [inburgeringsexamen](/guides/integration/inburgeringsexamen-preparation-guide-2026/).

### Limitations

There is no audio for most articles, so it is purely reading practice. No vocabulary explanations or translations — you will need to look up unknown words yourself. And it only helps with reading; you will not learn to speak or write Dutch from reading news alone.

### Who It's Best For

A2+ learners who want authentic reading practice. Make it a daily habit: read one NOS Makkelijke Taal article each morning with your coffee. Look up three to five unknown words per article and write them down. Over weeks and months, this builds reading fluency faster than any textbook.


## 7. NPO Start — Dutch TV with Subtitles

NPO Start is the free streaming platform for Dutch public television. Think of it as the Dutch equivalent of BBC iPlayer.

### What Is Actually Free

Most content on NPO Start is free to watch from within the Netherlands (or with a VPN). This includes talk shows, news programs, documentaries, reality TV, drama series, and children's programming. Many programs have Dutch subtitles available, and some have English subtitles.

### What I Like

Watching real Dutch TV is how I finally trained my ear to understand fast, natural spoken Dutch. You get exposure to different accents, slang, colloquial expressions, and the rhythm of actual conversations — things no textbook or app can teach. Dutch reality shows like "Wie is de Mol?" are addictive even if you only understand half of what is being said. Children's programs like "Het Klokhuis" are excellent for beginners because they use clearer pronunciation and simpler vocabulary.

### Limitations

Real Dutch TV is genuinely hard for beginners. Even with subtitles, the speed and colloquial language can be overwhelming at A1-A2 level. I recommend waiting until you are at least A2 before making this a regular part of your practice. Also, some NPO Start content is geo-restricted to the Netherlands.

### Who It's Best For

B1+ learners who want to improve listening comprehension and learn natural spoken Dutch. Start with children's programs or NOS Journaal, then gradually move to talk shows and series. This is also a brilliant way to learn about [Dutch culture](/guides/integration/dutch-culture-guide-expats-2026/) and pick up the kind of language you need to actually [make friends with Dutch people](/guides/integration/making-dutch-friends-guide-2026/).


## 8. Dutch Online by TU Delft (Coursera) — University-Quality for Free

The Delft University of Technology offers a Dutch language course on Coursera that you can audit for free.

### What Is Actually Free

When you click "Enroll for Free" on Coursera, choose the "Audit" option. This gives you access to all video lectures, reading materials, and quizzes. You only pay (roughly EUR 40-50 per month for Coursera Plus) if you want a graded certificate. The learning content itself is completely free.

### What I Like

This is by far the most structured and academically rigorous free Dutch course available. The video lectures are professionally produced, the explanations are clear, and the course follows a logical progression from basic pronunciation through grammar and practical conversations. The cultural context sections are particularly good — they were clearly designed by people who understand what foreign learners actually struggle with. The course covers A1-A2 level thoroughly and provides a foundation solid enough to continue to B1.

### Limitations

It is a university course, so the pace is slower and more academic than an app. You need to commit significant time (estimated 3-5 hours per week for 10 weeks) to complete it properly. The free audit does not include graded assignments or peer feedback, so you miss out on the writing exercises. And unlike live courses, you cannot ask the instructor questions.

### Who It's Best For

Self-disciplined learners who want a structured, high-quality course without paying. If you are the kind of person who finishes online courses (be honest with yourself), this is the best free option available. Pair it with Duolingo or Clozemaster for daily practice between the weekly lecture sessions.


## 9. r/learndutch Subreddit and Discord — Community Support

Learning a language in isolation is miserable. The r/learndutch subreddit and its associated Discord server provide a community of fellow learners and helpful native speakers.

### What Is Actually Free

Everything. Reddit and Discord are both free platforms. The r/learndutch subreddit has over 80,000 members, an extensive wiki with recommended resources, and regular discussion threads for grammar questions, pronunciation help, and homework checks. The Discord server offers voice channels for practice conversations and real-time help from native Dutch speakers.

### What I Like

When you are stuck on a grammar question at 11 PM, the subreddit or Discord will have someone who can help. The community is supportive and genuinely knowledgeable. The wiki is one of the best-curated free resource lists I have seen. And the Discord voice channels offer something almost no other free resource does: actual conversation practice with real people.

### Limitations

Quality of answers varies. Some advice on the subreddit is excellent; some is from learners who confidently share incorrect information. The Discord can be quiet during non-European hours. And online communities work best as a supplement to structured learning — they are not a course in themselves.

### Who It's Best For

Any level learner who wants community, motivation, and a place to ask questions. Join the subreddit on day one of your learning journey, read the wiki, and check in regularly. The Discord is particularly valuable once you reach A2+ and want conversation practice.

### Who It's Best For

Visual learners who want grammar explanations, pronunciation demonstrations, and cultural context. Bart de Pau's channel in particular can serve as a core learning resource if you work through his lessons systematically.

## Free vs. Paid: When Should You Invest?

Here is the honest truth that most "learn Dutch for free" articles will not tell you: free resources can take you to A2 level, maybe B1 if you are extremely disciplined. But at some point, most learners hit a wall.

### Where Free Resources Fall Short

**No personalized feedback.** Apps cannot hear that you are pronouncing the "ui" in "huis" wrong. They cannot tell you that your word order in subordinate clauses is consistently off. You need a human for that.

**No real conversation practice.** Talking to an app is not the same as talking to a person. The Discord channels help, but they are not structured learning.

**No accountability.** When it is just you and an app, it is very easy to skip a day, then a week, then a month. A scheduled lesson with a tutor keeps you on track.

### When to Consider Paid Resources

I recommend investing in paid resources when:

- You have been learning for 3-6 months and notice your progress slowing down
- You can read basic Dutch but cannot hold a conversation
- You need to pass the [inburgeringsexamen](/guides/integration/inburgeringsexamen-preparation-guide-2026/) and want structured exam preparation
- You want to speak Dutch at work (the gap between textbook Dutch and professional Dutch is significant)
- You keep making the same mistakes and have no one to correct them

The most cost-effective approach I have seen — and the one I recommend to the expats I coach — is to use free resources for daily self-study and invest in a tutor for weekly conversation practice and feedback.

## CEFR Language Levels Explained: A1 Through C2

If you have seen letters and numbers like "A2" and "B1" thrown around and felt confused, here is what they actually mean for your Dutch learning journey.

### A1 — Beginner

You can introduce yourself, ask and answer simple questions ("Where are you from?", "What do you do?"), and handle very basic interactions if the other person speaks slowly. This is where most free apps get you within the first 1-3 months.

### A2 — Elementary

You can handle routine daily tasks: shopping, ordering food, asking for directions, basic conversations about your family and work. This is the minimum level required for the **inburgeringsexamen** and what most free resources can realistically get you to.

### B1 — Intermediate

You can handle most situations you encounter while living in the Netherlands, describe experiences, express opinions, and understand the main points of clear standard speech. This is the level where you start feeling genuinely functional in daily life and where many expats get comfortable and stop progressing.

### B2 — Upper Intermediate

You can interact with native speakers fluently enough that conversations feel natural for both of you. You understand complex texts, can express yourself on a wide range of topics, and can function in a professional Dutch-speaking environment. This is required for admission to Dutch universities (NT2-II level).

### C1-C2 — Advanced to Proficient

Near-native fluency. You understand nuance, humor, implicit meaning, and can express yourself precisely in complex situations. Very few expats who learn Dutch as adults reach this level — and honestly, you do not need to. B2 is more than sufficient for full participation in Dutch life.

### What Level Do You Need?

| Goal | Level Needed |
|------|-------------|
| Survive daily life | A2 |
| Inburgeringsexamen | A2 |
| Feel comfortable socially | B1 |
| Work in a Dutch-speaking team | B1-B2 |
| Dutch university admission | B2 (NT2-II) |
| Sound basically native | C1+ |

## A Realistic 6-Month Study Plan Using Free Resources

Here is exactly what I would do if I were starting from zero today and wanted to reach A2 level in six months using only free resources. This is the plan I give to the expats I coach, adjusted for people with full-time jobs.

### Month 1-2: Building the Foundation

**Daily (20-30 minutes):**
- Duolingo Dutch: 2-3 lessons per day (roughly 15 minutes)
- 50Languages: 1 lesson per day (5-10 minutes)

**Weekly (2-3 hours):**
- Language Transfer: 3-4 audio lessons per week during commute or chores
- Bart de Pau YouTube: 1-2 grammar explanation videos per week

**Goal by end of Month 2:** Complete Duolingo Unit 1, finish Language Transfer course, know basic greetings, numbers, days, common verbs, and simple sentence structures.

### Month 3-4: Expanding and Practicing

**Daily (30-45 minutes):**
- Duolingo Dutch: continue 2-3 lessons per day
- Clozemaster: 10-15 minutes of vocabulary practice (switch from 50Languages)
- NOS Makkelijke Taal: read one article (look up 3-5 new words)

**Weekly (2-3 hours):**
- TU Delft Coursera course: 1-2 modules per week
- Bart de Pau or Dutch with Kim YouTube: 2-3 videos per week

**Goal by end of Month 4:** Complete Duolingo through Unit 3, understand basic written Dutch, know roughly 1,000-1,500 words, able to construct simple sentences.

### Month 5-6: Active Use and Consolidation

**Daily (30-45 minutes):**
- Clozemaster: 15 minutes vocabulary building
- NOS Makkelijke Taal: read one article
- Duolingo: review and practice sessions

**Weekly (3-4 hours):**
- NPO Start: watch one Dutch program with Dutch subtitles
- r/learndutch Discord: join one voice conversation session
- Review week's new vocabulary using a flashcard app (Anki is free)

**Goal by end of Month 6:** Reach A2 level. Understand and use familiar everyday expressions. Handle basic interactions at shops, restaurants, the gemeente. Read simple Dutch texts with some dictionary help. Ready to start the [inburgeringsexamen preparation](/guides/integration/inburgeringsexamen-preparation-guide-2026/) if needed.

### After Month 6: The Decision Point

At this stage, you have a solid A2 foundation. The question becomes: are free resources enough to keep progressing, or is it time to invest?

If your goal is B1 (comfortable conversation), I strongly recommend adding weekly conversation practice. This can be free (Discord, tandem partner, Dutch friends) or paid (a tutor on Preply). The expats I work with who add just one tutor session per week to their free self-study routine typically reach B1 within another 4-6 months.

## Tips from Expats Who Actually Learned Dutch

Over the years, I have talked to hundreds of expats about their Dutch learning experience. Here are the tips that come up again and again from people who actually reached conversational fluency.

### 1. Stop Letting Dutch People Switch to English

This is the number one obstacle every expat mentions. You try your best Dutch, and the person immediately replies in English. It is well-meaning but devastating for your learning.

**What works:** Start by saying "Ik wil graag Nederlands oefenen" (I would like to practice Dutch). Most people will accommodate you. If they switch to English anyway, gently switch back to Dutch. In shops and restaurants, stick to Dutch even if the interaction would be faster in English.

### 2. Change Your Phone and Social Media to Dutch

This one small change gives you hundreds of micro-exposures to Dutch throughout the day. Suddenly you are reading "Instellingen" instead of "Settings," "Zoeken" instead of "Search," and "Verwijderen" instead of "Delete." It adds up faster than you think.

### 3. Find a Tandem Partner, Not Just a Teacher

A tandem (or language exchange) partner is someone who wants to practice your language while you practice theirs. Websites like Tandem and ConversationExchange connect you with Dutch speakers who want to practice English. You spend 30 minutes speaking Dutch, then 30 minutes speaking English. Free, effective, and you often make a genuine friend in the process.

### 4. Talk to Yourself in Dutch

This sounds silly but it is one of the most effective techniques. Narrate your daily activities in Dutch: "Ik maak koffie. Ik pak een kopje. De melk staat in de koelkast." You practice production without the pressure of a conversation, and you quickly identify gaps in your vocabulary for everyday actions.

### 5. Set a "Dutch Hour" with Your Partner or Housemates

If you live with Dutch speakers (or other learners), agree on one hour per day when everyone speaks Dutch only. Even if conversations are painfully slow at first, the regular practice builds confidence and skill faster than any app.

### 6. Accept Being Bad at It for a While

This is the tip that expats who successfully learned Dutch always mention, and the one that expats who gave up always missed. You will sound like a child. You will make embarrassing mistakes. You will struggle to express thoughts that are effortless in English. This is normal, temporary, and the only path to fluency. The expats who learn Dutch are the ones who tolerate being uncomfortable.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I really learn Dutch for free?

Yes, you can reach a solid A2-B1 level using only free resources. Duolingo provides a full Dutch course at no cost, Delft University offers a free-to-audit course on Coursera, and YouTube channels like Learn Dutch with Bart de Pau provide hundreds of free lessons. The key limitation is that free resources rarely include live conversation practice with a tutor, which most learners need to push past intermediate level.

### How long does it take to learn Dutch with free resources?

With consistent daily practice of 30-60 minutes using free tools, most English speakers reach basic conversational Dutch (A2) within 4-6 months and functional conversational level (B1) within 8-14 months. Free resources often take slightly longer than paid courses because they lack structured feedback and personalized correction, but the timeline is very achievable with discipline.

### What is the best free app for learning Dutch?

Duolingo is the most complete free app for learning Dutch. Its full Dutch course from English covers vocabulary, grammar, listening, and speaking exercises without requiring payment. The free tier includes ads and limited mistakes per session, but the core learning content is fully accessible. For vocabulary building specifically, Clozemaster is an excellent free supplement.

### Is Duolingo enough to learn Dutch?

Duolingo alone will give you a vocabulary foundation and basic grammar, roughly up to A2 level. However, it does not teach you to hold real conversations, understand fast native speech, or handle the nuances of Dutch word order and idioms. I recommend combining Duolingo with listening practice (NOS Journaal in Makkelijke Taal), reading, and eventually conversation practice with a tutor or language partner.

### What level of Dutch do I need for the inburgeringsexamen?

The inburgeringsexamen requires A2 level on the CEFR scale, which covers basic everyday communication. This includes reading, writing, listening, and speaking components, plus knowledge of Dutch society (KNM). While A2 is achievable with free resources, many expats find that exam-specific preparation through a structured course or tutor helps them pass more efficiently. I have a detailed [inburgeringsexamen preparation guide](/guides/integration/inburgeringsexamen-preparation-guide-2026/) that covers everything you need to know.

### Are there free Dutch courses for expats in the Netherlands?

Yes. Many Dutch public libraries (bibliotheken) run free conversation groups through the Taalhuizen program. Some municipalities offer subsidized or free language classes for specific visa categories. Additionally, if you are required to integrate (inburgeren), you may qualify for a DUO loan that covers course costs and can be partially forgiven if you pass within the deadline.

### Can I learn Dutch by watching Dutch TV?

Watching Dutch TV is an excellent supplement but not sufficient on its own. NPO Start offers free Dutch programming, and NOS Journaal in Makkelijke Taal uses simplified language ideal for learners. Start with Dutch subtitles on, then switch to no subtitles as you improve. Combine TV watching with active study for the best results.

### When should I start paying for Dutch lessons?

Consider investing in paid resources when you hit a plateau, usually around A2-B1 level. At this stage, free apps and passive learning become less effective, and you need personalized feedback on pronunciation, grammar errors, and conversation skills. A tutor on a platform like [Preply](https://go.expatnetherlandshub.com/preply?ref=/guides/integration/learn-dutch-free-online-2026/) can identify your specific weak points and accelerate your progress in ways that self-study cannot.

### Is Dutch hard to learn for English speakers?

Dutch is one of the easiest languages for English speakers. The Foreign Service Institute classifies it as a Category I language, meaning it takes roughly 600-750 hours to reach proficiency. Dutch shares a huge amount of vocabulary and grammar structures with English, and word order (while tricky at times) follows patterns that English speakers can learn to predict. The most common challenges are the guttural "g" sound, gendered articles (de/het), and verb placement in subordinate clauses.

### What is the difference between NT2-I and NT2-II?

NT2 Staatsexamen I is at B1 level and is the minimum required for civic integration (inburgering). It tests reading, writing, listening, and speaking at a functional daily-life level. NT2 Staatsexamen II is at B2 level and is required for Dutch higher education and some regulated professions. If you are doing the inburgeringsexamen, you need A2 level — which is actually below NT2-I.
