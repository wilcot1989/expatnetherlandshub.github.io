Rahul and Priya contacted me in October 2024. Rahul had just accepted a position at ASML in Eindhoven — a senior optical systems engineer role, the kind of opportunity that does not come along twice. Priya, a product manager in Bengaluru, was planning to follow once her notice period ended. They had done their research online, of course. They knew the Netherlands was expensive. They knew it rained a lot. They had read the generic "moving to the Netherlands" guides. What they had not found was an honest, specific comparison written for people coming from India — not from the UK or the US.

That gap is why I am writing this article.

I have worked as an expat coach in the Netherlands for the better part of a decade, and in the last few years, Indian nationals have become the single largest group I advise. That reflects a broader trend: India is now the largest source country for non-EU knowledge migrants coming to the Netherlands. ASML's Veldhoven campus alone employs thousands of Indian engineers. Amsterdam's fintech and AI companies recruit heavily from Indian universities. Rotterdam's port and logistics firms have deep ties to Indian shipping operations. The pipeline is enormous, and it is growing.

But the adjustment from India to the Netherlands is unlike almost any other expat move. The cultural distance is large. The practical differences — from how you buy groceries to how you talk to your boss — are significant in ways that are hard to anticipate from a desk in Hyderabad or Pune. This guide tries to give you the honest version, numbers included.

## The Salary Reality: Numbers That Actually Mean Something

Before we go any further, let us deal with the number everyone wants to know. Is moving to the Netherlands worth it financially?

### Gross Salaries: What ASML and Dutch Tech Actually Pay

For senior knowledge migrant roles in 2026, typical gross salaries in the Netherlands look like this:

| Role | Netherlands (gross/year) | India equiv. (gross/year) |
|------|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| Senior software engineer | €65,000–85,000 | ₹20–40 lakh |
| Optical/hardware engineer (ASML) | €70,000–95,000 | ₹25–45 lakh |
| Product manager (tech) | €60,000–80,000 | ₹18–35 lakh |
| Data scientist | €60,000–80,000 | ₹18–30 lakh |
| Finance manager | €65,000–85,000 | ₹20–35 lakh |

At current exchange rates (roughly 1 EUR = 92 INR), €70,000 gross converts to approximately ₹64 lakh. That is already 2–3 times a top Indian private-sector salary for equivalent experience. But that is not the full picture.

### The 30% Ruling Multiplier

The [30% ruling](/guides/finance/30-percent-ruling-netherlands-2026/) is the single most significant financial benefit available to Indian knowledge migrants in the Netherlands. If you were recruited from abroad and meet the salary threshold — €46,107 gross in 2026 — your employer can designate 30% of your salary as a tax-free allowance for up to five years.

What this means in practice:

| Gross salary | Without 30% ruling (approx. net) | With 30% ruling (approx. net) |
|--------------|-----------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| €60,000 | €38,500 | €43,500 |
| €75,000 | €45,200 | €52,000 |
| €90,000 | €51,000 | €60,500 |

That difference can amount to €5,000–10,000 extra per year in your pocket for exactly the same gross salary. Over five years, that is a meaningful sum. Run your specific numbers using the [30% Ruling Calculator](/tools/30-percent-ruling-calculator/) before negotiating your package.

Apply the ruling early — it is not automatic. Your employer must request it from the Dutch tax authority, and if you miss the four-month window from your start date, you lose it permanently.

### Purchasing Power: The Part That Surprises People Most

Here is what the comparison looks like once you account for purchasing power. A Dutch gross salary of €70,000 with the 30% ruling gives you approximately €50,000 net per year. That is around €4,200/month.

After housing (€1,600/month for a decent one-bedroom in Eindhoven), health insurance (€155/month), transport (€80/month for a bike and occasional train), and groceries (€350–400/month), you have roughly €1,500–2,000 per month left over. In India, even on a generous senior salary, that level of monthly surplus is unusual.

And when you send that surplus home? At current rates, €1,000 converts to approximately ₹92,000. That pays for a lot in India.

This is where the sticker shock hits hardest for Indian expats. The Netherlands, particularly in knowledge-migrant hubs, has an extremely tight housing market.

|-----------|--------------------------|--------------------------|

Groceries cost more in absolute terms, but the quality of produce at Dutch supermarkets (Albert Heijn, Jumbo, Lidl) is consistently high. The thing that catches many Indian expats off guard is the lack of cheap domestic help — a subject I come back to in the housing section.

### Healthcare Costs

Everyone moving to the Netherlands must take out mandatory basic health insurance (basisverzekering) within four months of registering. In 2026, this costs roughly:

- **Premium:** €130–175/month depending on provider
- **Annual deductible (eigen risico):** €385 (you pay this before insurance kicks in for most treatments)
- **GP visits:** Covered after deductible
- **Specialist and hospital:** Covered after deductible
- **Dental (adults):** NOT covered in basic insurance

That last point surprises many Indian expats, particularly those used to Indian corporate health plans that include dental. You can add supplementary insurance for dental from around €15–30/month extra.

## Tax: The India-Netherlands Double Tax Treaty

### Dutch Income Tax in 2026

| Bracket | Rate |
|---------|------|
| Up to €75,518 | 36.97% |
| Above €75,518 | 49.50% |

These rates look alarming if you are used to India's 30% top rate. But the comparison is not quite that simple.

**The 30% ruling** brings your effective rate down significantly (see above). For a senior engineer on €75,000 gross with the ruling applied, effective income tax can be in the 24–28% range — lower than the headline Dutch rates suggest.

**The India-Netherlands Double Tax Treaty** means you will not be taxed twice on the same income. India and the Netherlands have had a tax treaty in place since 1988 (updated since). If you remain tax resident in India for part of the year, or have Indian income streams (rental property, FDs, dividends), the treaty determines where and how each source is taxed. It is worth getting advice from an expat tax specialist in your first year — this is one area where a €200 consultation saves you far more than it costs.

**Foreign assets and the 30% ruling:** One often-overlooked benefit is that with the 30% ruling, you can opt out of the Dutch wealth tax (box 3) on your foreign assets. If you have significant investments or property in India, this can be very valuable. The moment the 30% ruling expires, box 3 tax on those assets kicks in, so plan accordingly.

For a full breakdown of the Dutch tax system, see our [Dutch Tax System Guide for Expats](/guides/finance/dutch-tax-system-expats-guide-2026/).

## Work Culture: The Section Every Indian Expat Needs to Read

I am going to spend more time on this section than any other, because work culture is where I see the most difficulty, the most misunderstanding, and — eventually — the most growth among Indian expats in the Netherlands.

### The Flat Hierarchy

Indian professional culture is built on hierarchy. Seniority is respected. Decisions come from the top. You do not challenge your manager in a meeting. You address senior colleagues formally. This is not a flaw — it is a coherent system that functions well within its own logic.

Dutch workplaces run on a fundamentally different model. The "Poldermodel" — a term rooted in the Dutch tradition of cooperative water management — values consensus, individual input, and distributed decision-making. In practice, this means:

- **Your manager will ask for your opinion** — and mean it. Saying "yes, I agree with whatever you decide" is not a good answer.
- **Junior team members speak up in meetings** — sometimes disagreeing with the director. This is not disrespect; it is participation.
- **Titles matter less than expertise.** You might be in a meeting with a VP who asks you directly what you think of their idea. They want to know.

Rahul, from the introduction, told me his first team meeting at ASML felt "anarchic" — eight people, all talking, nobody clearly in charge, a decision being reached that he could not quite follow. Three months later, he understood it was one of the most efficient meeting formats he had encountered.

### Direct Communication: The Dutch Bluntness Factor

This is the point that comes up in every coaching session. The Dutch communication style is direct. When a Dutch colleague says "I don't think that's a good approach," they mean exactly that — no softening, no implicit request for you to infer the criticism from their tone. They are not being rude. They are being efficient.

For Indian professionals, who are typically trained to deliver and receive criticism indirectly — wrapped in positive framing, layered with relational context — this can feel like aggression. It is not. Once you understand the code, it becomes genuinely easier to operate in. You always know where you stand.

The flipside: your own communication style may be misread. When you say "yes" to a request while meaning "I need to think about this further," your Dutch colleagues will hear a firm yes. Being explicit about uncertainty or pushback — even if it feels blunt to you — is valued in Dutch professional settings.

### Work-Life Balance: The 5pm Culture

Dutch workers leave at 17:00. Full stop. Weekend emails are not sent, and if they are, they are not expected to be answered until Monday. Annual leave (typically 25 days) is taken fully. Part-time work — often 4 days a week — is extremely common, including at senior levels.

For Indian expats with backgrounds in companies where 9-to-9 hours are normal and weekend availability is implicit, this can be profoundly disorienting at first. You may feel like you are not working hard enough. You may feel guilty leaving at 5pm when you are in the middle of something. Both feelings pass.

The cultural contract in Dutch workplaces is: do the work well during working hours, and your time outside those hours is yours. Most Indian expats I work with describe this transition as one of the most positive aspects of the move — once they have made peace with it.

### Meeting Culture: Everyone Has a Say

Dutch meeting culture (vergadering cultuur) is famously consensus-driven. Decisions are rarely made by one person; instead, they emerge through a process of collective input. This takes longer than top-down decisions, but implementation tends to be faster because everyone has already bought in.

Key things to know:
- Come prepared with an opinion. Saying "I'll go with whatever the team decides" signals disengagement.
- Silence is not agreement — if you disagree, say so in the meeting, not in the corridor afterward.
- Long meetings with many stakeholders are deliberate. This is how Dutch organisations build alignment.

If you have not already, read our [Dutch Work Culture Guide for Expats](/guides/work/dutch-work-culture-guide-expats-2026/) for more depth on this.

### Part-Time Work: A Concept Worth Understanding

The Netherlands has the highest rate of part-time work in the EU. Around 50% of all Dutch workers work fewer than 35 hours per week, including many senior professionals. For Indian expats — particularly those who have moved as a couple — this creates interesting options.

Priya, once she arrived in the Netherlands and transitioned from her Bengaluru job to a local role, chose to work four days a week for the first year. It gave her time to settle, learn some Dutch, and explore the country. In India, asking for a four-day contract at her level would have been career suicide. In the Netherlands, her manager barely blinked.

## Housing: The Adjustment No One Warns You About

### Dutch Apartments Are Small

This cannot be overstated. A "spacious" apartment in Eindhoven city centre that costs €1,800/month might be 75 square metres. A two-bedroom in Amsterdam for €2,400 might be 80 square metres. If you have moved from a middle-class Indian home — a 3BHK flat in Bengaluru or a proper house in Hyderabad — the size reduction can feel dramatic.

There are reasons for this. The Netherlands is one of the most densely populated countries in the world. Land is scarce. Construction has historically prioritised density. And Dutch interior design culture has evolved to make efficient use of small spaces in ways that genuinely work once you adapt.

### No Domestic Help

This is the adjustment that catches Indian expat families off guard more than almost any other. In India, domestic staff — a cook, a cleaner, sometimes a driver — is affordable and standard for middle-class households. Most Indian professional families, even modestly paid ones, have some form of help at home.

In the Netherlands, domestic help exists but is expensive (€20–25/hour), relatively uncommon, and not the kind of arrangement you fall into naturally. You will cook your own meals, clean your own flat, and do your own laundry. If you are a couple where one person has traditionally managed household logistics, this requires a genuine renegotiation of domestic responsibilities.

### Furnished vs Unfurnished (Kaal)

One Dutch housing quirk that baffles newcomers: many rental properties are rented "kaal" — meaning bare. Not just unfurnished; bare. No light fittings, no kitchen, sometimes no floor covering. You supply everything. This is genuinely standard in Dutch private rental market.

When searching via Funda or Pararius, check whether a property is "gestoffeerd" (carpets and curtains), "gemeubileerd" (fully furnished), or "kaal" (bare). For new arrivals, expat-specific furnished rentals are significantly easier to start with, even if they cost more.

## Food and Social Life: The Indian Community in the Netherlands

### Indian Food Is Well Represented

The good news: you will find Indian food. The quality and availability varies by city, but the Netherlands has a substantial South Asian diaspora, and the infrastructure for Indian life is real.

- **Amsterdam:** Strong selection of Indian restaurants (particularly in De Pijp and Oost), multiple Indian grocery stores
- **Eindhoven:** Growing fast, particularly near Strijp and the ASML corridors — several South Indian restaurants have opened in the last two years
- **Den Haag:** Historically the best Indian food scene outside Amsterdam, partly due to the large Surinamese-Indian community
- **Rotterdam:** Good selection, particularly for North Indian and Bengali cuisine

For groceries, the two main chains worth knowing are **Toko chains** (typically Indonesian-Asian stores that often stock Indian staples) and **Amazing Oriental**, which has branches in Amsterdam, Rotterdam, Utrecht, and Den Haag and stocks a serious range of Indian ingredients — dal, rice varieties, fresh curry leaves, paneer, and spices.

For fresh vegetables and South Asian produce, Turkish and Moroccan greengrocers (groentemarkten) in city centres are often your best bet — cheaper than supermarkets and with better selection of aubergines, okra, bitter gourd, and the like.

### Indian Community and Belonging

Indian expat communities in the Netherlands are active and welcoming, particularly in Eindhoven (where the ASML cluster creates a natural gathering point) and Amsterdam. The major nodes:

- **Diwali celebrations:** Organised annually in several cities, including a large event in Amsterdam
- **WhatsApp and Facebook groups:** "Indians in Netherlands," city-specific groups for Chennai/Bangalore/Mumbai expats — these are genuinely useful for practical advice
- **Professional networks:** IIT alumni groups, Indian Chamber of Commerce Netherlands (ICCN)
- **Cricket:** Yes, there are cricket leagues. This matters to a lot of people.

Priya told me that finding her Tamil expat community in Eindhoven took about a month, and that once she did, the isolation she had felt in her first weeks evaporated quite quickly.

## Practical Challenges: The Honest List

### Dutch Language

You do not need Dutch to do your job. You probably do need some Dutch to function comfortably in daily life. GP appointments, housing contracts, municipal registrations, utility companies, school communications if you have children, interactions with neighbours — all of these become meaningfully easier with even basic Dutch.

Most Indian expats I advise plan to learn Dutch "eventually" and then don't, because English works so well in professional settings. I would gently push back on this. Learning Dutch accelerates your integration enormously, opens up a wider social world, and is genuinely valued by Dutch colleagues and neighbours who notice the effort.

Apps like Duolingo get you started, but structured classes — especially for the grammar — make a significant difference. See our guides on [Dutch language courses](/guides/integration/best-dutch-language-courses-2026/) and [best apps to learn Dutch](/guides/integration/best-apps-learn-dutch-2026/) for specific recommendations.

### Weather: The Biggest Complaint, Every Time

I have never coached an Indian expat who did not mention the weather. Bengaluru, Hyderabad, Chennai, Delhi, Mumbai — even in their most moderate seasons, these cities have more sunlight and warmth than the Netherlands manages in its best summer. The Netherlands averages roughly 1,700 hours of sunshine per year. Chennai averages around 2,800.

What actually gets to people is not the cold. It is the grey. October to March in the Netherlands is overcast, often drizzly, and persistently colourless. There is no dramatic snowfall to make it picturesque. There is just grey sky, grey water, grey pavement, and a slight dampness that never quite goes away.

Practical things that help:
- A good rain jacket and waterproof cycling shoes from day one
- A daylight therapy lamp (daglichtlamp) — widely used in the Netherlands and genuinely effective against the winter low mood
- Embracing cycling in all weather (this sounds unpleasant; it is actually fine once you have the right gear)
- Planning at least one trip somewhere sunny in January or February

### Family Distance and Time Zones

The time zone difference between India and the Netherlands is only +3.5 hours in summer and +4.5 hours in winter. This is genuinely manageable — evening calls with family are practical, and you are not dealing with the 12-hour gap that Indian expats in the US contend with.

What is harder is the physical distance. Amsterdam to Bengaluru is roughly 8.5–9 hours in the air. Flights are available (KLM, Air India, IndiGo, others) and reasonably priced when booked ahead, but getting home for a family wedding, a festival, a health emergency, or simply because you miss your parents requires real planning. Many Indian expats in the Netherlands target two or three trips home per year.

## Should You Make the Move? An Honest Assessment

### Who Thrives in the Netherlands as an Indian Expat

- **Knowledge migrants in tech, engineering, or science** who have a specific role lined up — the professional conditions are excellent and the financial case is strong
- **Couples where both partners are open to working** — the Dutch system rewards dual-income households, and professional opportunities for the accompanying partner are real
- **People who genuinely want to explore Europe** — the Netherlands is a superb base, with quick train connections to Belgium, Germany, France, and the UK
- **Those who value work-life balance and autonomy** — if you have been working 60-hour weeks and want a different way of operating, the Netherlands delivers
- **Indian expats planning to settle for 5+ years** — the 30% ruling, pension building, and housing equity all favour longer stays

### Who Might Find It Harder

- **Families with elderly parents who need regular support** — the distance and flight cost are manageable but emotionally taxing over time
- **People who find cold grey weather seriously affects their mood** — this is not a trivial thing, and it is worth being honest with yourself about it
- **Those expecting a lifestyle comparable to upper-middle-class India** — the spacious home, the domestic help, the cook, the driver — none of that is replicated at a Dutch salary, even a generous one
- **Anyone whose social world is primarily family and longstanding community** — building that from scratch in the Netherlands takes years, and the Dutch can be slow to warm to new friendships (see our guide on [making friends in the Netherlands](/guides/integration/making-friends-netherlands-expats-2026/))
- **Entrepreneurs or those planning to start a business quickly** — the Netherlands is good for startups eventually, but the regulatory environment is slower-moving than Indian private markets
