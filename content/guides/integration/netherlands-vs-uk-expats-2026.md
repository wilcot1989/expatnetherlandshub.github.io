I have one foot in each country. My mother is Dutch, my father is British, and I grew up shuttling between Rotterdam and South London. When I moved to the Netherlands full-time in 2015, I thought I knew exactly what to expect. Ten years later, I still catch myself surprised by the differences — some obvious, some completely invisible until you are living them. This comparison is the article I wish I had before I made the move. Whether you are a British expat considering the Netherlands or comparing both options from somewhere else entirely, here is an honest side-by-side of what life actually looks like in 2026.

## Salaries and Taxes: The Number That Changes Everything

### Gross Salary Comparison

For equivalent skilled roles, gross salaries in the Netherlands are broadly comparable to the UK, with a slight edge in the Netherlands for tech, finance, and engineering. A software engineer in Amsterdam can expect EUR 60,000–85,000; the same role in London pays GBP 55,000–90,000, which at current exchange rates puts them in a similar range. Outside London, UK salaries drop considerably. Manchester, Birmingham, and Edinburgh pay 15–25% less than the capital. Dutch salaries, by contrast, are more geographically consistent — an engineer in Eindhoven earns close to what the same role pays in Amsterdam.

### Dutch Income Tax in 2026

The Netherlands uses a two-bracket system:

| Bracket | Rate |
|---------|------|
| Up to EUR 75,518 | 36.97% |
| Above EUR 75,518 | 49.50% |

On paper, these rates look steep. But the 30% ruling changes the calculation entirely.

### The 30% Ruling Advantage

If you are recruited from abroad as a highly skilled migrant, your Dutch employer can pay 30% of your gross salary as a tax-free allowance for up to 5 years. This is one of the most valuable expat tax arrangements in Europe, and it does not exist in the UK.

| Scenario | Netherlands (with 30% ruling) | UK |
|----------|-------------------------------|----|
| Gross salary | EUR 70,000 | GBP 60,000 (~EUR 72,000) |
| Taxable income | EUR 49,000 (70%) | GBP 60,000 |
| Approximate income tax | EUR 15,700 | GBP 14,500 |
| Social contributions | ~EUR 5,200 | ~GBP 4,500 |
| **Approximate net income** | **~EUR 49,100** | **~GBP 41,000 (~EUR 49,200)** |

At comparable gross salaries, net pay ends up roughly similar — but the 30% ruling gives you a substantial buffer, and its value increases significantly at higher salaries. Run your own numbers with the [30% Ruling Calculator](/tools/30-percent-ruling-calculator/).

### UK Income Tax in 2026

The UK uses a graduated system with more brackets:

| Bracket | Rate |
|---------|------|
| Up to GBP 12,570 | 0% (personal allowance) |
| GBP 12,571–50,270 | 20% (basic rate) |
| GBP 50,271–125,140 | 40% (higher rate) |
| Above GBP 125,140 | 45% (additional rate) |

National Insurance adds a further 8% on earnings between GBP 12,570–50,270, tapering above that. The UK system is less punishing at mid-range salaries but offers no comparable incentive for internationally recruited workers.

## Cost of Living: Day-to-Day Numbers

The Netherlands is moderately more expensive than the UK overall, though the gap narrows considerably once you move outside London.

| Category | Netherlands | UK (London) | UK (Outside London) |
|----------|-------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Meal at mid-range restaurant (2 people) | EUR 70–100 | GBP 70–110 | GBP 50–80 |
| Monthly groceries (single) | EUR 280–380 | GBP 250–380 | GBP 200–300 |
| Coffee (cappuccino) | EUR 3.50–5 | GBP 3.50–5 | GBP 3–4.50 |
| Beer at a bar (0.5L) | EUR 5–8 | GBP 5–8 | GBP 4–6 |
| Monthly public transport pass | EUR 100–160 | GBP 180–250 (Travelcard) | GBP 60–120 |
| Utilities (monthly, apartment) | EUR 160–260 | GBP 150–280 | GBP 130–220 |
| Internet (monthly) | EUR 35–55 | GBP 30–55 | GBP 25–45 |

London and Amsterdam are expensive in broadly similar ways. Outside the capital, however, UK costs fall faster than Dutch costs. A comparable lifestyle in Rotterdam is cheaper than London but somewhat more expensive than Manchester or Edinburgh.

The one area where the Netherlands consistently wins on value is predictability. Energy prices, rental increases, and healthcare costs are more tightly regulated, which makes budgeting easier. The UK has experienced more volatility in energy bills and rents over the past three years.

## Housing: The Rental Market Compared

Housing is the biggest variable in the cost-of-living comparison and the area where your specific location matters most.

### Rent Comparison (Monthly, 2026)

| City | 1-Bedroom (Center) | 2-Bedroom (Center) |
|------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Amsterdam | EUR 1,600–2,400 | EUR 2,000–3,200 |
| Rotterdam | EUR 1,200–1,800 | EUR 1,500–2,400 |
| The Hague | EUR 1,100–1,700 | EUR 1,400–2,200 |
| Eindhoven | EUR 1,000–1,500 | EUR 1,300–1,900 |
| London | GBP 1,800–3,200 | GBP 2,400–4,500 |
| Manchester | GBP 1,000–1,600 | GBP 1,300–2,100 |
| Birmingham | GBP 900–1,400 | GBP 1,100–1,800 |
| Edinburgh | GBP 1,100–1,700 | GBP 1,400–2,200 |

[Amsterdam](/cities/amsterdam/) versus London is the fairest direct comparison: both are high-demand capital-region cities with severe housing shortages, and rents are broadly comparable in EUR/GBP terms. [Rotterdam](/cities/rotterdam/) is meaningfully cheaper than London but more affordable options in the Netherlands disappear if you need to live close to Amsterdam.

### Key Differences in the Rental Market

One thing that surprises many British expats is how bare Dutch rentals can be. Kaal (bare) apartments arrive with no flooring, no light fixtures, no kitchen appliances, and sometimes not even curtains. Budget an additional EUR 2,000–5,000 to make a kaal apartment liveable. UK rentals are almost universally offered furnished or semi-furnished, with white goods and flooring included.

Both markets have strong tenant protections, though the Dutch system has a points-based social housing sector (up to a certain rent threshold) with very long waiting lists — sometimes 10+ years in Amsterdam. The private rental market is where most expats end up.

Buying property is possible in both countries, though the Dutch mortgage market has its own quirks, including the mandatory use of a notary and transfer tax (overdrachtsbelasting) of 2% for primary residences.

## Healthcare: NHS vs Dutch Private Insurance

This is the most structurally different aspect of the two countries, and it catches a lot of British expats off-guard.

### The NHS (UK)

The National Health Service is funded by general taxation and is free at the point of use for UK residents. GP appointments, specialist referrals, hospital stays, surgery, and emergency care all cost nothing directly. Prescription costs are capped at GBP 9.90 per item in England (free in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland). The NHS covers everyone; there is no premium to pay, no deductible, and no insurance paperwork.

The downsides are well-documented: GP waiting times of 2–4 weeks for non-urgent appointments, specialist waiting lists of 3–18 months in many areas, and variation in quality between NHS trusts. Dental care is partially covered but access to NHS dentists has become increasingly difficult.

### Dutch Private Insurance

In the Netherlands, every resident must purchase a basic health insurance policy (basisverzekering) from a private insurer. Key 2026 figures:

| | Amount |
|-|--------|
| Basic monthly premium | EUR 130–175 |
| Annual mandatory deductible (eigen risico) | EUR 385 |
| Children under 18 | Free (covered under parent's policy) |
| GP visits | Covered, no deductible |
| Healthcare allowance (zorgtoeslag) for lower incomes | Up to EUR 150/month |

The Dutch system scores significantly better than the NHS on waiting times. You will typically see a GP within a day or two, and specialist appointments are available within weeks rather than months. Hospital quality is consistently high across the country.

The GP gatekeeper model applies in the Netherlands too — you need a referral to see a specialist — but the process is faster and less bureaucratic than in the UK. The out-of-pocket cost of EUR 385 per year is the main financial risk for healthy adults; beyond that, the insurer covers the rest.

| Factor | Netherlands | UK (NHS) |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| Monthly cost | EUR 130–175 | EUR 0 (tax-funded) |
| Annual deductible | EUR 385 | EUR 0 |
| GP waiting time | 1–3 days | 1–4 weeks |

For British expats, the transition to paid insurance is the most visceral cultural shift. It can feel wrong to pay EUR 160 a month for something that was free at home. In practice, most expats find the Dutch system faster and less frustrating than the NHS has become in recent years — but the cost is real and needs to be factored into your budget.

## Childcare and Education

Childcare costs are high in both countries, but structured differently.

| | Netherlands | UK |
|-|-------------|-----|
| Full-time nursery (under 4) | EUR 1,200–1,800/month | GBP 1,100–1,800/month |
| Government subsidy | Yes (kinderopvangtoeslag, up to 96% of cost) | 15–30 free hours/week from age 3 |
| Primary school | Free (state) | Free (state) |
| International school | EUR 12,000–25,000/year | GBP 12,000–30,000/year |
| University tuition | EUR 2,530/year (EU students) | GBP 9,250/year |

The Dutch childcare subsidy system (kinderopvangtoeslag) is income-linked and can be extremely generous, covering up to 96% of childcare costs for lower-income households. Higher earners still receive a partial subsidy. The administrative process can be complex, but the savings are substantial.

UK childcare is moving toward a universal offer of funded hours, but coverage is still partial and inconsistent across regions. Full-time nursery care for under-threes remains unsubsidized in most cases.

University tuition is a major divergence. If your children are EU/EEA citizens (which they may be if you hold Dutch residency and pursue citizenship), Dutch university fees cap at EUR 2,530 per year. UK students pay up to GBP 9,250 per year at English universities.

## Transport: Bikes, Trains, and the M25

### The Netherlands

The Netherlands is organized around cycling in a way that genuinely transforms daily life. In Dutch cities, the bicycle is not a recreational activity — it is the primary mode of transport. Protected bike lanes are everywhere, cycling infrastructure is integrated into urban planning, and most workplaces have secure bicycle parking. My daily commute in Rotterdam is 25 minutes by bike and costs nothing.

Intercity train travel with NS (Dutch Railways) is frequent and covers the entire country efficiently. Amsterdam to Rotterdam is 40 minutes; Amsterdam to Eindhoven is 75 minutes. Monthly train subscriptions are available, and most employers offer an OV-fiets (shared bicycle) subscription alongside public transport reimbursement.

### The UK

The UK's transport experience depends entirely on where you live. London's Underground is extensive and runs frequently, but the cost is high — a monthly Travelcard for zones 1–2 runs GBP 180–210. Intercity trains can be excellent (London to Manchester in 2 hours on a good day) but are expensive and prone to disruption. Outside major cities, car dependency rises sharply, and many areas have limited or infrequent public transport.

Cycling infrastructure in UK cities has improved but still lags significantly behind Dutch standards. Cycling in London or Manchester is possible but feels more like an adventure than a daily routine.

| Factor | Netherlands | UK |
|--------|-------------|-----|
| Cycling infrastructure | World-class | Improving, uneven |
| Intercity trains | Fast, frequent, reliable | Variable; can be expensive |
| Major city public transport | Good (trams, metro, buses) | Excellent in London; limited elsewhere |
| Car ownership | Less necessary in cities | Often necessary outside major cities |
| Monthly transport pass | EUR 100–160 | GBP 60–250 (varies by city) |

## Work Culture: Directness vs. Politeness

### Dutch Work Culture

Dutch workplaces operate with a flatness that can be disorienting for people used to a more hierarchical environment. Junior employees speak up in meetings, disagree with managers openly, and are expected to contribute rather than simply execute. Feedback is direct — sometimes bluntly so — but it is rarely personal. The Dutch distinction between professional critique and personal attack is strong.

The part-time culture is genuinely distinctive. The Netherlands has the highest rate of part-time employment in Europe. Working a 4-day week (parttime) is common across all levels and is considered a reasonable personal preference rather than a career-limiting move. Fathers working part-time is normal.

Working hours tend to be 9 to 17, and leaving on time is the norm. Sending emails late at night or expecting colleagues to respond on weekends is unusual.

### UK Work Culture

British work culture is more hierarchical and, outside of certain industries, more deferential to seniority. Directness is valued, but softened through politeness conventions that can make it harder to know where you genuinely stand. "That's quite interesting" may mean either genuine interest or polite disagreement — reading the room is a skill.

Long hours are more culturally embedded in the UK, particularly in finance, law, and consulting. Presenteeism (being seen to work long hours regardless of actual output) is more common. The pandemic shifted this somewhat, but the cultural baseline still differs from the Netherlands.

The UK's flexible working legislation has improved over the past decade, and remote work is well-established in many sectors. Part-time senior roles exist but are less normalized than in the Netherlands.

| Paid annual leave (statutory minimum) | 20 days + 8–11 public holidays | 28 days (inc. public holidays) |

| Green space in cities | Extensive | Variable |

| Safety (Global Peace Index 2025 rank) | #14 | #34 |
| Happiness (World Happiness Report 2025) | #6 | #23 |
| English-language media and services | Fully available | Native |

The Netherlands consistently ranks among the world's happiest countries. Factors that contribute to this include strong social safety nets, high trust in institutions, excellent work-life balance, and a widespread sense of civic responsibility. For expats, the combination of an English-friendly environment and high quality of life makes it an unusually accessible place to build a genuinely satisfying life outside your home country.

The UK, despite its current political and economic turbulence, remains a culturally rich, internationally connected country with a strong social fabric. London in particular is one of the most diverse and stimulating cities in the world. For many expats, the familiarity of English as the native language and the cultural proximity to Anglophone countries makes the UK easier to settle into quickly.

## Practical Matters: Money, Banking, and Moving Between the Two

One practical reality for British expats in the Netherlands is the ongoing need to move money between GBP and EUR — whether managing UK pensions, paying UK mortgages, or sending money to family. Exchange rates move, bank fees add up, and the wrong approach can cost hundreds of pounds per year.

The exchange rate is the real mid-market rate, fees are transparent and low (typically 0.3–0.6%), and transfers usually arrive within hours. If you are moving to the Netherlands from the UK and need to transfer savings, pay a UK mortgage while renting in the Netherlands, or receive GBP income in a EUR account, Wise is the most cost-effective option I have found.

For a Dutch bank account, most expats use ING, ABN AMRO, or Bunq (the most international-friendly option). You will need a BSN (citizen service number) to open a bank account in the Netherlands, which requires registration at your local gemeente.

## Visa and Post-Brexit Considerations

Post-Brexit, British nationals are third-country nationals in the EU. This means you need a Dutch residence permit to live and work in the Netherlands legally.

### Main Routes for British Expats

**Highly Skilled Migrant (Kennismigrant):** The fastest route if you have a Dutch employer willing to sponsor you. Processing takes 2–4 weeks. Minimum salary: approximately EUR 5,008/month gross (age 30+) or EUR 3,672 (under 30) in 2026. Your partner receives an open work permit.

**Orientation Year Visa (Zoekjaar):** If you graduated from a top-100 global university in the last 3 years, you can live in the Netherlands for one year while job-hunting, without a job offer.

**Self-Employment/Freelance (Zelfstandige):** Possible but requires demonstrating a business plan and sufficient income. More complex to obtain.

**EU/EEA Family Members:** If your partner is an EU citizen, you may have rights to reside in the Netherlands through their status.

In the UK, the Skilled Worker visa (replacing the old Tier 2) requires a job offer from an approved sponsor, a minimum salary of GBP 38,700 (2026), and sponsorship fees paid by either you or your employer. Processing takes 3–8 weeks.

The Dutch kennismigrant system is faster and more streamlined, partly because recognized sponsors handle most of the paperwork. The UK system involves more documentation but is well-established.

## The Verdict: Who Should Choose Which?

There is no objectively better choice. The right country depends on where you are in your career, your family situation, and what you value in daily life.

### Choose the Netherlands if:

- You qualify for the 30% ruling and want to maximize take-home pay for 5 years
- You work in tech, finance, energy, or international business
- You value cycling-friendly cities, flat hierarchies, and short working hours
- You want fast, high-quality healthcare without long waiting lists
- You prefer a compact, well-connected country where most things work reliably
- You are coming from the UK and want to remain in a highly English-friendly environment
- You have children and want access to the EU education system and lower university fees

### Choose the UK if:

- You want NHS healthcare with no monthly premium and no deductible
- London's specific career opportunities, cultural scene, or international connections are irreplaceable for your work
- You prefer not to deal with a foreign bureaucracy and language for daily life
- Your salary is in the range where the 30% ruling advantage would be modest
- You value the UK's cultural output, media scene, and proximity to the English-speaking world
- You want to buy property in a market you already understand

### The Hybrid Reality

Many British expats spend their 30% ruling years in the Netherlands, building savings and career capital, and reassess at the 5-year mark. Others move to the UK first for the career launch and later relocate to the Netherlands when they have a family and want a different rhythm of life. Both directions are common and neither requires burning bridges.

Whatever you decide, visit both countries, speak to people living the life you are considering, and factor in the specific city rather than the country as a whole. Life in Rotterdam is very different from Amsterdam; life in Manchester is very different from London. The city matters as much as the country.
