I have sent a number of clients to Tilburg over the years — most of them initially resistant to the idea. They had heard of Amsterdam, Utrecht, maybe Eindhoven, but Tilburg? The tenth-largest city in the Netherlands does not get much attention in expat circles, and I have never quite understood why. Once they settled in, most of them told me some version of the same thing: I expected it to be a stopgap. It turned out to be a place I actually want to live.

That is the Tilburg story for expats. Affordable, genuine, under-hyped, and increasingly interesting.

Let me put the numbers on the table first.

Tilburg sits in the province of Noord-Brabant, a region with a distinct identity in the Netherlands. Brabant is warmer in social tone than the Randstad — people make eye contact, strangers start conversations, and the café culture feels less transactional. If you have been living in Amsterdam and found it harder than expected to connect with people, moving to a Brabant city is a different experience.

The city's history is written into its neighbourhoods. Tilburg was the textile capital of the Netherlands through most of the 19th and 20th centuries, and when that industry collapsed in the 1960s and 70s, it left behind enormous industrial buildings that have since been converted into some of the most characterful residential and creative spaces in the country. The TextielMuseum in Oud-Noord is one of the best examples of industrial heritage turned into something genuinely alive — it is a working museum with textile labs, artist residencies, and a shop that sells fabrics made on-site.

### Tilburg University

Tilburg University has around 24,000 students and a strong international faculty, particularly in economics, law, and social sciences. It consistently ranks among the top universities in the Netherlands for teaching quality. If your move to Tilburg is connected to the university — as an academic, researcher, or postgraduate student — you will find a well-developed international staff network and a university town atmosphere that shapes the entire city.

### The ASML and Logistics Cluster

Tilburg sits 20 minutes by train from Eindhoven, which is home to ASML, NXP Semiconductors, and one of the densest clusters of high-tech manufacturing companies in Europe. A number of ASML staff and contractors based in the Eindhoven ecosystem choose to live in Tilburg for the lower rents, commuting by train or car. This has grown the English-speaking professional community in Tilburg noticeably over the past decade.

Tilburg itself also has significant logistics and distribution employers — the city's location near major motorway junctions (A58, A65, A261) makes it a distribution hub. Companies like Amazon, DHL, and various 3PL operators have major operations in and around the city.

### Loonse en Drunense Duinen: Nature You Did Not Expect

One of the most surprising things about living in Tilburg is the proximity to the Loonse en Drunense Duinen — a large nature reserve about 15 kilometres north of the city centre. It is an inland dune system, which sounds like a geographical contradiction in a country famous for flat polderland, but the dunes are real and genuinely striking: rolling sandy hills, heathland, pine forests, and wide open skies.

On a weekend, you can cycle from central Tilburg to the dunes in about 45 minutes. For expats who find Dutch urban life enjoyable but need regular access to proper nature, this is a meaningful quality-of-life factor that Tilburg has over most other cities at this price point.

## Neighbourhoods for Expats

### Centrum — Central, Walkable, Best Amenities

- **Vibe:** The compact city centre, with the Piushaven harbour area, the main shopping streets, and a growing restaurant and bar scene
- **Rent:** €900-€1,400/month (2-bed)
- **Best for:** Singles, couples, people who want everything within cycling distance
- **Pros:** Central location, best restaurant and café concentration, close to Tilburg University campus, the Piushaven waterfront is one of the best outdoor-socialising spots in the city
- **Cons:** Higher rents for Tilburg, some traffic noise, parking expensive

The Piushaven is the standout feature of Tilburg's centre for expats. It is a regenerated canal harbour area — converted warehouses now house restaurants, a café, a cinema, and event spaces along the water. On warm evenings, the terrace culture here is excellent. It took Tilburg residents a while to warm to it when it was redeveloped, but it has become a genuine neighbourhood anchor.

The **Spoorzone**, just south of Tilburg Centraal station, is an ongoing regeneration project transforming former railway yards into a creative and cultural district. The 013 concert venue and several art spaces are already there; more is being added. For expats who care about urban energy and are used to cities that invest in culture, the Spoorzone is worth knowing about.

### Oud-Noord — Character, Former Factories, Creative Energy

- **Vibe:** The former textile factory district north of the city centre — converted mill buildings, independent businesses, a strong neighbourhood identity, and a disproportionate number of artists and designers per square metre
- **Rent:** €850-€1,250/month (2-bed)
- **Best for:** Young professionals, creative workers, academics, anyone who wants an interesting place to live at a reasonable price
- **Pros:** The most architecturally interesting housing stock in Tilburg, the TextielMuseum, independent cafés and studios, genuinely neighbourhood-feeling rather than residential-slab
- **Cons:** Some streets still in transition, cycling to the university takes 20+ minutes, not ideal for families with school-age children

The old factory buildings in Oud-Noord have been converted at varying levels of quality, so it is worth viewing multiple options. The best conversions — high ceilings, industrial windows, solid insulation — are genuinely impressive apartments for the price. The worst are subdivided and under-maintained. Inspect carefully.

### Quirijnstok and the Southern Residential Areas — Quiet, Family-Friendly

- **Vibe:** Quiet, well-maintained residential neighbourhoods south of the city centre. Think leafy streets, detached and semi-detached houses, good schools, and an atmosphere that prioritises practical liveability over urban energy
- **Rent:** €950-€1,400/month (3-bed house)
- **Best for:** Families with children, people who value quiet and space over centrality
- **Pros:** Good schools, quieter streets, more space per euro than Centrum or Oud-Noord, cycling paths to the city centre are well-maintained
- **Cons:** Less walkable to restaurants and cultural venues, more car-dependent for daily errands

The **Wandelbos** area, adjacent to a large urban park of the same name, is particularly popular with families who want greenery on the doorstep. The park itself is well-maintained and used heavily by the local population.

### Reeshof — Modern, Affordable, Car-Oriented

- **Vibe:** A large modern district in the western part of Tilburg, built primarily in the 1980s and 90s. Less architectural character than the older neighbourhoods, but the most affordable rents in the city and good access to the Loonse en Drunense Duinen
- **Rent:** €800-€1,100/month (3-bed house)
- **Best for:** Families or couples on a tight budget, car owners who do not prioritise urban amenity
- **Pros:** Lowest rents, newest housing stock (better insulation and energy efficiency), large supermarkets nearby, close to nature
- **Cons:** Limited character, car ownership makes life considerably easier, public transport connections to the centre are less convenient

Beyond the university and logistics sector, Tilburg's main employers include:

**Technology and Industry:**
- **DAF Trucks** — one of the largest manufacturers in the Netherlands has its headquarters and main factory in Tilburg. A significant employer of technical and engineering staff
- **bol.com** — the Dutch Amazon equivalent has a major distribution centre in Tilburg
- **Fujifilm** — manufacturing and R&D facility in Tilburg

**Healthcare:**

**Public sector and education:**

## Brabant Culture: What Actually Makes Tilburg Different

I want to spend a moment on this because it is genuinely important for whether you will enjoy living here.

Noord-Brabant has a different cultural character from the Randstad. The historical influence of Catholicism, the agricultural and craft traditions, and the distance from The Hague's administrative culture have produced something distinct: people are warmer, less formal, more likely to engage strangers in conversation, and more focused on community and celebration.

**Carnaval** is the most visible expression of this. Tilburg takes carnaval seriously — three days before Lent (usually February), the city goes into full festive mode. The city gets its carnaval name "Krabbegat" (literally "crab hole" — the etymology is disputed and locals love arguing about it). Street processions, live music, elaborate costumes, and the kind of collective good humour that makes you feel like you have accidentally stumbled into a different country. Expats who arrive not knowing what carnaval is in Brabant often describe it as one of the highlights of their time in Tilburg.

**Gezelligheid** — the Dutch concept of warmth and togetherness in shared social space — functions differently in Tilburg than in Amsterdam. In Amsterdam, gezelligheid often happens in small groups you already know. In Tilburg, it can include the table next to you at the café, the people you pass on the cycling path, or the neighbours in your building. It is less guarded.

If you have found Dutch social culture harder to access than expected in a bigger city, Tilburg may recalibrate your experience.

## Transport and Getting Around

Tilburg is a cycling city in the Dutch tradition — flat, well-connected by dedicated cycling infrastructure, and small enough that most of the city is reachable by bike within 30 minutes from the centre. A second-hand bike for €80-€150 from Marktplaats will handle most of your daily transport needs.

**Public transport within Tilburg** is operated by Arriva (bus network). It is functional but not as frequent as you will find in Amsterdam or Rotterdam. Most expats in Tilburg rely primarily on cycling for daily transport and reserve buses or taxis for specific situations.

**Train connections:**
- Tilburg Centraal is on the main intercity line connecting Amsterdam, Utrecht, Den Bosch, Tilburg, and Eindhoven
- Trains to Eindhoven: ~20 minutes
- Trains to Den Bosch: ~20 minutes
- Trains to Breda: ~20 minutes
- Trains to Amsterdam: ~1h25

## The Tilburg Kermis: What You Need to Know Before You Move

The **Tilburg Kermis** runs for ten consecutive days each July and transforms the city centre into one of the largest travelling fairgrounds in Europe. With over 200 attractions covering kilometres of streets, hundreds of thousands of visitors, and a programme that runs late into the night, it is an event that the entire city organises itself around.

For expats this means: plan around it when moving in July. Rental viewings, moving trucks, and parking in the city centre become genuinely difficult during kermis week. If you have flexibility on your move date, aim for before or after. If you cannot avoid it, acknowledge the disruption and enjoy what is, by any measure, an extraordinary spectacle.

## Registering and Getting Your BSN

Once you have found your accommodation, your first administrative task is registering at the gemeente (municipality). For Tilburg, this is the **Gemeente Tilburg** on Stadhuisplein. You will need your rental contract, passport, and proof of address. Registration gives you your BSN (citizen service number), which is required for almost every subsequent Dutch administrative task.

## Is Tilburg Right for You?

Tilburg works best for expats who:

- Have a specific employer connection (the university, DAF, the logistics cluster, or commuting to Eindhoven)
- Want to pay meaningfully less rent than in the Randstad without compromising on quality of life
- Are open to a smaller-scale social scene with a warm local culture
- Value nature access (the Loonse en Drunense Duinen are exceptional) and cycling
- Are interested in a city with a distinct identity rather than a generic expat hub

It works less well for expats who need a large, established international community, prefer cosmopolitan variety in food and nightlife, or whose work requires frequent trips to The Hague or Amsterdam (the train is fine for occasional travel, less so for daily commuting).

## Arriving in Tilburg: First Week Admin

Registration in Tilburg is handled at the Stadswinkel (Stadhuisplein 130). You can book an appointment online through the gemeente website and usually get a slot within 1–2 weeks. Bring your passport, rental contract, and if applicable, your residence permit (if non-EU). Your BSN will be issued on the spot or sent within a few days. With the BSN, open a Dutch bank account — ING and Rabobank both have city centre branches, and Bunq or Wise work well as digital-first options. Sign up for health insurance within four months of registering; Zilveren Kruis and CZ are both headquartered in Tilburg, so there is a local connection if that matters to you, but any Dutch insurer is fine. The [Dutch health insurance guide](/guides/health/dutch-health-insurance-guide-expats-2026/) covers the full comparison. Apply for DigiD as soon as you have your BSN — you will need it for tax filings, toeslagen applications, and accessing Mijn Overheid. The DigiD activation letter takes about two weeks to arrive by post, so submit the application on your first or second day in the city. Do not leave it until you actually need it, because you will definitely need it and the two-week wait will be frustrating.
