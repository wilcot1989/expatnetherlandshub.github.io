Housing is the hardest part of moving to the Netherlands — full stop. The rental market in Amsterdam, Utrecht, and The Hague is among the tightest in Europe. Apartments go within hours. Landlords receive dozens of applications per listing. And as a new arrival without a Dutch credit history, you are starting at a disadvantage.

I have been through this process, and I have helped dozens of expats handle it. These guides give you a realistic picture of what to expect and the practical steps that actually improve your chances.

## The Dutch Rental Market in 2026

The Netherlands distinguishes between social housing (sociale huurwoningen) and private rental. Social housing is heavily regulated, with waiting lists of 10 to 15 years in Amsterdam. As an expat, you will almost certainly be looking in the private rental market, where prices are higher and protections are fewer — though new legislation in 2024 introduced rent regulation for mid-segment housing.

I cover how the point system (woningwaarderingsstelsel) now applies to more properties, what it means for your rent, and how to spot overpriced listings before you sign anything.

## City-by-City Cost of Living

The cost of living varies significantly across the Netherlands. Amsterdam is the most expensive city by a wide margin. Rotterdam and Eindhoven offer more affordable alternatives, often with shorter commutes and better quality of life for families. The Hague is a popular choice for internationally oriented professionals.

I break down average rents, grocery costs, transport, and childcare costs by city so you can compare realistically before you decide where to live.

## Buying Property as an Expat

Buying a home in the Netherlands as a non-resident or recent arrival is possible, but the mortgage rules differ from many countries. Most lenders require a permanent employment contract or at least a year of Dutch tax returns. I cover the full process — from finding a mortgage advisor (hypotheekadviseur) to understanding Dutch property law and the role of the notaris.

## Energy Providers

Choosing an energy provider is one of the first practical decisions you make after signing a lease. The Dutch energy market is deregulated, meaning you choose your own supplier for gas and electricity. Prices, contract terms, and green credentials vary significantly. I compare the main providers on price, contract flexibility, and customer service so you can make an informed choice.
