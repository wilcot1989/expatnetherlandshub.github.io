Three years ago, a client of mine — I'll call her Priya — arrived in Amsterdam after flying in from Bangalore. She had paid a £1,200 deposit two weeks earlier for a "fully furnished apartment" in De Pijp. The address she had was a real street. The photos were convincing. The landlord had replied promptly to every message. When she turned up at the door with two suitcases, a different family answered. They had lived there for six years. No one had ever listed the flat.

Priya had been scammed. The money was gone. She had nowhere to sleep that night.

I think about her every time I see a new client asking whether a listing "seems legit." The Dutch rental market is one of the tightest in Europe, and that pressure creates a perfect environment for fraudsters. When you are searching from abroad, when you have a start date at a new job, when every apartment you inquire about disappears in hours — the temptation to just send the deposit and secure the place is enormous. That is exactly what scammers count on.

This guide is everything I know about how these scams work, how to spot them before they cost you, and what to do if the worst has already happened. I have helped clients work through this after the fact. I would much rather you read this before you need to.

## Why Expats Are the Primary Target

Let me be direct about this: expats are specifically targeted because we are easier to defraud than locals.

Not because we are naive — many of my clients are highly educated professionals. But because we typically meet several of the conditions fraudsters look for:

- **Searching from abroad.** We cannot physically check the property before deciding.
- **Unfamiliarity with Dutch market prices.** It is hard to know whether €1,100 for a Rotterdam studio is a deal or a red flag if you have never rented in Rotterdam before.
- **Time pressure.** Starting a job, a visa countdown, a family move — there is always a deadline that makes us want to resolve the housing question fast.
- **Language barrier.** Legal Dutch documents, Dutch bank account checks, Dutch ownership registries — all feel daunting if you arrived last week.
- **Trusting of authority.** When someone presents a professional-looking contract and a legitimate-sounding agency name, it is not unreasonable to trust it.

The Dutch rental market has an additional complicating factor: genuine scarcity. When there are fifty applicants for one apartment, accepting a listing at face value without investigating feels rational. The waiting-list anxiety is real, and scammers exploit it ruthlessly.

## The 8 Most Common Rental Scams in the Netherlands

### 1. Fake Listings (Phantom Properties)

This is what happened to Priya. The property exists — the address, the street, sometimes even the interior photos are real. But the listing is entirely fabricated. The "landlord" does not own or control the property and has no intention of renting it to you.

How it typically works:

A scammer copies real listing photos from Funda, Pararius, or an estate agent's website, then reposts them on Craigslist, Facebook Marketplace, Kamernet, or international expat forums at a slightly below-market price. They respond quickly, are friendly and professional, and create a sense of urgency ("I have several other serious applicants"). They ask for a deposit to hold the property before you can view it. Once you transfer, they disappear.

**Red flags specific to this scam:**
- Photos that look professionally taken but the price seems low
- Landlord based "abroad" and unable to meet in person
- Requests to pay before viewing

---

### 2. Advance Payment Requests

Slightly different from the phantom property scam — here, there may actually be a property, but the "landlord" demands fees before you have signed anything or seen anything formal.

Common variants:
- "Administrative fee" to process your application
- "Reservation fee" to hold the listing for you while they check your documents
- First month's rent transferred "to show you're serious" before any contract is signed

I have seen this pattern dozens of times. The amounts are often calculated to be just plausible enough — €200 for an admin fee, €300 to reserve — that people pay without questioning it.

**The rule is simple:** in the Netherlands, no legitimate landlord or agent charges you money before you have a signed rental contract. Period. Agencies earn their fee at contract signing, not before.

---

### 3. Identity Theft via Document Collection

This one does not always involve losing money immediately — but the long-term damage can be severe.

A scammer poses as a landlord or letting agent and asks you to submit your full application pack: passport copy, BSN number (if you have one), employer letter, payslips, bank statements. They collect this from multiple applicants, then disappear, selling the identity data or using it themselves to open bank accounts, apply for credit, or commit other fraud.

You may not realise anything has happened until months later when a debt collection letter arrives or your credit record shows accounts you never opened.

**What to send and what not to send:**

You absolutely will need to provide documents to a genuine landlord or agency. The difference is context and timing.

- Do not send full documents to anyone you found on a free classifieds site
- Do not send your BSN number until you have verified the agency is real (check their KvK registration)
- Do not email passport copies to personal email addresses (Gmail, Hotmail, etc.)
- Legitimate agencies have business email domains and KvK-registered addresses

---

### 4. The Phantom Landlord (Third-Party Scam)

This scam involves a "middleman" who presents themselves as managing a property on behalf of an owner who is abroad or unavailable. The story is usually compelling: "The owner is a Dutch expat working in London, they've given me authority to manage viewings and collect the deposit."

Sometimes there is even a property to view. The middleman shows you around (often a friend's flat, or a legitimately rented flat the scammer lives in). Everything seems fine. You sign what looks like a contract, pay a deposit, and later discover that the supposed middleman had no authority at all — the real owner had no idea their flat was being "let."

I had a client — a French engineer moving to Eindhoven — who went through exactly this. He viewed a flat, liked it, paid €1,500 deposit. Two weeks later, the actual owner (who had their flat legitimately rented to someone else) found out their property was being used in a scam. My client lost the money and had to find last-minute accommodation.

**How to protect yourself:** Any legitimate arrangement where an owner uses a manager should be documented with a formal management agreement (beheerovereenkomst). Ask to see it, or ask to communicate directly with the owner to confirm the arrangement.

---

### 5. Bait-and-Switch

You view a genuinely good apartment, you love it, you agree to rent it. Then at the last moment — often after you've given notice on your old place — the landlord says there's been a "problem" with the first property, but they have a similar one available. The replacement is invariably worse, more expensive, in a less desirable location, or all three.

The bait-and-switch relies on you being committed and under pressure. You have already told your employer you've sorted housing. You have already made plans. Saying no and starting the search again feels like too much.

What I tell my clients: any legitimate switch at the last minute should make you very suspicious. A genuine landlord with an administrative issue would typically allow you to withdraw and receive a full refund. If the "replacement" is presented as take-it-or-leave-it, walk away.

---

### 6. Fake Rental Agencies

The Netherlands has plenty of legitimate letting agencies (makelaars and verhuurmakelaars). It also has fraudulent ones that have gone to some effort to look real: a professional-looking website, an office address (sometimes a co-working space), email addresses on a custom domain, even fake reviews.

The typical pattern: you pay a "finding fee" (bemiddelingskosten) to the agency, sign a contract, pay a deposit — and then discover that either the property does not exist, the agency has no connection to the actual owner, or the agency vanishes after collecting money.

**How to verify a rental agency:**

1. Look up their KvK (Chamber of Commerce) number at kvk.nl. Every legitimate business in the Netherlands is registered there.
2. Check whether they are a member of NVM, VBO, or VastgoedPRO — the main professional associations for Dutch estate agents.
3. Google the agency name with "oplichting" (fraud) or "scam" appended.
4. Call the number on their website, not a number given to you in a message.

One important legal note: under Dutch law, letting agents are not allowed to charge a finder's fee (bemiddelingskosten) to tenants for contracts signed after 1 July 2023. If an agency demands such a fee, it is either acting illegally or is not a legitimate agency. Either way, stop.

---

### 7. Deposit Theft

You have done everything right: you viewed the property, you signed a contract, you paid your deposit to a seemingly legitimate landlord. Then, at the end of your tenancy, the landlord simply refuses to return it.

Deposit theft is not always as dramatic as the phantom property scam, but it is remarkably common — and it happens to people who did nothing wrong at the start. Landlords may cite fictitious damages, make up cleaning costs, or simply ignore messages, hoping you will give up, particularly if you have already moved abroad.

This is why documentation at move-in is so important. Under Dutch rental law, a landlord cannot legally deduct from your deposit without evidence that you caused damage beyond normal wear and tear.

**What to do to protect your deposit:**

- Before moving in, photograph every room, every wall, every fixture, every appliance. Date-stamp the photos.
- Ask for a signed incheck report (inspectierapport) at the start of the tenancy.
- Keep copies of all communication with your landlord.
- If a deposit dispute arises, you can use the Huurcommissie (Rent Tribunal) — a free, legally binding dispute resolution service for eligible tenancies.

---

### 8. Illegal Sublet Scams

Someone who is renting a property themselves advertises it on Airbnb, Facebook, or expat forums and rents it out again — without their landlord's knowledge or permission. You pay them, move in, and a few weeks later the real landlord discovers the situation and legally evicts you. You lose both your temporary home and the money you paid.

This is illegal subletting, and it is far more common than most people realise — especially in Amsterdam, where housing pressure is so intense that some tenants see it as a financial opportunity.

I have seen this happen with furnished short-term rentals and with longer-term setups. The warning signs are subtler than with other scams: the person renting it to you may genuinely live there and may not even fully understand they are breaking their contract.

**What to ask before signing:**

- "Do you own this property or are you a tenant?" Ask directly.
- "May I see the deed or proof of ownership, or the name of your landlord?"
- If they are a tenant subletting, ask to see written permission from the landlord.

## The Red Flags Checklist

Print this out, save it to your phone, send it to anyone you know who is searching for housing in the Netherlands.

### Immediate Danger Signs (Walk Away Now)

- [ ] Price is significantly below market rate for the area — more than 20% below comparable listings
- [ ] Landlord is based abroad and cannot arrange an in-person viewing
- [ ] You are asked to pay any amount before signing a contract
- [ ] Payment is requested via bank transfer to a personal account, cash, cryptocurrency, or a service like Western Union
- [ ] You are told other applicants are ready to pay immediately and you must decide today
- [ ] The landlord refuses to show you the property in person
- [ ] The listing photos appear on another address when you do a reverse image search (try Google Images or TinEye)
- [ ] Contract is sent in a format that cannot be easily verified (e.g., a low-quality PDF with no agency header)
- [ ] The agency has no KvK registration or cannot provide one

### Serious Warning Signs (Proceed With Extreme Caution)

- [ ] Landlord communicates only via WhatsApp or personal email
- [ ] Price seems like a genuine bargain for the location
- [ ] Landlord avoids your questions about their ownership or agency registration
- [ ] You are asked for unusual documents (e.g., full passport scan and bank statements before any viewing)
- [ ] The agency charges a finder's fee (bemiddelingskosten) — illegal in the Netherlands since July 2023
- [ ] Contract wording is vague about deposit return conditions
- [ ] Landlord pressures you to sign quickly without reading the contract

### Lower Risk but Worth Checking

- [ ] Agency or landlord name does not appear in Kadaster records for the property
- [ ] Listing has been online for an unusually long time in a competitive market (may mean others already spotted something wrong)
- [ ] Photos in the listing do not match the address on Google Street View

## How to Verify a Landlord or Property

### Step 1: Check the Kadaster

The Kadaster is the Dutch Land Registry — it records who owns every property in the Netherlands. You can use it to check whether the person or agency renting to you actually has any connection to the property.

**How to do it:**

1. Go to [kadaster.nl](https://www.kadaster.nl)
2. Search for the property address
3. Pay the small fee (approximately €3–5) to view the ownership record
4. Compare the registered owner's name with the name on your rental contract or the person you are dealing with

If they do not match, ask directly and in writing before proceeding. A legitimate landlord will have a clear explanation (they may manage the property for a family member, for example) and will not object to you asking.

### Step 2: Verify the Agency's KvK Registration

If you are dealing through an agency, every legitimate Dutch business is registered with the Kamer van Koophandel (Chamber of Commerce).

1. Go to [kvk.nl](https://www.kvk.nl)
2. Search for the agency name or their stated KvK number
3. Check that the registered address matches the one on their communications
4. Verify that their registered business activity includes property management or rental

An agency that cannot provide a KvK number or whose registration does not match their stated details is a serious warning sign.

### Step 3: Do a Reverse Image Search on Listing Photos

Many fake listings use stolen photos from legitimate listings. Before engaging with a listing, download two or three of the photos and run them through Google Images or TinEye.

If the same images appear at a different address — or on a listing in a completely different country — you are almost certainly looking at a scam.

### Step 4: Verify the IBAN

Before paying anything, ask for the full IBAN of the account you would be paying to. Dutch IBANs start with NL and are followed by two check digits and a bank code.

A few useful checks:
- If the IBAN is not Dutch (starts with DE, GB, FR, etc.), be very suspicious — why is a Dutch landlord or agency using a foreign bank account?
- Use the bank number (the four letters after the check digits) to verify which Dutch bank it belongs to
- A personal IBAN (rather than a business account) for an agency is a red flag

Not all places to search for housing carry the same level of risk.

### Generally Lower Risk

| Kamernet.nl | Verified landlord profiles; dedicated student/young professional market |

## What to Do If You Have Been Scammed

If the worst has happened, act fast. The first 24 hours matter enormously.

### Immediate Steps

**1. Contact your bank immediately.**

If you transferred money and it was a SEPA bank transfer, you have a window — usually 24 hours, sometimes a little longer — to request a recall (terugroeping). Call your bank directly, do not rely on their app or website for this.

Be honest about what happened. Banks vary in how much they can recover, but the sooner you act, the better your chances.

**2. Preserve all evidence.**

Do not delete anything. Take screenshots of:
- The original listing
- All messages with the "landlord" or "agency"
- Payment confirmations
- Any contracts or documents you received

If you can, also note down the URL of the listing, any phone numbers used, and any email addresses.

**3. File a police report (aangifte bij de politie).**

You can file a report online at [politie.nl](https://www.politie.nl) or in person at your local police station. You will need your evidence. The police report is also important for any subsequent insurance claims or bank disputes.

Filing a report feels like a lot of effort when you are already stressed and upset, but it matters — both for your own case and because patterns in reports help police identify active scammers.

**4. Report to Fraudehelpdesk.**

The Fraudehelpdesk (fraudehelpdesk.nl) is a national reporting centre for fraud. They cannot get your money back, but they maintain a database that alerts other potential victims and shares information with police. Your report may prevent the same scammer taking money from someone else.

**5. Report the listing to the platform.**

Whether the listing was on Funda, Pararius, Kamernet, Facebook, or anywhere else — report it immediately so it can be removed. Most platforms have a "report this listing" option.

### Longer-Term Steps

- **Contact your home contents insurer** — some policies cover certain types of fraud. Worth checking.
- **Monitor your identity** — if you sent personal documents before discovering the scam, be vigilant for identity fraud. Consider placing a fraud alert with major credit bureaus.
- **Legal advice** — if the amount is significant (over €1,000), speaking with a legal adviser is worthwhile. Some municipalities have a free Juridisch Loket (legal desk) for residents.

## Real Situations I Have Seen With Clients

I want to include some specific examples — with details changed to protect privacy — because abstract warnings are easier to dismiss than real stories.

**The "I'm a professor in Edinburgh" listing.**
A client searched for housing in Amsterdam and found a beautiful two-bedroom in Oud-Zuid, €1,400 per month — very low for that area. The "landlord" explained via email that he was a Dutch academic temporarily working in Scotland, and that he would post the keys by registered mail once a deposit was paid. The email was well-written, professional, and included a convincing story about why he could not meet in person. My client nearly paid. A quick Kadaster check showed the flat was owned by a housing association, not a private individual. No academic. No flat.

**The viewing that was not the flat.**
A client was shown a flat in Rotterdam by a person who said he was the landlord's son, managing the property during his father's illness. The flat was lovely. He paid €800 deposit. Three weeks later, when he arrived with his belongings, the actual owner — who had the flat listed with a legitimate agency — had no idea any of this had happened. The "son" had simply had a key to the communal building entrance and had shown a flat in the same block that happened to be empty between tenants.

**The agency that charged a fee.**
A client was approached by an agency for a listing in Utrecht. The contract was professional, the flat was real, but the agency charged a €650 "bemiddelingskosten." My client asked me about it. I advised him that this fee has been illegal since July 2023. He raised it with the agency; they became evasive. He walked away. The flat was likely real, but an agency willing to charge an illegal fee is an agency willing to cut other corners.

**The deposit that never came back.**
A client rented a flat in Leiden for two years without incident — real landlord, real contract, legitimate tenancy. At the end of the tenancy, the landlord refused to return the €1,200 deposit, claiming there was damage to a bathroom wall. My client had no photos. The landlord had no evidence of the damage either. They went to the Huurcommissie. Without a move-in inspection report, the case was harder to resolve. This is why I always say: photograph everything on day one.

## Building a Scam-Proof Search Process

After years of helping clients find housing, here is the process I now give to every new client before they start searching:

1. **Set a market rate anchor.** Before looking at any listings, spend an hour researching typical prices on Funda and Pararius for the area and property type you want. Anything more than 15-20% below that anchor deserves extra scrutiny.

2. **Only engage with listings where you can do an in-person viewing.** If you are genuinely abroad, request a live video call where the person walks through the flat in real time and shows you proof of identity and ownership on camera.

3. **Run the Kadaster check before you fall in love with a property.** It takes ten minutes and costs less than a coffee. Do it early, not after you are emotionally committed.

4. **Never pay anything before signing.** Not a reservation fee. Not an admin fee. Not a holding deposit. Nothing. Sign first, pay after.

5. **Pay only by IBAN bank transfer to a business account.** Keep the transfer description clear (e.g., "deposit apartment [address]"). Never cash, never crypto, never money transfer apps.

6. **Read the contract.** I know it can feel tedious if your Dutch is limited, but a quick translation via DeepL of the key clauses — especially the deposit return conditions and the notice period — takes 20 minutes and could save you thousands. See our guide on [Dutch rental contract rights](/guides/legal/dutch-rental-contract-rights-expats-2026/) for a full breakdown of what to look for.

7. **Photograph everything at move-in.** Date-stamp your photos. Keep them somewhere secure, not just on your phone.

## A Final Word

I do not want to make the Dutch rental market sound like a minefield. It is not. The majority of landlords are legitimate. The majority of listings are real. Hundreds of thousands of expats find housing in the Netherlands every year without incident.

But the ones who do get scammed are not careless or naive — they are people under pressure, making fast decisions in an unfamiliar system. The best protection is not scepticism of everything, but a clear, consistent process: verify ownership, never pay before signing, and always view in person.

If you are currently searching and something feels off about a listing — trust that instinct. Ask more questions. Take more time. The right place will still be there tomorrow. A scam counts on you not waiting.

Good luck with your search. I genuinely hope this guide means you never need the "what to do if scammed" section.
