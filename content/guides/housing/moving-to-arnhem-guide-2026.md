I have been placing expat clients in Dutch cities for over a decade, and Arnhem is consistently the one that surprises people most. They come expecting a quiet, unremarkable regional city somewhere in the east, and they find something that is harder to categorise: a place where forested heathland begins where the suburbs end, where a fashion quarter has grown organically out of a deprived neighbourhood, and where the shadow of one of the Second World War's most famous battles still shapes how the city thinks about itself.

Arnhem sits on the Rhine (here called the Neder-Rijn), on the western edge of the Veluwe — the largest continuous nature reserve in the Netherlands. It is not a city anyone moves to by accident. People come because of a job at Engie or Rijnstate, because they want the Randstad within train range without paying Randstad prices, or because they have done their research and realised that living next to 900 square kilometres of national park while being 1 hour 20 minutes from Amsterdam is not a combination you find easily.

The **Veluwe** is Arnhem's defining advantage. Most Dutch cities have a park. Arnhem has a national park. The Hoge Veluwe National Park — 5,400 hectares of heathland, forest, and drifting sand dunes — is within cycling distance of the city centre. The park includes the Kröller-Müller Museum, which holds the second-largest Van Gogh collection in the world and one of Europe's most significant outdoor sculpture gardens. On weekdays outside peak summer, you can cycle through it in near-silence.

The **Battle of Arnhem** in September 1944 — Operation Market Garden, immortalised in the film *A Bridge Too Far* — is not just a historical footnote here. It is a lived part of the city's identity. The Airborne Museum at Hartenstein in nearby Oosterbeek is one of the finest Second World War museums in Europe. The John Frost Bridge, rebuilt after being destroyed in the battle, still carries traffic across the Rhine every day. Arnhem was heavily bombed and rebuilt, which is why the city centre architecture is largely post-war rather than historic — but the surrounding countryside and villa-belt neighbourhoods survived, giving the city its green, spacious character.

**ArtEZ University of the Arts** has put Arnhem on the international fashion map. The university's Fashion & Design programme has produced a disproportionate number of Dutch designers, and graduates have set up studios and boutiques throughout the Klarendal neighbourhood. The annual Arnhem Mode Biennale brings international fashion attention. If you are in a creative field, this matters — it gives Arnhem a distinct professional identity you do not find in a pure corporate or academic city.

## Neighbourhoods for Expats

### Centrum — Practical, Central, Post-War Character

- **Vibe:** The rebuilt city centre, pedestrian-friendly shopping streets, Koningsplein square, the covered market, and good access to the main station
- **Rent:** €950-€1,400/month (2-bed)
- **Best for:** Singles and couples who want to walk everywhere and be close to the station
- **Pros:** Everything within reach, excellent transport links, Markt and Koningsplein lively in good weather, direct cycling access to Sonsbeekpark
- **Cons:** The architecture is largely 1950s-1970s and lacks the canal-street character of Amsterdam or Leiden, some streets feel commercial rather than residential, higher rents for what you get

Arnhem Centrum is functional and convenient without being the most beautiful Dutch city centre. The post-war rebuild gave it wide streets and practical shopping infrastructure. What it lacks in historic charm it compensates with good transport — the Arnhem Centraal station is one of the largest in the Netherlands and connects you to Amsterdam, Utrecht, Germany, and Nijmegen all from the same building.

### Klarendal — Creative, Up-and-Coming, Exceptional Value

- **Vibe:** Former working-class neighbourhood turned creative quarter, ArtEZ influence throughout, independent boutiques and ateliers, colourful street art
- **Rent:** €850-€1,250/month (2-bed)
- **Best for:** Expats in creative industries, young professionals who want character over convenience, budget-conscious singles
- **Pros:** The most interesting neighbourhood in Arnhem, excellent value for its character, fashion quarter gives it an identity, independent cafés and shops, cycling distance to Centrum (15 min) and Sonsbeekpark (5 min)
- **Cons:** Still gentrifying — some parts feel rough around the edges, not everyone's aesthetic, less practical day-to-day than Centrum

Klarendal has gone through a deliberate transformation. When ArtEZ fashion graduates began setting up studios here in the 2000s, the neighbourhood was one of the city's most deprived. The mix of affordability and space attracted creatives, and the result is now a proper fashion and design quarter with a genuine street-level energy. The **Klarendalseweg** is its main artery — walk it on a Saturday morning and you will see the boutiques and coffee spots that have made this Arnhem's most talked-about area.

### Sonsbeek — Green, Affluent, Family Favourite

- **Vibe:** Leafy villa neighbourhood surrounding Sonsbeekpark, established residential character, quiet streets with independent food shops
- **Rent:** €1,100-€1,700/month (2-bed), houses €1,600-€2,400
- **Best for:** Families with children, professionals who want calm and green surroundings, those who can afford slightly higher rents for quality of life
- **Pros:** Sonsbeekpark on your doorstep (one of the most beautiful parks in the Netherlands), good schools, cycling distance to both Centrum and the Veluwe edge, quiet and safe, excellent housing stock
- **Cons:** Higher rents, less nightlife and neighbourhood café culture than Klarendal, can feel quiet for younger singles

**Sonsbeekpark** is the centrepiece here — a 90-hectare park with waterfalls, sculpture trails, and a famous cascade. It connects northward into the Arnhemse Bovenbiezen, a nature area that ultimately links to the Veluwe. Living in the Sonsbeek neighbourhood means you are a 5-minute walk from this park and a 15-minute cycle from the main station. For families, this combination of green access and practical transport is hard to beat.

### Presikhaaf — Affordable, Multicultural, Practical

- **Vibe:** Post-war housing estate east of the centre, predominantly Dutch working-class and immigrant communities, practical and affordable
- **Rent:** €800-€1,150/month (2-bed)
- **Best for:** Budget-conscious expats, those prioritising affordability over neighbourhood character
- **Pros:** The cheapest rents in central Arnhem, good bus and cycling connections, large supermarkets nearby, genuinely practical for families who want space at a lower price
- **Cons:** Limited neighbourhood identity, architecturally plain, not the setting most expats have in mind when they picture Dutch city life

Presikhaaf is not glamorous, but it is functional. If you need a 3-bedroom apartment on a budget, this is where you will find it. The shopping centre is one of the larger suburban centres in the region, and public transport links to the centre are good. For expats who spend most of their time outside the city anyway — at work in a business park or exploring the Veluwe — it provides a clean, affordable base.

### Velp — Suburban, Green, Village Feel

- **Vibe:** Technically its own municipality (part of Rheden), but effectively the eastern suburb of Arnhem, villa-street character, quiet and family-oriented
- **Rent:** €950-€1,400/month (2-bed), houses more common and better value than in the city
- **Best for:** Families who want a house rather than an apartment, those who value quiet over urban proximity
- **Pros:** Genuinely beautiful street character with large villas and tree-lined roads, direct train and bus to Arnhem Centraal (5-10 min), entry to the Posbank heathland (part of the Veluwe) within 20 minutes by bike, lower density and more space than Arnhem proper
- **Cons:** Limited nightlife and restaurant options locally, requires a commute for most amenities, feels suburban rather than urban

Velp is where Arnhem-based expats with children often end up after the first year. The combination of house space, garden access, very short train connection to the centre, and almost immediate access to Veluwe nature is genuinely compelling for families. It is quieter than anything in the city proper, which for some people is exactly the point.

## The Main Employers

Arnhem is the provincial capital of Gelderland and functions as a regional corporate and public sector hub. The employer base is more diverse than a pure university city like Groningen or a startup hub like Eindhoven.

**Engie Netherlands (formerly GDF Suez):** One of the largest employers in the city, with its Netherlands headquarters here. Engie employs several thousand people in Arnhem across energy management, technical operations, and support functions. A significant number of international staff work here, making Arnhem more English-friendly in corporate settings than its population size would suggest.

**Arcadis:** The global engineering and consultancy firm has deep roots in Arnhem — it was founded here as Heidemij in 1888. The Arnhem office focuses on infrastructure, environmental services, and urban planning. International engineers and project managers regularly relocate here for project work.

**Rijnstate Hospital:** One of the largest non-academic hospitals in the Netherlands, with around 4,000 staff. International medical professionals — doctors, nurses, researchers — are a consistent relocation group. The hospital has an active international recruitment programme for specialists.

**Provincie Gelderland and municipality:** The provincial government and city administration together employ a large number of public sector workers. Less relevant for most expats, but worth knowing as context for the professional character of the city.

**Logistics and distribution:** Arnhem sits on a major logistics corridor connecting Rotterdam to Germany. Several large distribution and logistics operations are based in the wider metropolitan area — DSV, Ceva Logistics, DB Schenker all have facilities nearby. This creates a consistent stream of logistics and supply chain professionals relocating here.

**ArtEZ University of the Arts:** Employs academic staff across its programmes in Arnhem, and the fashion and design cluster it has generated creates a secondary employer base of studios, agencies, and boutiques in Klarendal.

## Nature and Outdoor Life

This is where Arnhem separates itself from almost every Dutch city of comparable size.

**Sonsbeekpark:** The large park directly adjacent to the Sonsbeek neighbourhood, with its famous 18th-century cascade, sculpture trail, and walking paths. On a summer evening it is full of Arnhemmers of every age cycling, running, and sitting on the grass. It connects northward into the Arnhemse Bovenbiezen nature area, which in turn connects to the Veluwe.

**Veluwe National Park:** The main event. At 900 square kilometres, the Veluwe is the largest continuous nature reserve in the Netherlands — a mosaic of ancient forest, heathland, drifting sand dunes, and wetlands. The Hoge Veluwe National Park, enclosed within the larger Veluwe area, requires an entrance fee but provides access to 5,400 hectares of protected heathland and forest, including the Kröller-Müller Museum. The museum's permanent collection includes 90 Van Gogh paintings and one of Europe's most significant outdoor sculpture gardens. Most Arnhem residents cycle there on a Sunday — it is that close.

**Openluchtmuseum (Dutch Open Air Museum):** Located on the northern edge of Arnhem, this is one of the most visited museums in the Netherlands — a 44-hectare outdoor museum with historic Dutch buildings, windmills, farms, and workshops from across the country's history. It sounds niche; it is genuinely remarkable. Many expat families end up visiting multiple times with guests.

**Burgers' Zoo:** One of the most respected zoos in Europe, also on the northern edge of the city, known for its large naturalistic habitat enclosures. For families with children, having this 10 minutes by bike is not nothing.

**Cycling in and around Arnhem:** Arnhem has very good cycling infrastructure within the city, and the Veluwe trail network offers hundreds of kilometres of off-road cycling routes. The [cycling in the Netherlands guide](/guides/daily-life/cycling-netherlands-expat-guide-2026/) covers everything you need to know about getting started with Dutch cycling culture.

## Transport

**Train:** Arnhem Centraal is an important rail junction — not just for domestic routes but also for international trains to Germany. Intercity Direct trains to Amsterdam run every 30 minutes and take 1 hour 20 minutes. To Utrecht it is around 50 minutes; to Nijmegen 12-15 minutes. The station building itself, completed in 2015, is one of the most architecturally striking in the Netherlands. International services: direct connections to Düsseldorf and Oberhausen make Arnhem practical for expats with work in Germany.

**Bus:** Arnhem has an extensive urban bus network covering all major neighbourhoods. Regional buses connect to Velp, Oosterbeek, the Veluwe villages, and surrounding municipalities. The main bus terminal is integrated with the train station.

**Car:** For those who drive, Arnhem is on the A12 (connecting Rotterdam and Germany), the A325, and the A50. The city is easy to drive in compared to Amsterdam or Rotterdam — though commuter-hour congestion on the Rhine bridges is a regular feature.

**Cycling:** Within the city, cycling is the dominant mode of transport. Most Arnhem residents cycle daily, and the infrastructure is designed accordingly. For Veluwe access, a decent bicycle is a genuine necessity, not a luxury.

## Practical Checklist for Moving to Arnhem

Here is what the first few weeks look like in practice:

**Before you arrive:**
- Secure temporary housing via Kamernet or a relocation agency (short-term furnished rentals are available in Centrum and Klarendal)
- Open a Dutch bank account (many banks allow online pre-registration)
- Arrange health insurance to start from day one of arrival
- Transfer your deposit and first month's rent in advance — use [Wise](https://wise.com/invite/ihpc/willemt52) to avoid bank conversion fees

**First week:**
- Register at Gemeente Arnhem — book an appointment online; you need your passport, rental contract, and proof of address
- Receive your BSN number (usually same-day or within a few days)
- Register with a huisarts (GP) — Arnhem has several English-speaking practices, particularly in and around Sonsbeek and Centrum

**First month:**
- Arrange a Dutch SIM card — see the [best SIM card guide for expats](/guides/daily-life/best-sim-card-netherlands-expats-2026/) for current options
- Buy a bicycle — second-hand is fine for city use; expect to pay €80-€150 at a local fietsenwinkel or via Marktplaats
- Explore the Veluwe on a weekend — cycle north from the Sonsbeek edge and keep going

**Ongoing:**
- Consider a public transport OV-chipkaart for train and bus travel — cheaper than buying single tickets
- Look into whether you qualify for the 30% ruling if you are a highly skilled migrant — it significantly reduces your tax liability

## The Battle of Arnhem: What Every Expat Should Know

I mention this not as a tourist tip but because it genuinely shapes how the city presents itself and how locals feel about international visitors. The Battle of Arnhem in September 1944 was one of the bloodiest and most dramatic Allied operations of the Second World War. British and Polish paratroopers attempted to hold the Rhine bridge while ground forces advanced — they held it for nine days against overwhelming German resistance before being overwhelmed. The city was subsequently largely destroyed in a German retaliatory evacuation order.

The result is a city that has a complex, deep relationship with its wartime past. The **Airborne Museum Hartenstein** in Oosterbeek (10 minutes by bike from Arnhem) is exceptional — interactive, emotionally powerful, and genuinely useful for understanding the local identity. The annual **Airborne Remembrance Ceremony** in September draws tens of thousands of visitors and British veterans' families to the Oosterbeek War Cemetery. For expats from the UK especially, this connection to shared history is something residents take seriously and are glad to share.

## Final Assessment

Arnhem is not for everyone. If you need to be in Amsterdam three days a week, the 1h20 train starts to accumulate. If you want the full Dutch canal-city aesthetic, Arnhem's post-war centre is not Leiden or Haarlem.

But for expats working at the right employers, or for those who have looked at the Dutch property market and decided they are not willing to pay Amsterdam prices for a small flat, Arnhem offers something genuinely attractive: affordable, green, surprisingly culturally interesting, with one of Europe's best national parks as an immediate neighbour.

I have placed clients here who came for a two-year assignment and bought property. The Veluwe does that to people.
