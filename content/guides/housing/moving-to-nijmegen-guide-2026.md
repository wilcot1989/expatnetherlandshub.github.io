The first thing people ask when I mention Nijmegen to clients looking at Dutch cities is: where exactly is that? It is a fair question from someone who has only heard of Amsterdam, Utrecht, and The Hague. The second question, once they have looked it up, is usually: why does no one talk about it more?

Nijmegen sits in the far east of the Netherlands, on the banks of the Waal river, close to the German border. It is the oldest city in the Netherlands — Roman soldiers were stationed here in the first century AD, and the city has been continuously inhabited for longer than almost anywhere else in the country. There is a hill in the middle of the city centre, the Valkhof, from which you can look out over the wide sweep of the Waal and, on a clear day, see into Germany. This is unusual for the Netherlands, a country that is mostly flat and has no shortage of modesty about its physical geography.

More practically: Nijmegen is the home of Radboud University and Radboud University Medical Centre, one of the top research hospitals in Europe. NXP Semiconductors has a major R&D facility here. The Novio Tech Campus is a growing biomedical and technology cluster. The city has a student population of around 30,000, which keeps it younger and more energetic than its regional setting might suggest, and it is substantially cheaper than the Randstad cities without requiring you to move to the far north of the country.

I have placed a number of clients in Nijmegen over the years — medical researchers, engineers, a few people who simply wanted affordable space and a good quality of life with a reasonable train to Amsterdam. Almost all of them have stayed longer than they planned.

## Why Nijmegen?

A few facts worth knowing before we go into the detail.

Nijmegen was awarded **European Green Capital** in 2018, which is not a marketing label — it reflects genuine investment in cycling infrastructure, river ecology, and parks. The city has one of the highest cycling rates in the Netherlands, which is a high bar. The Ooijpolder, a nature reserve east of the city, begins almost at the edge of the urban area, and the Waal floodplains offer a kind of spacious, flat-water scenery that is genuinely beautiful on a good day.

The university connection means the city skews young. Radboud University has around 23,000 students, many of them international, and Radboud UMC employs researchers and clinicians from across the world. The result is a city that is more English-friendly than its size and location would suggest. In the neighbourhoods around the university, you can go an entire day without needing to speak Dutch.

For expats who need nature access to feel sane in a Dutch city, Nijmegen is genuinely one of the best options in the country.

## Neighbourhoods for Expats

### Centrum — Historic, Walkable, Lively

- **Vibe:** The elevated old city centre, with the Valkhof hill, Roman ruins, brick streets, and a compact but well-developed shopping, restaurant, and bar scene
- **Rent:** €1,050-€1,600/month (2-bed)
- **Best for:** Singles, couples without children, those who want to walk everywhere and live inside the city's character
- **Pros:** Everything within reach on foot, the best restaurants and bars, direct access to the Waal riverfront and Valkhof Park, strong cycling connections to all other parts of the city
- **Cons:** Highest rents in Nijmegen, some street noise near the nightlife concentration, limited parking, older buildings with variable insulation

Centrum is where you feel Nijmegen most clearly. The Grote Markt is the main square — lively with market days, outdoor café terraces in summer, and the usual Dutch streetlife. The Kronenburgerpark sits at the edge of the old city walls, a well-maintained green space a five-minute walk from the centre. The view from the Valkhof itself, looking out over the Waal, is one of the better urban panoramas in the Netherlands.

### Bottendaal — Young Professionals, Character, Great Cafés

- **Vibe:** A neighbourhood of small streets, independent shops, canal-side paths, and a café culture that draws the young professional crowd. Often compared in atmosphere to Amsterdam's Pijp or Utrecht's Wittevrouwenveld, but cheaper
- **Rent:** €1,000-€1,500/month (2-bed)
- **Best for:** Young professionals, academics, couples who want neighbourhood character over pure centrality
- **Pros:** Independent cafés, restaurants, and bakeries, cycling distance to both the university and the city centre, strong neighbourhood community feel, Nieuwe Markt nearby
- **Cons:** Slightly less central than Centrum itself, parking difficult, housing can be older with quirks

Bottendaal is where I usually point clients who want to feel like they are actually living in Nijmegen rather than just renting a flat in it. The neighbourhood around the Stevenskerk (the city's main Gothic church) and stretching south toward the university has a genuinely pleasant daily-life quality. Several of my clients who moved here for a two-year position have bought property and stayed.

### Heyendaal — University Neighbourhood, Practical, Close to Radboud

- **Vibe:** The academic neighbourhood, immediately adjacent to Radboud University and Radboud UMC. Predominantly researchers, medical staff, and students, with the infrastructure to match
- **Rent:** €950-€1,400/month (2-bed)
- **Best for:** Radboud staff, medical researchers, anyone who wants a 10-minute cycle or even walk to work at the university or hospital
- **Pros:** Short commute to both the university and UMC, good local amenities including the Goffert Park nearby, quieter than Centrum, reasonable prices for proximity
- **Cons:** Less character than Bottendaal or Centrum, predominantly functional in atmosphere, student-heavy which can mean noise

If you are taking up a position at Radboud UMC or the university and your primary concern is an easy commute and affordable rent, Heyendaal is the obvious choice. The **Goffert Park** — a large, well-maintained municipal park with a cycle track, swimming pool, and open green space — is a genuine asset for families and anyone who wants outdoor space within cycling distance of home.

### Lent — Modern, Family-Friendly, North of the Waal

- **Vibe:** A modern residential district developed on the north bank of the Waal river, connected to the city centre by the distinctive Oversteek pedestrian bridge and regular bus services. Newer housing stock, family focus, more space
- **Rent:** €1,000-€1,600/month (3-bed houses)
- **Best for:** Families with children, those who want modern housing and space, people who do not need to be in the city centre daily
- **Pros:** Newer buildings with better insulation and energy ratings, more space per euro, quieter environment, pleasant river views, good schools, modern infrastructure
- **Cons:** Less walkable to the historic centre, crossing the river adds 10-15 minutes to most city-centre trips, less urban character

Lent was deliberately developed as a flood-safe modern district — the Waal floodplains programme that created the iconic room-for-the-river programme in this part of the Netherlands also shaped Lent's built environment. The Oversteek bridge (The Crossing) linking Lent to the city centre has become one of Nijmegen's architectural symbols. For families, the combination of newer housing, lower density, and good schools makes it worth considering.

## Getting Around — Commutes and Cycling

### Cycling

Nijmegen was European Green Capital for a reason. The cycling infrastructure is exceptional, with well-maintained separated paths across the city and a network that connects all neighbourhoods to the centre, the university, and the train station. Most expats living in Nijmegen's central neighbourhoods cycle everywhere. A second-hand Dutch bike costs €80-€200 and will serve you well; locking it properly is more important in Nijmegen than almost anything else — bike theft is a real issue in university cities.

## Working in Nijmegen

### Radboud University Medical Centre (Radboud UMC)

Radboud UMC is one of the Netherlands' eight academic medical centres and consistently ranks among Europe's top research hospitals. It employs around 10,000 people across clinical practice, research, and education. A significant proportion of its researchers and specialist physicians are international, and the working language in research contexts is predominantly English. If you are in medicine, biomedical research, nursing, or clinical psychology, Radboud UMC is the dominant employer in the region and the main reason many expats end up in Nijmegen.

### Radboud University

The university itself has around 23,000 students and employs several thousand academic and support staff. It is particularly strong in fields including neuroscience (the Donders Institute), linguistics, environmental science, philosophy, and the humanities. The Donders Institute for Brain, Cognition and Behaviour is internationally recognised and draws researchers from across the world. Many of my clients who have arrived via the Donders Institute have found a genuinely international research environment where English is the functional language.

### NXP Semiconductors

NXP is one of the world's largest semiconductor companies, and Nijmegen is its original home — the company spun out of Philips' semiconductor division, which had been in Nijmegen since the 1950s. NXP employs several hundred engineers and researchers in Nijmegen, with roles in chip design, automotive electronics, and security technology. For electrical engineers and embedded software developers with international profiles, NXP Nijmegen is a significant employer that is genuinely competitive with larger tech centres.

### Novio Tech Campus

Novio Tech Campus is Nijmegen's technology and life sciences innovation hub, based in the south of the city adjacent to the university. It houses around 70 companies working primarily in areas spun out of Radboud research — biomedical diagnostics, materials science, medical devices, and digital health. Companies here include Sioux Technologies, Merus, and a range of smaller spinouts. For expats arriving in deep-tech or biomedical roles, this is where much of the startup and scale-up activity in Nijmegen is concentrated.

### Working Language

English functions as a practical working language throughout Radboud, NXP, and Novio Tech. Outside these contexts — in the broader services sector, local government, schools, and everyday business — Dutch remains the default. Learning Dutch will substantially improve your daily life in Nijmegen; most local employers outside the university ecosystem expect at least a functional level. Radboud's Language Centre offers courses for internationals. See how Nijmegen compares to other options in our [best cities for expats guide](/guides/housing/best-cities-netherlands-expats-2026/).

## Registering and Admin

### BSN Registration

Within five days of arriving in the Netherlands, you are legally required to register at your local gemeente (municipality). In Nijmegen this means **Gemeente Nijmegen** on Mariënburg in the city centre. Registration gives you your **BSN** (Burger Service Nummer) — the identification number you need for almost every formal transaction in the Netherlands: employment, bank account, health insurance, tax.

Our [BSN registration guide](/guides/legal/bsn-registration-guide-2026/) covers exactly what documents to bring (passport, rental contract or address confirmation, birth certificate for family members) and what to expect from the appointment.

### Health Insurance

Health insurance is mandatory in the Netherlands from your first day of work or registered residence. The basic insurance package (basisverzekering) is standardised by law; all providers offer the same core coverage and compete on supplementary packages and service. In 2026, the basic premium is approximately €130-€145/month for an adult. Children under 18 are covered for free.

You need your BSN before you can formally sign up, which means registering at the gemeente first. Do not delay — if you register late, you may face a retroactive premium calculation.

Our [Dutch health insurance guide](/guides/health/dutch-health-insurance-guide-expats-2026/) explains the full system, how to choose a provider, and what the supplementary packages actually cover.

### Banking

You cannot open most Dutch bank accounts without a BSN. Once you have it, the main options in Nijmegen are the major banks (ABN AMRO, ING, Rabobank, all with branches in the city) or a digital account. Many expats use a combination — a Dutch current account for local transactions and salaries, and **Wise** for international transfers and managing money across currencies.

> **Wise for expats:** If you are sending money home, managing accounts in multiple currencies, or receiving a salary in euros while paying expenses abroad, Wise is one of the most cost-effective tools available. No hidden exchange rate markups, transparent fees, and direct integration with Dutch banking. [Open a Wise account](https://wise.com/invite/ihpc/willemt52).

## International Community

Nijmegen's international community is smaller than Amsterdam or The Hague but more concentrated. Because the main entry points are Radboud University, Radboud UMC, and NXP, most expats in the city share a professional context — which makes networking and social connection easier than in cities where internationals arrive from every direction for unrelated reasons.

**Useful starting points:**

- **Radboud University International Office** — dedicated support for international staff and their families, including housing assistance, orientation programmes, and Dutch language courses
- **Radboud UMC Expat Centre** — specific support for medical and research staff arriving at the hospital
- **InterNations Nijmegen** — monthly social events, active online community
- **Expat Spouses Initiative (ESI) Nijmegen** — support network for partners of Radboud staff
- **Nijmegen International** — informal community group active on Facebook

English is widely used in academic and professional settings. In daily life — shops, local services, administration — Dutch is the standard, though most people, particularly those under 50, will switch to English without difficulty.

## Nijmegen Culture, Nightlife, and Nature

### The Vierdaagse — Four Days Marches

Every third week of July, Nijmegen hosts the **Vierdaagse** — the International Four Days Marches, the largest walking event in the world. Around 50,000 participants from over 70 countries walk routes of 30 to 50 kilometres per day for four consecutive days. The event has been held (with some interruptions) since 1909.

The **Vierdaagsefeesten** — the accompanying ten-day street festival — is one of the largest outdoor festivals in Europe. The city centre fills with stages, markets, and outdoor bars. The atmosphere is jubilant and genuinely exceptional if you are in the mood for it. If you are not, plan to be elsewhere.

For expats considering a move: the week of the Vierdaagse is not a good time to move in. Accommodation prices spike, the city is logistically chaotic in the best possible way, and cycling through Centrum becomes an exercise in patience. Book anything you need for that week well in advance or avoid it entirely.

### Cultural Life

Nijmegen's cultural scene is solid for a regional city of its size:

- **Stevenskerk** — the 14th-century Gothic church that dominates the city skyline; open for visits and organ concerts
- **Valkhofmuseum** — archaeological and art museum on the Valkhof hill, covering both Roman-era history and modern Dutch art
- **Museum Het Valkhof** — houses the largest collection of Roman artefacts in the Netherlands, reflecting the city's origins as Ulpia Noviomagus Batavorum
- **LUX cinema and arts centre** — independent cinema and cultural programme, particularly popular with the student and academic community
- **Matrixx at the Park / Doornroosje** — the main live music venues; Doornroosje has a long reputation for quality programming
- **Gebroeders van Limburg Festival** — biennial comics and illustration festival held in Nijmegen, reflecting the city's historical connection to the van Limburg brothers who illuminated the Très Riches Heures

### Nightlife

The student population of 30,000 keeps Nijmegen's nightlife more active than a city of this size would normally sustain. The concentration is in the Molenstraat and Grote Markt area, with bars ranging from traditional Dutch bruine kroegen to larger student-focused venues. Clubs are fewer than in Amsterdam but the bar scene is broad. Wednesday nights are often more lively than Friday in university term time — the Dutch student culture of the Wednesday-night social (borrel) is fully operational here.

### Nature: Ooijpolder and the Waal

The **Ooijpolder** is Nijmegen's defining natural asset. This polderland nature reserve lies immediately east of the city and contains wetlands, hay meadows, old river channels, and wild cattle and horses as part of the room-for-the-river management programme. It is accessible by bike from Bottendaal in 15 minutes. The view in early morning light — flat, wide, with the Nijmegen hills visible in the background — is the kind of thing that makes expats from flat countries feel immediately at home and surprises those who expected urban Netherlands to be unrelentingly developed.

The **Waal riverfront** has been significantly redeveloped in the past decade, with the creation of new beaches, parks, and the Oversteek bridge connecting the city to Lent. On warm days in summer, the Waal beach areas fill with swimmers and sunbathers — not something you expect in a Dutch river city of this size.

The **Wylerbergmeer** and **Reichswald** forest on the German border (20 minutes by bike or car) add hiking and forest cycling to the outdoor options. For expats who need to get out of the city regularly, Nijmegen's surrounding countryside is genuinely excellent.

## Practical Steps for Moving to Nijmegen

### Before You Arrive

1. Start your housing search on [Funda](https://www.funda.nl), [Pararius](https://www.pararius.com), and [Kamernet](https://kamernet.nl) 6-8 weeks before arrival. Good apartments in Heyendaal and Bottendaal go quickly
2. Contact the Radboud International Office or UMC Expat Centre if you are arriving for a university or hospital position — they often have housing leads and relocation support
3. Use the [housing budget checker](/tools/housing-budget-checker/) to confirm your affordable rent range before you start viewing
4. If you are arriving in July, check the exact Vierdaagse dates and avoid scheduling your move or initial flat viewings for that period

### First Two Weeks

1. Register at **Gemeente Nijmegen** (Mariënburg, city centre) — this gives you your BSN. Our [BSN registration guide](/guides/legal/bsn-registration-guide-2026/) explains exactly what to bring
2. Arrange [health insurance](/guides/health/dutch-health-insurance-guide-expats-2026/) — mandatory, and you need to sort it within days of registration
3. Open a bank account. You need your BSN first. Set up [Wise](https://wise.com/invite/ihpc/willemt52) in parallel if you have overseas financial commitments
4. Buy a bike. This is not optional. The second-hand market is active around the university

### First Month

1. Apply for DigiD — the digital identity used for all Dutch government services, from tax to health insurance
2. Register with a **huisarts** (GP). In Nijmegen, some practices near the university can be heavily subscribed — register as soon as possible
3. Explore the Ooijpolder by bike. It will tell you more about why people stay in Nijmegen than anything else
4. Find Dutch language courses. The Radboud Language Centre is the most convenient option for university and UMC staff; there are also private schools in the city centre

## Honest Pros and Cons

I try to give clients a clear picture of what they are actually choosing, not a sales pitch.

**What genuinely works well in Nijmegen:**

- The cost of living is meaningfully lower than Amsterdam and Utrecht. On a Radboud researcher's salary, you can rent a comfortable two-bedroom apartment in a good neighbourhood without financial stress — which is not a given in Utrecht and is almost impossible in Amsterdam
- The Ooijpolder and the Waal riverfront make Nijmegen one of the best Dutch cities for outdoor access within cycling range
- The Radboud and NXP ecosystems are internationally oriented and well-resourced. If you are in the right sector, you will not feel like you are in a provincial backwater
- The cycling infrastructure really is exceptional, and the city is compact enough that you can reach almost anywhere in 20 minutes by bike
- The Vierdaagse is, genuinely, one of the most remarkable events in the Netherlands. If you time a visit during the festival week before committing to a move, it will tell you a lot about whether the city's character appeals to you

**What to be clear about before you commit:**

- **Distance from the Randstad.** Amsterdam is 1 hour 15 minutes — manageable for regular visits but not a practical daily commute. If you or your partner work in Amsterdam and the other is in Nijmegen, this is a real logistical factor. Utrecht at 50 minutes is more workable
- **The city is not internationally diverse.** Nijmegen's expat population is concentrated in specific professional sectors. Outside the Radboud ecosystem and NXP, the international community is smaller and less visible. If you are used to Amsterdam's multi-layered international scene, Nijmegen will feel more provincial
- **Dutch required outside work.** In daily life — schools, local services, informal social contexts — Dutch is the norm in a way it is not in Amsterdam or The Hague. You can manage without it, but your social integration will be slower and some practical tasks more difficult
- **Limited nightlife for non-students.** The nightlife scene is primarily student-driven. It is active but not particularly varied for older expats or those who want a sophisticated bar and restaurant scene beyond cafés
- **No major international airport nearby.** Schiphol is 1 hour 15 minutes by train — fine for occasional travel but adds up if you fly frequently
