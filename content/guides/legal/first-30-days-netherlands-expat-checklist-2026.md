## Before You Arrive: The Two-Week Runway (Week -2 to Day 0)

The work starts before you step off the plane. Several things can only be done from your home country, and a handful of others are dramatically easier to arrange before you land.

### Get Your Documents Apostilled

The Netherlands requires foreign documents — birth certificates, marriage certificates, degree certificates, criminal record checks — to be officially legalised before they will be accepted at a gemeente or by an employer. The standard method is an **apostille**: a standardised international certification under the Hague Convention.

The key documents to apostille before you move:

- **Birth certificate** (required for gemeente registration)
- **Marriage certificate** (if applicable; your spouse needs theirs too)
- **Degree certificate** (if you are applying for the 30% ruling or a highly skilled migrant visa)
- **Criminal record check** (required for some visa types and many employers)

Apostilles are issued by the relevant authority in your home country — in the UK, that is the Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office (FCDO). The process typically takes 1-3 weeks in the UK and varies elsewhere. Do not assume it is quick; I have seen people wait six weeks.

My full [apostille and legalisation guide for the Netherlands](/guides/legal/apostille-legalization-netherlands-2026/) walks through the process country by country, including which documents are in scope and which can be notarised instead.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Identify which documents you need apostilled
- [ ] Submit your documents to the relevant authority in your home country
- [ ] Request multiple certified copies — you will need originals for registration and want backups

### Book Temporary Accommodation and Research Neighbourhoods

Before you can register at the gemeente, you need a Dutch address. You cannot register at a hotel, an Airbnb, or a co-living space that is not formally a residential property on the BRP (Basisregistratie Personen). This catches a lot of people out.

Options for the first weeks:

- **Corporate housing**: Many employers organise this for the first 4-8 weeks. Ask your HR team explicitly if this is on offer.
- **A room with a registered address**: Private landlords who rent individual rooms can sometimes register you if the property is set up for it. Confirm this explicitly before you sign anything.
- **Short-term furnished apartments via HousingAnywhere or Pararius**: Some short-let landlords will register tenants for stays of 2+ months. Ask before booking.

While you are searching for temporary accommodation, start your research on permanent neighbourhoods too. Where you end up will affect your commute, the schools available to your children, and your overall cost of living. ## Week 1: Your First Priorities (Days 1–7)

This first week has one overriding priority: get your gemeente appointment booked. Everything else in the Dutch administrative system is downstream of your BSN, and your BSN comes from the gemeente. The earlier you book, the earlier everything else can happen.

### Day 1–2: Register at the Gemeente

On your very first day, your first task is to go to the gemeente (municipal government) website and book a registration appointment. Do not wait until you have unpacked, until you have rested, until you have explored the city. Book it first.

Each municipality has its own booking system. In Amsterdam, it is via the Mijn Amsterdam portal. In Rotterdam and Utrecht, through their respective municipal sites. Use the [BSN planner tool](/tools/bsn-planner/) to find the direct booking link for your municipality and see your deadline clearly.

**What you need for the appointment:**

| Document | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| Valid passport or national ID | EU citizens can use national ID |
| Birth certificate | Must be apostilled or legalised |
| Proof of address in the Netherlands | Rental contract, or declaration signed by your landlord |
| Residence permit / MVV sticker | Non-EU citizens only |
| Marriage certificate | If applicable; apostilled |

At the end of the appointment, the gemeente officer processes your registration and you receive a letter with your BSN printed on it. The BSN itself is assigned instantly — you leave the office with it.

My [BSN registration guide](/guides/legal/bsn-registration-guide-2026/) covers the full process in granular detail: what the appointment is actually like, how to handle common problems (wrong address, incorrect documents), and tips for getting earlier appointments through cancellation slots.

**What if my appointment is weeks away?**

Book the first available slot, then:
- Check for cancellations daily (set a reminder)
- In Amsterdam, new slots often appear early in the morning
- Consider whether a nearby smaller municipality could offer an earlier appointment (this only works if you are genuinely residing there)

While you wait for your appointment, you are not completely stuck. Use the time to complete the steps below.

- **N26**: German digital bank operating in the Netherlands, no Dutch IBAN but widely usable.

### Day 3–5: Apply for DigiD

DigiD (Digitale Identiteit, or digital identity) is the single login system used for virtually every Dutch government service. You will use it to:

- Log into Mijn Overheid (the national government portal)
- Access the Belastingdienst (Tax Authority) for tax returns and allowances
- Apply for zorgtoeslag (healthcare allowance) and huurtoeslag (rent allowance)
- Register with health insurance providers
- Access your pension overview at mijnpensioenoverzicht.nl
- Manage your municipality registration

You can only apply for DigiD after you have your BSN. Go to digid.nl immediately after your gemeente appointment and submit your application. The process involves:

1. Enter your BSN, date of birth, and postcode
2. Choose your security level (DigiD with SMS is standard; DigiD with identity document (ID check) gives higher access)
3. An activation letter is sent by post to your registered address — this takes approximately 5 business days

You **cannot** rush this. The letter must arrive by post. If you move address before it arrives, the letter will go to the wrong place. Make sure you are at your registered address during this window.

My [DigiD guide for expats](/guides/legal/digid-guide-expats-2026/) covers the full application process, the difference between security levels, and what to do if your activation letter does not arrive.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Apply for DigiD at digid.nl on the same day as your BSN appointment
- [ ] Note the expected letter arrival date
- [ ] Ensure you will be at your registered address to receive the letter

### Day 5–7: Arrange Dutch Health Insurance

Dutch health insurance is mandatory. Every person legally residing and working in the Netherlands must take out a basisverzekering (basic health insurance) within 4 months of arrival. The 4-month window is not a reason to delay — I recommend sorting it in week one.

**How Dutch health insurance works:**

- The basic package (basisverzekering) is standardised by law — every insurer offers the same core coverage. The difference is price, customer service, and supplementary packages.
- In 2026, the average monthly premium is around €150-170 for the basic package.
- The **eigen risico** (excess/deductible) is €385 per year. You pay this yourself before the insurer covers costs. You can voluntarily raise this to €885 to lower your monthly premium.
- You pay premiums monthly. If your income is below a certain threshold, you may qualify for zorgtoeslag (a government healthcare allowance) — more on that in Week 3.

**How to compare and choose:**

Use <a href="https://www.awin1.com/cread.php?awinmid=8558&awinaffid=2776410&ued=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.independer.nl%2Fzorgverzekering" rel="noopener nofollow sponsored" class="cta-affiliate">Independer's health insurance comparison tool</a> — it is the most widely used comparison platform in the Netherlands and shows all major insurers side by side. Filter by price, supplementary packages (tandarts/dental is the big one), and English-language customer service.

For a fuller picture of what to look for, read my [Dutch health insurance guide for expats](/guides/health/dutch-health-insurance-guide-expats-2026/), which covers the basisverzekering in detail, compares the main insurers, and explains the supplementary (aanvullende) packages worth adding.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Compare policies on Independer
- [ ] Choose and activate your basisverzekering
- [ ] Cancel your SafetyWing coverage once your Dutch policy is confirmed active (overlap by a day or two to be safe)

### Register With a Huisarts (GP)

In the Netherlands, your huisarts (family doctor / GP) is your gateway to all healthcare. You cannot go directly to a specialist, an A&E for non-emergencies, or a hospital outpatient department without a referral from your huisarts. This makes registering with a GP early an absolute priority — not because you are ill now, but because you need to be registered *before* you need them.

**How to find and register:**

1. Search for huisartspraktijken (GP practices) near your address on Zorgkaart Nederland (zorgkaartnederland.nl)
2. Check whether the practice has capacity for new patients — many practices in Amsterdam and other cities are full (vol patiëntenbestand)
3. Contact the practice directly (by phone or via their website) and ask to register
4. Some practices require you to show your BSN and proof of Dutch health insurance when registering

English-speaking GPs are common in Amsterdam, The Hague, and Utrecht, less so in smaller cities. My [guide to finding an English-speaking doctor in the Netherlands](/guides/health/find-english-speaking-doctor-netherlands-2026/) lists vetted practices by city and explains how to register, including what to do if every practice near you is full.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Search for GP practices near your address
- [ ] Contact 2-3 practices and register with the first one that accepts you
- [ ] Keep your BSN card and insurance details to hand for the registration form

## Week 3: Financial Setup (Days 15–21)

By week 3, your DigiD activation letter should have arrived and most of your core setup is in place. This week is about the financial layer: allowances, Dutch payment culture, and your most time-sensitive tax decision.

### Activate Your DigiD

When your activation letter arrives, activate your DigiD immediately at digid.nl. You will need the activation code from the letter, your BSN, and your mobile number.

Once activated, log in to Mijn Overheid (mijnoverheid.nl) and:

- Set up your berichtenbox (digital mailbox) — this is where all government correspondence now goes, replacing postal letters for most services
- Link your DigiD to your BSN in the system
- Check that your address is registered correctly

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Activate DigiD as soon as the letter arrives
- [ ] Set up Mijn Overheid berichtenbox
- [ ] Verify your personal details are correct

### Check Your Zorgtoeslag and Huurtoeslag Eligibility

The Dutch government offers several toeslagen (allowances) to help with living costs. Two are highly relevant to most expats in their first year:

**Zorgtoeslag (healthcare allowance):** A monthly government contribution towards your health insurance premium. In 2026, if your income is below approximately €37,000 per year (single person), you are likely eligible. The maximum monthly contribution is around €123. Apply via the Belastingdienst (tax authority) using your DigiD.

**Huurtoeslag (rent allowance):** A monthly government contribution towards your rent. Eligibility is income-tested (roughly €31,000 or below for a single person in 2026) and also depends on your rent being below the liberalisation threshold (currently around €879/month). You apply via the Belastingdienst.

These allowances are not applied automatically. You must apply yourself, and they can be applied retroactively but only within the same calendar year. Do not put this off — if you are eligible, you are potentially leaving €100+ per month unclaimed.

My detailed guide on [zorgtoeslag and huurtoeslag for expats](/guides/finance/zorgtoeslag-huurtoeslag-expats-2026/) covers exact income thresholds, how to apply step by step using DigiD, and common mistakes that cause applications to be rejected.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Check zorgtoeslag eligibility at toeslagen.belastingdienst.nl
- [ ] Check huurtoeslag eligibility if your rent is below the threshold
- [ ] Apply via Mijn Toeslagen using your DigiD

### Set Up Tikkie

Tikkie is to the Netherlands what Venmo is to the US — an instant, app-based payment request system used for splitting bills, paying back friends, and informal transactions. You cannot fully participate in Dutch social and practical life without it.

To use Tikkie, you need:
- A Dutch phone number
- A Dutch bank account (with ABN AMRO, ING, Rabobank, or other participating banks)

Download the Tikkie app (iOS / Android) and link your Dutch bank account. You will immediately be able to send and receive payment requests.

You will also encounter iDEAL everywhere — the standard Dutch online payment method that links directly to your bank account. Your Dutch bank account automatically gives you access to iDEAL, which is used for everything from online shopping to paying rent.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Download Tikkie and link your Dutch bank account
- [ ] Test by sending yourself a small payment request to confirm it works

### Employer Applies for the 30% Ruling (If Eligible)

If you are a highly skilled migrant who was recruited from abroad, your employer may be able to apply for the 30% ruling on your behalf. This allows them to pay 30% of your gross salary as a tax-free allowance, effectively reducing your tax burden significantly.

Key facts for 2026:

- The ruling applies for a maximum of **5 years** (formerly 8)
- Your employer must apply via the Belastingdienst **within 4 months of your start date** — missing this deadline means losing the benefit permanently
- Minimum salary requirements apply (approximately €46,107 gross per year for those 30 and over; €35,048 for under-30s with a master's degree)
- You must have lived at least 150 km from the Dutch border for at least 16 of the 24 months before starting work

This is your most time-sensitive financial item. If you think you might qualify, raise it with your HR or payroll team this week — not next month. I have spoken to expats who lost out on tens of thousands of euros because their employer did not apply in time.

My detailed [30% ruling guide for the Netherlands](/guides/finance/30-percent-ruling-netherlands-2026/) covers eligibility in full, the application process, what happens if you change employer, and the partial ruling that applies after year 3.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Check whether you meet the eligibility criteria
- [ ] Raise it with your HR/payroll team in week 1 if you have not already
- [ ] Confirm your employer has submitted the application

## Week 4: Integration (Days 22–30)

By week 4, the urgent admin is done. Your BSN is active, your bank account is open, your health insurance is running, your DigiD works. Now the longer-term work begins: building a life here, not just managing paperwork.

### Sign Up for Dutch Language Lessons

Dutch is famously accessible in the Netherlands — almost everyone in the Randstad speaks excellent English, and you can get by without Dutch for years. I know expats who have lived in Amsterdam for a decade and speak barely a word of Dutch.

I also know how isolated that can make you feel.

Learning Dutch opens doors that English does not. It changes how people interact with you. It gives you access to Dutch media, Dutch friends beyond the expat bubble, Dutch culture. Even a functional B1 level transforms your experience of living here.

Options for Dutch lessons in 2026:

- **Apps for self-study**: [Best apps to learn Dutch](/guides/integration/best-apps-learn-dutch-2026/) — my review covers Babbel, Duolingo, and others. - **Online tutors**: Preply and iTalki both have excellent Dutch tutors for one-to-one lessons. I have a [Preply vs iTalki comparison](/guides/integration/preply-vs-italki-dutch-lessons-2026/) if you want a live tutor.

### Register for Inburgering (If Required)

Inburgering (civic integration) is a formal government programme that requires certain categories of immigrants to pass language and civic knowledge exams within a set period of arriving in the Netherlands. Since January 2022, the rules changed significantly under the new Wet inburgering.

**Who is required to inburger:**
- Non-EU nationals who arrive under certain permit types (family reunification, asylum, some work permits)
- This is **not** required for EU/EEA citizens or highly skilled migrants (kennismigranten)

**Who is exempt:**
- EU/EEA citizens
- Highly skilled migrants (kennismigrant)
- Holders of the Blue Card
- Those who completed inburgering before 2022 under the old system

If you have received a letter from the IND or your gemeente about inburgering, or if you are unsure whether it applies to you, do not ignore it. Missing the inburgering deadline results in fines. My [inburgeringsexamen preparation guide](/guides/integration/inburgeringsexamen-preparation-guide-2026/) covers the exams, preparation resources, and how the personal inburgeringsplan (PIP) works.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Check your IND letter or ask your gemeente whether inburgering is required for you
- [ ] If required, contact the DUO (Dienst Uitvoering Onderwijs) to start your inburgeringsplan

### Join Expat Communities

One of the things I tell every new expat client: the first few months are socially disorienting even when they go smoothly on the admin side. You are building a social life from scratch, often without the networks that took years to establish back home.

Actively investing in community in week 4 — rather than waiting until you feel settled — makes an enormous difference.

Options:

- **Internations**: The largest expat social network globally, with active chapters in Amsterdam, Rotterdam, Utrecht, and The Hague. Monthly events, professional networking, and interest-based groups.
- **Meetup.com**: Hugely active in Dutch cities. Search for expat groups, language exchanges (tandem Dutch/English is very popular), sports groups, and professional networks.
- **Facebook groups**: "Amsterdam Expats", "Expats in The Hague", "Utrecht Expats" etc. are genuine communities with useful local knowledge and frequent social events.
- **Local clubs and associations**: Sport clubs (sportverenigingen), running clubs, amateur theatre — joining a Dutch-language activity is the fastest route to Dutch friendships beyond the expat bubble.

My guide on [making friends in the Netherlands as an expat](/guides/integration/making-friends-netherlands-expats-2026/) goes deeper into the specific challenges of building a social life in the Netherlands (Dutch directness, the famous closed social circles), and practical strategies that actually work.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Join one or two expat online groups for your city
- [ ] Sign up for at least one in-person event or activity in week 4
- [ ] Start a Dutch language tandem or conversation group

## A Note on Timelines and Overlap

One thing I want to be honest about: these timelines are optimistic. In Amsterdam in particular, gemeente appointments routinely take 4-6 weeks, which pushes the BSN — and everything downstream of it — into week 5 or 6 rather than week 1.

That does not mean the rest of this guide falls apart. It means you have to work the parallel tracks:

- **Start the gemeente appointment booking on day one** (cannot overstate this)
- **Use Wise and SafetyWing** to cover the gap before your bank account and Dutch insurance are active
- **Use the waiting period** to read all the guides, compare health insurance options, and research the housing market

I have had clients who arrived in Amsterdam, booked their gemeente appointment immediately, got a slot for day 38, and still had everything else sorted by day 50 — because they used the waiting time well. The process is slow, but it is manageable if you approach it systematically.

The [complete guide to moving to the Netherlands](/guides/legal/complete-guide-moving-to-netherlands-2026/) has more context on the broader relocation process for those earlier than the arrival stage.

The first 30 days in the Netherlands are genuinely demanding. The bureaucracy is not designed to be convenient for newcomers, the housing market is one of the tightest in Europe, and doing everything in the right order requires knowledge that is not written on any official government page.

But I have walked hundreds of people through this process, and I have never seen someone who prepared properly — who booked the gemeente appointment on day one, sorted their insurance gap, and used the waiting period productively — come out the other side overwhelmed. It is a lot, but it is manageable. You will get there.
