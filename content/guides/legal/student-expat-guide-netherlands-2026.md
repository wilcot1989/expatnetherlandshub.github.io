When I first started advising international students on their move to the Netherlands, I assumed the process would be simple — apply to university, get a visa, find a room, done. But after years of helping students from dozens of countries, I can tell you it is far more layered than that. The Netherlands has some of the best universities in the world, a genuinely international student culture, and a surprisingly liveable lifestyle on a student budget. But there are also serious pitfalls: a housing crisis that catches students off-guard, financial support rules that most people never know exist, and a visa process that varies enormously depending on where you come from.

This guide covers everything I wish every student knew before they arrived.

---

## Who This Guide Is For

Whether you are a first-year bachelor's student from Germany, an Indian master's student heading to TU Delft, or an American PhD candidate joining a research group in Leiden, your practical situation as a student in the Netherlands depends heavily on your nationality. I have structured this guide accordingly. Where the rules differ between EU and non-EU students, I say so clearly.

## Part 1: Visas and Entry

### EU/EEA and Swiss Students

If you hold a passport from an EU or EEA member state, or from Switzerland, you have the right to live and study in the Netherlands without any visa or residence permit. You simply need to register at your local municipality (gemeente) within five days of arriving and setting up your address. This gives you your BSN (burgerservicenummer), the citizen service number you will need for almost everything — opening a bank account, accessing healthcare, setting up a phone contract.

The registration process requires:
- Valid passport or national ID card
- Proof of address (a rental contract or a letter from your landlord)
- Completed registration form (available at the gemeente)

Some universities, particularly in Amsterdam, offer assistance with municipal registration during student onboarding weeks. Make use of it — it saves a lot of running around.

For a detailed look at what the BSN process involves and how to get yours quickly, see our [BSN registration guide](/guides/legal/bsn-registration-guide-2026/).

### Non-EU Students: The Entry Visa (MVV) and Residence Permit

If you are coming from outside the EU/EEA (and are not Swiss or from a handful of other exempt nationalities), the process is more involved.

**Step 1: Apply through your university**

Dutch universities act as IND-recognised sponsors for student visa applications. Once you have received an unconditional offer and paid your tuition deposit, your university's international office submits a residence permit application to the IND (Immigratie- en Naturalisatiedienst) on your behalf.

This differs from the process for skilled workers, where the employer initiates. For students, everything goes through the university. Familiarise yourself with our [highly skilled migrant visa guide](/guides/legal/highly-skilled-migrant-visa-netherlands-2026/) if you are planning to work professionally after graduation — the post-study orientation year visa links directly to that pathway.

**Step 2: The MVV sticker (if required)**

Citizens of most non-EU countries need an MVV (Machtiging tot Voorlopig Verblijf) — a provisional residence permit issued as a sticker in your passport at a Dutch embassy or consulate in your home country. Nationals of the US, Canada, Australia, Japan, South Korea, New Zealand, and a few others are exempt.

Your university will tell you whether you need an MVV and will guide you through obtaining one.

**Step 3: Collect your residence permit card**

Once in the Netherlands, you collect your formal residence permit card (verblijfspas) from the IND desk, typically in the first few weeks of your programme. Your university will schedule this appointment.

**Timeline:** Start the process at least 3-4 months before your programme begins. IND processing typically takes 4-8 weeks, and delays at Dutch consulates abroad can add further time.

**What your student residence permit allows:**
- Full-time study at the registered institution
- Work up to 16 hours per week (or full-time in June, July, August)
- No travel restrictions within the Schengen zone

## Part 2: Choosing a University and Programme

The Netherlands has a well-regarded higher education system with a strong international reputation. A few things worth knowing:

### University Types

**Research Universities (Universiteiten):** Award bachelor's, master's, and PhD degrees. Strong on research and theory. Examples: University of Amsterdam (UvA), Leiden University, Utrecht University, TU Delft, Maastricht University, Groningen University.

**Universities of Applied Sciences (Hogescholen):** Practical, professionally-oriented programmes. Awards bachelor's and professional master's degrees. Examples: HvA (Amsterdam), Rotterdam University of Applied Sciences, The Hague University.

### Top Programmes for International Students

The Netherlands is one of the most popular study destinations in Europe for English-taught programmes. The country has over 2,100 English-language programmes — more than any other non-English speaking country in the world.

Strong international reputations by field:
- **Business and economics:** Rotterdam School of Management (Erasmus), Amsterdam Business School, Tilburg University
- **Engineering and technology:** TU Delft (consistently top-10 globally), Eindhoven University of Technology (TU/e), University of Twente
- **Law and social sciences:** Maastricht University, Leiden Law School
- **Science and research:** Leiden, Groningen, Utrecht
- **Liberal arts:** University College Utrecht, University College Maastricht, Amsterdam University College

### Tuition Fees in 2026

This is where EU and non-EU students face a significant split.

| Student Category | Tuition Fee (per year) |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| EU/EEA/Swiss students (statutory rate) | EUR 2,530 |
| Non-EU, bachelor's at research university | EUR 8,000 – 15,000 |
| Non-EU, master's at research university | EUR 10,000 – 20,000 |
| Non-EU, MBA / specialist programmes | EUR 15,000 – 30,000+ |

**Important note on the statutory fee:** EU/EEA students generally qualify for the statutory (wettelijk) tuition fee of EUR 2,530 per year. However, this applies to your first bachelor's degree in the Netherlands. If you are completing a second degree, or if you are enrolled part-time, different rates apply. Check with your specific institution.

Non-EU students pay institutional fees, which vary significantly between universities and even between programmes at the same university. Always check the specific fee on the university's website.

## Part 3: Student Housing — The Hard Truth

I am going to be direct with you: finding student housing in the Netherlands is genuinely difficult, and it is one of the main reasons students run into serious problems before their studies have even started. The housing shortage is not just an inconvenience — students have had to defer their enrolment because they could not find a place to live.

### The Scale of the Problem

The Netherlands has a structural student housing shortage. In Amsterdam alone, there are over 20,000 students registered on social housing waiting lists managed by student housing corporations like DUWO and ROOM. Average waiting times in major student cities:

| City | Average Social Housing Wait |
|------|-----------------------------|
| Amsterdam | 5-7 years |
| Leiden | 4-6 years |
| Utrecht | 3-5 years |
| Delft | 3-5 years |
| Groningen | 1-3 years |
| Maastricht | 6-18 months |
| Eindhoven | 1-2 years |

As an incoming student, you almost certainly cannot access social student housing in time. Register on waiting lists anyway — it may be useful if you extend your stay — but plan for the private market.

The platform is widely used across Dutch university cities and many universities have partnership listings on it.

**Kamernet** is the largest Dutch room-sharing platform. Listings are in Dutch and English, and it covers everything from student rooms in shared houses to studio apartments. It is where most Dutch students look, so the volume of listings is high. I recommend using both HousingAnywhere and Kamernet simultaneously.

**Apply for housing the same week you receive your offer letter** — even before you have confirmed your enrolment. Waiting until the summer before your September start date is one of the most common and costly mistakes I see. Many landlords and platforms list rooms several months in advance, and the best options go to those who are fastest.

Register on your university's housing waiting list immediately on acceptance. Some universities — particularly TU Delft and Wageningen University — have dedicated housing services that reserve a proportion of rooms for international students. Check whether yours does.

## Part 4: DUO Studiefinanciering — Student Finance

This is possibly the most under-communicated benefit available to international students in the Netherlands, and it applies to far more people than most students realise.

### What Is DUO?

DUO (Dienst Uitvoering Onderwijs) is the Dutch government body responsible for student finance. It provides grants, loans, and the OV-studentenkaart (travel card) to eligible students.

### Who Is Eligible?

**Dutch students:** Full access to all DUO support.

**EU/EEA and Swiss students:** Also eligible, but with one key condition — you must be working in the Netherlands for at least 32 hours per month (roughly 8 hours per week). Part-time work counts. This rule exists because DUO support is tied to being a "worker" under EU free movement law, not just a student.

**Non-EU students:** Not eligible for DUO studiefinanciering.

### What DUO Offers

**Basisbeurs (basic student grant):**
For the 2025-2026 academic year, the basic grant is approximately EUR 465 per month for students living away from home. This is a grant, not a loan — you do not repay it if you pass your degree on time.

**Aanvullende beurs (supplementary grant):**
Based on your parents' income. Can be up to EUR 460 per month on top of the basic grant. Also a grant if you graduate within the nominal duration of your programme.

**Studentenlening (student loan):**
You can borrow up to the maximum monthly cost of living (approximately EUR 1,400/month) minus your grants. The loan is repaid over 35 years after graduation, with income-contingent repayments.

**OV-studentenkaart:**
A free public transport card. I cover this separately below.

### How to Apply

1. Register at your Dutch university
2. Ensure you are registered at your gemeente (municipality)
3. Obtain your BSN
4. Apply at MijnDUO (mijn.duo.nl) with your BSN and DigiD

For the work requirement as an EU student, document your employment carefully. A part-time student job, a zero-hours contract, or any registered paid work counts, provided it totals at least 32 hours per month on average.

## Part 5: Health Insurance for Students

Health insurance is one area where students consistently ask me the wrong questions — usually "which Dutch insurer is cheapest?" when the first question should be "do I even need Dutch insurance?"

### The Dutch Health Insurance System

The Netherlands has a mandatory health insurance system for anyone who is legally resident and working here. The basic insurance (basisverzekering) costs roughly EUR 140-160 per month in 2026. You also pay an annual deductible (eigen risico) of EUR 385.

Under 18s are covered for free on a parent's policy.

### Who Needs Dutch Health Insurance as a Student?

**Dutch students and EU students who work and register:** If you work and are legally resident, you are generally required to take out Dutch basic health insurance.

**Non-EU students:** You are not required to take out Dutch health insurance (unless you work), but you must have coverage. Your university may require proof of international health insurance as part of your enrolment conditions.

**EU students who do not work:** You may be covered by your home country's European Health Insurance Card (EHIC) for emergency treatment, but the EHIC does not cover everything, and it is not sufficient for long-term residence. You should take out either Dutch insurance or a suitable international student policy.

## Part 6: Opening a Bank Account as a Student

You will need a Dutch bank account to receive your salary from a part-time job, to pay rent, and to use Dutch payment apps like Tikkie. Opening one as a student is generally easier than opening one as a newly arrived professional, since universities often have arrangements with specific banks.

### Main Options for Students

**ING, Rabobank, ABN AMRO:** The three major Dutch banks all offer student accounts with no monthly fee during your studies. You need a BSN and proof of student registration. ING and Rabobank have the largest branch and ATM networks.

**Bunq:** A Dutch mobile bank popular with international students and expats. Opens quickly via app with no branch visit required, supports multiple currencies, and offers English-language support throughout. Monthly fee of EUR 2.99 for the basic plan.

**Wise:** Not a full bank account but a multi-currency account that is exceptionally useful for students who receive money from abroad, or who send money home. You can open a Wise account before you leave your home country, which is useful for covering your initial costs.

## Part 7: Working While Studying

### EU/EEA Students

EU/EEA students can work without any restrictions on hours or employer. You do not need a work permit. This is the same right any EU worker has under free movement law.

If you work enough to qualify (32 hours/month), this unlocks DUO student finance as described above.

### Non-EU Students

Non-EU students on a study residence permit can work up to 16 hours per week during the academic year, or full-time during the summer months of June, July, and August.

**Important:** Your employer must obtain a work permit (Tewerkstellingsvergunning, or TWV) from the UWV before you start working. This is a simple administrative step, but the employer must do it — you cannot work without it regardless of the hours involved. Most student job employers in university cities (cafes, supermarkets, student bars, university assistantships) are familiar with this process.

Working without a permit or exceeding your hour limit puts your residence permit at risk. Keep records of your hours.

**Typical student jobs for international students:**
- Research assistant or teaching assistant at your university
- Hospitality (cafes, restaurants, bars)
- Supermarkets (Albert Heijn, Jumbo — both hire internationally)
- Tutoring (English, maths, science)
- Customer service/call centre roles (English-speaking, very common in the Netherlands)

**Student minimum wage (2026):** EUR 15.41 per hour (age 21+). Rates for 18-20 year olds are slightly lower.

## Part 8: The OV-Studentenkaart — Free Public Transport

If you qualify for DUO student finance, one of its most tangible benefits is the OV-studentenkaart: a public transport card that gives you free travel by train, tram, bus, and metro anywhere in the Netherlands.

You choose between:
- **Weekday card:** Free travel Monday–Friday
- **Weekend card:** Free travel Saturday–Sunday

At all other times, you get a 40% discount on standard fares when you use the card. Most students choose the weekend card and use their bicycle for daily commuting — a very Dutch arrangement that saves money and is quicker in many cities anyway.

The card is linked to your DUO account. If you stop receiving DUO finance (for example if you graduate or if your work hours drop below the threshold), the card is deactivated.

## Part 9: Getting Around — The Dutch Cycling Culture

I cannot write a student guide to the Netherlands without talking about cycling. In most Dutch university cities, cycling is not a preference — it is the primary mode of transport. Universities are built around it. Student cities like Groningen, Delft, Leiden, Utrecht, and Eindhoven have some of the world's highest cycling rates, with infrastructure designed accordingly.

As a student, a bicycle is your most important purchase. Second-hand bikes sell for EUR 50-150 and are available at student markets, Marktplaats (the Dutch eBay equivalent), and at most universities' second-hand markets at the start of the academic year.

A few practical points:
- Register your bike's serial number with the police. Theft is common in student cities.
- Always lock your bike twice — once through the frame and wheel with a heavy lock, and once to a fixed point like a rack or fence.
- Lights are legally required after dark.
- Cycling rules are taken seriously — do not use your phone while cycling, and obey traffic lights.

## Part 11: Learning Dutch

You can study in the Netherlands for years without ever needing Dutch — English is widely spoken, university programmes are often taught entirely in English, and social life in international student circles is predominantly English.

But I encourage every student I work with to at least learn the basics. Here is why:

- It opens up far more job opportunities for part-time work
- It makes daily admin (dealing with the gemeente, reading official letters) much less stressful
- Dutch people genuinely appreciate the effort, even if they immediately switch to English
- It is useful preparation if you plan to stay in the Netherlands after graduation

The most effective approach for students is combining a language app with occasional group lessons. Babbel and Duolingo both work for basic Dutch, but neither will get you to conversational level on their own.

## Part 12: Social Life and Student Culture

Dutch student life has its own culture, and understanding it helps you settle in faster.

### Student Associations (Studentenverenigingen)

Most Dutch universities have established student associations with clubhouses, sports teams, and social events. These are more structured and socially prominent than their UK or US equivalents — some have over a century of history. Joining one is an excellent way to build a lasting social network, and many have international student programmes.

The larger associations in cities like Amsterdam (ASVA, Unitas), Utrecht (USS, Argo), and Groningen (Vindicat, USVA) offer significant social infrastructure. Initiation processes (ontgroeningsweken) can be intense, though rules have been tightened in recent years.

### International Student Networks

Most universities have a student association specifically for international students (often called AEGEE, ESN — Erasmus Student Network, or similar). These organise city tours, day trips, language exchanges, and social events, and are usually the fastest way to build a friend group in your first weeks.

### Dutch Directness

Dutch social culture can initially read as blunt or unfriendly to students from more indirect communication cultures. It is not — Dutch people are genuinely friendly, but they express it differently. Direct feedback is a sign of respect, not hostility. Showing up on time is a social expectation, not a suggestion. If you are invited to bring a dish to dinner, bring a dish.

Before you land, work through the following:

- [ ] Confirm your university offer and pay deposit

- [ ] Non-EU students: begin visa process with your university's international office
- [ ] Research health insurance options

**6-8 weeks before:**
- [ ] Non-EU students: collect MVV sticker from Dutch embassy/consulate
- [ ] Book flights
- [ ] Open a Wise account for transferring money during your initial period
- [ ] Confirm your housing arrangement

**First week in the Netherlands:**
- [ ] Register at gemeente within 5 days of moving in (EU students: bring ID and rental contract)
- [ ] Collect BSN
- [ ] Open Dutch bank account (ING, Rabobank, Bunq, or ABN AMRO)
- [ ] Register at university and attend onboarding events
- [ ] Non-EU students: attend IND appointment to collect residence permit card

- [ ] Buy a second-hand bicycle
- [ ] Apply for DigiD (Dutch digital ID — needed for DUO, healthcare access, tax)
- [ ] If eligible: apply for DUO student finance at mijn.duo.nl

## My Final Advice

After working with international students for over a decade, the pattern I see most clearly is this: the students who arrive prepared — with housing confirmed, finances in order, and visa paperwork complete — settle quickly and enjoy themselves. Those who arrive hoping to figure it out on arrival tend to spend their first semester stressed about logistics rather than their studies.

The Netherlands rewards preparation. The systems work well, the support is genuinely available, and the student experience here is excellent. But you have to engage with the admin early.

Start with housing. Everything else has a solution — but a bad housing situation poisons the entire experience.
