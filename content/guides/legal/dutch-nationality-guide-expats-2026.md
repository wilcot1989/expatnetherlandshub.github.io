Over the past ten years, I've helped dozens of expats through the Dutch naturalisation process. I've sat in municipality waiting rooms, chased apostilled documents across three continents, and talked people through the dual nationality decision at midnight when they were panicking about what renunciation actually means.

What I can tell you with confidence is this: the process is a genuine paperwork marathon. The rules have real grey areas. But it is absolutely doable, and for most people who've built their lives in the Netherlands, it is worth every form, every appointment, and yes, every euro of the leges.

This guide covers everything: who qualifies, what the process looks like, the dreaded dual nationality question, the option procedure for those who qualify, costs, timelines, documents, and the mistakes I see people make over and over. By the end, you'll know exactly where you stand and what your next step is.

## Who Can Apply for Dutch Nationality?

There are two main routes to Dutch nationality: **naturalisation** (naturalisatie) and the **option procedure** (optie). Which one applies to you depends on your personal situation.

### Naturalisation

This is the standard route for most expats. You apply to the IND (Immigratie- en Naturalisatiedienst), the Dutch immigration authority, and if you meet the requirements, the municipality grants you Dutch nationality at a formal ceremony.

You can apply via naturalisation if you are:

- A foreign national who has lived legally in the Netherlands for at least **five continuous years**
- A foreign national who has been married to or in a registered partnership with a Dutch citizen for at least **three years**, and has lived in the Netherlands for at least **three years** during that partnership
- A stateless person who has lived in the Netherlands for at least **three years**
- A recognised refugee who has lived in the Netherlands for at least **three years**

"Legal residence" means you had a valid residence permit throughout. Gaps matter. A gap of more than six consecutive months abroad can reset the clock, so if you've been a frequent traveller for work, check your passport stamps carefully before you apply.

### The Option Procedure

The option procedure is faster, cheaper, and available to a much smaller group. I'll cover it in detail in its own section below.

## Naturalisation Requirements

Meeting the residence requirement is only the start. You also need to tick several other boxes.

### 1. Five Years of Continuous Legal Residence

As mentioned, this is the baseline. You count back five years from the date you intend to submit your application. Every day of that five-year period, you needed a valid residence permit. If you have a permanent residence permit (verblijfsvergunning voor onbepaalde tijd) or an EU long-term residence permit, that also counts.

If you are unsure about your residence history or permit status, check the [IND's permit records](https://ind.nl) and cross-reference with your [BSN registration history](/guides/legal/bsn-registration-guide-2026/). The BSN record at your municipality is often the cleanest proof of continuous residence.

### 2. The Inburgeringsexamen (Civic Integration Exam)

This is the requirement that trips people up most often. To naturalise, you must demonstrate Dutch language skills and knowledge of Dutch society. The standard way to do this is by passing the inburgeringsexamen at B1 level. The exam covers:

- Speaking (Spreekvaardigheid)
- Listening (Luistervaardigheid)
- Reading (Leesvaardigheid)
- Writing (Schrijfvaardigheid)
- Knowledge of Dutch Society (Kennis van de Nederlandse Maatschappij — KNM)
- Orientation on the Dutch Labour Market (Oriëntatie op de Nederlandse Arbeidsmarkt — ONA)

Alternatively, you can demonstrate your language level by showing:

- A Dutch diploma at MBO, HBO, or university level
- A state exam NT2 (Dutch as a Second Language), programme I or II
- A DALF or other recognised language certificate at B1 or higher
- You attended primary or secondary school in the Netherlands for at least eight years

### What the Inburgeringsexamen Actually Tests

There are six components, and you must pass each one:

- **KNM** (Kennis van de Nederlandse Maatschappij) — knowledge of Dutch society, history, politics, and everyday customs
- **Leesvaardigheid** (reading) — understanding written Dutch texts at B1 level
- **Schrijfvaardigheid** (writing) — producing written Dutch at B1 level
- **Luistervaardigheid** (listening) — understanding spoken Dutch
- **Spreekvaardigheid** (speaking) — answering questions and holding a basic conversation, assessed via a phone-based system
- **ONA** (Oriëntatie op de Nederlandse Arbeidsmarkt) — a portfolio-based component about your place in the Dutch job market

You do not sit all six in one sitting. Each is a separate exam, booked and taken at a DUO exam centre.

**Pass rates and retakes:** For well-prepared candidates, pass rates are around 90%. If you fail an individual component, you retake only that component — you do not need to redo the ones you have already passed. The retake fee is roughly €290 per attempt, which adds up quickly if you are underprepared for the speaking section in particular. That one surprises people because it is automated rather than with a live examiner, which takes some adjustment.

**Exemptions:** You do not need to sit the inburgeringsexamen if you:
- Hold a Dutch diploma from an MBO, HBO, or WO programme taught in Dutch (the key word is *taught in Dutch* — an English-medium Dutch degree does not count)
- Hold a state exam NT2 (Dutch as a Second Language) diploma at programme I or II level
- Are aged 67 or older
- Attended school in the Netherlands for at least eight years

**What "equivalent qualification" means:** An HBO or WO degree taught in Dutch counts as proof of civic integration. It is not about where you studied — it is about the language of instruction. If your Dutch university programme was English-medium, you still need the inburgering exam.

**Cost:** The DUO exam fee is approximately €290 per attempt. Language courses on top of this can run anywhere from €500 to €3,000 depending on the provider and your starting level.

**My advice:** Start preparing 12 to 18 months before you intend to apply for naturalisation. The speaking component in particular takes longer to develop than people expect. My [full inburgeringsexamen preparation guide](/guides/integration/inburgeringsexamen-preparation-guide-2026/) covers the best study approaches in detail. My [inburgering route builder tool](/tools/inburgering-route-builder/) can help you figure out which path fits your situation.

If you are at the early stages of learning Dutch, [Babbel vs Duolingo for learning Dutch](/guides/integration/babbel-vs-duolingo-learn-dutch-2026/) is a useful starting point, and I also have a roundup of [the best apps to learn Dutch in 2026](/guides/integration/best-apps-learn-dutch-2026/).

### 3. No Serious Criminal Record

You cannot naturalise if, in the four years before your application, you were convicted of an offence and received a sentence of more than six months. This includes sentences served abroad. Fines do not automatically disqualify you, but the IND does look at the full picture.

### 4. Renouncing Your Original Nationality

The Netherlands has a general rule against dual nationality. If you naturalise, you are normally required to renounce your current nationality. I'll explain all the exceptions in the dual nationality section below.

### 5. Not Receiving Social Assistance (Bijstand)

If you are receiving bijstand (social welfare) at the time of your application, this can be grounds for refusal. There are exceptions for people who became dependent on benefits through no fault of their own, but it's a grey area. If you're in this situation, get proper legal advice before applying.

## The Naturalisation Process, Step by Step

Here is what actually happens from start to finish.

### Step 1: Check Your Eligibility

Before you spend any money, verify that you meet all the requirements. Use the IND's online check tool at ind.nl, or book a consultation with your municipality's civil affairs desk (Burgerzaken). Some municipalities offer free pre-application advice sessions.

If you arrived in the Netherlands on a [highly skilled migrant visa](/guides/legal/highly-skilled-migrant-visa-netherlands-2026/), your residence period counts in full — provided you have maintained uninterrupted legal status.

### Step 2: Gather Your Documents

This takes longer than you'd think. Start collecting documents at least three months before you intend to apply. The core documents are:

- Valid passport
- Birth certificate (original or certified copy)
- Proof of continuous legal residence (passport stamps, IND residence history extract)
- Proof of passing the inburgeringsexamen (or exemption certificate)
- If applicable: marriage certificate, divorce certificate, proof of partnership
- Extract from the Personal Records Database (BRP — your municipality can provide this)
- Criminal record certificate from your country of origin (VOG buitenland), if you lived abroad in the past four years

Foreign documents almost always need to be legalised or apostilled. This is one of the biggest sources of delays. For a detailed breakdown, see my guide on [apostille and legalisation in the Netherlands](/guides/legal/apostille-legalization-netherlands-2026/).

### Step 3: Submit Your Application

You submit the naturalisation application at your municipality (gemeente), not directly to the IND. You book an appointment at the Burgerzaken desk. At the appointment, you hand over your documents, pay the fee (€1,048 in 2026), and receive a receipt.

The municipality checks your documents, then forwards the file to the IND for a decision. This handover can take a few weeks.

### Step 4: IND Assessment

The IND reviews your application. The statutory decision period is 12 months, but many applications are decided in six to nine months. The IND may request additional documents during this period. Respond quickly — delays in replying extend the timeline.

If the IND approves your application, they send the decision to the municipality.

### Step 5: The Naturalisation Ceremony

Once approved, your municipality will invite you to a naturalisation ceremony. In most cases, you have one year to attend. At the ceremony, you make a solemn declaration (plechtige verklaring) stating your allegiance to the Netherlands and your commitment to uphold its laws.

This is a legal requirement. If you miss it without a valid reason, your naturalisation does not take effect. Most municipalities hold ceremonies regularly — monthly or quarterly — and they range from rather formal to quite cheerful, depending on where you live.

After the ceremony, you receive your certificate of naturalisation (naturalisatiebesluit). You can then apply for a Dutch passport at the same municipality.

### Step 6: Apply for Your Dutch Passport

A Dutch passport costs €81.40 in 2026. You can apply at your municipality immediately after the ceremony. Processing time is typically five working days. You'll also want to apply for a Dutch identity card (€72.80) if you want a cheaper travel document for EU travel.

Once you have your Dutch ID, you'll want to update your [DigiD](/guides/digid-guide-expats-2026/) and relevant government registrations.

## The Option Procedure

The option procedure is a shorter, cheaper, and less bureaucratic route to Dutch nationality. It is available to people with a specific connection to the Netherlands or to Dutch citizens.

You can use the option procedure if you fall into one of these categories:

**Born in the Netherlands or raised here:** If you were born in the Netherlands and are stateless, or if you have lived in the Netherlands since birth and are now an adult, you may qualify.

**Adult child of a Dutch citizen:** If your father or mother is Dutch (or was Dutch at the time of your birth), you may be able to opt for Dutch nationality, even if you grew up abroad.

**Married or partnered with a Dutch citizen for 15+ years:** If you have been married to or in a registered partnership with a Dutch citizen for at least 15 years, and have lived in the Netherlands for the last 10 years, you can opt for Dutch nationality rather than going through the full naturalisation process. The residence requirement is different from the three-year rule that applies to naturalisation.

**Former Dutch citizen:** If you previously held Dutch nationality and lost it — for example, because you voluntarily acquired another nationality — you may be able to opt back in, subject to conditions.

**Born in the Netherlands Antilles or Aruba:** Specific rules apply to people from the former Dutch Caribbean territories.

The option procedure is submitted at the municipality and costs €188 (compared to €1,048 for naturalisation). The decision is made by the municipality itself, not the IND, which generally makes it faster — often just a few months.

Not everyone who qualifies knows they qualify. If you have any Dutch heritage or a long-term relationship with a Dutch citizen, it's worth checking whether the option procedure applies before you default to the standard naturalisation route.

## Dual Nationality: The Rules and Exceptions

This is the question I get most often, and it deserves a thorough answer because the rules are genuinely complex.

### The General Rule: No Dual Nationality

The Netherlands operates on the principle that naturalising Dutch citizens should renounce their original nationality. This is intended to prevent divided loyalties — a somewhat old-fashioned idea, but it's still the law.

When you naturalise, you make a formal declaration that you will renounce your current nationality. The Dutch side does not verify that you have actually done so — that is between you and your original country. But signing the declaration while having no intention of renouncing is legally problematic.

### Exceptions: When You CAN Keep Your Original Nationality

The exceptions are substantial, and they cover a large proportion of expats living in the Netherlands.

**1. EU/EEA and Swiss citizens**

If you hold the nationality of an EU member state, an EEA country (Norway, Iceland, Liechtenstein), or Switzerland, you are exempt from the renunciation requirement. You can become Dutch and keep your EU passport. This is probably the most significant exception.

**2. Recognised refugees**

If you have refugee status in the Netherlands (a verblijfsvergunning asiel), you are not required to renounce your nationality. In practice, many refugees cannot safely contact their country of origin to renounce, so this exception is both practical and humane.

**3. Stateless persons**

If renouncing your nationality would make you stateless, you are exempt.

**4. Impossible or unreasonable renunciation**

If your country of origin does not allow you to renounce your nationality, or if doing so would cause you serious harm (for example, the loss of property rights or pension entitlements that depend on citizenship), you may apply for an exemption. This is decided case by case by the IND.

**5. Married to a Dutch citizen for 15+ years**

Under specific long-term partnership rules, some applicants can retain their nationality.

**The Netherlands acquiring dual nationality through naturalisation abroad**

Note that this works in both directions. If you become Dutch and later voluntarily acquire another nationality, you may automatically lose your Dutch nationality. There are exceptions for EU citizens living in EU countries and for people married to a citizen of the country they are naturalising in. This is something to think about before you naturalise elsewhere.

### My Practical Advice

Before your application, sit down and think clearly about what renouncing your original nationality actually means for you. Does it affect your right to live and work in your home country? Your inheritance rights? Your pension? In some countries — Iran, for example — renunciation is legally impossible. In others, it is bureaucratically painful but possible. Get country-specific advice if you are unsure.

## Costs and Timeline

Let me be direct: this process is not cheap and it is not fast.

| Option procedure fee | €188 |
| Dutch passport | €81.40 |

| Document translations (estimate) | €100–€400 |
| Apostille/legalisation (per document) | €20–€100 |
| Language courses (if needed) | €500–€3,000 |

Children under 18 included in a parent's naturalisation application are free.

If you are currently benefiting from the [30% ruling](/guides/finance/30-percent-ruling-netherlands-2026/) or managing your tax situation as an expat, be aware that Dutch nationality itself does not change your tax status. But it does affect certain long-term financial planning. See my [Dutch tax system guide for expats](/guides/finance/dutch-tax-system-expats-guide-2026/) for context.

### Timeline

| Stage | Duration |
|---|---|
| Document collection | 1–3 months |
| Municipality appointment to submission | 2–4 weeks |
| IND processing | 6–12 months |
| Ceremony scheduling | 1–3 months after approval |
| Passport application | 5 working days |
| **Total (realistic estimate)** | **10–18 months** |

The IND's statutory maximum is 12 months for the processing stage alone. In busy periods, or if your documents need supplementing, the total process can take 18 months or more. Start early.

## What Changes After You Become Dutch?

A lot, actually. Here is what becoming a Dutch citizen means in practice.

### Freedom of Movement

You get an EU passport. You can live and work freely in any EU/EEA country and Switzerland without a permit. For many expats who came from outside the EU, this is the single biggest motivator. No more visa anxiety, no more IND renewals, no more wondering whether your permit will be extended.

If you use the [visa permit finder tool](/tools/visa-permit-finder/) currently, you'll find after naturalisation that it becomes mostly irrelevant for your own situation — though still useful if you're helping family members.

### Voting Rights

You can vote in all Dutch elections, including the Tweede Kamer (parliament), provincial elections, and European Parliament elections. As a resident you already had the right to vote in municipal elections, but national elections become open to you.

### No More IND Renewals

For expats who have spent years tracking permit expiry dates and renewal windows, the freedom from IND bureaucracy is genuinely liberating. Your residence is no longer conditional on employment, partnership, or any other factor.

### Access to Certain Jobs

Some government positions and specific roles in defence, intelligence, and the judiciary require Dutch nationality. If those career paths interest you, naturalisation opens doors that were previously closed.

### Dutch Passport

The Dutch passport consistently ranks among the most powerful travel documents in the world, offering visa-free or visa-on-arrival access to around 190 countries. If you currently hold a passport with more limited access, this is a meaningful practical upgrade.

### Some Administrative Updates

After naturalisation, update your records at your municipality (BRP), notify the [DigiD system](/guides/digid-guide-expats-2026/) of any changes, and update your details with the Belastingdienst. If you run a business, update your [KvK registration](/guides/work/kvk-registration-expat-guide-2026/) accordingly.

## Common Mistakes — And How to Avoid Them

After helping many expats through this process, I see the same errors repeatedly.

### Mistake 1: Starting the Document Collection Too Late

Birth certificates from certain countries — India, China, Morocco, Turkey, Egypt, and many others — can take weeks or months to obtain, translate, and legalise. I have seen applications delayed by four to six months purely because of a birth certificate. Start this the moment you decide to apply.

### Mistake 2: Not Checking for Residence Gaps

People assume five years of residence means five years of living in the Netherlands. In reality, it means five years without a gap of more than six consecutive months abroad. If you spent a long stretch working abroad, on sabbatical, or caring for family overseas, get an official residence history from the IND before you apply. Discovering a disqualifying gap after you've paid €1,048 is a miserable experience.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring the Dual Nationality Rules

Some people naturalise without fully understanding the renunciation requirement and then find themselves in an awkward legal position. Others who are EU citizens don't realise they don't need to renounce and spend months worrying unnecessarily. Read the dual nationality section of this guide carefully. When in doubt, consult a lawyer.

### Mistake 4: Not Preparing for the Inburgering Exam Early Enough

The inburgeringsexamen takes time to prepare for, especially if your Dutch is still developing. Many people underestimate how much preparation is needed for the speaking component in particular. Start formal preparation 12 to 18 months before you intend to apply for naturalisation.

### Mistake 5: Missing the Ceremony

The ceremony is a legal requirement. Missing it invalidates your approval. Put the date in every calendar you own, tell your partner, tell your employer. Take the day off work. Don't schedule anything else for that day.

### Mistake 6: Not Including Children

If you have minor children who are registered in the Netherlands, they can be included in your application for free. Some parents forget to list them, then face a separate (and expensive) process later. Check the eligibility criteria for each child when you apply.

### Mistake 7: Underestimating the Cost of Living Adjustment Period

This isn't directly about naturalisation, but I mention it because it's relevant to why some people delay applying: the overall cost of building a life in the Netherlands. If the application fee feels like a lot right now, my [cost of living in the Netherlands guide](/guides/housing/cost-of-living-netherlands-2026/) can help you plan your finances around major milestones like this.

## After Naturalisation: A Few Things Worth Knowing

Once you have your Dutch passport, a few practical questions tend to come up.

**Can I travel on my old passport?**
Until your old passport expires, it remains a valid travel document for countries that accept it. However, when entering and leaving the Netherlands and the EU, use your Dutch passport, since you are now a Dutch citizen and are legally required to use it.

**What happens to my DigiD?**
Your DigiD account continues to work. You may need to update your personal details if your nationality is recorded there. Check your account and make any necessary corrections.

**Do I need to inform my employer?**
If your employment is linked to a specific permit (such as a highly skilled migrant permit), naturalisation means the permit is no longer necessary. Inform your employer's HR team. This can simplify things considerably on both sides.

**What about my home country?**
If you renounced your original nationality as part of naturalisation, you need to check the legal implications for property ownership, inheritance, and any rights that depend on citizenship in your country of origin. Some countries impose restrictions on former citizens. Get local legal advice if necessary.

## Conclusion: Your Action Steps

Dutch nationality is a significant milestone. It is a legal, practical, and — for many people — emotional commitment to the life they have built here. The process is long and the paperwork is real, but it is manageable if you approach it systematically.

Here is what I suggest you do next:

1. **Check your eligibility today.** Count back five years from today and confirm your residence has been continuous and legal. If you have Dutch heritage or a long-term Dutch partner, check whether the option procedure applies.

2. **Start your document collection now.** Don't wait until you're ready to submit. Birth certificates and foreign criminal record extracts take time.

3. **Assess your inburgering situation.** Do you have a qualifying certificate already? If not, start your language learning journey. Use the [inburgering route builder](/tools/inburgering-route-builder/) to find the right path.

4. **Understand the dual nationality rules for your specific situation.** Particularly if you hold a non-EU passport, think carefully about what renunciation means for you.

5. **Budget for it.** Set aside at least €1,500 to €2,000 to cover fees, translations, and documents comfortably.

6. **Book a pre-application consultation at your municipality.** Most offer them, many are free, and they can save you from costly mistakes.

One last practical note: of all the things that cause delays in this process, apostilled documents are the biggest. Birth certificates from India, Morocco, Turkey, Egypt, and China in particular can take weeks or months to obtain, and the apostille or legalisation on top adds more time. Start collecting and legalising your documents now — well before you think you need them. Everything else in this process has a workaround or a fast-track option. The foreign birth certificate does not.
