## What Is an Apostille?

An apostille (pronounced "ah-po-STEEL") is a certificate that authenticates the origin of a public document so that it can be recognised in another country. It does not verify the content of the document — it confirms that the signature, seal, or stamp on the document is genuine.

The system was established by the Hague Convention of 5 October 1961, formally known as the Convention Abolishing the Requirement of Legalisation for Foreign Public Documents. If both the country where the document was issued and the country where it will be used are members of the Hague Convention, an apostille is all you need. No embassy queues, no additional legalisation steps.

The Netherlands has been a member of the Hague Convention since 1965. As of 2026, there are over 120 member countries — including the UK, the US, Australia, India, South Africa, and most of Europe. This means that for the majority of expats coming to the Netherlands, an apostille on your home-country documents is sufficient.

## Apostille vs Full Legalisation: Which Do You Need?

This is where I see a lot of confusion. The answer depends entirely on where your document was issued.

**If your country is a Hague Convention member:** You need an apostille. Once the apostille is on the document, it is valid in the Netherlands without any further steps (though you may still need a sworn translation — more on that below).

**If your country is NOT a Hague Convention member:** You need full legalisation. This is a multi-step process:

1. Authentication of the document by the relevant authority in your country (often the Ministry of Foreign Affairs or an equivalent body).
2. Legalisation by the Dutch embassy or consulate in your country.

Some countries have bilateral agreements with the Netherlands that simplify or bypass this process, so it is always worth checking directly with the Dutch embassy in your home country before assuming you need the full chain.

| Process | When it applies | Steps | Typical timeline |
|---|---|---|---|
| Apostille | Both countries are Hague Convention members | 1 step (apostille in country of origin) | Days to weeks |
| Full legalisation | One or both countries NOT in Hague Convention | 2–3 steps (national auth + Dutch embassy) | Weeks to months |
| Bilateral exemption | Countries with specific treaties with NL | Varies | Varies |

Countries outside the Hague Convention that many expats come from include several African nations, parts of the Middle East, and some Southeast Asian countries. Always verify current membership at the HCCH website (hcch.net) — membership does change.

## Which Documents Do Expats Typically Need Apostilled?

In my experience working with expats relocating to the Netherlands, these are the documents that most commonly require an apostille or legalisation:

**Birth certificate** — Required for registering at your gemeente (municipality), family reunification applications, and obtaining a [BSN number](/guides/legal/bsn-registration-guide-2026/). If you have children, their birth certificates will be needed too.

**Marriage or civil partnership certificate** — Required if you are registering a spouse or partner, applying for a family reunification permit, or updating your civil status in the Dutch system.

**University degree and transcripts** — Often required for a [highly skilled migrant visa](/guides/legal/highly-skilled-migrant-visa-netherlands-2026/) application, or when your employer needs to verify your qualifications for a regulated profession. You may also need these evaluated by EP Nuffic using our [diploma evaluator tool](/tools/diploma-evaluator/).

**Police clearance certificate** — Sometimes required for certain work permits, regulated professions (such as healthcare), or by specific employers. Also called a Certificate of Good Conduct or Criminal Record Check, depending on your country.

**Divorce certificate** — If you are previously divorced and registering a new relationship or marriage in the Netherlands, you will need proof of the dissolution of your prior marriage.

**Death certificate** — Required in some cases for inheritance matters, pension claims, or changes to civil status.

**Power of attorney documents** — If you have had a notary in your home country draw up a power of attorney for use in the Netherlands, it will typically need an apostille.

## How to Get Dutch Documents Apostilled

If you have a Dutch document that needs to be used abroad — for example, a Dutch birth certificate for use in the UK, or a Dutch degree for use in the US — you need to get it apostilled by a Dutch authority.

In the Netherlands, apostilles are issued by the **rechtbank** (district court). There are 11 district courts across the country, and you can go to any of them regardless of where the document was issued.

### Step-by-step process:

1. **Check eligibility.** The document must be a public document: issued by a Dutch authority, signed by a public official, or authenticated by a notary. Personal copies and unofficial documents cannot receive an apostille.

2. **Go to the rechtbank.** Most courts handle apostille requests at their civil registry desk (griffie). You can walk in or, at some courts, submit by post. Check your local rechtbank's website for current procedures — some require an appointment.

3. **Submit your document.** You bring (or send) the original document. For documents that were issued by a private notary, the notary's signature must already be registered with the rechtbank.

4. **Pay the fee.** The current fee is **€22.80 per document** (as of 2026). This is a fixed court fee and is the same across all district courts.

5. **Collect the apostille.** Standard processing is 3 to 5 working days. Some courts offer a same-day or next-day service — call ahead to confirm.

The apostille certificate is physically attached to or stamped on your document. It will be in Dutch and French (the official languages of the Hague Convention process) and will include a reference number you can use to verify its authenticity.

## How to Get Foreign Documents Apostilled for Use in the Netherlands

This is the direction most expats need to go: getting documents from your home country apostilled so they are valid in the Netherlands.

The short answer: **you need to get the apostille in the country where the document was issued.** A Dutch rechtbank cannot apostille a British birth certificate or an American university degree. Each country designates its own competent authorities for issuing apostilles.

### By country of origin (examples):

**United Kingdom:** The UK Legalisation Office (part of the Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office) handles apostilles. Postal applications currently take around 10 working days; a faster service is available for an additional fee. Some documents must be notarised first.

**United States:** Each US state has its own apostille authority, usually the Secretary of State's office. Processing times and fees vary by state — some states process within 24 hours, others take several weeks.

**India:** The Ministry of External Affairs (MEA) in New Delhi handles apostilles for Indian documents, with Regional Authentication Centres in major cities. Processing times have improved significantly in recent years but can still take 1 to 2 weeks.

**South Africa:** The Department of International Relations and Cooperation (DIRCO) issues apostilles.

**Australia:** The Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade (DFAT) handles Australian apostilles.

For any country, I always recommend:
- Checking the HCCH website for the official designated authority
- Requesting a certified copy from the issuing authority before sending the original anywhere
- Allowing at least 4 weeks in your planning, even if the official timeline is shorter

## Sworn Translations: When You Need Them

An apostille confirms that a document is genuine. It does not translate the document. If your document is in a language that Dutch authorities cannot read, you will need a certified translation.

In the Netherlands, certified translations for official purposes must be produced by a **beëdigd vertaler** — a sworn translator registered in the Wbtv (Wet beëdigde tolken en vertalers) register. Only sworn translators can produce translations that are accepted by the IND, municipalities, courts, and other government bodies.

You can find sworn translators at: **www.bureauwbtv.nl** — this is the official register. Always verify that the translator is currently registered before commissioning a translation.

### What to expect:

- Sworn translations are priced per word or per page. For a standard birth certificate (1–2 pages), expect to pay roughly €100–€200.
- Turnaround is typically 3 to 5 working days for standard requests; express services are available.
- The translator attaches a signed declaration confirming the accuracy of the translation. This declaration is what makes it "sworn."
- The apostille goes on the original document. The sworn translation is a separate document that accompanies it.

I always advise clients to get the apostille and the sworn translation at roughly the same time — there is no need to wait for one before commissioning the other.

## Common Pitfalls (and How to Avoid Them)

**Starting too late.** This is by far the most common mistake. If you need documents for a work permit application or a BSN registration, build in at least 6 to 8 weeks for the full process, including obtaining originals, getting the apostille, and commissioning a translation.

**Apostilling a photocopy.** An apostille can only be placed on the original document or an official certified copy from the issuing authority. Your home scanner copy will not be accepted.

**Using an unregistered translator.** I have seen clients pay for translations from bilingual friends or unlisted translation agencies, only to have them rejected by the IND. Always use the Wbtv register.

**Assuming the apostille is enough.** The apostille authenticates the document. It does not automatically mean the document will be accepted by every Dutch authority in every situation. The IND has specific requirements, as do municipalities and professional bodies. Always check the requirements of the specific institution you are submitting to.

**Forgetting that some documents expire.** Police clearance certificates, in particular, are often only valid for 6 months. Get them as close to your application date as possible.

**Not checking if your country is a Hague member.** Before assuming you need an apostille, confirm that your home country is actually a member of the Hague Convention. If it is not, the apostille route does not apply and you will need full legalisation — a different and longer process.

**Sending originals without keeping copies.** Always scan or photograph every document before sending it anywhere. Originals get lost — rarely, but it happens.

## FAQ

**How long does it take to get an apostille in the Netherlands?**

At a Dutch rechtbank (district court), the standard processing time is 3 to 5 working days. Some courts offer an express service where you can collect the apostille the same day or next day, though this varies by location. If you are getting a foreign document apostilled in your home country, timelines differ widely — the UK Legalisation Office, for example, currently processes postal applications in around 10 working days, with a faster service available for an additional fee.

**Do I need a sworn translation alongside my apostille?**

It depends on how the document will be used. The apostille itself authenticates the signature or seal on the document — it does not translate the content. If your document is in a language other than Dutch, English, French, or German, most Dutch authorities (including the IND, municipalities, and courts) will require a certified translation by a beëdigd vertaler (sworn translator) registered with the Rechtspraak. For documents in English, many institutions accept them without translation, but always confirm this with the receiving body before you pay for a translation.

**My country is not a member of the Hague Convention. What do I do?**

If your country has not signed the Hague Convention, the apostille process does not apply. Instead, your documents typically need to go through full legalisation: authentication by your country's foreign ministry or equivalent authority, followed by legalisation at the Dutch embassy or consulate in your country. This process is slower, costs more, and the exact steps depend on your country of origin. Contact the Dutch embassy in your home country for country-specific requirements before you start.

**Can I get an apostille on a copy rather than the original document?**

Generally, no. The apostille is attached to or stamped on the original document (or an officially certified copy issued by the relevant authority). A photocopy you have made yourself will not be accepted. In some cases, a notary can certify a copy and that certified copy can then receive an apostille — but this varies by document type and country. When in doubt, request an official certified copy from the issuing authority (e.g. your local registry office) and get the apostille placed on that.

## Step-by-Step: Getting Foreign Documents Apostilled for Use in the Netherlands

If you need to apostille a foreign document for use in the Netherlands — for example, a birth certificate, marriage certificate, or degree — here is the general process:

**Step 1: Identify the competent authority in your home country.**
The Hague Conference on Private International Law maintains a country-by-country list of competent authorities authorised to issue apostilles. In the UK, the FCDO Legalisation Office issues apostilles. In the US, it depends on the state — usually the Secretary of State's office for state documents, and the US Department of State for federal documents.

**Step 2: Obtain a certified copy of your document.**
Most apostille authorities will not apostille a personal photocopy. Request an official certified copy from the issuing body (e.g. the register office, university, or court) if you do not have the original.

**Step 3: Submit to the competent authority.**
Submit your document with the required fee. Processing times vary: the UK FCDO typically takes 5–15 business days by post; some US states offer walk-in service. Many countries now offer electronic apostilles (e-apostilles) — check whether the Dutch receiving authority accepts these.

**Step 4: Obtain a certified translation if needed.**
If your apostilled document is not in Dutch, English, French, or German, most Dutch authorities will require a certified translation by a beëdigd vertaler. Translations must include the translator's statement of accuracy and their certification credentials.

**Step 5: Present to the Dutch authority.**
Bring or submit the original apostilled document (with translation if needed) to the IND, gemeente, court, or other Dutch body that requested it. Keep a certified copy for your records.
