## What Relocation Services Actually Do

The term "relocation service" gets used loosely. It can mean anything from a single housing agent to a full-service company that holds your hand from before you land until six months after. When you are comparing options, the most important thing to establish is what, specifically, is included.

Here is a breakdown of the main services relocation companies offer in the Netherlands.

### Visa and Immigration Support

This is where the real complexity lives, especially for non-EU nationals. A good relocation company will either manage your IND (Immigration and Naturalisation Service) application themselves or work closely with an immigration lawyer to do so.

For people on a [highly skilled migrant (kennismigrant) visa](/guides/legal/highly-skilled-migrant-visa-netherlands-2026/), the employer usually handles the actual application, but a relocation company can manage the document gathering, chase the IND on timelines, and prepare you for what comes next. They will also know the nuances: when a document needs an apostille, which gemeente is fastest for post-arrival registration, whether your family member qualifies for a dependent permit.

What they do not do, usually, is provide legal advice. If your situation is genuinely complicated — previous visa refusals, complex marital situation, dual nationality issues — you will often need an actual immigration lawyer, not a relocation coordinator.

### Housing Search

The Dutch housing market is brutal. I say this having watched it up close for years. Vacancy rates in Amsterdam, Utrecht, and The Hague hover around 1–2%. For expats without a Dutch rental history, a Dutch employer reference in the right format, or a guarantor, getting a landlord to pick your application is genuinely hard.

Relocation companies have relationships with landlords and real estate agents (makelaars). They know which properties are not listed publicly. They know what landlords in Amsterdam want to see in an application versus what landlords in Eindhoven prefer. They can negotiate rental terms, review the contract before you sign it, and flag the clauses that should concern you.

This is, in my opinion, the area where relocation services provide the most tangible value for money — particularly for expats arriving without a Dutch network.

See my full guide to [finding housing in the Netherlands as an expat](/guides/housing/finding-housing-netherlands-expats-2026/) for what the process looks like with and without a relocation company.

### School Search

For families moving with children, finding the right school is often the most stressful part of the whole move. Relocation companies can assess your children's language level, age, and educational background, then shortlist appropriate options: local Dutch schools, international schools, or bilingual schools.

They will know which international schools have waiting lists that stretch 18 months (most of them in Amsterdam), which schools have places available, and what the admission process looks like. They can arrange school visits, attend them with you if needed, and help you understand the differences between British, American, IB, and Dutch curricula.

My full guide to [international schools in the Netherlands](/guides/daily-life/international-schools-netherlands-2026/) covers the full range of school options in detail — but if you are moving as a family, this is the one service I would keep even if I cut everything else.

### Gemeente Registration

Registration at your local municipality (gemeente) is mandatory, and it unlocks your [BSN number](/guides/legal/bsn-registration-guide-2026/) — the key that opens every other administrative door in the country.

A relocation company can help you book the appointment (which in Amsterdam can take 4–6 weeks to get), prepare your documents correctly, and sometimes accompany you to the appointment. In a handful of cities, companies with established relationships can facilitate faster appointments through dedicated expat desks.

That said: gemeente registration is also something most people can do entirely themselves. The documents you need are predictable. The process, while slow, is not technically difficult. Unless you have a genuinely unusual situation (complex family structure, foreign documents that need special legalisation), I would not pay for this as a standalone service.

### Bank Account Opening

Opening a Dutch bank account as a new arrival is more difficult than it should be. Traditional banks like ING, ABN AMRO, and Rabobank require a BSN, which you do not have until you have registered, which you cannot do until you have an address, which you cannot secure without — in some cases — proof of income from a Dutch bank account. It can feel circular.

Relocation companies can guide you through the sequence and help you open an account with the right bank for your situation. Some have relationships with expat banking desks at major banks that can speed things up.

My recommendation, regardless of whether you use a relocation service: set up a Wise account before you arrive. It works without a BSN, handles international transfers at fair mid-market exchange rates, and will carry you through the first weeks while you wait for everything else to fall into place.

### Orientation Tours

Most full-service packages include one or two orientation days in your new city: a tour of neighbourhoods, introductions to local services, guidance on transport, sometimes a briefing on Dutch culture and workplace norms.

These are genuinely useful if you are arriving with no prior knowledge of the Netherlands. If you have visited before, done your research, or have Dutch colleagues, they are probably not worth paying for separately.

## What Relocation Services Cost in the Netherlands

Prices vary significantly by company, city, and package scope. Here is an honest picture based on what I see in the market.

**Basic package — single person, immigration and registration focus**
Typically €1,500–€3,000. Usually covers: visa document preparation, BSN appointment support, and one or two hours of consultation. Does not include housing search.

**Mid-range package — immigration + housing**
Typically €3,000–€7,000. Covers: immigration support, housing search (usually with a set number of property viewings), rental contract review, gemeente registration, and some administrative follow-up. Most suitable for single people or couples without children.

**Full-service family package**
Typically €7,000–€15,000. Covers everything in the mid-range package plus school search, orientation days, settling-in support, and sometimes help with health insurance, car import, and social registration follow-up. For families with two or more children moving to Amsterdam or The Hague, this is the price range you are looking at.

**Employer-negotiated packages**
If your company contracts directly with a relocation company, the rates are often higher — €10,000–€20,000 per family — but you are not paying, your employer is. In that case, the price is not your concern; the quality of the company is.

A few things to watch for:

- **Add-on creep.** Some companies advertise a low base price, then add charges for every service beyond the initial consultation. Always ask for a written list of what is and is not included.
- **Hourly billing vs. flat packages.** Some companies bill by the hour. If your move takes longer than expected (which it usually does), costs can exceed the flat-fee equivalent.
- **VAT.** Most relocation companies charge 21% BTW on top of their listed price. Make sure you are comparing VAT-inclusive figures.

## When a Relocation Service Is Worth the Money

Let me be direct: relocation services are not a luxury for people who cannot be bothered. For the right situations, they are genuinely worth it.

### You Are on an Employer-Sponsored Move

If your employer is paying, this is not a difficult calculation. Use the service. Use all of it. The only risk is that the company your employer has contracted with is not very good — in which case, push back and ask to use a different one. (This happens more than people realise. Employers often have a preferred vendor contract that is years old and no longer reflects the best options.)

### You Are a Family Moving to Amsterdam, Utrecht, or The Hague

Housing in these cities is genuinely difficult. The combination of a competitive rental market, international school waiting lists, and the need to get everything moving simultaneously before a job start date makes it very hard to manage alone. A good relocation company pays for itself if it gets you a housing contract two months faster than you would have managed on your own.

### You Are Non-EU and Your Visa Situation Is Anything Other Than Simple

If you are coming on a highly skilled migrant visa and everything is in order, you can likely manage the immigration paperwork with your employer's HR team. But if your partner is coming from a third country, if there are children with different nationalities, or if any of your documents involve countries with complicated authentication requirements, having someone who knows the IND processes well is worth paying for.

### You Have a Short Runway Before Your Start Date

If your employer needs you in the Netherlands in six weeks and you have not found housing yet, the speed and contacts that a relocation company provides become genuinely valuable. Under normal circumstances, you might spend three months finding a rental in Amsterdam. A relocation company with active connections can sometimes compress that timeline considerably.

### You Have Never Lived Abroad Before

There is a cognitive load to international relocation that is easy to underestimate. If you have moved countries before, you know how to pace yourself, what to prioritise, and what bureaucratic chaos to expect. If this is your first time, having someone manage the sequence for you — so nothing critical falls through the cracks — has real value beyond just the administrative tasks themselves.

## When You Should Skip It

For a lot of expats, particularly those who move to the Netherlands independently, a full relocation package is overkill.

### You Are an EU Citizen Making a Relatively Simple Move

EU citizens do not need visa support. You can register at the gemeente yourself, which is free. The process is the same regardless of whether you have a relocation company beside you. You still need to sort housing and banking yourself, but these are manageable with a good guide and some patience.

### You Are Moving Alone, Without Children, to a Mid-Sized City

Housing in Eindhoven, Groningen, Arnhem, or Tilburg is significantly more available than in Amsterdam or Utrecht. The school question does not apply. The administrative steps are the same as anywhere else in the Netherlands, but the waiting times are shorter. A self-guided approach using good resources (like [my checklist for your first 30 days in the Netherlands](/guides/legal/first-30-days-netherlands-expat-checklist-2026/)) is entirely feasible.

### You Have Already Secured Housing Before Arrival

If your company has put you up in corporate housing, or if you have secured a rental through a contact or a private arrangement, the biggest variable is already handled. What remains is administrative work that, while tedious, is manageable.

### You Have Time

The main reason relocation companies add speed is because most expats are racing against a job start date. If you have two or three months before you need to be fully settled, you can do most of the research, admin, and appointments yourself without the pressure that makes professional help attractive.

## How to Evaluate a Relocation Company: What to Ask

If you have decided that a relocation service makes sense for your situation, the next question is which one. The quality difference between companies is significant. Here is how I would evaluate them.

### Ask for a Written Service List

"We handle everything" is not a service list. Ask them to give you a written document that specifies exactly which services are included, how many property viewings are covered, how many school consultations, and what happens if the first housing options do not work out. If they cannot or will not produce this, do not work with them.

### Ask Who Will Actually Be Your Contact

Some companies front their pitch with a senior consultant, then hand off day-to-day contact to a junior coordinator. Ask directly: who will I be working with for housing, and who will accompany me to the gemeente? Seniority and experience matter when the IND sends an unexpected request or your rental contract has a clause you do not understand.

### Check EuRA Membership

EuRA (European Relocation Association) is the main industry body for European relocation companies. Membership does not guarantee quality, but it does mean the company has agreed to a code of conduct and professional standards. SARA (the Dutch relocation industry association) is the Dutch equivalent. Neither is a substitute for checking reviews, but they are a basic filter.

### Read the Reviews Carefully

Look for reviews from people in a similar situation to yours. A company that gets excellent reviews from single tech workers may not be the right choice for a family with three children who needs a school in The Hague within eight weeks. Trustpilot and Google are both useful; so are expat Facebook groups for the Netherlands, which tend to have unfiltered opinions.

### Ask About Their Housing Network

Specifically: how many landlords or property management companies do they have active relationships with? In which cities? Do they work with social housing (unlikely to be relevant for most expats) or purely in the private rental market? How many of their placements are in properties not listed on public platforms like Pararius or Funda?

### Be Wary of Package Inflation

Some companies add services to their packages that sound impressive but cost almost nothing to deliver: welcome packs, city maps, lists of expat Facebook groups. These are fine as extras, but they should not be justification for a higher price point. Focus on the three things that genuinely require local knowledge and relationships: housing, immigration, and school search.

## The Hybrid Approach (What Most Clients of Mine Actually Do)

Many of my clients do not buy a full relocation package. What they do instead is:

1. Use their employer's HR team for the visa and IND application.
2. Hire a housing specialist (a makelaar or dedicated housing search agent) specifically for the rental search — often at a cost of one month's rent, which is standard in the Netherlands.
3. Handle gemeente registration, BSN, and bank account themselves, using guides like this one.
4. Book a session or two with me (or another expat coach) to ask questions and get their documents checked before appointments.

This approach typically costs €1,500–€3,000 all in for a single person or couple, and it concentrates the spending where local expertise actually matters. For a family that also needs school help, adding a school search consultation brings the total to perhaps €4,000–€5,000.

That is meaningfully less than a full-service package, and in my experience the outcomes are very similar — as long as you are willing to put in some time yourself.

## After the Relocation Company Hands Off

One thing worth knowing: most relocation companies consider their job done once you have a rental contract signed, a BSN on paper, and a school place confirmed. What comes after — sorting health insurance, importing a car, figuring out Dutch income tax, understanding the 30% ruling, registering a business if you are self-employed — is often not covered.

Make sure you know when the service ends, and what you will need to sort for yourself afterwards. ## A Word on What Relocation Companies Cannot Fix

I want to be honest about the limits, because some people arrive with expectations that no relocation company can meet.

**They cannot speed up the IND.** The IND processes visa applications on its own timeline. A relocation company can make sure your documents are complete and submitted correctly, but they cannot expedite the decision.

**They cannot guarantee housing in a specific neighbourhood.** In Amsterdam, the supply constraints are so severe that even with the best connections, your options may be limited. A company that promises to find you housing in De Pijp within three weeks is probably overselling.

**They cannot make the Dutch rental market affordable.** If the budget in your relocation package does not match what properties actually cost in your target city, expect friction. This is a conversation worth having before you sign anything.

**They cannot prevent culture shock.** The Netherlands is a wonderful country to live in. It is also, for many expats, unexpectedly difficult: the directness, the bureaucracy, the housing pressure, the grey weather in winter. A relocation company handles the logistics; the adjustment is yours to make.

## My Honest Summary

After eight years in this industry, here is what I actually tell friends when they ask me whether to hire a relocation company:

If your employer is paying, take the full service and focus your energy on choosing a good company rather than questioning whether to use one.

If you are paying yourself, and you are a single person or couple without children, moving to anywhere other than Amsterdam or Utrecht, and you have more than six weeks before your job starts — you probably do not need a full-service package. Invest in a good housing agent, use free guides for the administrative steps, and hire a coach for an hour or two to check your documents.

If you are moving as a family, moving to one of the main cities, or dealing with a complex visa situation — the right relocation company is worth the fee. Just make sure it is the right company, not the first one your employer's HR department recommends without checking the reviews.

The Netherlands is not an easy country to settle into. But it is manageable. Millions of expats have done it, many without professional help. The question is not whether you could do it alone — you probably could. The question is whether the time, stress, and risk of doing it alone is worth more or less than what a good relocation company would charge you to handle it.

Only you can answer that. But at least now you have an honest picture of what you would be buying.
