I left the Netherlands for the first time after four years. I had a list of things to do, a rough timeline in my head, and confidence that it would all be fairly simple. It was not. Within two weeks of deregistering at the gemeente, I had received a letter from my health insurer saying I owed money for cancelled coverage, a message from the Belastingdienst about zorgtoeslag I had not properly stopped, and a DigiD activation reminder I could no longer act on from abroad.

Deregistering — uitschrijven from the BRP (Basisregistratie Personen) — is a single administrative act, but it triggers a chain of consequences across health insurance, tax, benefits, pension, and banking. None of those things sort themselves out automatically. Every one of them needs a deliberate action on your part, and most have specific timing rules that catch people out.

This guide takes you through each step, in the right order, with the right timing. I have also added a master timeline at the bottom — three months out, one month out, one week out, and departure day — so you can plan backwards from your date.

For a broader picture of everything involved in leaving the country, my [leaving Netherlands expat checklist](/guides/legal/leaving-netherlands-expat-checklist-2026/) covers the full picture including employer, visa, and schooling matters. This article goes deeper on the administrative and legal deregistration process specifically.

## What Is the BRP and Why Deregistration Matters

The **BRP** (Basisregistratie Personen) is the Dutch population register. Every person legally resident in the Netherlands is registered in it with their gemeente (municipality). Your entry in the BRP is what connects your BSN to your address, your tax file, your health insurance entitlement, your benefit eligibility, and your pension accrual.

When you deregister, the gemeente marks you as *geëmigreerd* (emigrated) in the BRP. That entry is then shared with:

- The Belastingdienst (Dutch Tax Authority)
- The Sociale Verzekeringsbank (SVB — pension)
- The Centraal Administratie Kantoor (CAK — health insurance oversight)
- Your health insurer (notified by the CAK)
- The Belastingdienst Toeslagen (benefits department)

Some of those downstream effects happen automatically. Most require follow-up actions from you. The sections below cover each one.

## Step 1: Deregister at the Gemeente (Uitschrijven BRP)

This is the anchor of everything else. Get this step right and the timing of everything downstream becomes clear.

### When to go

You can deregister **up to 5 days before your departure date** or **within 5 days after leaving**. I recommend doing it as close to your actual departure date as possible — on the day before, or the morning of departure if your gemeente is open early enough.

Deregistering too early creates an awkward gap: you are administratively gone but physically still here, which complicates health insurance, tax residency, and benefit entitlement.

### What to bring

- Valid passport or Dutch ID card
- Your BSN (bring a payslip or letter showing it, in case)
- Your confirmed departure date
- Your destination address (or at minimum, the destination country)

If you do not yet have a permanent address abroad, you can provide just the country. You will be registered as *vertrokken onbekend waarheen* if completely unknown, but providing at least the country is better practice.

### How to do it

Visit the burgerzaken desk at your gemeente in person. Most municipalities do not allow this by post or online, although a small number (including Amsterdam for certain cases) have online options — check your gemeente's website first. Bring identification.

The process takes about 10–15 minutes. You will receive a confirmation letter or printout. **Keep this document** — it is your proof of deregistration date and you will need it for your health insurer, your tax filing, and potentially your employer.

## Step 2: Cancel Dutch Health Insurance

### The timing rule

Your legal obligation to hold Dutch health insurance (basisverzekering) ends on your **uitschrijfdatum** — not before, not after. Cancelling even one day early exposes you to a fine from the CAK. Forgetting to cancel means you continue paying premiums for coverage you cannot legally use.

Contact your insurer as soon as you have your confirmed deregistration date and ask them to end the policy on that exact date. Most major insurers (Zilveren Kruis, CZ, Menzis, VGZ) have an emigration cancellation form — either online or downloadable from their website.

### What you will need

- Your uitschrijving confirmation letter from the gemeente
- Your policy number
- Your departure date / deregistration date
- A forwarding address for the final invoice and any refund

### Premiums and refunds

Premiums are usually collected monthly in advance. If you deregister mid-month, you may be owed a partial premium refund. The insurer will calculate this and send a final settlement — usually within 4–6 weeks. Make sure they have a bank account that will still be active.

### Aanvullende verzekering (supplemental insurance)

If you have supplemental cover, that also needs to be cancelled separately in most cases. Check your policy documents or call the insurer's emigration line directly.

## Step 3: File Your Final Tax Return — the M-Biljet

### What the M-form is

The **M-biljet** (or M-form) is the migration tax return. It covers two partial periods in the same tax year: the months you were a Dutch resident, and the rest of the year when you were not. It is filed separately from the standard P-form (jaaraangifte) that residents use.

You file it for the calendar year in which you left — so if you leave in June 2026, you file the M-form in early 2027 (after the tax year ends). The Belastingdienst will usually send you a C-form invitation automatically once your deregistration is processed, but even if they do not, you are obliged to file.

### Why you are often owed a refund

Dutch employers withhold tax based on a full year's income assumption. If you leave in June, only six months of income is taxed in the Netherlands — but your employer withheld as if you were earning for twelve. The M-biljet recalculates this and usually generates a refund. I have seen refunds of €1,500–€3,000 for people leaving mid-year.

### What deductions you can still claim

For the period you were resident, you can still claim:

- Mortgage interest deduction (hypotheekrenteaftrek) for the months you were resident
- Healthcare deductions (if relevant)
- Charitable donations
- Study costs (where applicable)
- Any remaining 30% ruling benefit

### How to file

The M-form is unfortunately not available in the standard online Mijn Belastingdienst portal. You must download it as a PDF or use the specific **Aangifte M** app (separate from the standard belastingdienst app). It is more time-consuming than the regular return. Consider using a tax advisor if you have a complex situation — 30% ruling, company car, or business income.

## Step 4: Cancel Zorgtoeslag and Huurtoeslag

### Why timing is critical

**Zorgtoeslag** (healthcare benefit) and **huurtoeslag** (rent benefit) are both means-tested subsidies paid in advance. If you do not stop them on your deregistration date, the Belastingdienst Toeslagen will eventually discover you were no longer resident and demand the money back — with interest. This process is called **terugvordering** (reclaim) and it can catch you months or even years after leaving.

### How to stop them

Log into [toeslagen.nl](https://www.belastingdienst.nl/wps/wcm/connect/nl/toeslagen/) using your DigiD and stop both benefits effective from your uitschrijfdatum. You will need your DigiD — see the section below on why this needs to happen before you lose BRP access.

You can also call the Belastingdienst Toeslagen line (0800-0543) to stop them by phone, but online is faster and gives you a confirmation.

### What happens to overpayments and underpayments

Toeslagen are settled at the end of the year via a definitieve toekenning (final assessment). If you received too much (because the benefit ran past your departure), you repay the overage. If you received too little (unusual in emigration cases), you receive a small extra payment. Either way, a bank account still receiving Dutch payments is important for at least 12 months after leaving.

For a full overview of zorgtoeslag and huurtoeslag eligibility rules, see my [zorgtoeslag and huurtoeslag guide for expats](/guides/finance/zorgtoeslag-huurtoeslag-expats-2026/).

## Step 5: Sort Your DigiD Before You Leave

This is one of the most overlooked steps and one of the most consequential.

### What happens to DigiD after deregistration

Your DigiD account is tied to your BSN registration in the BRP. After you deregister, your access level may be reduced — and if your account lapses or needs to be reactivated, you will not be able to receive the Dutch-address activation letter. Reinstating DigiD from abroad is possible but requires a visit to a Dutch consulate, which is inconvenient.

### What to download before leaving

Go through MijnOverheid and every government portal you use, and download:

- Tax returns for the last 5 years (PDF from Belastingdienst)
- Pension overview from Mijnpensioenoverzicht.nl
- Jaaropgave (annual income statements) from employers
- Toeslagen letters and settlement documents
- CAK / health insurance correspondence
- Any official rulings (30% ruling decision, residence permit)
- Gemeente registration history (uittreksel BRP — you may need this to prove Dutch residency abroad for pension purposes)

You can request a BRP uittreksel (extract) at your gemeente appointment when you deregister. Ask for one — it costs about €15 and can save significant hassle later.

### Keeping DigiD alive from abroad

If you will still need to file the M-biljet (you will), check your pension statements, or interact with Dutch government services from abroad, you want to keep DigiD functional. The **DigiD app** is the most reliable way to maintain access from a foreign phone number. Set this up and test it before you leave.

See the full [DigiD guide for expats](/guides/legal/digid-guide-expats-2026/) for setup steps and how to manage your account from abroad.

## Step 6: Bank Accounts

### Keep at least one account open

Refunds arrive after you leave. Health insurance premium refunds, M-biljet tax refunds, zorgtoeslag final settlements, and rental deposit repayments can all arrive 3–12 months after your departure date. You need a Dutch IBAN to receive them without international transfer fees.

My recommendation: keep one Dutch current account open for at least 12 months after leaving. Most banks (ABN AMRO, ING, Rabobank) will allow this for a fee — or switch to a fee-free account before leaving if you do not want the monthly cost.

### Which banks allow non-resident accounts

- **ABN AMRO:** Allows non-resident accounts with some restrictions. Notify them of your address change.
- **ING:** Similar policy; non-resident maintenance fees may apply.
- **Rabobank:** Will generally close accounts once you are deregistered unless you take action beforehand.
- **bunq:** Cloud-based and generally flexible for non-residents.
- **N26:** Typically closes accounts when you lose Dutch residency.

Contact your bank before your departure date and ask specifically about their non-resident account policy.

## Step 7: Pension — AOW and Occupational Pension

### State pension (AOW)

The **AOW** (Algemene Ouderdomswet) is the Dutch state pension. You accrue 2% of the full amount for every year you are registered in the Netherlands between age 15 and the current AOW age (currently 67). Gaps in residency reduce your final AOW amount.

When you deregister:
- Accrual stops immediately
- Years already accrued are preserved permanently
- When you reach AOW age, SVB (Sociale Verzekeringsbank) will pay your partial AOW to a foreign bank account

You do not need to do anything to preserve your AOW. SVB tracks residency history and calculates your entitlement automatically. Keep a record of your registration and deregistration dates.

### Voluntary AOW contributions (vrijwillige verzekering)

If you plan to return to the Netherlands later or want to fill the gap in your AOW record, you can pay voluntary SVB contributions while abroad. This keeps your AOW accrual running for up to 10 years after leaving. Applications must be submitted within **1 year of deregistration** — after that, the option closes.

Contact SVB (svb.nl) before leaving to discuss this option if you think it applies to you.

### Occupational pension (werknemerspensioen)

Your employer pension, managed through a pension fund (pensioenfonds), is separate from AOW. When you leave employment in the Netherlands:

- Accumulated pension is preserved (slapersrecht)
- You receive a pension letter from the fund confirming your entitlement
- At Dutch pension age, the fund pays it to your foreign account
- You can also transfer it to a pension scheme in another EU/EEA country in some cases (value transfer — waardeoverdracht)

Register your new address with your pension fund before leaving so they can find you when the time comes. Mijnpensioenoverzicht.nl lists all Dutch pension funds you have entitlements with.

## Step 8: Phone and Internet Contracts

Dutch mobile and internet contracts have a standard **opzegtermijn** (cancellation notice period) of one month, though some fixed-line contracts run for 2 years with break clauses.

### What to do

1. Check your contract end date and notice period — these are in your original agreement or in your provider's customer portal
2. Send a written cancellation notice (email or certified letter) at least 1 month before your last required payment date
3. Return any rented equipment (router, decoder) — failing to do so results in an additional charge

If you are leaving mid-contract, check whether an emigration clause (emigratieclausule) applies. T-Mobile, KPN, and Ziggo all allow contract termination on emigration proof — usually your deregistration confirmation from the gemeente. You may need to pay a reduced exit fee rather than the full remaining contract value.

Keep your Dutch SIM active until at least your DigiD account is secured on the app — many two-factor authentication steps still use Dutch mobile numbers.

## Step 9: Rental Contract

Rental contracts in the Netherlands typically have a one-month notice period for indefinite-term contracts (huurovereenkomst voor onbepaalde tijd). Fixed-term contracts (for example, 12-month) end on the agreed date without additional notice required, though some landlords will want confirmation.

### Steps for ending your tenancy

1. Send written notice (aanzegging) at least 1 calendar month before your last day — email with read receipt is acceptable for most contracts, but check your contract for required method
2. Arrange a final property inspection (eindinspectie) with your landlord or letting agent
3. Get the deposit return process started in writing — your landlord has up to 14 days after the final inspection to return the deposit (waarborg)
4. Transfer utilities to your landlord or new tenant before leaving

If your deposit is being withheld unfairly, you have the right to dispute it through the Huurcommissie (rental tribunal) — even from abroad. Do not let a foreign departure stop you from claiming what is owed.

For a full breakdown of your rights as a tenant, see my [Dutch rental contract rights guide for expats](/guides/legal/dutch-rental-contract-rights-expats-2026/).

## Step 10: Redirect Your Mail

**PostNL Verhuisservice** allows you to redirect all mail from your Dutch address to a new address — including internationally. The service costs around €35–€55 for six months.

Set this up at [postnl.nl/verhuisservice](https://www.postnl.nl) at least one week before your departure. You can redirect to a foreign address, a family member's address in the Netherlands, or a Dutch postal service box if you want to collect mail during visits.

Important: mail redirection does not catch registered post (aangetekende post) — things like legal notices and tax correspondence. Make sure you update your address directly with:

- Belastingdienst (via MijnOverheid or by letter)
- SVB (pension authority)
- Your bank(s)
- Your pension fund(s)
- Any Dutch subscriptions or memberships

## Step 11: Car — Sale or Export

### Selling in the Netherlands

If you sell your car before leaving, the new owner handles the name change (tenaamstelling) at the RDW. You do not need to do anything extra at the RDW — the buyer initiates the transfer. Cancel your car insurance from the transfer date.

### Exporting your car

If you are taking your car abroad:

1. Submit an **RDW afmelding** (export deregistration) — this can be done online at rdw.nl or in person at an RDW counter
2. Receive your kentekenbewijs deel I and part II confirmed as exported
3. Cancel your Dutch vehicle insurance from the export date
4. Complete any vehicle registration requirements in your destination country

The RDW afmelding stops your motorrijtuigenbelasting (road tax) from that date. If you have prepaid road tax, you are entitled to a refund for the unused months.

For more detail on vehicle registration and deregistration, see my [car registration Netherlands guide](/guides/daily-life/register-car-netherlands-expat-2026/).

## Step 12: Pets — Export Health Certificates

Leaving the Netherlands with a pet (cat, dog, ferret) requires an **EU pet passport** and an official health certificate. If you are moving to a non-EU country, the requirements are stricter and vary by destination.

### What is usually required

- Valid microchip
- Up-to-date rabies vaccination (at least 21 days old for primary vaccination)
- EU pet passport (issued by a vet registered in the Netherlands)
- Official health certificate (AHV, Ambtelijk Handelsdocument voor Vee) for non-EU destinations — must be issued by an accredited vet and sometimes endorsed by the NVWA
- Some countries require a tapeworm treatment certificate (UK, Ireland, Finland, Norway)

Book your vet appointment at least 3–4 weeks before departure to allow time for any required waiting periods. Start researching destination country requirements early — they vary significantly and the NVWA website has a country-by-country tool.

### 1 Week Before

- [ ] Confirm gemeente appointment date and time
- [ ] Confirm DigiD app is working with current phone number
- [ ] Download any remaining documents you might need
- [ ] Confirm health insurer has your emigration cancellation request on file

- [ ] Arrange final property inspection with landlord / letting agent
- [ ] Return rented telecom equipment (router, decoder) if contract ending

- [ ] Attend gemeente appointment — uitschrijven from BRP, collect deregistration confirmation letter
- [ ] Log into toeslagen.nl and stop zorgtoeslag/huurtoeslag effective today
- [ ] Send final confirmation to health insurer with deregistration date
- [ ] Take final meter readings for gas/electricity and send to supplier
- [ ] Collect keys from property — get written receipt from landlord

### After Leaving (First Year)

- [ ] File M-biljet for the tax year (after 1 January of the following year)
- [ ] Confirm deposit return from landlord (up to 14 days after inspection)
- [ ] Receive and check health insurance final settlement
- [ ] Receive and check zorgtoeslag/huurtoeslag final settlement
- [ ] Monitor Dutch bank account for any incoming refunds

## Frequently Asked Questions

The most common questions I get about deregistering are covered in the FAQ section above. A few extra points worth noting:

**Can I deregister by post or by someone else?**
Some municipalities accept a written authorisation (machtiging) allowing a third party to deregister on your behalf. This is useful if you leave unexpectedly. Check with your specific gemeente — not all accept this.

**What if I forget to deregister?**
The municipality will eventually mark you as *vertrokken onbekend* (departed, destination unknown) if mail bounces and you stop appearing in records. This is not a criminal matter but it creates complications: toeslagen may continue to be paid out and later reclaimed, and your address becomes unreliable for legal correspondence.

**Do I need to inform the IND (immigration service)?**
If you were on a work permit, student visa, or other residence permit, the IND will be notified of your deregistration automatically. Your permit becomes void when you leave. If you had a permanent residence permit (verblijfsvergunning voor onbepaalde tijd), leaving for more than 6 months can result in it lapsing — relevant if you plan to return later.

**What about the 30-day rule for EU free movement?**
EU/EEA citizens do not need IND permits, but the same deregistration process applies. Free movement rights do not extend to Dutch administrative records — you still need to uitschrijven.

## Final Thought

Leaving the Netherlands is genuinely more administrative than most people expect. I have helped dozens of expats through this process and the most common mistake is deregistering correctly but then failing to follow through on the downstream steps — especially the M-biljet and zorgtoeslag cancellation. Those two alone account for the majority of post-departure headaches I see.

Start early, work through this list in order, and keep copies of everything. The Dutch system is thorough and well-documented — which means it will find you if you have left something unresolved, even a year or two after leaving.
