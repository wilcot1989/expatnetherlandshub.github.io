When I moved to the Netherlands, I had a clear checklist of things to arrange. When I left for a year abroad, I assumed leaving would be simpler. It was not. Deregistering alone triggered a chain reaction: health insurance cancelled automatically, my DigiD access changed, and the Belastingdienst needed a special tax form I had never heard of. Missing even one item cost me months of back-and-forth from a different country.

This checklist is what I wish I had. It is structured chronologically, starting three months before your departure date, so nothing catches you off guard at the last minute.

---

## Why Leaving the Netherlands Takes More Planning Than You Think

The Dutch administrative system is deeply interconnected. Your BSN ties together your tax record, health insurance, pension contributions, and municipality registration. When you deregister (uitschrijven) from the BRP — the population register — a cascade of consequences follows automatically.

Some of those consequences are good (your health insurance obligation ends). Some require action on your part (pension preservation, final tax filing). A few can cause financial pain if you ignore them (the 30% ruling, outstanding energy contracts).

Work through this list in order. The earlier sections buy you time for the items that take the longest.

## 3 Months Before Departure

### Confirm Your Departure Date and Tell Your Employer

Your employer needs time to process your exit — final payslip, end-of-contract paperwork, and any company pension contributions. Give at least two months' notice if your contract allows. If you are on a highly skilled migrant (kennismigrant) permit, your employer must also notify the IND when your employment ends.

Make sure your last pay date is clear. Your Dutch income tax is calculated per day of residency in the tax year, so a clean final payslip date makes the M-form filing much easier later.

### Review Your 30% Ruling Status

If you have been using the **30% ruling** (belastingvoordeel voor ingekomen werknemers), leaving the Netherlands ends it immediately on your last working day. There is no grace period. This affects:

- Your final payslip tax calculation
- Your final tax return for the year
- Any partial-year refund you may be owed

If you re-enter the Netherlands within 25 years and take up employment again, you may be able to reclaim the remaining portion of the ruling — but this requires specific conditions and a new employer application. Note this in your records before leaving.

Read my [Dutch tax system guide](/guides/finance/dutch-tax-system-expats-guide-2026/) for a full breakdown of how the 30% ruling interacts with emigration.

### Start Pension Research

Pension is one of the most neglected areas when expats leave. Most employees in the Netherlands accrue pension through a sectoral pension fund (like ABP for government employees or PMT for metal industry workers) or through their employer's arrangement with a private insurer.

When you leave the Netherlands, you have three options:

| Option | What It Means |
|--------|--------------|
| **Preserve (slaaperspensioen)** | Leave the accrued pension in the fund; collect from Dutch retirement age (67) |
| **Value transfer (waardeoverdracht)** | Transfer the value to a pension fund in your new country (if a bilateral agreement exists) |
| **Cash out** | Generally not possible in the Netherlands unless the amount is below the small pension threshold (€594.89/year in 2026) |

Contact your pension fund directly to understand which options apply to your situation. For ABP (government and education), visit abp.nl. For other funds, check your annual pension overview (Uniform Pensioenoverzicht) for the fund name and contact details.

Value transfers to pension funds in EU/EEA countries are typically possible. Outside the EU, it depends on bilateral treaties. This process takes 3–6 months, so start now.

### Review Your Rental Contract

Dutch rental law gives landlords and tenants specific notice periods. For private rentals, the standard tenant notice period is one calendar month, but your contract may specify longer. For social housing (sociale huur), confirm the notice period directly with your housing corporation.

Send written notice (email with read receipt, or registered post) and get confirmation in return. Make sure you have documented the condition of the property for the deposit return.

See my full guide on [rental contract rights in the Netherlands](/guides/legal/dutch-rental-contract-rights-expats-2026/) for deposit recovery rules and tenant protections.

## 1 Month Before Departure

### Give Notice on All Contracts

Go through every recurring contract and give notice in writing. Work through this list:

- **Health insurance (zorgverzekering)** — Do NOT cancel yet. You must deregister from the BRP first. The cancellation must be backdated to your deregistration date.
- **Energy (gas and electricity)** — Standard notice is 30 days. Contact your provider and give a final meter reading date.
- **Internet and TV** — Check your contract for notice periods. Some providers (Ziggo, KPN) have 30-day rolling notice; others lock you in for a contract year.
- **Mobile phone** — If on a subscription, check the end date. Prepaid is simpler.
- **Gym, subscriptions, magazines** — Cancel anything with an auto-renewal.

For energy, schedule a final meter reading on or just before your departure day. Take photos of all meters with a timestamp as backup.

### Notify Your Bank

Contact your bank to discuss your options. The situation varies by bank:

| Bank | Non-resident policy |
|------|-------------------|
| **ABN AMRO** | Can often retain account with non-resident status; fees may apply |
| **ING** | Allows non-resident accounts under certain conditions; contact customer service |
| **Rabobank** | Generally closes accounts upon deregistration; exceptions apply |
| **bunq** | Requires EU residency; account may be restricted or closed |
| **N26** | Requires EU address for correspondence |
| **Revolut** | Can be used globally; not affected by Dutch deregistration |

If you need to keep a Dutch account for receiving pension payments or tax refunds later, arrange this before you deregister. Once you are deregistered, updating your bank details becomes harder without a Dutch address.

### Arrange Mail Forwarding

PostNL offers a mail forwarding service (PostNL Verhuisservice) for domestic redirects. For international forwarding, the service is more limited — check PostNL's current international offering as it changes periodically.

More reliable for expats: inform all senders of your new address directly. Priority list:

1. Belastingdienst (tax office) — critical for M-form correspondence
2. Your pension fund(s)
3. Your bank
4. UWV (if you received unemployment or sick pay)
5. SVB (if you received child benefits or AOW)
6. Any Dutch healthcare providers where you have outstanding treatments

You can update your address with the Belastingdienst via belastingdienst.nl or by calling the Belasting Telefoon (0800-0543).

### Sort Out Your OV-chipkaart

If you have a personal OV-chipkaart (not anonymous), you can claim back your deposit (€7.50) and any remaining balance. Do this via ov-chipkaart.nl or at a service desk at major train stations. You will need your card, BSN, and a Dutch bank account for the refund.

If you have an anonymous card, load down the balance and use it up before you leave — you cannot reclaim it.

Public transport subscriptions (NS-jaarkaart, for example) need to be cancelled separately. Check ns.nl for cancellation terms.

## Final Week Before Departure

### Deregister at the Gemeente (Uitschrijven BRP)

This is the most important administrative step. You must notify your local municipality that you are leaving the Netherlands. The legal requirement is to do this within 5 days of departure, but most municipalities allow you to do it slightly in advance.

**What to bring:**
- Your passport or ID
- Your BSN
- Your departure date and destination country address (if known)

Some municipalities allow online deregistration (online uitschrijving emigratie); others require a physical visit or a form sent by post. Check your municipality's website — they vary considerably.

After deregistration, you receive an emigration confirmation. Keep this document. You will need it for:

- Cancelling health insurance with the correct backdated date
- The M-form tax filing
- Proof of non-residency for your bank or pension fund

Your BSN remains valid after deregistration. Non-residents are registered in the Registratie Niet-Ingezetenen (RNI) rather than the BRP. Your BSN does not change; it simply moves to a different register. You will still use it for tax correspondence and pension administration from abroad.

If you used your BSN to register for your [DigiD](/guides/legal/digid-guide-expats-2026/), your DigiD account remains accessible after deregistration. Keep your login details — you will need DigiD for the M-form filing.

Also check the [BSN Planner](/tools/bsn-planner/) if you have any last-minute BSN-related admin to complete before leaving.

### Cancel Your Health Insurance

Dutch health insurance (basisverzekering) is legally mandatory only while you are registered as a resident. Once you are deregistered, your obligation ends — but cancellation does not happen automatically.

Contact your insurer (Zilveren Kruis, VGZ, CZ, Menzis, etc.) and request cancellation backdated to your deregistration date. You will need your emigration confirmation from the gemeente as proof.

If you cancel and then return to the Netherlands within 4 months, you must take out new insurance without any coverage gap penalty. Gaps longer than 4 months result in a financial penalty.

Use my [Health Insurance Wizard](/tools/health-insurance-wizard/) to understand your options and compare coverage if you need expat or international health insurance for your new location.

Note: supplementary insurance (aanvullende verzekering) is a separate contract and must be cancelled independently.

### Final Meter Readings and Key Return

On your last day (or as close to it as possible):

- Take final meter readings for gas, electricity, and water
- Photograph all meters with timestamp
- Return all keys to the landlord and get written confirmation
- Complete the property inspection (oplevering) and sign off

This protects your rental deposit. Without a signed inspection report, disputes become difficult from abroad.

### Return Library Cards and Other Deposits

A few often-forgotten items:

- **Library card (bibliotheek)** — return any borrowed items and close your membership to avoid ongoing fees
- **Bicycle deposit** — if you rented a bike through your employer or a scheme like Swapfiets, arrange return in advance
- **Parking permit** — return any municipal parking permits to reclaim the unused portion

## After Departure

### File Your Final Tax Return: The M-Form

The M-form (M-biljet / migratieaangifte) is the Dutch tax return for people who emigrated or immigrated during the tax year. It covers only the period you were a Dutch tax resident — from January 1 until your deregistration date.

**When to file:** The M-form becomes available from the Belastingdienst after the tax year ends (so if you left in 2026, you can file from early 2027). The standard deadline is May 1 of the year after, with extensions available upon request.

**Where to file:** The M-form can be filed online via belastingdienst.nl using your DigiD. A paper version is also available, but the online version is significantly easier.

**What to include:**
- All Dutch-source income from your period of residency
- Any income from abroad that was subject to Dutch tax during your residency period
- Deductions: mortgage interest (if applicable), healthcare costs above threshold, donations, 30% ruling correction if relevant

**Common outcome:** If you left mid-year, you likely overpaid payroll tax — Dutch PAYE (loonbelasting) assumes full-year residency. Many emigrants receive a refund.

The refund is paid to your bank account on file. Make sure the Belastingdienst has your correct (non-Dutch) bank account details, or a Dutch account that remains open. IBAN transfers to foreign bank accounts are possible.

For a deeper explanation of how Dutch income tax works, read my [Dutch tax system guide](/guides/finance/dutch-tax-system-expats-guide-2026/).

### Monitor Outstanding Correspondence

Even after leaving, Dutch correspondence may follow you for 1–3 years:

- **Belastingdienst** — final tax assessments, any queries about your M-form
- **Pension fund** — annual statements, information on value transfers
- **Former employer** — corrected payslips, end-of-year employer declarations (jaaropgaaf)
- **SVB** — AOW pension statements if you accrued state pension rights

Make sure MijnOverheid (mijnoverheid.nl) is set to forward official messages to your email address. You can do this via your MijnOverheid account using your DigiD, and it works from abroad.

### Check Your AOW State Pension Entitlement

The Dutch state pension (AOW) accrues at 2% per year of residency between ages 15 and 67. If you lived in the Netherlands for 10 years, you have accrued roughly 20% of the full AOW.

You can check your AOW accrual via the SVB (svb.nl) using DigiD. Even a partial AOW is worth tracking — it is paid from Dutch retirement age regardless of where you live, provided you apply for it at the time.

If you have pension rights in multiple EU countries, cross-border pension aggregation rules apply. The SVB can advise on this.

## Common Mistakes Expats Make When Leaving

**Cancelling health insurance before deregistering.** Your insurer needs the official emigration date from the gemeente to process the cancellation. Do it in the wrong order and you may face gaps or disputes.

**Assuming the M-form is optional.** It is not. Failing to file means the Belastingdienst estimates your income and sends an assessment — almost always higher than actual liability. You may also miss a refund.

**Closing the bank account before the tax refund arrives.** If your M-form results in a refund (common for mid-year departures), you need a live bank account to receive it. Keep at least one account open until the final assessment arrives.

**Ignoring the pension.** Dormant pension rights do not expire, but if you move repeatedly over a career, tracking small pots from different countries becomes administratively messy. Deal with it while the details are fresh.

**Not keeping the emigration confirmation.** This single document unlocks the health insurance cancellation, the M-form, and non-residency status for your bank. Get it, scan it, store it securely.

## Timing Your Departure: What Happens Month by Month

If you know your departure date, here is the practical timeline I recommend:

**3–4 months before leaving:**
- Notify your landlord in writing (typically 1–3 months' notice depending on your contract)
- Start researching international health insurance or coverage in your destination country
- Check whether your employer needs advance notice for 30% ruling termination or pension arrangements
- Contact your pension provider about the options: transfer to your home country, leave in Dutch system, or convert to a value transfer

**2 months before leaving:**
- Cancel standing orders for Dutch services (health insurance, energy, internet) with proper notice periods
- Begin the process for M-form tax filing (you file this after departure for your final Dutch tax year)
- Request your Uittreksel BRP (extract from the personal records database) for any administrative needs abroad
- Notify your bank about your upcoming departure — some accounts can be maintained as non-resident; others are closed

**1 month before leaving:**
- Complete the uitschrijving (deregistration) at the gemeente — you can deregister up to a month before your actual departure date; do it close to your departure date, not too early, as many services depend on BRP registration
- Cancel your Dutch health insurance effective from your last day of BRP registration (you lose coverage from the deregistration date)
- Return any items rented through Dutch services (OV-chipkaart, library cards, etc.)
- Conduct your rental property check-out formally with your landlord and request your deposit back in writing

**After you leave:**
- File the M-form (migrantenformulier) with the Belastingdienst for your final partial-year Dutch tax return — this is usually due the following year
- Keep your BSN and DigiD active — you may need them for pension queries, outstanding tax matters, or accessing past Dutch records
- Check whether any toeslagen reconciliation (terugvordering) is due — this arrives by post at your last Dutch address, or via Mijn Overheid if you keep DigiD active

## What You Can Keep After Leaving

Several Dutch accounts and registrations remain useful after you leave:

**Dutch bank account:** ABN AMRO and ING allow non-resident accounts for a period after you leave. Useful for receiving Dutch pension payments or tax refunds. Check their non-resident account terms before closing — it may be worth keeping open.

**DigiD:** Remains active as long as you use it at least once every 3 years. Keep it active for Mijn Belastingdienst access, pension queries, and any ongoing Dutch administrative matters.

**AOW pension rights:** Your accrued AOW (state pension) rights remain in the Dutch system. You can request payment from SVB when you reach Dutch pension age (67 in 2026), even if you live abroad. Notify SVB of your address abroad so they can contact you.

**Dutch occupational pension:** If your employer had a pensioenfonds (pension fund), your accrued rights remain with them. Request a Pensioenoverzicht (pension overview) before leaving and register your international address with the fund. PensionTracker.nl can help locate old pension pots if you lose track.
