## Why Dental Care Is Not Covered for Adults

The Dutch basisverzekering (basic health insurance) covers most medically necessary care — GP visits, hospital treatment, mental health care, and more. But dental care for adults was moved out of the basic package in 1995. The reasoning at the time was that dental health had improved significantly in the Netherlands, and adults were deemed able to pay for routine dental care themselves.

The consequence is that every adult in the Netherlands is responsible for their own dental costs unless they have taken out a separate aanvullende verzekering that includes tandartszorg.

Children up to and including age 17 have dental care covered under the basic package. The moment a child turns 18, they lose that coverage and become responsible for their own costs.

For expats this is often a surprise, particularly those coming from the UK (NHS dental care), Germany (statutory insurance covers many dental treatments), or the USA (where dental insurance is standard in employer packages). In the Netherlands, you are on your own unless you arrange additional coverage.

## Understanding Dutch Dental Pricing

Dutch dental fees are not completely unregulated. The Nederlandse Zorgautoriteit (NZa) — the healthcare market authority — sets maximum tariffs for each type of dental treatment. Every treatment is assigned a code (prestatiecode), and the NZa publishes the maximum fee for that code.

This means there is a ceiling on what a regular tandarts practice can charge. It does not mean every practice charges the same rate; they can charge less, and many do. Private practices and those targeting expats often charge the maximum or close to it.

**Key treatments and approximate 2026 cost ranges:**

| Treatment | Approximate Cost (EUR) |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Routine checkup (examination) | 25–40 |
| Bitewing X-rays (4 films) | 25–50 |
| Professional cleaning (scaling) | 50–100 |
| Small filling (amalgam) | 50–80 |
| Composite filling (tooth-coloured) | 80–150 |
| Tooth extraction (simple) | 60–120 |
| Surgical extraction (complicated) | 120–250 |
| Root canal (per canal) | 120–250 |
| Crown (metal-ceramic) | 300–500 |
| Crown (full ceramic) | 400–600 |
| Implant (crown and placement) | 1,500–2,500 |
| Emergency appointment | 30–80 (plus treatment cost) |

These are NZa-regulated practices. Some practices in major expat cities charge a separate "treatment room" or "consultation" surcharge on top of the treatment fee — always ask upfront.

Cosmetic procedures (whitening, veneers, purely aesthetic work) are fully private and can vary enormously. These are not regulated by the NZa and are not covered by any standard aanvullende verzekering.

## How to Find an English-Speaking Dentist

### Method 1: Expat-Specific Directories

Several organisations maintain directories of English-speaking healthcare providers in the Netherlands, including dentists:

**ACCESS NL (accessnl.com):** A long-running expat information service with a healthcare provider database filtered by English proficiency and specialty.

**Expat Republic / ExpatHub:** Community forums and directories where expats share recommendations. These are particularly useful for finding dentists in smaller cities where expat directories are thinner.

**Google Maps + "English speaking tandarts [city name]":** Direct searching. Check reviews specifically mentioning English service. Many practices now list English as a language on their Google profile.

### Method 2: Ask Your Huisarts

Your GP (huisarts) can often recommend a nearby tandarts. GPs and dentists in the same neighbourhood frequently know each other professionally, and a GP who sees expat patients will often know which dental practices in the area speak English. If you haven't yet registered with a GP, that is a parallel priority — see the [find an English-speaking doctor guide](/guides/health/find-english-speaking-doctor-netherlands-2026/) for how to do that.

### Method 3: Employer or Expat Network

If you work for an employer with a significant expat workforce, HR or colleagues who have been in the Netherlands longer are a valuable resource. In larger companies, these recommendations circulate informally and are often more reliable than online listings.

### Method 4: Contact Practices Directly

The most direct method: email or call dental practices near your home or workplace. In Dutch cities, the response to an English-language email inquiry tells you immediately whether English is workable at that practice. Most Dutch dental practices will reply in English if you write in English.

## City-by-City Tips

### Amsterdam

Amsterdam has the highest concentration of expat-friendly dental practices in the Netherlands. Several practices in the Jordaan, De Pijp, Amsterdam-Zuid, and near the Zuidas explicitly market to expat and international patients. Expect slightly higher prices in these practices — they are often at the top of the NZa tariff range. Waiting times at popular expat-friendly practices can be several weeks for a new patient registration.

**Emergency:** Amsterdam has a 24-hour Spoedeisende Tandheelkunde service. Check the current location on tandartspost.nl.

### Rotterdam

Rotterdam has a large international population, particularly in the Centre, Kralingen, and Hillegersberg areas. Erasmus University MC runs a dental school (ACTA partnership) which accepts patients at reduced rates — useful if you need extensive work and are on a budget. English proficiency among Rotterdam dentists is generally excellent.

**Emergency:** Spoedtandarts Rotterdam operates outside office hours.

### The Hague (Den Haag)

The Hague has a uniquely high expat concentration due to international organisations, embassies, and the International Court of Justice. The Statenkwartier, Bezuidenhout, and Benoordenhout neighbourhoods have several practices used to treating international patients. Dental practices in The Hague are generally priced at NZa maximum tariffs.

### Utrecht

Utrecht has a younger population and a large student and international workforce. The university city environment means dental practices are used to multilingual patients. The Binnenstad and Lombok areas have several English-speaking options.

### Eindhoven

Eindhoven's tech and ASML expat community has driven demand for English-speaking healthcare, including dentistry. Several practices near the Eindhoven city centre and in the Strijp and Stratum areas cater to international patients.

### Smaller Cities and Towns

In smaller municipalities, the general rule applies: most Dutch dentists speak English, but not all have English-language intake forms or consistently English-speaking reception staff. A brief email in English before booking will confirm whether communication will be smooth.

## How to Register as a Patient

Unlike the huisarts system, there is no strict requirement to register with a tandarts in advance. You can in theory walk into any practice and ask to become a patient. In practice, popular practices have waiting lists for new patient registrations, particularly in cities.

The registration process is simple:
1. Contact the practice by phone or email (email is easier for English speakers)
2. Confirm they are accepting new patients
3. Complete an intake form — new patient dental history, current medications, allergies
4. Book an initial appointment (usually a checkup and X-rays)

Many practices in the Netherlands now have online patient portals (often through Dental Orange or similar platforms) where you can complete registration forms digitally.

**What to bring to your first appointment:**
- Your passport or ID
- Your DigiD login details (useful if the practice uses digital health records)
- Details of any existing dental conditions, previous X-rays, or ongoing treatments
- Your health insurance details (even though dental is not covered by basic insurance, the practice may have relationships with aanvullende verzekeraar providers)

## Private vs. Regular Tandarts

This distinction causes confusion for expats. In the Netherlands, most dental practices are what would be called "private" in other countries — they are not NHS-style public practices. The majority of tandarts practices are independent or part of small groups, and they charge the NZa-regulated fees.

The distinction that matters for expats is between:

**Regular NZa-regulated practices:** Charge within the NZa tariff ceiling. Most practices in the Netherlands fall into this category. Adequate facilities, standard Dutch care quality (which is generally high), and NZa oversight.

**Purely private/cosmetic practices:** These typically focus on cosmetic dentistry (veneers, whitening, smile makeovers) and are not bound by NZa tariffs for cosmetic work. Their fees can be significantly higher. Routine dental work at these practices may still follow NZa tariffs, but the consultation and facility fees may not.

**Dental schools and teaching clinics:** ACTA (Amsterdam) and several university clinics treat patients at reduced rates, with treatments supervised by qualified dentists. Wait times are longer, but costs are 30–50% lower. Useful for patients needing expensive treatments like root canals or crowns.

For most expats, a regular NZa-regulated practice with English-speaking staff is the right choice. Cosmetic and dental school options are worth knowing about for specific situations.

## Supplementary Dental Insurance: Is It Worth It?

Since dental care is not in the basic package, the question is whether to add dental coverage through an aanvullende verzekering.

### How Aanvullende Dental Coverage Works

Dutch health insurers offer supplementary packages at different price points. Dental coverage within these packages typically works as follows:

- A maximum annual reimbursement for dental care (e.g., EUR 250, EUR 500, EUR 1,000, or unlimited)
- Some packages cover only checkups; others cover fillings, root canals, and crowns up to the maximum
- Orthodontic coverage is usually in higher-tier packages only
- Implants are almost never covered, or only partially in premium packages

Waiting periods apply with some insurers — you typically cannot sign up in January and immediately claim for a crown in February. Check the exact terms.

### Comparing the Costs

A basic aanvullende tandarts package might cost EUR 8–15 per month (EUR 96–180 per year) and reimburse up to EUR 250 per year. That is barely break-even with two checkups. It starts making sense if you expect a filling or cleaning on top of the checkup.

A more extensive package at EUR 20–35 per month (EUR 240–420 per year) might cover EUR 500–1,000 of dental care. If you need one filling and a crown in a single year, that easily exceeds the premium.

The honest answer: if your teeth are in good shape and you only need annual checkups, the cheapest packages barely pay for themselves. If you have older fillings that will eventually need replacement, or if you have been told you will need more significant dental work, fuller dental coverage is worth the premium.

## Emergency Dental Care

Dental emergencies happen. A tooth that breaks on a Saturday afternoon, severe pain that does not resolve over a weekend, a lost filling that makes eating impossible.

### Finding Emergency Dental Care

**Outside office hours:** Most dental practices have a voicemail message that includes either an emergency number or a referral to the regional spoedtandarts (emergency dental) service. Listen to the voicemail carefully — even in Dutch, the number is usually clearly stated.

**Tandarts posten (dental posts):** Major Dutch cities have designated after-hours dental emergency services. These are staffed by duty dentists (dienstdoende tandartsen) who handle acute cases. Search for "spoedtandarts [city name]" or "tandarts post [city name]" to find the relevant service.

**Hospitals:** Dental emergencies involving facial trauma, jaw injuries, or infections that have spread are handled by hospital maxillofacial surgery (kaakchirurgie) departments. These are available through A&E.

**Cost:** Emergency dental visits are charged at a higher tariff than regular appointments. Expect to pay an emergency surcharge of EUR 30–80 plus the cost of any treatment. Insurance reimbursement depends on your aanvullende package.

### What Counts as a Dental Emergency

Genuinely urgent situations that justify after-hours care:

- Severe toothache that does not respond to over-the-counter pain relief
- Knocked-out tooth (if reimplantation is possible, this is extremely time-sensitive — within 30 minutes)
- Broken tooth with sharp edges causing soft tissue damage
- Abscess or swelling in the jaw, particularly if accompanied by fever
- Significant bleeding from a tooth socket after extraction

Issues that feel urgent but can typically wait until the next working day:

- Lost filling (seal with dental wax available at pharmacies)
- Broken crown (use denture adhesive if the crown can be re-seated)
- Mild toothache
- Chipped tooth without pain or sharp edges

## Dental Costs and the Cost of Living

Dental costs are one of the more manageable healthcare expenses in the Netherlands compared to the USA, but they are higher than in the UK (where NHS dentists charge standardised low fees) or some Eastern European countries.

The cost of living context matters: see the [cost of living in the Netherlands guide](/guides/housing/cost-of-living-netherlands-2026/) for how dental costs fit into the broader picture of expenses as an expat in the Netherlands.

Budget roughly EUR 80–120 per year for routine dental maintenance (one checkup plus X-rays and a professional clean). If you have dental work pending, budget significantly more and consider whether supplementary insurance makes sense before that work begins.

## Children's Dental Care in the Netherlands

Children's dental care in the Netherlands is handled somewhat differently from adult care, and the insurance rules are more generous for younger patients.

**Insurance coverage for children under 18:** Basic dental care for children up to age 18 is covered by the Dutch basisverzekering at no extra cost. This includes checkups, X-rays, fillings, and extractions. No supplementary insurance is needed for standard treatment. The eigen risico (annual deductible) does not apply to children's care.

**Orthodontic treatment for children:** Orthodontic work is covered by the basic insurance if it is medically indicated and meets specific criteria. Minor cosmetic orthodontics is not covered. A referral from a dentist to an orthodontist (orthodontist) is required, and the treatment must start before age 18 to fall within the basic coverage.

**After age 18:** Once a child turns 18, they move to adult rules — dental is no longer covered by basic insurance and they should consider an aanvullende verzekering. Many Dutch families add dental supplementary insurance for their children when they turn 18 as a matter of course.

**Practical tip for expat families:** When you register at the gemeente and receive your BSN, also register your children with a Dutch tandarts immediately. Popular practices in cities — particularly ones familiar with international children — have waiting lists. Getting children on the list early means continuity of care as they grow.

## Oral Health in the Netherlands: Cultural Notes

The Dutch approach to dental care is preventive by default. A typical Dutch patient visits their tandarts once a year for a checkup and professional cleaning (tand reiniging). The focus is on catching problems early, not treating advanced decay.

This contrasts with countries where many people only visit the dentist when there is a problem. Dutch dentists will note if you have gaps in your care history and may want to take a full set of X-rays at your first appointment to establish a baseline.

What this means practically:

- Do not delay registering with a dentist when you arrive. Even if your teeth feel fine, establishing a patient relationship now is worth it.
- If you have been a sporadic dental attender in your home country, expect the first appointment to involve a thorough assessment and possibly X-rays — plan for it to be longer than a standard checkup.
- Dutch dentists are generally direct about what needs attention and what does not. They are not in the business of recommending unnecessary treatments, but they will tell you clearly if something needs addressing.

The quality of Dutch dental care is high. The Netherlands trains dentists at ACTA (Amsterdam-Leiden) and other dental faculties with rigorous international standards. Treatment protocols follow evidence-based guidelines. For routine dental care, you are in good hands.
