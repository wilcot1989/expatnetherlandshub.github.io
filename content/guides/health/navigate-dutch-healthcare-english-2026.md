When I first needed to see a doctor in Amsterdam, I rang the nearest huisarts practice and the receptionist answered entirely in Dutch. I fumbled through a combination of schoolbook Dutch and hand gestures until someone at the desk switched to English — but not before I had accidentally agreed to a Tuesday appointment I did not want. Ten years later, I have helped dozens of expat clients work through the same situation more efficiently. Here is what actually works.

The Dutch healthcare system is excellent. The language barrier, however, is real — and it is not just about finding a GP. It is about knowing what to say at a pharmacy when you need something for a child's fever, how to describe your symptoms accurately enough that a doctor does not send you away, and what to do when you end up in a hospital without having planned for it.

This guide covers the practical side: finding English-speaking care, the vocabulary you actually need, and what to do when English is not available.

## How the Dutch Healthcare System Works (The Part That Matters for Expats)

Before getting into language specifics, the structure matters because it affects who you can access. For the full picture, see the [Dutch healthcare system explained guide](/guides/health/dutch-healthcare-system-explained-2026/) and [what Dutch health insurance covers](/guides/health/what-dutch-health-insurance-covers-2026/). Here is the short version:

**The huisarts (GP) is your gatekeeper.** You cannot walk into a hospital or see a specialist without a referral from your GP, except in genuine emergencies. This means getting registered with a GP immediately after arriving in the Netherlands is not optional — it is urgent.

**You must register with a huisarts practice.** You are not automatically assigned one. You register with a specific practice, and that practice is responsible for your primary care. If that practice is full, they are allowed to turn you away, and you need to find another.

**Insurance is mandatory.** All residents of the Netherlands are legally required to have Dutch health insurance (basisverzekering) within 4 months of registering. The [Dutch health insurance guide](/guides/health/dutch-health-insurance-guide-expats-2026/) covers the details. If you are not yet insured, that affects which services you can access and what you will pay out of pocket.

## Finding an English-Speaking GP in the Netherlands

### Use Doctolib First

[Doctolib](https://www.doctolib.nl) has become the dominant GP and specialist booking platform in the Netherlands. The search filters include language spoken — you can specifically search for huisarts practices with English-speaking doctors and check availability in real time.

Steps:
1. Go to doctolib.nl
2. Search "huisarts" in your city
3. Filter by language (Engels/English)
4. Check "accepting new patients" (nieuwe patiënten: ja)
5. Book directly or call to confirm

Doctolib does not list every practice in the Netherlands — some independent practices are not on the platform. But for urban areas it covers the majority.

### Zorgkaart Nederland

[Zorgkaart Nederland](https://www.zorgkaartnederland.nl) is a government-adjacent quality and review portal for Dutch healthcare providers. You can search for GPs, specialists, hospitals, dentists, physiotherapists, and mental health providers. The language filter is available, and each provider has patient reviews.

Zorgkaart is particularly useful for finding specialists who speak English, since Doctolib is stronger on GP access than specialist access.

### Google Maps

Searching "English speaking huisarts Amsterdam" (or your city) on Google Maps works better than you might expect. Practices that specifically cater to expats often include language information in their Google Business profile or their own website. Look at the reviews — expat patients regularly mention whether English is spoken.

### Expat Community Groups

Facebook groups and Reddit communities (r/Amsterdam, r/Rotterdam, r/expats) have threads specifically about English-speaking doctors. These are valuable because you get real patient experiences, not just a directory listing. Recommendations from other expats who have actually been treated at a practice are worth more than any algorithm.

### What to Say When You Call

Many expats feel uncertain calling a Dutch medical practice. Here are the key phrases:

**To check if they take English-speaking patients:**
> "Hello, do you have any English-speaking doctors? My Dutch is not yet good enough for medical consultations."

**To check if they are accepting new patients:**
> "Are you accepting new patients? I recently moved to [city] and need to register."

**If they answer in Dutch and you are unsure:**
> *"Spreekt u Engels? Mijn Nederlands is helaas niet zo goed."*
> (Do you speak English? Unfortunately my Dutch is not that good.)

## Registering With a Huisarts: What You Need

Once you find a practice, you need to register before you can make an appointment. Most practices require:
- Proof of identity (passport)
- BSN number
- Dutch health insurance details (policy number, insurer name)
- Proof of address in the Netherlands (rental contract, gemeentebewijs)

Some practices have online registration forms. Others ask you to come in person. A small number of practices serving a high expat population have English-language registration forms — Doctolib often shows this.

**If practices near you are full:** This is common in Amsterdam, Utrecht, and The Hague. Try:
1. Asking the full practice if they have a waiting list
2. Checking practices in adjacent postcodes
3. Contacting your municipality (gemeente) — they can sometimes intervene when you genuinely cannot find a GP
4. Using a huisartsenpost (GP out-of-hours service) for urgent care while you search — though this is not a long-term solution

## At the Pharmacy (Apotheek): Getting What You Need

Dutch pharmacies are efficient and the staff almost universally speak excellent English in cities. But there are things worth knowing.

### Prescription Medications

Dutch prescriptions are digital in most cases — your GP sends the prescription electronically to your registered pharmacy (stamapotheke). You do not carry a paper prescription in most cases. Tell the pharmacy your name and date of birth, and they will have it in the system.

**Register with a pharmacy** as soon as you register with a GP. Your stamapotheke (registered pharmacy) holds your full medication history, checks for drug interactions, and is your first port of call for questions about medications. You can register with any pharmacy — you do not have to use the one closest to your GP.

### Over-the-Counter Medications

Many medications that require a prescription in other countries are available over the counter in Dutch pharmacies (drogisterijen and apothekers). Common examples:
- Paracetamol and ibuprofen: freely available
- Antihistamines: most are OTC
- Nasal corticosteroids: some brands OTC
- Thrush treatments: OTC
- UTI tests: OTC

**Dutch brand names versus generic:** The pharmacist will often offer a generic (huismerk) version of any medication. These are bioequivalent to the branded version and significantly cheaper. Unless your GP specifically prescribed a brand name, the generic is fine.

### Useful Pharmacy Phrases

**Asking for something OTC:**
> *"Heeft u iets voor [symptom]?"*
> (Do you have something for [symptom]?)

**Asking about interactions:**
> *"Kan ik dit combineren met [ander medicijn]?"*
> (Can I combine this with [other medication]?)

**Asking about dosage:**
> *"Hoe vaak per dag moet ik dit nemen?"*
> (How often per day should I take this?)

**If you have a prescription:**
> *"Ik heb een recept van mijn huisarts."*
> (I have a prescription from my GP.)
> Your name and date of birth is all they usually need.

## Requesting an English-Speaking Specialist

When your GP refers you to a specialist (specialist in a hospital or polyclinic), you have the right to choose which hospital or specialist you see, within your insurer's network.

### How to Request an English-Speaking Specialist

1. **Tell your GP** at the time of referral: "Can you refer me to an English-speaking specialist?" — most GPs in expat-heavy areas are used to this request
2. **Ask the GP to note it in the referral letter** — a written request for an English-speaking provider carries weight
3. **When the hospital contacts you**, confirm language availability before your appointment

### Teaching Hospitals and International Departments

Several Dutch hospitals have dedicated international patient services or departments with guaranteed English-language capacity:
- **Amsterdam UMC** (Amsterdam) — strong international department
- **Erasmus MC** (Rotterdam) — research hospital with extensive English
- **LUMC** (Leiden) — academic hospital near The Hague
- **Radboudumc** (Nijmegen) — academic hospital with English services

Private clinics and international health clinics (particularly in Amsterdam and The Hague) often offer consultations entirely in English, though these may not be in the basic insurance network — check coverage before booking.

## Hospital Admissions in English

If you are admitted to hospital (planned or emergency), here is what to expect:

### During the Admission Process

Bring your:
- Passport
- BSN number
- Insurance card (zorgpas) or insurer name and policy number
- Medication list if applicable

Admission staff at most hospitals speak English. If you encounter a language barrier at any point, ask explicitly:
> *"Heeft u een tolk beschikbaar?"* (Do you have an interpreter available?)

Dutch hospitals are required to ensure effective communication. Many have access to telephone interpreter services if no bilingual staff member is available. You can also ask for written information in English — larger hospitals often have English versions of consent forms and patient information leaflets.

### During the Hospital Stay

Nursing staff in Dutch hospitals vary in English proficiency. Specialists (doctors) almost universally speak English. If you have a question or concern and cannot communicate with the nurse on duty, ask for the charge nurse (hoofdverpleegkundige) or the treating doctor (behandelend arts).

**For planned procedures:** It is reasonable to ask in advance whether an English-speaking anaesthesiologist is available, particularly for procedures requiring consent for anaesthesia. This is not guaranteed but often accommodated.

## Mental Health Support in English

Finding an English-speaking therapist is harder than finding a GP, and the waiting lists for mental health care in the Netherlands are long. I want to be honest about both of those things.

For context on what the system covers and the waiting list situation, see the [mental health support for expats guide](/guides/health/mental-health-support-expats-netherlands-2026/).

### Where to Find English-Speaking Therapists

**Psychology Today Directory:**
[psychologytoday.com/intl/counselors/nl](https://www.psychologytoday.com/intl/counselors/nl) — filter by language (English) and insurance accepted. This is the most complete English-language directory.

**Open Up:**
Subscription-based mental health platform used by some Dutch employers. Sessions in English with psychologists and coaches. Worth asking your employer's HR whether this is included in your benefits.

**Expat-specific practices:**
Search for "expat therapy Amsterdam" or "English speaking psychologist [your city]." Several practices in Amsterdam and The Hague specifically serve the expat community.

**University counselling services:**
If you are studying in the Netherlands, your university almost certainly has English-language student counselling. This is typically faster to access than GGZ services.

### The Waiting List Reality

GGZ (public mental health care) waiting lists in the Netherlands commonly run 6-12 months for non-urgent cases. This is a documented problem that the Dutch government is working on but has not solved. Private psychologists with English capacity often have shorter waits but may not be fully covered by your basic insurance — a limited number of sessions may be covered under the aanvullende verzekering (supplemental insurance).

**While you wait:**
- Your GP can provide short-term support and screening
- Online therapy platforms (Open Up, iPractice Online) often have shorter waits
- The mental health helpline (Stichting Korrelatie: 0900 1450) has English-speaking counsellors for short-term support

## Emergency Care in English

**Emergency number: 112** (police, fire, ambulance)

Emergency dispatchers in the Netherlands are trained to handle calls in multiple languages, including English. If you call 112 and start speaking English, they will switch immediately. Stay calm, state your location first, then describe what is happening.

### At the Emergency Department (Spoedeisende Hulp / SEH)

Most emergency departments at Dutch hospitals have English-speaking staff, particularly at the triage desk. You will not be refused treatment due to language.

**Essential emergency phrases:**

| Situation | Dutch | English meaning |
|-----------|-------|-----------------|
| I need an ambulance | Ik heb een ambulance nodig | — |
| I'm having chest pain | Ik heb pijn op de borst | — |
| I'm having difficulty breathing | Ik heb moeite met ademen | — |
| I'm allergic to [X] | Ik ben allergisch voor [X] | — |
| I'm pregnant | Ik ben zwanger | — |
| I take these medications | Ik gebruik deze medicijnen | — |
| Does anyone speak English? | Spreekt iemand Engels? | — |

**Medical alert cards:** If you have a serious allergy, condition like epilepsy or diabetes, or take medication that emergency staff must know about, carry a card in Dutch with this information. Your GP can help you draft one.

### Huisartsenpost (GP Out-of-Hours Service)

For urgent but non-life-threatening situations outside your GP's opening hours, call the huisartsenpost in your area. These are centralized out-of-hours GP services, not emergency rooms. The number for your local huisartsenpost is on your registered GP practice's voicemail or website.

Call before showing up — you will almost always be triaged by phone first. English-speaking staff are available at most huisartsenposten in urban areas.

## Dental Care in English

Dentists (tandartsen) in the Netherlands are not covered by basic health insurance for adults — you pay per treatment or via supplemental insurance. Most dental practices in larger Dutch cities have English-speaking dentists, and the quality of care is generally high.

For finding English-speaking dentists specifically, see the [English-speaking dentist guide for the Netherlands](/guides/health/english-speaking-dentist-netherlands-2026/).

Dental care is not cheap without supplemental insurance. A filling typically costs EUR 40-80, a crown EUR 300-600. If you plan to stay in the Netherlands longer than a year, dental supplemental insurance (tandverzekering) is worth calculating — it covers a percentage of costs up to an annual maximum. Compare options on Independer when you choose your annual policy.

## My Practical Recommendations

If I were advising someone who just moved to the Netherlands, here is the specific order I would suggest:

**Week 1:** Register with a huisarts. Do not wait until you are sick. Use Doctolib to find one accepting new patients. If you are in Amsterdam or The Hague, there are practices specifically serving expats — worth looking for.

**Week 2:** Register with a stamapotheke near your home. They will have your records on file and can advise on medication questions quickly.

**Month 1:** Sort your health insurance if you have not. Use Independer to compare. Make sure your aanvullend package covers physio and dental if you use those services regularly.

**On arrival:** Save the 112 number and find the number for your local huisartsenpost. Put both in your phone contacts before you need them.

The Dutch healthcare system does most things well once you are inside it. Getting inside it — registered, insured, with an English-speaking GP you can reach — is the part that requires some effort upfront. Put in that effort in your first two weeks and you will not regret it.
