Finding a doctor in the Netherlands was not something I thought about before I moved. I assumed it would be simple — look up a GP, book an appointment, done. What I did not expect was to spend three weeks calling practices, being told they had a waiting list, and eventually discovering that the whole system works nothing like what I was used to in the UK. If you are new here and need to register with a GP, this guide will save you a lot of frustration.

Before anything else: sort out your [Dutch health insurance](/guides/health/dutch-health-insurance-guide-expats-2026/) and your [BSN number](/guides/legal/bsn-registration-guide-2026/) first. You cannot register with a huisarts without both. Use the [BSN Planner](/tools/bsn-planner/) if you have not done this yet, and the [Health Insurance Wizard](/tools/health-insurance-wizard/) to find the right policy. Once those are in place, finding a GP becomes much simpler.

## How the Huisarts System Works

The huisarts (GP or general practitioner) is the foundation of Dutch primary healthcare. Unlike the UK's NHS, where you may have a choice of several practices within your area, or the US system, where you can often walk into many clinics, the Dutch system is built around having one registered GP who knows your medical history and acts as a gatekeeper to all specialist care.

Here is what that means in practice:

- You register with a single huisarts practice for your address
- All non-emergency medical care goes through your huisarts first
- If you need a specialist, your huisarts refers you — you generally cannot self-refer
- The practice has a list of registered patients; they do not see people who are not on their list (with a few exceptions)
- Your insurer covers GP visits in full — there is no eigen risico applied to huisarts appointments

This last point is worth noting. Seeing your GP in the Netherlands is free at the point of use. There is no co-pay. That is not always well publicised for people coming from other countries.

The downside of this system is that practices have a finite capacity. In high-demand areas — particularly central Amsterdam and Utrecht — practices frequently close their lists to new patients. This means finding a GP who is accepting registrations can take effort.

## What You Need to Register

When you contact a practice to register, have the following ready:

| Document | Why You Need It |
|----------|----------------|
| **BSN (burgerservicenummer)** | Required by all practices; links your care to your insurance |
| **Dutch health insurance proof** | Your insurer card or policy number |
| **Proof of address** | Usually your gemeente registration; shows you live in the practice catchment area |
| **Passport or ID** | For identity verification |
| **Previous medical records (optional)** | Not required, but useful for continuity of care |

Most practices will only accept patients who live within a specific postcode area. If you move, you may need to switch GPs, even if you were happy with your previous one.

## How to Find an English-Speaking GP

There are several reliable ways to find an English-speaking huisarts in the Netherlands.

### Zorgkaart Nederland

**zorgkaart.nl** is the main Dutch platform for finding and reviewing healthcare providers. You can search by postcode, filter by type of provider (huisarts), and — critically — filter by language. Search for "huisarts" in your area, then look for practices that list English under languages spoken.

Patient reviews on Zorgkaart are generally reliable and give a real sense of whether a practice communicates well with international patients.

### Your Health Insurer's Website

Most Dutch insurers — CZ, Zilveren Kruis, VGZ, Menzis — have a "find a provider" tool on their website. These often let you filter by language and show whether the practice is accepting new patients. Since your insurer has to cover your GP care, they also have an interest in helping you find one.

### Expat Community Groups

Facebook groups and Reddit communities (r/Netherlands, r/Amsterdam, r/Rotterdam and so on) are genuinely useful here. Other expats have done the legwork and can point you to practices that are known to be English-friendly and currently accepting patients. Information can go stale quickly in high-demand areas, so always verify with the practice directly.

### Your Employer or Relocation Agent

If you are moving to the Netherlands for work, your HR department or relocation agent may have a shortlist of GP practices they recommend to new international staff. This is worth asking about before you arrive.

## How to Register: Step by Step

1. **Find a practice** using Zorgkaart Nederland or your insurer's website. Check that they are accepting new patients and that you are within their catchment area.

2. **Contact the practice** by phone or online registration form. Many practices now have a form on their website; if English is listed as a language they support, the form may also be in English.

3. **Submit your documents.** You will typically be asked for your BSN, insurance details, and address. Some practices ask for your medical history from your previous GP.

4. **Wait for confirmation.** Once registered, the practice will confirm and you will be added to their patient list. This can take a few days.

5. **Attend a first consultation** if the practice offers one. Some GPs schedule a brief intake appointment for new patients to get your baseline health information on file.

One thing that surprises many people: you do not usually book your first GP appointment online. You call the practice, explain briefly what the appointment is for (a nurse or assistant called a doktersassistente will ask), and you are given a slot. The appointment itself is typically ten minutes. If you have multiple issues, mention this when booking — they may give you a longer slot.

## Tips for Major Cities

Finding a GP in the Netherlands varies significantly depending on where you live.

### [Amsterdam](/cities/amsterdam/)

Amsterdam has the tightest GP market in the country. Practices in the city centre and popular expat neighbourhoods like De Pijp, Oud-West, and Jordaan regularly have closed patient lists. Tips that work:

- Check GPs in your immediate postcode area first — practices only take patients within a few postcodes of the surgery
- Try practices slightly further from the centre; areas like Noord, Nieuw-West, and Zuidoost often have more availability
- Several international practices in Amsterdam specifically cater to expats and have English-speaking staff. Search Zorgkaart for "international" alongside "huisarts Amsterdam"
- If you are stuck, call your insurer directly — they are obligated to find you a GP

### [Rotterdam](/cities/rotterdam/)

Rotterdam generally has better GP availability than Amsterdam. The international community is well-established here, particularly in areas like Kralingen and Hillegersberg. Several practices near the Erasmus MC and in the city centre advertise English-language services. Use Zorgkaart and filter by Rotterdam + English.

### [The Hague](/cities/the-hague/)

The Hague has a large expat population due to the concentration of international institutions, embassies, and multinationals. As a result, there are several practices with strong English-language capacity, particularly in Benoordenhout, Statenkwartier, and Scheveningen. The Hague is one of the easier cities in the Netherlands for finding an English-speaking GP.

### [Eindhoven](/cities/eindhoven/)

Eindhoven has a substantial international workforce from ASML, NXP, and other tech companies. English-speaking GPs are relatively accessible here. Check Zorgkaart for practices in Woensel, Strijp, and the city centre. The smaller scale of the city also means you can phone around more quickly.

### [Utrecht](/cities/utrecht/)

Utrecht is popular with expats and students, which puts pressure on GP availability in the centre. The Lombok, Wittevrouwen, and Overvecht areas can be difficult. Practices in Leidsche Rijn and Vleuten-De Meern (newer residential areas to the west) often have more capacity. If you live centrally, be prepared to try several practices before finding one with availability.

## What to Do If No Practice Accepts You

This happens more often than it should, particularly in Amsterdam and Utrecht. If you have contacted several practices in your area and none are accepting new patients, here is what to do:

**Contact your health insurer.** Under Dutch law (the Zorgverzekeringswet), insurers are legally required to ensure their policyholders have access to a GP within a reasonable travel distance. If you report to your insurer that you cannot find a GP, they must help you. Keep a record of which practices you have already tried.

**Contact your gemeente.** Local councils sometimes maintain lists of practices with availability or can direct you to the regional GP coordination service.

**Zorgkaart Nederland's "Huisarts Zoeken" function.** The site has a dedicated search function that shows practices currently accepting patients. Filter results by "accepting new patients" if this option is available.

**Be persistent and follow up.** Patient lists do open up — people move, leave the country, or switch practices. Calling back after a few weeks is not unusual.

## Emergency Care vs. Your GP

It is worth being clear on when to go where, because the Dutch system draws a firm line.

| Situation | Where to Go |
|-----------|-------------|
| Non-urgent medical question or issue | Call your huisarts during office hours |
| Urgent but not life-threatening (evenings, weekends) | Huisartsenpost (GP out-of-hours clinic) |
| Life-threatening emergency | 112 (ambulance) or A&E (Spoedeisende Hulp) |
| Prescription refill or repeat | Contact your own huisarts |
| Mental health support | Via referral from your huisarts |

### The Huisartsenpost (Out-of-Hours GP Clinic)

If you need to see a GP outside normal surgery hours — evenings, weekends, public holidays — you call the **huisartsenpost**, not the A&E. The number for your local huisartsenpost is usually on your GP practice's voicemail message. You can also find it at **thuisarts.nl**.

The huisartsenpost is for urgent but non-life-threatening issues: a high fever that will not come down, a wound that needs attention, sudden severe pain. They triage calls and decide whether you need to come in, receive a phone consultation, or can wait until your GP is open.

Going to the A&E (Spoedeisende Hulp) with a non-emergency will often result in being redirected to the huisartsenpost anyway — and may come with a cost if your situation is assessed as non-emergency. The Dutch system takes triage seriously.

## A Few Practical Notes from Experience

The Dutch GP system is genuinely good once you are in it. My own huisarts speaks excellent English, runs on time, and gives clear advice without over-medicalising things. Dutch GPs tend to take a "watchful waiting" approach to many conditions — they will not immediately prescribe antibiotics or refer you to a specialist for something that is likely to resolve on its own. Coming from a British background, I found this reassuring rather than dismissive. It is worth knowing this in advance, so you understand it as a feature of the system rather than a brush-off.

If you have a specific chronic condition or complex medical history, mention this when you register. Some practices have GPs or nurses with particular expertise, and it can make a difference to request a GP who has experience with your condition.

Finally: do not wait until you are ill to find a GP. Register as soon as you arrive. The process takes time even when it goes smoothly, and you do not want to be searching for a practice while you are unwell.
