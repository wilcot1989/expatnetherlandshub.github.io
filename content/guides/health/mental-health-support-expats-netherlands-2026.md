When I moved from London to Rotterdam eleven years ago, I thought I had it all sorted. I had a job lined up, a flat, a Dutch boyfriend who could translate anything I didn't understand. What I hadn't planned for was the particular flavour of loneliness that arrives about three months in — after the novelty wears off and before the roots have grown in. I didn't feel sad exactly. I felt invisible.

I didn't seek help for another year. That's a year I wish I had back.

Mental health is something the expat community doesn't talk about enough. We share tips about the [BSN registration process](/guides/legal/bsn-registration-guide-2026/) and argue passionately about which neighbourhoods have the best cycling infrastructure. But the emotional side of uprooting your life — the grief for the familiar, the exhaustion of adapting constantly, the relationships that stretch and sometimes break under the distance — that stays quietly in the background.

This guide is for anyone who is ready to bring it to the foreground. Whether you're feeling low, overwhelmed, or simply want to know what support exists before you ever need it, here's what I've learnt about finding mental health support as an expat in the Netherlands.

---

## How the Dutch Mental Health System Works

The Dutch healthcare system is structured in tiers, and mental health care follows the same logic. Understanding the pathway makes it far less daunting.

**Tier 1 — Your huisarts (GP)**

Everything starts with your huisarts. If you haven't registered with one yet, that's the first step — you need a GP to access almost anything in Dutch healthcare. Your GP is the gatekeeper, and in mental health care, that role is taken seriously. A good huisarts won't simply hand you a referral and wish you luck. They'll ask questions, assess urgency, and often provide initial support themselves.

If you're still sorting out your health insurance, have a read of the [Dutch health insurance guide for expats](/guides/health/dutch-health-insurance-guide-expats-2026/) — registration with a GP is only possible once you have a BSN and ideally basic insurance in place.

**Tier 2 — POH-GGZ (Practice-Based Mental Health Worker)**

Many GP practices now have a POH-GGZ (Praktijkondersteuner Huisarts – Geestelijke Gezondheidszorg) attached. This is a mental health practitioner — often a psychologist or social worker — who works directly within the GP setting. They provide short-term support: typically 4–8 sessions focused on mild to moderate symptoms such as anxiety, low mood, stress, or adjustment difficulties.

This is enormously useful for expats, because adjustment difficulties (which is exactly what many of us experience) fall squarely within this tier. Sessions with the POH-GGZ are free of extra charge under your basic insurance — no additional deductible applies here.

Ask your GP specifically: "Do you have a POH-GGZ, and do they speak English?" Not all do, but many in larger cities do.

**Tier 3 — Specialistische GGZ (Specialist Mental Health Care)**

For more complex or persistent issues — clinical depression, PTSD, personality disorders, eating disorders, OCD — your GP will refer you to specialist GGZ care. This is provided by mental health organisations (GGZ instellingen) such as GGZ inGeest, Arkin, Parnassia, or PsyQ, among many others.

This tier involves proper psychological or psychiatric assessment and treatment. Waiting times here are the main challenge in the Dutch system: they can be several months. However, if your GP marks a referral as urgent, the process can move faster.

## Getting a Referral Through Your GP

The referral process is simpler than it sounds, but it does require you to be open with your huisarts. Many expats — myself included at first — downplay their symptoms in the GP's office because they worry about being dramatic or don't have the Dutch vocabulary to explain how they feel.

A few practical tips:

**Speak plainly.** You don't need clinical language. "I've been feeling low for several weeks and it's affecting my work and sleep" is entirely sufficient.

**Request an English-speaking GP if needed.** In Amsterdam, Rotterdam, The Hague, and other international cities, many GP practices cater specifically to expats and have English-speaking staff. Ask your employer, expat network, or housing service for a recommendation.

**Write it down.** If you struggle to articulate your feelings verbally — especially across a language gap — bring a written note. Dutch GPs are used to this and won't think it's odd.

**Don't wait until it's severe.** The Dutch system, unlike some others, does support early intervention. You don't need to be in crisis to ask for help.

Once your GP issues a referral (verwijsbrief), it is valid for six months and should specify whether it's for basis-GGZ (short-to-medium complexity) or specialistische GGZ.

## What Is Covered by Insurance

Health insurance coverage for mental health care in the Netherlands is part of the basic package (basisverzekering), but the specifics matter.

**What is covered:**

- POH-GGZ sessions at your GP practice: covered in full, no deductible applies
- Basis-GGZ (generalistische GGZ): covered after your annual deductible (eigen risico) of €385 in 2026
- Specialistische GGZ: covered after the eigen risico, for recognised diagnoses
- Crisis care: always covered, no deductible threshold required

**What is not covered under basic insurance:**

- Relationship or couples therapy
- Coaching (as distinct from psychotherapy)
- Sessions with therapists who are not registered with the BIG-register (Dutch healthcare professional register)
- Therapy for conditions not classified as a DSM disorder (e.g. life events, work stress without a clinical diagnosis)

This last point catches people out. If you see a life coach or a counsellor who is not BIG-registered, it will not be covered — even if they are excellent and even if a GP has spoken positively about them.

Some supplementary insurance (aanvullende verzekering) packages do cover a number of sessions with psychologists or alternative practitioners per year, even without a clinical diagnosis. If mental health support is a priority for you, it's worth checking this when choosing your insurance. ## English-Speaking Therapists and Where to Find Them

Finding an English-speaking therapist who is both good and available is genuinely one of the harder parts of this process. Here's where to look.

**PsyQ**
PsyQ is part of the Parnassia Group and operates clinics in Amsterdam, Rotterdam, The Hague, Utrecht, and other cities. They have a strong international reputation and offer English-language treatment for a range of conditions. Referral from a GP required. Covered by basic insurance after eigen risico.

**iPractice**
Amsterdam-based, explicitly international in focus. iPractice offers English-language therapy for anxiety, burnout, depression, trauma, and relationship difficulties. They work with most Dutch health insurers and have both in-person and online options.

**Expat Psychology**
A smaller practice but one that specifically serves the international community. Based in Amsterdam but also offers online sessions. Not all sessions are insurance-covered; check with them directly.

**The NVPA (Nederlandse Vereniging van Psychoanalytische Psychotherapie) directory**
You can filter for English-speaking therapists. Useful if you prefer psychoanalytic or psychodynamic therapy.

**Zorgkaart Nederland (zorgkaartnederland.nl)**
The main Dutch review platform for healthcare providers. You can filter by language, location, and specialism. Reading reviews from other patients can help you get a feel for a practice before committing.

**Your employer's EAP (Employee Assistance Programme)**
If you work for a larger international company, check whether your employer has an EAP. Many do, and these often include a number of free, confidential therapy sessions (usually 4–8) with no GP referral needed and no insurance paperwork.

## Online Therapy Options

Online therapy has expanded significantly in the Netherlands since 2020, and for expats it has a particular appeal: you can access it from anywhere, sessions can be in English, and you avoid the awkward Dutch waiting rooms.

**OpenUp**
One of the best options for international professionals. OpenUp offers English and Dutch-language therapy through video sessions. It works with many employers on a B2B basis, so your company may offer it as a benefit. Also available direct to consumers. Sessions with a psychologist are available on a coaching or therapy basis.

**Therapieland**
More focused on structured, evidence-based online programmes (cognitive behavioural therapy, mindfulness-based therapy). Primarily in Dutch, but some programmes are available in English. Can be combined with a therapist for a blended approach.

**BetterHelp and Talkspace**
American platforms available to Netherlands-based users. Not covered by Dutch insurance, but useful if you want a therapist from your home country or if Dutch insurance coverage isn't a priority. The cost is typically around €200–€350 per month depending on the plan.

**Online GP platforms**
Some Dutch online GP services (such as Livi or Dokter Dichtbij) can issue referrals digitally, which can speed up access to the wider system.

## Crisis Support and Emergency Numbers

Please save these. Even if you never need them, you may be able to share them with someone who does.

**113 Zelfmoordpreventie (Suicide Prevention)**
- Free, 24/7
- Phone: **113**
- Free national number: **0800-0113**
- Online chat: **113.nl**
- Available in Dutch; English-speaking staff available on request

**Sensoor**
- Emotional support, not just for crisis situations
- Phone: **0900-0767** (charged at standard rate)
- Online chat and email via sensoor.nl
- Available in Dutch; limited English availability

**Emergency services**
- If someone is in immediate danger: **112**

**International SOS / Expatriate support lines**
If your employer or insurance provides International SOS membership, they offer 24/7 mental health support in English. Worth checking your policy documents.

I want to say something here that I mean genuinely: reaching out in a moment of crisis is one of the hardest things to do. The language barrier makes it harder. But **113 is free, always available, and the people who answer it are there specifically for moments when everything feels impossible.** Please use it.

## Expat-Specific Mental Health Challenges

Expats face a particular set of pressures that don't always fit neatly into clinical categories, but are very real.

**Adjustment and culture shock**
The Netherlands is considered a relatively easy country to settle in — and in many ways it is. But "easier than average" doesn't mean easy. Dutch directness, a well-documented preference for privacy and keeping circles small, and a bureaucratic system that can feel impenetrable all add up. Understanding [Dutch work culture](/guides/work/dutch-work-culture-guide-expats-2026/) helps with the professional side of this, but the personal adjustment takes longer.

**Language isolation**
Even if you function perfectly in English at work and in daily life, the inability to communicate subtly — to make a joke land, to pick up on social cues, to advocate clearly for yourself in a medical setting — creates a specific kind of exhaustion. It's one of the reasons I recommend starting to learn Dutch early. Apps and tools for this are covered in our [best apps to learn Dutch guide](/guides/integration/best-apps-learn-dutch-2026/).

**Loss of identity and social network**
Your identity is partly constructed by your relationships and your environment. When you move, you lose the people who know your history. Building a new network takes years, not months. This isn't failure — it's the actual timeline.

**Relationship strain**
Whether you moved as a couple, as a family, or alone, relocation puts pressure on relationships. One partner may be professionally thriving while the other struggles. Children may find it easier to adapt while parents feel adrift. These dynamics are worth addressing early.

**Financial stress**
The Netherlands is an expensive country. The pressure of managing [cost of living in the Netherlands](/guides/housing/cost-of-living-netherlands-2026/) alongside the cost of building a new life can be significant — and financial stress is a known risk factor for poor mental health.

**Integration anxiety**
The [inburgeringsexamen](/guides/integration/inburgeringsexamen-preparation-guide-2026/) and other integration requirements create real pressure, especially for expats who did not originally plan to stay long-term. The uncertainty of visa status, the fear of not integrating "well enough" — these weigh on people.

## Self-Help Resources and Communities

Professional support is valuable, but it's not the only resource available to you.

**Expat communities**
InterNations (internations.org) has chapters in all major Dutch cities and organises regular social events. The emphasis is on connection rather than therapy, but social connection is protective for mental health.

**Facebook groups**
Groups like "Expats in Amsterdam", "Expats in the Netherlands", and city-specific variations can be surprisingly useful for finding recommendations and sharing experiences. The quality varies, but for specific questions ("can anyone recommend an English-speaking therapist in Utrecht?") they tend to deliver.

**Meetup.com**
Hobby and interest-based meetups are often a gentler way to meet people than formal expat networking, and building friendships around shared activities is one of the most reliable ways to reduce loneliness.

**Mindfulness-based apps**
Headspace and Calm are available in English and have strong evidence bases for mild anxiety and stress. Not a substitute for therapy, but a useful daily tool.

**Books**
"The Art of Happiness" by the Dalai Lama and Howard Cutler. "Lost Connections" by Johann Hari (genuinely useful on the social determinants of depression). "F**k It: The Ultimate Spiritual Way" by John C. Parkin (written in the Netherlands, incidentally, and more thoughtful than the title suggests).

## Costs Breakdown

Here's an honest overview of what you might pay.

| Service | Cost to You | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| POH-GGZ (at your GP) | €0 | Covered by basic insurance, no deductible |
| Basis-GGZ (insured therapist) | €0 after eigen risico | €385 eigen risico applies per year |
| Specialistische GGZ (insured) | €0 after eigen risico | Referral required |
| Private therapist (BIG-registered) | €80–€150 per session | May be partially covered by aanvullende |
| Private therapist (not BIG-registered) | €60–€120 per session | Not covered by any insurance |
| Online therapy (OpenUp, iPractice) | Varies | Insurance coverage depends on provider |
| BetterHelp / Talkspace | €200–€350 per month | Not covered by Dutch insurance |
| EAP (via employer) | €0 | Check your employment contract |

The eigen risico resets every calendar year on 1 January. If you've already met it for other healthcare costs in 2026, your GGZ treatment will be fully covered.

If cost is a genuine barrier, be upfront with your GP. There are subsidised options and sliding-scale fees available in some areas, particularly through public GGZ providers.

## Conclusion: Your Action Steps

Mental health support in the Netherlands is genuinely accessible — more so than in many countries — but it requires you to know the system and be willing to ask for help. Here's what I'd suggest doing now, rather than waiting until you need it urgently.

1. **Register with a GP** if you haven't already. This is step one for everything.
2. **Check your insurance** — know your eigen risico status and whether you have aanvullende cover for mental health. Use the [health insurance wizard](/tools/health-insurance-wizard/) if you're unsure what you have.
3. **Save the crisis numbers.** 113 and 0800-0113. Right now, in your phone.
4. **Talk to your GP about how you're feeling** — even if it feels premature. The POH-GGZ route is designed for exactly the kind of adjustment stress many expats experience.
5. **Look into what your employer offers.** An EAP can give you access to immediate, free, English-language support.
6. **Find your community.** Professional support and social connection work together, not in isolation.

Moving to a new country is one of the more demanding things a person can do. It asks you to be brave, patient, and adaptable — simultaneously and indefinitely. That takes a toll, and it's allowed to take a toll.

You're not struggling because you're weak. You're struggling because you're doing something hard. There is support here, and you deserve to use it.
