## What a Dutch Notaris Is

The Dutch notarial profession is unlike its Anglo-Saxon equivalents. A notaris is a state-licensed legal officer with a monopoly on certain types of official acts (notariële akten). They are appointed by the Crown (Koning) after rigorous training and serve a public function as much as a private one.

Their role is impartial. When two parties sign a deed in front of a notaris, the notaris is not representing either of them — they are ensuring the deed is legally correct, that both parties understand what they are signing, that registration is properly performed, and that the public record is accurate. This impartial role is why Dutch property transactions work the way they do: a single notaris handles the deed for both buyer and seller.

The acts that legally require a notaris in 2026:

- **Property transfer (leveringsakte)** — every house, apartment, or land transfer in NL
- **Mortgage deed (hypotheekakte)** — every mortgage on Dutch property
- **Marriage contract (huwelijkse voorwaarden)** — pre or post-marriage asset agreement
- **Civil partnership contract** — equivalent to marriage contract for partnerschapsregistratie
- **Will (testament)** — though simple wills can be self-written, a notarial will is recommended for any but the simplest situation
- **BV / NV incorporation** — every limited company formation
- **Partnership amendment for VOF / Maatschap** — for certain changes
- **Donation deed (schenkingsakte)** — for gifts above the annual exemption
- **Inheritance declaration (verklaring van erfrecht)** — typically needed to access deceased's accounts

If your situation involves any of these, you need a notaris.

## When You Will Need One as an Expat

Most expats encounter the notaris at one of these moments:

### Buying a House

This is the most common interaction. Every property purchase in the Netherlands legally requires a notaris to:

1. Draft the leveringsakte (transfer deed) based on the koopovereenkomst (purchase agreement)
2. Verify the seller is the legal owner via the kadaster (land registry)
3. Check for outstanding mortgages, liens, and encumbrances
4. Calculate and collect the buyer's costs (kosten koper)
5. Witness signing at the notaris office
6. Register the transfer at the kadaster
7. Disburse funds (you transfer the purchase price to the notaris escrow account; they pay the seller after registration)

If you are also taking a mortgage, the same notaris draws up the hypotheekakte at the same signing.

For a fuller view of buying a house, see my [Dutch mortgage explained](/guides/finance/dutch-mortgage-explained-expats-2026/) and [buying a house in NL guide](/guides/finance/buying-house-netherlands-expat-guide-2026/).

### Making a Will

Strongly recommended for expats with assets in multiple countries or non-Dutch family. Dutch inheritance law has different rules than UK, US, German, or French law. Without a will, Dutch intestacy rules apply, which may not match what you want. A notarial will costs EUR 350 to EUR 700 and is one of the highest-value EUR 500 you can spend in your Dutch life.

I cover the full landscape in my [inheritance law and wills guide](/guides/legal/dutch-inheritance-law-wills-expats-2026/).

### Getting Married or Entering a Partnership

If you marry in NL without a marriage contract, the default regime since 2018 is "limited community of goods" (beperkte gemeenschap van goederen). Pre-marital assets stay separate; assets acquired during marriage are joint. If this default does not match your wishes — common for international couples with assets abroad, or significant pre-marital wealth — you need a huwelijkse voorwaarden contract drafted by a notaris.

Similarly, registered partnerships (geregistreerd partnerschap) follow the same default and require a notarial contract for variations.

### Starting a BV (Limited Company)

If you incorporate a Dutch BV (the most common limited company form), the formation deed must be executed by a notaris. Cost: typically EUR 800 to EUR 1,500 including statutory drafting. ZZP / sole trader registration via the [KvK](/guides/legal/kvk-business-register-complete-guide-2026/) does not require a notaris — only BV and NV formations do.

For business setup options, see my [ZZP registration guide](/blog/zzp-registration-netherlands-step-by-step-2026/).

### Receiving an Inheritance

If a relative dies and leaves you Dutch property or substantial Dutch assets, you typically need a verklaring van erfrecht (declaration of inheritance) drawn up by a notaris before banks and authorities will release funds to you. Cost: EUR 350 to EUR 800 depending on complexity.

## What It Costs

Notaris fees in the Netherlands are not regulated. Each notaris office sets its own fees, which means the same act can vary by 30 to 60 percent between offices. This is the most under-appreciated fact about Dutch notarial costs.

### Typical 2026 Ranges

| Act | Typical range |
| --- | --- |
| Property transfer deed alone | EUR 700-1,400 |
| Mortgage deed alone | EUR 600-1,200 |
| Combined house + mortgage | EUR 1,300-2,400 |
| Simple will (one person) | EUR 350-550 |
| Couple's wills (two people) | EUR 550-900 |
| Marriage contract | EUR 500-900 |
| Donation deed | EUR 350-650 |
| BV incorporation | EUR 800-1,500 |
| Verklaring van erfrecht (simple) | EUR 350-650 |
| Verklaring van erfrecht (complex with foreign assets) | EUR 800-2,000 |

For property purchases, the notaris fee is one component of your kosten koper budget. The total kosten koper is typically 5 to 6 percent of the purchase price for an existing home and includes notaris fees, transfer tax (overdrachtsbelasting), and bank costs.

### Where the Variation Comes From

A "simple" notaris office in a smaller town may charge EUR 750 for a property deed. A premium boutique notaris in Amsterdam Zuid serving high-end clients may charge EUR 1,400 for the same work. Both are legally identical. The expensive office gives you slightly more time, more polished service, and typically better English. The cheaper office may be perfectly adequate.

### Always Get a Written Quote

Before instructing any notaris, ask for an offerte (written quote). Specify exactly what you need (e.g., "leveringsakte plus hypotheekakte for purchase at address X, price Y, mortgage Z"). Compare two or three offertes. Ask what is included (e.g., kadaster fees, BTW). The variance will surprise you.

## How to Find an English-Speaking Notaris

Three approaches.

### 1. Recommendations from Your Network

If you know expats who have bought property in your area, ask who they used. Personal recommendations are the highest-quality signal because the notaris-client relationship is intimate — you are signing important documents, and rapport matters.

### 2. KNB Search

The Royal Dutch Association of Civil-Law Notaries (Koninklijke Notariële Beroepsorganisatie, KNB) has a search at notaris.nl. You can filter by location and by English-language specialism. This is reliable for finding a qualified notaris but does not give you quality differentiation.

### 3. Expat-Focused Comparison Sites

Several Dutch sites specifically compare notaris fees: degoedkoopstenotaris.nl, notarisscan.nl, notaristarieven.nl. These let you enter your transaction (e.g., property purchase at price X) and receive multiple quotes. I have used notarisscan.nl when buying my apartment in 2019 and saved roughly EUR 350 versus the first notaris my mortgage advisor recommended. The cheapest is not always the right pick — but knowing the price range arms you for negotiation.

### What to Look for in an English-Speaking Notaris for Expats

- Genuinely fluent English (test by asking a question in your initial email; written response quality is a good proxy for in-person clarity)
- Volume of expat clients (ask: "what percentage of your clients are international?")
- Familiarity with cross-border situations (e.g., wills involving UK / US / German assets)
- Clear written quote provided before instruction
- Reasonable response time on email (within 1-2 business days)
- An associate or paralegal who handles routine queries (saves you the senior notaris' time)

## What Happens at the Signing

For a property purchase signing — the most common expat interaction — here is what to expect.

### Before the Signing

You will receive draft documents typically 5 to 10 days before. Read them. The leveringsakte details the property, price, conditions, and warranties. The hypotheekakte details the mortgage terms. You have the right to ask for changes — though substantive amendments are rare at this stage as terms have already been agreed.

You will be sent the final figure to transfer to the notaris escrow account. ### At the Office

Signing takes 60 to 90 minutes for a property purchase with mortgage. The notaris reads through the deed, explains key clauses, asks if you understand and consent, and witnesses the signatures. The seller is present. You both sign. Coffee is served (Dutch notaris offices are unfailingly civilised).

For wills or marriage contracts, the signing is shorter — 30 to 45 minutes — but the consultation that precedes it (drafting the document) is more involved.

### After the Signing

The notaris registers the deed at the kadaster. The seller receives the funds (after payoff of any seller's mortgage). You receive the keys. The kadaster registration formally completes the transfer.

The notaris also handles the income tax declaration of the transfer (the kadaster informs the Belastingdienst automatically, but the notaris ensures all relevant tax filings are correct).

## When You Need a Notaris vs an Advocaat

This trips up many expats.

**Notaris**:
- Property purchase / sale
- Will
- Marriage contract
- BV incorporation
- Inheritance declarations
- Donation deeds

**Advocaat (lawyer)**:
- Employment disputes
- Divorce
- Civil litigation
- Immigration appeals
- Tax disputes (with belastingadviseur)
- Contract disputes
- Tenant-landlord disputes

Some firms host both notaris and advocaat services under one roof. For complex situations (e.g., divorce involving property), you may need both: an advocaat to negotiate the divorce settlement, a notaris to execute property transfers that result.

## Common Mistakes Expats Make

### Mistake 1: Accepting the First Notaris Recommended

Real estate agents and mortgage advisors often have a preferred notaris. They are usually competent — but they are also frequently more expensive than the market rate. Always get a second quote.

### Mistake 2: Skipping the Will

About half the expats I work with do not have a will. For singles or couples without children where intended beneficiaries align with Dutch intestacy rules, this may be acceptable. For anyone with children, complex family situations, assets in multiple countries, or specific wishes (e.g., a particular item to a particular person), the absence of a will is a real risk. EUR 500 to EUR 700 for a notarial will is one of the most under-spent items in expat estate planning.

### Mistake 3: Not Understanding Marriage Default

Couples marrying in NL without a marriage contract default to limited community of goods. International couples often do not realise this until divorce or death. If you have any pre-marital wealth, a partner with assets abroad, or a desire to keep finances separate, talk to a notaris about huwelijkse voorwaarden before the wedding — not after.

### Mistake 4: Assuming "Notary" in Your Home Country Is the Same Thing

US notaries witness signatures. UK notaries are rare and limited. German notars are similar to Dutch notarissen. The Dutch notaris role is much broader than what an American or British client expects — they are a substantive legal advisor on the act, not a rubber stamp.

### Mistake 5: Not Asking Questions

The notaris is being paid for their expertise. Ask them what every clause means. Ask about implications. Ask "what would you advise?" — they often will not volunteer advice (impartial role) but will answer direct questions. A good signing is an interactive one.

## Notaris in Other Languages

While English is the most common second language at Dutch notaris offices, many serve clients in:

- German — particularly in border regions and Amsterdam
- French — Amsterdam and The Hague international firms
- Spanish — limited but available in Amsterdam and Rotterdam
- Italian, Portuguese — rare but findable in Amsterdam

For non-English-speaking expats, a notaris fluent in your home language eliminates a significant risk of misunderstanding.

## Final Thought

The Dutch notaris is unfamiliar to most expats but, once you understand the role, surprisingly reassuring. The impartiality, the public-record function, and the legal weight of the akte all combine to make Dutch property and inheritance law more transparent than many home-country equivalents. The cost is real but the outcome is a properly registered, legally certain transaction.

Two practical steps:

1. The next time you have a major life event (house purchase, marriage, child, inheritance), do not skip the notaris consultation. EUR 350 to EUR 700 for a will or marriage contract is excellent value.
2. For property purchases, always compare three quotes. The savings are real, the work is identical, and the difference between EUR 1,300 and EUR 2,000 on the same act is pure overpayment if you do not shop.
