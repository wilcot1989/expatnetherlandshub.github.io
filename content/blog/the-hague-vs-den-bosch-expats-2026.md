When I moved to the Netherlands from the UK, The Hague was my first base. I lived in Bezuidenhout for two and a half years before relocating, and I have visited Den Bosch dozens of times since — both for work with clients in the south, and because my partner's family is Brabant. So when expats ask me about these two cities, I am answering from real experience in both.

The Hague and Den Bosch are an unusual pairing. They are not often compared — most expats weighing cities consider Amsterdam, Rotterdam, and Utrecht. But I get this specific question more than you might expect, because the two cities offer genuinely different propositions: The Hague as the international, English-friendly capital of governance; Den Bosch as a quieter, more authentically Dutch southern city with a lower cost of living.

This is my 2026 comparison.

## The Quick Verdict

If you are reading this on a phone in a hurry: **The Hague** if you are coming for a job in international institutions, NGOs, embassies, or law; if you have school-age children needing English-language education; if you do not yet speak Dutch and need an English-functional city. **Den Bosch** if you have a Dutch partner, are willing to learn Dutch, want a calmer southern lifestyle, and value cost of living over career options.

The rest of this guide unpacks why.

## City Profiles at a Glance

### The Hague (Den Haag)

- Population: roughly 565,000 (third-largest Dutch city)
- International population: about 22 percent (one of the highest in NL)
- Economy: government, international institutions, NGOs, legal services, oil and gas (Shell HQ moved partially out but support functions remain)
- Coastal — Scheveningen and Kijkduin beaches inside city limits
- Tram and bus network: very dense
- Train: 50 minutes to Amsterdam, 25 to Rotterdam, 35 to Schiphol

### Den Bosch ('s-Hertogenbosch)

- Population: roughly 160,000 (provincial capital of Noord-Brabant)
- International population: about 6 percent
- Economy: Heineken (HQ), financial services (Achmea, ASR), retail HQs, regional government, healthcare
- Inland — historic walled centre with canals (binnendieze)
- More cycling-oriented, smaller bus network
- Train: 30 minutes to Utrecht, 25 to Eindhoven, 50 to Amsterdam

The most immediate difference: scale. The Hague is roughly 3.5 times larger and has a fundamentally different urban character. Den Bosch is a midsize Dutch city; The Hague is a metropolitan capital.

## Cost of Living: Real 2026 Numbers

I track Dutch cost of living closely as part of my work. Here are the numbers as they stand in mid-2026.

### Housing

| Type | The Hague | Den Bosch |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1-bed centre | EUR 1,650-2,100 | EUR 1,150-1,450 |
| 1-bed outside centre | EUR 1,300-1,650 | EUR 900-1,200 |
| 3-bed family rental | EUR 2,500-3,500 | EUR 1,650-2,300 |
| Buy: 100 m2 apartment | EUR 480,000-650,000 | EUR 320,000-420,000 |
| Buy: 150 m2 family house | EUR 750,000-1.1m | EUR 480,000-680,000 |

The housing gap is significant — about 40 percent more expensive in The Hague at every level. For a family considering buying, this is the single biggest financial decision input.

I cover the Dutch housing search in detail in my [finding housing in the Netherlands guide](/guides/housing/finding-housing-netherlands-expats-2026/) — the principles apply to both cities, but Den Bosch is materially less competitive than The Hague.

### Daily Costs

- Cup of coffee: EUR 3.80 (The Hague) vs EUR 3.20 (Den Bosch)
- Beer in a bar: EUR 5.50 vs EUR 4.20
- Dinner for two mid-range: EUR 75-95 vs EUR 55-75
- Monthly transport pass: EUR 95 (The Hague, includes tram) vs EUR 75 (Den Bosch, mainly bus)
- Groceries: roughly equal, both are within standard Dutch supermarket pricing — see my [Dutch supermarkets guide](/guides/daily-life/dutch-supermarkets-guide-expats-2026/)

A frugal single expat can live in Den Bosch on EUR 2,200-2,500 per month all-in. The same lifestyle in The Hague needs EUR 2,800-3,300.

### Income Side

The Hague's salaries are 5-12 percent higher for comparable roles, which partly offsets the cost of living gap but does not close it. Den Bosch retains a real cost-of-living advantage.

For the salary side specifically, see my [average salary in the Netherlands guide](/guides/work/average-salary-netherlands-2026/).

## The Job Market

This is where the cities diverge most.

### The Hague

The Hague is unique in the Netherlands for the density of English-functional employers:

- International institutions: ICC, ICJ, Europol, OPCW, Eurojust
- 100+ embassies and consulates
- NGOs and human rights organisations
- Major international law firms
- Shell and other multinationals' Dutch operations
- Tech startups in the Wijnhaven and Beatrixkwartier districts
- Significant freelance and consulting market serving the above

If you are job-hunting without Dutch fluency, The Hague offers more genuinely English-language roles than any Dutch city other than Amsterdam — and proportionally more, given its smaller size. My [how to find a job in the Netherlands as an expat guide](/blog/how-to-find-job-netherlands-expat-2026/) covers the practical steps; The Hague is the easiest city in NL to apply that playbook to.

### Den Bosch

Den Bosch is more of a Dutch corporate town:

- Heineken HQ
- Achmea (insurance)
- ASR Nederland (insurance)
- Jumbo Supermarkets HQ (15 minutes away in Veghel)
- Regional ICT and engineering firms
- Strong healthcare sector around Jeroen Bosch Hospital

These employers do hire internationals — particularly in IT, finance, and engineering — but most roles assume B2 Dutch or higher. If you arrive without Dutch, Den Bosch is a harder market.

The compensation: if you can get a role in Den Bosch, the lower cost of living means your euro goes further. A senior IT role at EUR 75,000 in Den Bosch produces meaningfully better quality of life than the same role at EUR 80,000 in The Hague.

## Housing Markets in Practice

### The Hague Housing

The market is competitive but more transparent than Amsterdam. International-friendly neighbourhoods include:

- **Bezuidenhout**: where I lived. Quiet, residential, 10-15 minute cycle to centre, popular with expats and Dutch families
- **Statenkwartier**: leafy, family-oriented, near international schools, expensive
- **Archipelbuurt**: classic, central, tree-lined streets, very expat-popular
- **Scheveningen**: beach proximity, mix of luxury and standard, Dutch and international
- **Voorburg / Leidschendam**: cheaper, suburban, good for families willing to commute slightly

Avoid: Schilderswijk and Transvaal unless you understand the trade-offs. They are not unsafe, but they are working-class, less English-friendly, and not where most expats settle long-term.

The Hague rental search is competitive — using a relocation agent or platforms like [HousingAnywhere](/guides/housing/housinganywhere-vs-kamernet-vs-funda-2026/) significantly reduces the time-to-housing for newcomers.

### Den Bosch Housing

Den Bosch is calmer. The city is small enough that you can comfortably search across all neighbourhoods:

- **Binnenstad (centre)**: historic, beautiful, walkable, but limited supply
- **Het Zand**: residential, 15 minute walk to centre, family-friendly
- **De Muntel / Boschveld**: trendy, gentrifying, popular with younger professionals
- **Maaspoort**: suburban, family-oriented, cheaper

You will find housing within 6-10 weeks searching seriously. In The Hague, the same search often takes 10-16 weeks for international newcomers without Dutch.

## International Schools

This is decisive for many families.

**The Hague:**

- The British School in the Netherlands (Voorschoten and Leidschendam)
- American School of The Hague (Wassenaar)
- The European School The Hague
- The International School of The Hague (Kijkduin)
- HSV International Primary School
- Several IB primary options

The cluster is mature, well-funded, and accommodates roughly 8,000 international students. Waiting lists exist for the most popular options but are manageable.

**Den Bosch:**

- No major international school inside the city
- Closest options: International School Eindhoven (~35 min drive), Maurick College (some bilingual streams), regional Dutch schools with strong English curriculum support

For families, this is the strongest argument for The Hague over Den Bosch.

## Healthcare and English-Speaking Doctors

Both cities have functional Dutch healthcare. The differences:

**The Hague**: Large concentration of GPs explicitly serving the international community. Several practices in Statenkwartier and Bezuidenhout advertise English service. Specialist care at HMC and Bronovo Hospital is competent and English-friendly.

**Den Bosch**: Jeroen Bosch Hospital is the regional reference and well-regarded. Finding an English-speaking GP is harder — possible, but usually requires asking around or contacting practices directly.

## Social Life and Community

### The Hague

The international community in The Hague is large, organised, and easy to plug into:

- Active InterNations chapter
- Multiple expat Facebook groups
- ACCESS Foundation (volunteer-run expat support)
- Numerous English-language Meetup groups
- A density of bars, restaurants, and cultural events serving internationals
- The diplomatic and NGO scene generates frequent receptions and networking events

A new arrival in The Hague who makes any effort can build a social life within 2-3 months.

### Den Bosch

The international community is smaller, scattered, and largely informal:

- Some expat Facebook groups, but lower activity
- A few English-language Meetup groups, mostly Eindhoven-focused
- Brabant culture is genuinely warmer than the Randstad — Dutch friendships are easier to form here than in Amsterdam or The Hague
- But: most socialising happens in Dutch

If your goal is to make Dutch friends and integrate, Den Bosch supports this better than The Hague. If your goal is an English-functional expat life, The Hague is much easier.

## Culture and Lifestyle

### The Hague Culture

- Beaches: Scheveningen and Kijkduin within city limits
- Art: Mauritshuis, Gemeentemuseum, KM21
- International events year-round
- More restaurant variety
- Slightly more formal — government town atmosphere
- Cycling is good but tram is also a real option

### Den Bosch Culture

- The Binnendieze (canals running under the medieval city) — genuinely unique in the Netherlands
- Sint Jan Cathedral — one of the finest Gothic churches in NL
- Carnaval (Den Bosch becomes "Oeteldonk" for five days in February) — a Brabant tradition that the Hague does not have
- Strong food culture — bossche bol pastries, café terraces, southern Dutch warmth
- More cycling-dominant, less public transport
- Smaller scale means everything is walkable or short cycle

Den Bosch has a charm that the Hague, for all its dignity, does not match. Walking the historic centre at dusk in summer is a genuinely beautiful Dutch experience.

## Commuting Reality

If you work elsewhere and live in either city:

**From The Hague:**
- To Rotterdam: 25 min by train, every 15 min
- To Amsterdam: 50 min, every 15 min
- To Schiphol: 35 min, every 15 min
- To Utrecht: 40 min direct

**From Den Bosch:**
- To Eindhoven: 25 min direct
- To Utrecht: 30 min direct
- To Amsterdam: 50 min direct
- To Rotterdam: 45 min direct
- To Tilburg: 15 min direct

Den Bosch is, surprisingly, an excellent rail base — it sits at the centre of the southern Netherlands network. If you need to move around the country, Den Bosch is barely worse than The Hague and is much better than Eindhoven or Maastricht.

## Weather and Outdoor Life

The Hague is coastal and wetter on average. Beach access in summer is a real lifestyle benefit (and an underrated one). Winters are slightly milder due to maritime moderation.

Den Bosch is inland, slightly drier in summer, slightly colder in winter. The surrounding Brabant countryside is beautiful — woodland, heath, and small villages within a 20-minute cycle. The river Maas is nearby for paddleboarding and walks.

Both cities are intensely cycle-friendly. See my [cycling in the Netherlands guide](/guides/daily-life/cycling-netherlands-expat-guide-2026/) for the basics that apply in both.

## Family Life

For families with school-age children, the calculus is largely:

- Need international school: The Hague
- Comfortable with Dutch school system or bilingual streams: either, with cost advantage to Den Bosch
- Want substantial international community for spouse: The Hague
- Want a calmer, safer, more village-like environment: Den Bosch

Crime statistics favour Den Bosch (low across the board). The Hague is also safe but, as a larger city, has more variation by neighbourhood.

For family-specific guidance, see my [moving to NL with kids guide](/guides/daily-life/moving-to-netherlands-with-kids-school-guide-2026/) and [expat family guide](/guides/daily-life/expat-family-guide-raising-kids-netherlands-2026/).

## Single Expat Life

For singles in their 20s and 30s, The Hague offers more — clubs, bars, dating pool size, organised social activities, English-functional dating apps with adequate density. Den Bosch is smaller and more couples-and-family-oriented; the dating market is thinner if you are not Dutch-speaking.

I have written about [single expat life in the Netherlands](/blog/single-expat-netherlands-guide-2026/) — the dynamics are easier to navigate in The Hague.

## When Each City Wins

**The Hague wins for:**
- International institution employees
- Lawyers, NGOs, diplomats
- Families needing English-language schooling
- Single expats with no Dutch
- Expats wanting beach access
- Anyone whose career requires English-functional networking

**Den Bosch wins for:**
- Expats with Dutch partners
- Career stability with willingness to learn Dutch
- Cost-conscious families willing to use Dutch schools
- Lifestyle preference: smaller, calmer, more genuinely Dutch
- Heineken / Achmea / ASR / regional financial sector employees
- Anyone tired of Randstad pace and expat-bubble life

## My Honest Recommendation

If you are unsure: start in The Hague. The infrastructure for English-speaking expats is more developed; the job market is more open; the schools exist; the social scene is easier to access. You can always move to Den Bosch later when your Dutch is stronger and you know what you want.

If you are decided: Den Bosch is a wonderful city for the right person — particularly the partner-of-a-Dutch-person profile, or the Brabant-headquartered employee. Cost of living and quality of life are genuinely better than the Randstad. But it is a Dutch city that happens to host some internationals, not an international city. Go in clear-eyed about what that means.

## Final Thought

I made the move from The Hague to a smaller Dutch town partly for cost-of-living reasons, partly because my Dutch had progressed enough that I no longer needed the international scaffolding. That progression is real for many expats: The Hague as a soft-landing first city, smaller cities like Den Bosch as a longer-term home once Dutch fluency is reasonable.

Both cities are excellent. Pick the one that matches where you are now, not where you imagine yourself being in five years. You can always move later.
