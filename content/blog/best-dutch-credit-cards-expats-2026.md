## Why the Netherlands does not love credit cards

In the US, a credit card is your primary payment method, your credit score builder, and your travel insurance. In the Netherlands, a credit card is a niche product. Dutch culture is debt-averse. The phrase "op de pof" (buying on credit) carries social weight. Most daily transactions happen on iDEAL bank transfers or Maestro debit.

Three consequences for expats:

**Acceptance is patchy in small shops**. Many Dutch supermarkets, bakeries, and corner shops only accept PIN (debit). I have stood at the till at Albert Heijn watching tourists realise their Visa is useless.

**Issuance is conservative**. Banks issue credit cards reluctantly and with strict income requirements. Default rates in the Netherlands are very low precisely because the gating is tight.

**Limits are modest**. A €5,000 limit on your first card is normal. €10,000 is generous. The €30,000 limits that exist on US cards are unheard of here.

This is not a problem if you adjust your expectations. Treat a Dutch credit card as a travel and online-shopping tool, not as your primary payment method. Use your debit card for everyday spending.

In your first 3 to 6 months in the Netherlands, you almost certainly will not qualify for a Dutch credit card. Here is what to do instead.

**Keep your home country credit card active**. Pay it off monthly in full from a Wise or Revolut account. The FX fees on a UK or US card used in euros are typically 2.5 to 3 percent — not great but workable for occasional purchases.

**Open a [Revolut account](https://go.expatnetherlandshub.com/revolut){target="_blank" rel="nofollow sponsored noopener"} for spending in different currencies**. These two together cover 90 percent of what most expats actually need a credit card for: hotel pre-authorisations, online shopping in dollars, and not-paying-FX-fees-while-travelling.

The only thing they do not do is build a Dutch BKR credit history or unlock chargeback protection on large EU purchases. For those you do eventually want a real credit card.

## My ranking of Dutch credit cards for expats in 2026

After helping a lot of friends through this process, here is my honest ranking.

### 1. ICS Platinum Mastercard — best for travel and serious expats

ICS Cards is owned by ABN AMRO and is the largest standalone credit card issuer in the Netherlands. The ICS Platinum is the card I use myself.

What you get:
- No foreign transaction fees on most travel-related spending
- Comprehensive travel insurance (worldwide, including medical)
- Purchase protection on items above €100
- Rental car CDW insurance
- Annual fee around €100 to €120

Catch: the ICS Platinum requires at least one year of Dutch tax residency and a verifiable income. New arrivals will be rejected.

### 2. ICS Visa World Card — best entry-level Dutch credit card

The standard ICS Visa World is the easiest "real" Dutch credit card to qualify for. It is what I had for my first two years.

What you get:
- No annual fee in the first year, around €20 to €30 thereafter
- Standard purchase protection
- Limited travel insurance
- Mobile app and online statements

Catch: a 1.5 percent FX fee on non-euro transactions. Acceptable for occasional use; not great for travel.

### 3. ABN AMRO World Mastercard — best if you already bank with ABN

ABN AMRO offers a credit card to its current account holders. If you already have an ABN AMRO checking account, this is a one-form application.

What you get:
- World Mastercard tier (decent travel benefits)
- Annual fee around €30
- Integrated with the ABN AMRO mobile app
- Direct debit from your ABN current account

Catch: 1.2 percent FX fee, mediocre rewards, only available to ABN current account holders. Decent but not exciting.

### 4. ING Credit Card (Visa) — solid if you bank with ING

ING's credit card sits alongside its current account. Application is straightforward if your salary lands at ING.

What you get:
- No annual fee for some account tiers
- 1.5 percent FX fee
- Decent app integration

Catch: limited travel benefits, basic purchase protection, no significant rewards. Functional rather than exciting.

### 5. Bunq Credit Card — good for digital-first expats

Bunq is a Dutch neobank that has expanded its credit card offering. It is the most expat-friendly application process — fully online, no branch visit needed.

What you get:
- Multi-currency support and mid-market FX rates
- Tied to Bunq's premium subscription tiers
- Modern app and instant transaction notifications

Catch: requires a paid Bunq subscription (€2.99 to €17.99 per month depending on tier). The credit limits offered are typically lower than at ABN or ICS.

### 6. American Express Gold / Platinum — niche but worth knowing

Amex operates in the Netherlands but acceptance is patchy outside hotels, airlines, and premium retail. The Gold and Platinum cards have strong travel rewards and Membership Rewards points that transfer to KLM Flying Blue.

What you get:
- Strong travel and lifestyle rewards
- Excellent customer service
- High issuance threshold (Platinum effectively requires high income)

Catch: many Dutch shops do not accept Amex. You will always need a Mastercard or Visa as backup.

## Application process: what actually works

I have helped at least a dozen expats apply for Dutch credit cards. Here is the pattern that works.

**Step 1**: Wait until you have at least 3 months of Dutch payslips. Banks want to see income that lands in a Dutch account. A foreign salary, even a high one, often gets rejected.

**Step 2**: Have your basics in order. Active Dutch bank account with regular salary deposits, BSN, registered address (proof of registration from the gemeente), and a Dutch phone number.

**Step 3**: Apply at the bank where your salary lands. Cross-bank applications (e.g. salary at ING, applying at ABN) succeed less often than same-bank applications.

**Step 4**: For ICS, apply directly through ics.nl and use the online form. Phone follow-up if you do not hear back within ten days. ICS is more flexible than the high-street banks.

**Step 5**: If rejected, wait three months before reapplying. Multiple rejections in quick succession look bad in BKR.

For expats who do not yet qualify, the alternative is straightforward: use Wise and Revolut for everything until your three-month payslip threshold is met.

## Foreign transaction fees and why they matter

This is where Dutch credit cards underperform versus modern alternatives.

A typical Dutch credit card charges:
- 1.2 to 1.5 percent FX fee on non-euro transactions
- A markup on the FX rate of around 0.5 to 1 percent
- Effective total cost: around 2 to 2.5 percent on every dollar or pound spent abroad

A Wise card charges:
- Mid-market FX rate (no markup)
- A flat fee of 0.4 to 0.6 percent on most currencies
- Effective total cost: around 0.5 percent

For a travel spend of €5,000 in foreign currency, the difference is around €100 per year. Not enormous, but meaningful. This is why most savvy expats I know carry a Dutch credit card for chargeback protection on big purchases and a Wise card for everything else.

## Travel insurance: read the policy carefully

The Platinum-tier credit cards (ICS Platinum, Amex Platinum, some ABN World Mastercards) include travel insurance. This is genuinely useful, but read the small print.

Common gotchas:
- **Trip must be paid for using the card** for cover to apply. Booking a flight on a different card and then claiming on your Platinum will be rejected.
- **Geographic limits**. Some policies exclude the US, Canada, or specific high-cost destinations.
- **Medical excess**. Most cards have a €100 to €250 excess on medical claims.
- **Pre-existing conditions**. Almost universally excluded unless declared in advance.

For long-term expats, dedicated travel insurance is usually better. ## Building Dutch credit history

The Dutch BKR system is binary, not score-based. There is no "credit score" to optimise. What matters is:

- **No negative records** (missed payments, defaults, settled debts)
- **No excessive open credit** (a credit card limit, an overdraft, a phone contract financing plan all count)
- **Stable, reportable income** in the Netherlands

For expats planning to apply for a Dutch mortgage within 2 to 5 years, having a single Dutch credit card with timely payments helps. Multiple cards do not help — they hurt by signalling open credit lines.

My rule: one Dutch credit card, paid in full each month, no overdraft, no consumer financing. Keep BKR clean for the mortgage application.

## Honest recommendation

For most expats arriving in the Netherlands in 2026, the right answer is:

**Months 0 to 6**: Wise and Revolut. Skip Dutch credit cards entirely. Use your home country credit card sparingly and pay it off monthly.

**Months 6 to 12**: Apply for the ICS Visa World Card or your bank's standard credit card. Use it lightly for hotel deposits, online shopping, and travel.

**Year 2 onward**: If you travel a lot, upgrade to ICS Platinum or consider Amex Gold. Otherwise stick with the basic card and continue using Wise for FX-heavy spending.

The mistake to avoid is applying for multiple cards in the first six months, getting rejected at all of them, and burning credit applications that may show up in BKR for the next year.

Treat the Dutch credit card system as designed: cautious, conservative, and modest. It is not the US or the UK. Once you accept that, the system is actually fine.
