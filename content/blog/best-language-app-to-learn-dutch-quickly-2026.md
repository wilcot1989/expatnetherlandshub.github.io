## Why most language apps fail for Dutch specifically

Dutch is what linguists politely call a "less commonly taught" language. There are about 24 million native speakers worldwide, which sounds like a lot until you compare it to Spanish (560 million) or French (310 million). For app developers, that means Dutch is a side project. The Spanish course gets the budget. The Dutch course gets a stagiair on a Tuesday afternoon.

This shows up in three ways:

1. **Smaller content libraries.** A Babbel Spanish learner has dozens of refresher modules, podcasts, and games. Babbel Dutch has the core course and not much else.
2. **Less updated content.** Duolingo's Spanish tree was rebuilt with the CEFR framework. The Dutch tree was last given real love years ago.
3. **Pronunciation is hard to teach via app.** Dutch has sounds — the guttural "g", the "ui" diphthong, the rolling "r" depending on region — that no AI speech recognition handles cleanly in 2026. My Duolingo app cheerfully accepted my pronunciation of "schaatsen" when I sounded like a cat coughing up a hairball.

Add to this that Dutch people themselves switch to English the moment they hear an accent. You cannot practice in the wild the way you can with Spanish in Madrid. Every conversation is a tiny battle to keep them speaking their own language to you. Apps will not prepare you for that social negotiation. Only a tutor will.

That is the unfair landscape. Now let me get into each tool.

## Babbel: the structured workhorse

I subscribed to Babbel in late 2020 after a friend who learned German through it swore by the platform. I paid roughly 7 euros per month on the 12-month plan, which works out to around 84 euros per year. The monthly rate is closer to 13 euros if you pay month-to-month.

Babbel's Dutch course is organised into beginner, intermediate, and advanced tracks, with each lesson lasting around 10 to 15 minutes. The structure is the part I genuinely loved. Unlike Duolingo, which throws vocabulary at you in random order, Babbel introduces grammar concepts deliberately. You learn the present tense, then you practice it across multiple lessons before they introduce the perfect tense. You build, you do not guess.

**What worked for me:**

- **Real grammar explanations** in English with examples. When I finally understood the difference between "er" used four different ways, it was thanks to a Babbel grammar tip popup.
- **Review system** that brings back vocabulary at spaced intervals. I retained roughly 70 percent of what Babbel taught me versus maybe 30 percent of what Duolingo crammed in.
- **Sentence-level practice** rather than word matching. You translate full sentences, which forces you to think about word order — and Dutch word order is its own circle of grammatical hell with the verb at the end of subordinate clauses.

**What did not work:**

- **Limited speaking practice.** The speech recognition was generous to a fault. I could mumble and it would mark me correct.
- **Content runs out.** I finished the entire Dutch beginner and intermediate tracks within about five months of consistent daily use. After that, I was just reviewing.
- **No real conversation simulation.** Babbel teaches you to read and write Dutch reasonably well. It does not teach you to handle a Dutch grandmother asking what time you need to be picked up from the station.

Babbel got me from absolute zero to high A2. It was the right choice for the foundation phase. I recommend it if you are someone who learned grammar in school and likes structured workbooks. If you are allergic to grammar tables, you will find Babbel tedious.

## Mondly: gamified but thin for Dutch

I downloaded Mondly during a free week promotion in early 2022. The interface is genuinely beautiful — colourful, animated, with AR features where you can apparently summon a Dutch chatbot into your living room. Cute idea.

The reality of using Mondly for Dutch was disappointing. The course felt machine-translated in places. Several phrases struck me as awkward Dutch — technically correct but not the way a real Dutch person would say it. I asked Margriet (my eventual Preply tutor) to look at some of the example sentences and she winced.

**Pricing:** Mondly runs around 9.99 euros per month, with annual plans dropping that to roughly 4 to 5 euros per month. They aggressively advertise lifetime deals around 90 euros.

**What worked:**

- **Daily challenges** with a streak mechanic. If you are competitive, this drives consistency.
- **Multiple source languages.** You can learn Dutch from Polish, Arabic, Mandarin, and so on. Babbel does not offer that range.
- **Vocabulary categories** organised by theme — at the airport, at the doctor, etc. Useful for travellers.

**What did not work:**

- **Shallow course depth.** I felt I had seen all the unique content within three weeks.
- **Weak grammar explanations.** They exist, but they are buried and brief.
- **Awkward translations.** Trust me, you do not want to repeat what Mondly sometimes asks you to say.

Mondly is a fine app for a casual learner who wants to dabble. It is not the tool I would recommend if your goal is to pass the inburgering exam or hold a real conversation with your in-laws. Skip it for Dutch specifically and use that budget on a Preply tutor instead.

## Duolingo: free, addictive, and frustratingly limited

Duolingo is the obvious starting point. Everyone has it. The owl is a meme. The streaks are a known psychological trick. And it is free, which is its single greatest feature.

I started Duolingo in 2019, dropped it, started again in 2020, dropped it, and started again in 2021. My longest streak peaked at 187 days. I am not proud of how much of that was me unlocking the app at 11:58pm to do one lesson on auto-pilot.

**What worked:**

- **Free tier is genuinely usable.** You can complete the entire Dutch tree without paying a cent.
- **Habit formation.** The streak mechanic is dark UX magic. It works.
- **Vocabulary exposure** at high volume. I learned hundreds of nouns I would not have picked up otherwise.

**What did not work:**

- **No real grammar instruction.** You are expected to absorb grammar by repeated exposure, which works for some patterns and completely fails for others. I never understood Dutch separable verbs from Duolingo. Babbel explained them in 15 minutes.
- **Conversational gaps.** I could conjugate "to eat" eighteen ways and not order a sandwich at Albert Heijn.
- **The Dutch tree feels stale.** Listening exercises use the same robotic voice. The new "Stories" feature has not been ported to Dutch in any depth.
- **Premium nags.** Free tier is workable but the interruptions are constant. Super Duolingo runs about 13 euros per month, which is harder to justify when Babbel costs the same and teaches more.

I still open Duolingo most mornings while drinking coffee. It is a warm-up, not a workout. Treat it as your free supplement, not your main course.

## CEFR levels — what A1 through B2 actually means

The Common European Framework of Reference for Languages (CEFR) is the standard used across Europe to grade language ability. The Dutch inburgering exam tests at A2. Most office jobs that "require Dutch" want B1 or B2. For more on the framework, see the [Council of Europe's CEFR page](https://www.coe.int/en/web/common-european-framework-reference-languages).

- **A1 — Beginner.** You can introduce yourself, order food, ask basic directions. Roughly 90 to 150 hours of study.
- **A2 — Elementary.** You can handle everyday transactions, describe your job, talk about the weather. The inburgering threshold. Roughly 180 to 260 hours total.
- **B1 — Intermediate.** You can follow most TV news with effort, hold a conversation about familiar topics, write emails. The level where Dutch friendships become possible. Roughly 350 to 490 hours total.
- **B2 — Upper Intermediate.** You can work in Dutch, follow rapid native speech, read newspapers. Roughly 500 to 700 hours.

I am being optimistic with these ranges. Add 30 percent if you are over 35, do not have language-learning hobbies, or work in English all day.

## My 6-month learning plan combining apps and tutoring

This is what I would do if I were starting over from zero today, with realistic working-adult time constraints (roughly 45 to 60 minutes per day plus two tutoring sessions per week).

**Month 1 — Foundation**
- Babbel: 15 minutes daily, work through beginner track
- Duolingo: 10 minutes morning warm-up
- Preply: 1 lesson per week (focus on pronunciation and basic conversation)
- Target: A1

**Month 2 — Building Vocabulary**
- Babbel: continue beginner, start intermediate
- Duolingo: maintain streak
- Preply: 2 lessons per week (introduce role-plays — at the doctor, at a café)
- Add: Bart de Pau on YouTube for listening practice (free)
- Target: solid A1

**Month 3 — Bridging to A2**
- Babbel: intermediate track
- Preply: 2 lessons per week (start writing exercises)
- Add: 1 podcast episode per week (NPO Radio 1 short news — Eenvoudig Nederlands if you can find it)
- Target: approaching A2

**Month 4 — A2 consolidation**
- Reduce Babbel to maintenance, 10 mins per day
- Preply: 2 lessons per week (move from role-play to free conversation)
- Add: read children's books (Jip en Janneke is genuinely useful)
- Target: A2 confirmed

**Month 5 — Pushing into B1**
- Drop Babbel if completed, or do refreshers
- Preply: 2 lessons per week (current events discussion)
- Add: watch a Dutch series with Dutch subtitles (NPO Start has many)
- Target: low B1

**Month 6 — B1 territory**
- Preply: 2 lessons per week (specialised content — work, immigration topics, whatever you need)
- Add: read NRC or Volkskrant short articles
- Try: one social interaction in Dutch per day (ordering coffee, small talk with neighbours)
- Target: solid B1

## Free alternatives if your budget is zero

If you genuinely cannot pay anything, you can still make real progress. It takes longer but it is doable.

- **NPO Start** — Dutch public broadcasting. Free with VPN if you are outside NL. Many programmes have Dutch subtitles. Start with kids' shows (Zappelin) and work up to soap operas (Goede Tijden Slechte Tijden is, against my will, an effective learning tool).
- **Bart de Pau on YouTube** — easy Dutch lessons, slow Dutch news, free pronunciation guides. He runs a free "1000 most common Dutch words" course that is genuinely well-made.
- **Taalunie resources** — the Dutch Language Union ([taalunie.org](https://www.taalunie.org/)) publishes free educational material and the authoritative reference for spelling and grammar.
- **NT2 textbooks from your local library** — most Dutch libraries lend "Nederlands als tweede taal" coursebooks. The library card itself costs around 20 to 30 euros per year, sometimes free for residents.
- **Language exchange (tandem partners)** — apps like Tandem and HelloTalk let you swap English for Dutch with a native speaker. Quality varies wildly but it costs nothing.
- **Inburgering courses** — if you are required to inburgeren, DUO offers loans of up to 10,000 euros for approved courses. For some residence permit holders these loans are partially or fully forgiven if you complete on time.

Free is slower but real. I would still strongly recommend even one Preply trial lesson per month — at 5 euros, it is cheaper than a pint and it will tell you whether your pronunciation is going off the rails.

## What I would do differently if I started over

Three things, in retrospect.

**First**, I would have started Preply in month one, not month thirty-six. I wasted two years thinking I needed to be "good enough" before I deserved a real tutor. That is backwards. A tutor is most valuable at the beginning, when bad habits are still soft clay.

**Second**, I would have done more output and less input from week one. I drilled vocabulary endlessly when I should have been talking to a real person — even badly — much sooner.

**Third**, I would have leaned harder on listening practice. Dutch native speech is much faster than Babbel's careful recordings. Get your ears trained early.

If you take one thing from this article: apps alone will not get you fluent in Dutch. They will get you to A2, slowly. To go further you need a human being who corrects your pronunciation, drags you through awkward sentences, and refuses to switch to English when you panic.

For most expats, the highest-ROI combination is **Babbel for structure** + **Duolingo for free daily reps** + **[Preply for weekly real conversation](https://go.expatnetherlandshub.com/preply?ref=/blog/best-language-app-to-learn-dutch-quickly-2026/)**.
