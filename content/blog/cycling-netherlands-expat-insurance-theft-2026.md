In my first year in the Netherlands I had two bikes stolen. The first was a €120 second-hand omafiets I bought from a sketchy guy near Centraal Station — I still believe he stole it back from me three weeks later. The second was a proper €600 Gazelle I locked outside the supermarket with a single ring lock. Gone in under ten minutes.

The second loss changed how I think about cycling here. Bikes are not optional in this country. They are how you actually live. And bike theft is a real, structural part of that life. The numbers are eye-watering: roughly 500,000 bikes reported stolen each year. The actual figure is probably closer to 700,000 because most expats do not bother filing a police report.

This is the guide I wish someone had given me on my first day. It covers what a sensible bike costs, how to lock it properly, when bike insurance is worth it, what to do if it is stolen, and the e-bike rules that catch expats out.

## What kind of bike should you actually buy?

Walk into any Dutch bike shop and you will be offered four broad categories.

**Stadsfiets (city bike, omafiets)**: The classic Dutch upright bike. Heavy, simple, basically theft-magnet shaped. New from €350; second-hand from €100. Perfect first bike if you live in a city centre and lock it outside.

**Hybrid / commuter**: Lighter, with gears, sometimes with a derailleur instead of internal hub. From €600. Good if you commute longer distances.

**E-bike**: The Dutch market is dominated by Gazelle, Stella, Sparta, and Trek. Genuine e-bikes start at €1,800 and go up to €4,500. The Cube and Riese-Müller premium models go higher.

**Speed pedelec**: 45 km/h electric bike. Counts legally as a moped. Requires helmet, number plate, and AM driving licence. Niche but increasingly popular for long commutes.

My advice for new arrivals: **start with a second-hand stadsfiets between €150 and €300**. Lock it well, learn the rhythm of Dutch cycling, and upgrade later. If you buy a €1,500 bike in your first month and it is stolen in your second, you will hate this country.

I know expats who arrived determined to buy "a proper road bike" and then watched it disappear from the bike rack at Utrecht Centraal in week three. The same expats now ride €200 omafietsen and laugh about it.

## How bike theft actually works here

Three things I did not understand before living here.

**Locks are rated and insurers care**. The Dutch ART certification system rates locks from ART 1 (lowest) to ART 5 (highest). Most insurers require minimum ART 2 for a regular bike and ART 3 for e-bikes. Buying a non-rated lock from a hardware store is fine for groceries but voids most insurance policies.

**Locking technique matters more than lock cost**. A €100 lock used poorly is worse than a €40 lock used well. The rules: lock the frame to a fixed object (not just the wheel), use two locks of different types, and never just rely on the ring lock around the rear wheel.

**Where you lock matters**. Bike theft is heavily concentrated around train stations, university campuses, and tourist hotspots. If you lock at Amsterdam Centraal, Leiden Centraal, or Utrecht Science Park, treat your bike as expendable.

The official guarded bike parking (bewaakte fietsenstalling) at major train stations costs €1.40 a day or about €100 a year for a season pass. For any bike worth more than €400 this is the cheapest insurance you can buy.

## My current locking setup

After two thefts and a lot of reading, this is what I now use:

1. **Built-in ring lock (AXA Defender)** — the lock that sits on the rear frame and immobilises the back wheel. Most Dutch bikes come with one. ART 2 rated.

2. **Heavy chain lock (AXA Newton or Abus Granit)** — through the front wheel, frame, and a fixed object. ART 3 or higher.

3. **At home or at work**: stored inside whenever possible. Outdoor storage is where insurers reject the most claims.

Total cost: about €120 for both locks, used for years. Compared to a single €700 bike loss, this is nothing.

For e-bikes I add a third element: a hidden GPS tracker (Trackting, Invoxia, or Apple AirTag). Trackers do not prevent theft but they have led to recoveries. I know two expats who got their e-bikes back this way.

## Bike insurance: when it actually pays

For a €200 omafiets, bike insurance rarely makes sense. The premium of €60 to €100 a year over five years equals or exceeds the bike value.

For anything above €400, it starts to make sense. For an e-bike above €1,500, it is essential.

**Standalone bike insurance** typically covers:
- Theft from any location (with proper locking)
- Damage from accidents and vandalism
- Sometimes pickup and replacement bike service
- Sometimes new-for-old replacement for the first 1 to 3 years

Premiums in 2026 are roughly:
- Regular bike (€500 to €1,000): €60 to €120 per year
- Premium bike (€1,000 to €2,000): €120 to €200 per year
- E-bike (€2,000 to €4,000): €180 to €380 per year

**Contents insurance with outdoor theft cover** (inboedelverzekering met buitenshuis dekking) is often a better deal because it covers your bike, laptop, phone, and other items together. I now run my bike under my contents policy.

## What to do when (not if) your bike is stolen

This will probably happen to you. When it does:

**Step 1**: Take a photo of where the bike was locked. The fragments of broken lock, the cut chain on the ground, all of it. Insurers want this evidence.

**Step 2**: File a police report online via [politie.nl](https://www.politie.nl/). The form is in Dutch but Google Translate handles it. You need the frame number, last known location, and approximate time of theft. The police case number is what insurers require.

**Step 3**: Contact your insurer within 48 hours. Most policies have a strict reporting deadline.

**Step 4**: Check the [VerlorenOfGevonden](https://verlorenofgevonden.nl/) website and your gemeente's lost property listings. Recovered bikes often end up at municipal storage. Roughly 5 percent of stolen bikes are eventually recovered through this channel.

**Step 5**: Check the police RegisterJeFiets database if you registered the frame number.

I followed all five steps after my second theft. The bike never came back, but the insurance paid out €450 within three weeks. Without the police report and the photos, the claim would have been refused.

## E-bike rules expats keep getting wrong

E-bike legislation has tightened in the Netherlands and confusion is widespread.

**Pedelec (regular e-bike, max 25 km/h with pedal assist)**: No licence needed, no helmet required, no insurance mandatory. Treated legally like a regular bike.

**Speed pedelec (45 km/h)**: Counts as a moped. You need an AM driving licence (or equivalent), a yellow number plate, third-party liability insurance (WA), and a helmet. You are not allowed on most regular cycle paths.

**Throttle-only e-bikes**: Mostly illegal in the Netherlands. The bike must require pedalling to engage the motor. Imported throttle e-bikes from outside the EU are often non-compliant.

**Modified e-bikes**: Tuning your e-bike to remove the speed limiter is illegal, voids insurance, and triggers fines up to €400. Police occasionally do roadside checks with rolling-road equipment.

If you are buying a new e-bike, **check the EN 15194 certification sticker** before you pay. Without it the bike may not be legal on Dutch cycle paths.

## Cycling etiquette and the rules nobody tells expats

A short list of rules that will save you embarrassment, fines, or accidents.

**Hand signals are mandatory**. Stick out your arm before turning. Failing to signal can trigger a €100 fine and is genuinely dangerous in Dutch traffic.

**Do not stop on the cycle path**. Pull off entirely. Stopping in the middle of a fietspad to check your phone causes pile-ups.

**Cyclists from the right have priority**. At unsigned junctions, the cyclist coming from the right goes first. This is the rule that catches the most expats.

**Lights are mandatory after dark**. Front white, rear red. €55 fine if you are caught without. Cycle paths are heavily policed for this in winter.

**No phone in your hand**. Since 2019 it is illegal to hold a phone while cycling. €140 fine. Use a handlebar mount or stop properly.

**Trams have absolute priority**. If you hear or see a tram, get out of the way. Tram tracks also cause more cyclist injuries than any other infrastructure feature in Amsterdam — cross them at an angle, never parallel.

## Buying second-hand: where to look and what to avoid

Second-hand bikes turn up on:

**Marktplaats**: The biggest market. Many listings are stolen bikes resold quickly. Always meet at a public place, never at a flat or alley. Ask for the original purchase receipt or photos of the bike with the seller from before.

**Local Facebook expat groups**: Often expats leaving the country offload bikes at fair prices. Lower theft risk because the seller is identifiable.

**Gemeente bike auctions**: Most cities auction abandoned and unclaimed bikes monthly. Prices start at €20. Quality varies enormously.

**Bike shops with second-hand stock**: A premium of 30 to 50 percent over private sales but you get a service warranty and a clean provenance. Worth it for new arrivals who do not know what to look for.

The single test I always do before buying any second-hand bike: **squeeze both brake levers fully and try to push the bike forward**. If the wheel does not lock instantly, the brakes are tired and need work — €60 to €120 of immediate cost.

## My honest recommendation for new expats

Start cheap. Lock well. Insure if the bike is worth more than €500.

For your first six months, ride a €200 stadsfiets you will not cry over. Get used to Dutch cycling rhythms, the rain, the traffic, the cobblestones. Lose this bike and learn the lesson cheaply.

After six months, if you still want to upgrade, buy a proper bike from a reputable shop, take out a contents policy with outdoor theft cover, and use two locks every time. From that point on you will probably never lose another bike.

The Dutch cycle infrastructure is the best in the world. Once you are settled, cycling will become the most enjoyable part of your daily life here. Just do not skip the locks.
