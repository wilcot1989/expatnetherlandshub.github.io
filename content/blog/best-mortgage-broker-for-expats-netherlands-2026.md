## Why Dutch Mortgages Are Harder for Expats

The Dutch mortgage market is well-regulated — the [Autoriteit Financiële Markten (AFM)](https://www.afm.nl/en/consumers/themas/hypotheek) sets strict guidelines on what lenders can offer and on what basis. Those rules are written for Dutch citizens with stable Dutch employment histories, Dutch payslips, and Dutch tax records. Expats often fall outside the assumptions built into those rules.

Here is where the friction typically shows up.

### Temporary Contracts

Most Dutch employment starts on a one-year tijdelijk contract. Lenders see temporary contracts as risk. Many will simply decline, or offer far less than your income would otherwise support. The workaround — and it works surprisingly well — is an intention verklaring: a letter from your employer stating they intend to extend or make your contract permanent. Not all employers will write one, but most HR departments at multinational companies know the document and will issue it without much fuss. A good expat mortgage broker will tell you exactly what this letter needs to say and who to ask.

### Foreign Income Documentation

If you received income in another country before moving to the Netherlands — even for just six or twelve months — some lenders will want to see documentation of that income. Pay stubs from Germany, a P60 from the UK, a W-2 from the United States: these need to be translated, often notarially. An expat broker knows which lenders require this and which are willing to work from Dutch payslips alone.

### Short Dutch Employment History

The standard Dutch mortgage application asks for two to three years of payslips and tax assessments. If you arrived twelve months ago, you have twelve months of payslips. Some lenders will not go further; others — the ones that expat brokers know — will accept a shorter history for applicants with strong salaries or senior roles.

### The 30% Ruling Complication

If you benefit from the [30% ruling](/blog/30-percent-ruling-extension-2024-changes-2026/), your net salary is significantly higher than your gross. But mortgage lenders calculate borrowing capacity on gross income, before the ruling. This means two things: your real spending power is higher than the mortgage offer implies, and you need a broker who understands how to present your income accurately — including which lenders will partially factor in the ruling and which will not.

### IBAN From a Foreign Bank

During the mortgage application process, the lender will want to see bank statements. Statements from a foreign bank — particularly from outside the EU — sometimes cause delays because loan processors do not recognise the format. This is more an administrative annoyance than a real obstacle, but it adds time. If you have Dutch bank statements alongside foreign ones, use both. If you are still setting up Dutch banking, the [best banking options for expats](/blog/best-banking-expats-netherlands-2026/) guide covers what is available.

## What to Look for in an Expat Mortgage Broker

Before you contact anyone, know what you are looking for:

**English communication throughout.** Not just the first call — the offer letter, the lender correspondence, the notary preparation. If the broker hands you off to Dutch-language processes after the initial intake, that is not actually English service.

**Experience with temporary contracts.** Ask directly: "How often do you arrange mortgages for clients on temporary contracts?" If the answer is vague, push for a number.

**Access to multiple lenders.** A broker tied to one bank or one lender group cannot give you independent advice. Ask how many lenders they work with. Independent brokers typically have access to fifteen or more.

**AFM registration.** All mortgage advisors operating in the Netherlands must be registered with the AFM. You can check at afm.nl. Do not work with anyone who is not registered, regardless of how convincing their website looks.

**Transparent fees.** Avoid percentage-of-mortgage fees. On a €400,000 mortgage, a 1% fee is €4,000 — dramatically more than the fixed-fee alternatives offer for the same service.

## The Dutch Mortgage Process Step by Step

Understanding the stages helps you manage the timeline — and the anxiety.

### Step 1: Orientation — What Can You Borrow?

The first step is a borrowing capacity calculation. Dutch lenders generally lend up to four to five times your gross annual salary, reduced by any existing debts (student loans, car finance, personal loans). According to [Nibud](https://www.nibud.nl/consumenten/uw-hypotheek/), the national budget advisory body, Dutch mortgage standards are designed to ensure monthly payments do not exceed a certain percentage of income — the exact percentage varies by interest rate environment.

With a gross income of €60,000, you might expect to borrow €240,000–€300,000. With two incomes, the second income is partly counted (typically 100% of the lower income plus 90% of the higher). Your broker will run an initial calculation in the first meeting and give you a realistic number before you start bidding.

This is also when you will discuss the 30% ruling, if it applies to you. [Read more about the 30% ruling and what changed in 2024–2026](/blog/30-percent-ruling-extension-2024-changes-2026/).

### Step 2: Document Preparation

Dutch mortgage applications require a specific set of documents. For expats, the list typically includes:

- Recent payslips (last three months minimum)
- Annual income statement (jaaropgave) if available
- Employment contract
- Intention verklaring if contract is temporary
- Passport or residence permit
- Recent bank statements (last three months)
- DigiD details
- BSN (Burger Service Nummer)

If you do not yet have a BSN, you need to get one before you can complete a Dutch mortgage. [The BSN application process](/blog/expat-bsn-application-guide-2026/) is separate from the mortgage and needs to happen first.

### Step 3: House Hunting and Making an Offer

While document preparation happens in parallel, you start house hunting. When you find a property and your offer is accepted, the mortgage process accelerates. A valuation (taxatierapport) must be commissioned — this typically costs €400–€700 and must be done by an independent, certified valuer.

The seller's notary drafts the preliminary purchase agreement (koopovereenkomst), usually within three business days of the offer being accepted. The standard cooling-off period for buyers is three days. The finance clause (ontbindende voorwaarde) typically gives you four to six weeks to arrange financing — if the mortgage falls through, you can exit without penalty.

For a feel of cost differences across cities, see [Amsterdam vs Rotterdam cost of living for expats](/blog/cost-of-living-amsterdam-vs-rotterdam-2026/) — Rotterdam is consistently cheaper and the property market moves slightly slower, which can help first-time buyers.

### Step 4: Mortgage Application and Offer

Your broker submits the application to the chosen lender. Processing takes two to four weeks depending on the lender's workload. The lender issues a mortgage offer (bindend aanbod) — a legally binding document. You have a minimum of fourteen days to consider before signing.

### Step 5: Notary and Transfer

Once the mortgage is accepted, both parties agree on a transfer date. The notary prepares the deed of transfer (leveringsakte) and the mortgage deed (hypotheekakte). You sign both at the notary's office. The notary registers the mortgage with the Kadaster (land registry) and you receive the keys.

Timeline from accepted offer to keys: typically eight to twelve weeks if everything goes smoothly. Delays from lender processing or document issues can extend this to four to five months, as mine did.

## The 30% Ruling and Your Mortgage

I want to spend a moment on this because it catches a lot of expats off guard.

The 30% ruling means that 30% of your salary is paid tax-free as a cost-of-living allowance. Your take-home is substantially higher than it would be on the same gross salary without the ruling. This is one of the key financial benefits of coming to the Netherlands on an expat package.

However, mortgage lenders calculate how much you can borrow based on gross income — the number before the 30% ruling is applied. From the lender's perspective, the ruling might end, you might leave the Netherlands, you might change jobs. They do not want to lend you money based on a tax arrangement that could stop.

Some lenders are willing to factor the 30% ruling into their calculations — not fully, but partly. This can increase your borrowing capacity by 10–15% in some cases. The brokers who know which lenders will do this — and Hanno specifically names this as an area they work in — are the ones worth your money.

If your ruling is coming to an end, that is also worth discussing with your broker. [The 2024 changes to the 30% ruling](/blog/30-percent-ruling-extension-2024-changes-2026/) reduced the term, which affects how lenders view future income stability for expats on the ruling.

## Life Insurance and Mortgage Protection

When your mortgage is approved, your lender will almost certainly ask whether you want to take out a life insurance policy (overlijdensrisicoverzekering) to cover the mortgage in the event of your death. In the Netherlands this is not legally mandatory, but many lenders require it as a condition of the mortgage.

For expats, life insurance tied to a Dutch mortgage can be complicated by your country of origin, your travel patterns, and whether the insurer considers your situation standard. ## Tax Implications of Owning a Home in the Netherlands

Mortgage interest is tax-deductible in the Netherlands under certain conditions. This is a significant financial benefit — the Belastingdienst allows you to deduct the interest you pay on your mortgage from your taxable income, reducing your annual tax bill. The rules around this (hypotheekrenteaftrek) are specific: the mortgage must be on your primary residence, it must be a repayment mortgage (annuity or linear — interest-only mortgages no longer qualify for new purchases), and the deduction tapers for higher earners.

When you file your [annual tax return as an expat](/blog/tax-return-netherlands-employee-expats-2026/), your mortgage interest deduction will be one of the most valuable items on the form. Keep records of every mortgage statement from day one.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can expats get a mortgage in the Netherlands?**
Yes. Expats with a valid residence permit, a registered Dutch address, and stable income can apply for a Dutch mortgage. The main complications are temporary contracts and foreign income documentation, but specialist expat mortgage brokers know how to handle these.

**Can I get a Dutch mortgage on a temporary contract?**
Many mortgage lenders will consider applicants on a temporary contract if an employer provides an intention verklaring — a written statement of intent to extend the contract. Expat-specialist brokers like Hanno have experience presenting these cases to the right lenders.

**Does the 30% ruling affect my mortgage application?**
The 30% ruling boosts your net take-home pay, but mortgage calculations are based on gross income before the ruling. Some lenders are willing to factor in the ruling; others are not. An expat mortgage specialist will know which lenders are most flexible on this point.

**What is the minimum income to qualify for a Dutch mortgage?**
There is no absolute minimum, but as a rough guide most lenders will lend up to four to five times your gross annual salary. With a gross income of €50,000, you might borrow up to €225,000–€250,000. The actual amount depends on your outstanding debts, partner income if applicable, and the property value.

**How long do I need to live in the Netherlands before I can apply for a mortgage?**
There is no fixed requirement, but in practice most lenders want to see at least one year of Dutch employment history. Some expat-specialist lenders are more flexible, especially if you have a long-term contract or a senior role.
