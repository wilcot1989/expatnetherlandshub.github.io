## What Tax Filings a Freelance Expat Owes

In the Netherlands, freelance work usually means ZZP (zelfstandige zonder personeel — self-employed without staff), operating as an eenmanszaak (sole proprietorship) or sometimes via a BV (private limited company).

For a typical eenmanszaak ZZP'er, the annual filing rhythm is:

**Quarterly:** BTW (VAT) returns, due roughly one month after each quarter ends:

- Q1: due 30 April
- Q2: due 31 July
- Q3: due 31 October
- Q4: due 31 January

**Annual:** Income tax return (inkomstenbelasting), due 1 May of the following year.

**Possibly monthly:** Income tax provisional payments (voorlopige aanslag) — the Belastingdienst estimates your annual liability and you pay it monthly throughout the year.

**One-time:** KvK registration when you start, and updates if your business structure changes.

If you have a BV instead of an eenmanszaak, add corporate tax (vennootschapsbelasting) returns and director payroll filings to the list. BVs are administratively heavier — most solo freelancers under ~€80,000/year stick with eenmanszaak.

## Income Tax for Freelancers: How It Works

Freelance income is taxed in Box 1 of Dutch income tax — the same box as employment income, with progressive rates.

### 2026 Box 1 Rates (provisional)

| Income band | Rate |
|------------|------|
| Up to ~€38,000 | ~36.5% |
| ~€38,000 to ~€76,000 | ~37.5% |
| Above ~€76,000 | ~49.5% |

Exact thresholds adjust each year. The Belastingdienst publishes confirmed rates each January.

### Freelance-Specific Deductions That Reduce Taxable Income

These are the big ones:

**Zelfstandigenaftrek (self-employed deduction).** €3,750 in 2026, deducted from your taxable profit before tax. Available if you spend at least 1,225 hours per year on your business (the urencriterium / hour criterion). Track your hours from day one — Belastingdienst can audit this.

**Startersaftrek (starter's deduction).** Additional €2,123 in 3 of your first 5 freelance years, on top of zelfstandigenaftrek.

**MKB-winstvrijstelling (SME profit exemption).** Reduces taxable profit by 12% (after zelfstandigenaftrek and startersaftrek). Applies to all eenmanszaak/freelancers automatically.

**FOR (oudedagsreserve, retirement reserve).** You can set aside up to ~9.4% of profit (capped) tax-deferred toward retirement. This was being phased out — check current rules each year as it has changed.

For a freelancer earning €60,000 gross profit, these deductions can reduce taxable income by €10,000–€13,000 — saving €4,000–€6,000 in actual tax.

### Real Example: ZZP'er with €60,000 profit in 2026

| Step | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Profit | €60,000 |
| Less zelfstandigenaftrek | -€3,750 |
| Less startersaftrek (if applicable) | -€2,123 |
| Subtotal | €54,127 |
| Less MKB-winstvrijstelling (12%) | -€6,495 |
| Taxable income (Box 1) | €47,632 |
| Approx income tax | ~€17,500 |
| Plus health insurance contribution | ~€3,200 |
| **Net cash kept** | **~€39,300** |

The same €60,000 paid as employment salary would net roughly €40,500 — but as a freelancer you're also responsible for your own pension, healthcare, and disability insurance, which a typical Dutch employee gets bundled.

## BTW (VAT) for Freelancers

Most Dutch freelance services are subject to 21% BTW. Some specific categories — books, food in supermarkets, hairdressing, public transport, repairs — fall under 9%. A handful of categories (medical care, education, financial services) are BTW-exempt.

**You charge BTW** on invoices to Dutch and most international clients (with reverse-charge for EU B2B clients with valid VAT IDs).

**You pay BTW** on most business expenses — laptops, software subscriptions, professional services, business meals.

**Your quarterly BTW return** reports BTW collected minus BTW paid. Net amount is paid to the Belastingdienst (or refunded if you paid more than you collected, common for first-year freelancers buying equipment).

### KOR (Small Business Scheme)

If your annual turnover is below €20,000, you can opt into KOR and skip BTW entirely — no charging it, no claiming back input BTW, no quarterly returns. You apply at the start of the year and stay in for at least 3 years.

KOR makes sense if:

- You're a side-hustle freelancer with one or two big clients
- Your business expenses are low (no equipment-heavy operations)
- You serve consumers (not businesses) and the BTW would be a real cost increase

KOR rarely makes sense if you're investing in equipment or growing — the BTW you can't reclaim on inputs becomes a real cost.

## The 30% Ruling Question

Many expats ask whether they can use their 30% ruling as a freelancer. The short answer: not directly through ZZP/eenmanszaak.

The 30% ruling is an employee benefit — your Dutch employer treats up to 30% of your gross salary as a tax-free reimbursement of extraterritorial costs. It requires an employment relationship.

**If you go freelance:**

- Existing 30% ruling on your previous Dutch employment ends the day that employment ends.
- New ZZP/eenmanszaak income does not qualify.
- You can set up a Dutch BV where you are the director-employee on a salary. The BV applies for the 30% ruling on your director salary. This adds significant administrative cost (roughly €1,500–€3,500/year for accounting and payroll services) but can be worth it if your income is high enough to make the 30% saving exceed the administrative overhead — typically €80,000+ annual personal income from the BV.

For most freelancers under €70,000/year, eenmanszaak is the better structure even without the 30% ruling.

## Deductible Business Expenses

Track every reasonable business expense from day one. Common deductible categories for freelancers:

**Equipment.** Laptops, monitors, phones (business portion), software subscriptions, office furniture. Items above ~€450 are typically depreciated over 3–5 years rather than expensed.

**Home office.** Specific rules. If you have a dedicated, permanent workspace at home that is "self-contained" (separate room, own entrance, own toilet), you can deduct part of housing costs proportionally. Most home offices don't qualify under the strict rules — but you can still deduct utilities, internet, and supplies pro-rated to business use.

**Professional services.** Accountant, tax advisor, lawyer, business consultants — all fully deductible.

**Business travel.** €0.23/km in 2026 for car travel; full cost of train/plane if business purpose; hotels and meals at 80% deductibility for client meetings.

**Professional development.** Courses, books, conferences, professional memberships, subscriptions to industry publications.

**Insurance.** Business liability (bedrijfsaansprakelijkheid), professional indemnity, disability insurance (AOV) if business-purpose, equipment insurance.

**Banking and finance fees.** Business account fees, payment processor fees (Stripe, Mollie), [Wise](https://go.expatnetherlandshub.com/wise?ref=/blog/tax-filing-freelance-expat-netherlands-2026/) fees on business currency conversions.

**KvK fees.** Annual KvK fee is fully deductible.

**Office supplies and consumables.** Notebooks, printer ink, postage, etc.

**Business meals.** Client meetings at restaurants — 80% deductible. Solo lunches are not.

**Marketing.** Website hosting, domain, ads, design.

What's NOT typically deductible: clothes (even if "for client meetings"), commuting from home to a regular workplace (unless you have a different setup), personal phone/internet portion, gym memberships.

## Documentation and Record-Keeping

Dutch tax rules require you to keep business records for **7 years**. Belastingdienst can audit anytime in that window.

What to keep:

- All invoices issued (with sequential numbering, mandatory)
- All invoices and receipts for expenses
- Bank statements (business account or business-portion of personal account)
- Mileage logs if claiming car expenses
- Hour-tracking logs (for the 1,225-hour urencriterium)
- BTW return submissions and confirmations
- Annual income statements and tax filings

## DIY Filing on the Belastingdienst Portal

The Belastingdienst portal (Mijn Belastingdienst, requires DigiD) supports the standard freelance income tax form (P-aangifte for residents).

For DIY, you'll need:

- Your DigiD
- All your income statements (own bookkeeping)
- All deductible expense documentation
- Your asset and liability snapshot as of 1 January (for Box 3)
- Bank balances on 1 January for all accounts (Dutch + foreign)

The portal is in Dutch by default. Browser auto-translate works reasonably well; the structure is logical. Filing time for a simple eenmanszaak return is 2–4 hours if your bookkeeping is in order.

DIY makes sense for:

- Single-income ZZP'ers under €40,000/year
- No foreign client revenue
- No US citizenship or other dual filing obligations
- Standard deductions only
- Comfort with reading Dutch tax-form language

## When to Hire a Tax Advisor

Hire a registered Dutch tax advisor (belastingconsulent or fiscaal adviseur) if any of these apply:

- Revenue above ~€60,000/year (deductions get optimized in ways DIY rarely captures)
- US citizenship (dual jurisdiction filing complexity)
- Foreign clients in multiple countries (treaty rules, withholding tax credits)
- BV structure or considering one
- Complex deductions (home office, multiple income types, prior losses to carry forward)
- 30% ruling situations
- Past audit issues or filings you suspect were wrong

Typical fees:

- Simple ZZP return: €400–€800/year
- Mid-complexity (multiple revenue streams, deductions): €800–€1,500
- Complex (BV, US citizen, multi-country): €1,500–€4,000

## Provisional Tax Assessments (Voorlopige Aanslag)

The Belastingdienst will set up monthly tax payments for freelancers based on your previous year's income. This avoids one big bill at year-end.

If your income changes substantially (up or down) during the year, log into Mijn Belastingdienst and request a recalculation. Otherwise:

- If you under-paid, you'll owe the balance plus a small interest charge at year-end
- If you over-paid, you'll get a refund — but you've given the government a 0% loan for the year

## Common Mistakes Freelance Expats Make

**Not tracking hours.** Without 1,225+ hours documented, you lose zelfstandigenaftrek and startersaftrek — easily €4,000–€6,000 in extra tax.

**Forgetting BTW on invoices.** Domestic Dutch B2B invoices need BTW (or correct reverse-charge notation for EU). Issuing without and discovering later means you owe BTW out of pocket.

**Mixing personal and business banking.** Use a separate account from day one. The cleanup cost otherwise is enormous.

**Missing the 1 May deadline.** Penalty plus interest. Always extend if you need to — it's free until 1 September via the portal.

**Failing to declare foreign income.** Dutch residents are taxed on worldwide income. Foreign clients, foreign rental property, foreign dividends — all reportable.

**Box 3 misreporting on investments.** Your DEGIRO/Trade Republic balance on 1 January goes in Box 3. See [DEGIRO vs Trade Republic](/blog/degiro-vs-trade-republic-expats-2026/) for how their tax statements help with this.

**Not separating receipts and notes for what each expense was.** The Belastingdienst can ask "what was this €127 for?" three years later. Annotated receipts save you.

## US Citizens: The Dual Filing Reality

US citizens face the most complex situation:

- Dutch tax filing on worldwide income (full freelance package above)
- US Form 1040 with Schedule C reporting freelance income
- FBAR (FinCEN 114) reporting foreign account balances if combined balances exceed $10,000 at any point in the year
- FATCA reporting (Form 8938) if balances exceed higher thresholds
- Self-employment tax considerations (US Social Security)
- Foreign Earned Income Exclusion or Foreign Tax Credit to prevent double taxation

A specialist who knows both jurisdictions is essentially mandatory. Budget €1,500–€4,000/year for combined filing.

## VAR-Style Status: Are You Really a Freelancer?

The Belastingdienst defines who counts as a freelancer (zelfstandige) versus a hidden employee. The DBA Act (Wet Deregulering Beoordeling Arbeidsrelaties) tries to ensure that ZZP'ers aren't actually employees in disguise.

Risk factors that suggest you're hidden employment (schijnzelfstandigheid):

- Single client representing 70%+ of your annual revenue
- Working set hours under client direction
- Using client's equipment, tools, or premises
- Receiving training from the client
- Long-term contract with no specific deliverables
- No independent business presentation to other markets

Risk factors that suggest genuine freelance:

- Multiple clients
- You set your own hours and methods
- Use your own equipment
- Bill on deliverables, not time-attendance
- Run an independent business presence (website, marketing, KvK active)
- Bear genuine financial risk (no guaranteed income)

If most of your income is from one client and the relationship looks employment-like, the Belastingdienst can reclassify it. Both you and the client face back-tax and social-contribution exposure.

Mitigating practices:

- Diversify client base (3+ clients, none above 60%)
- Issue clearly itemized invoices with deliverables
- Maintain a public business presence
- Use your own equipment
- Bear realistic project risk

This particularly matters for software developers, consultants, and creatives who often work long-term with one main client.

## Pension Planning as a Freelance Expat

Freelancers don't get an employer pension contribution (pillar 2). You're responsible for your own retirement savings.

Three options:

**Pillar 3 (lijfrente / annuity products).** Tax-deductible up to specific annual limits (jaarruimte / reserveringsruimte) based on your income and existing pension situation. Funds are locked until retirement. Common with banks and insurers — Brand New Day, Bright Pension, others offer modern low-cost lijfrente products.

**Self-managed retirement investing.** Build a regular ETF portfolio in Box 3 with [DEGIRO or Trade Republic](/blog/degiro-vs-trade-republic-expats-2026/). No tax deduction on contribution, but full liquidity and control.

**FOR (oudedagsreserve).** A book entry, not real money. Sets aside profit tax-deferred but stays in business. Phased down significantly in recent years.

For most freelancers, a combination of pillar 3 (for the deduction) and Box 3 ETF investing (for flexibility) is the practical answer. See my [Dutch pension transfer guide](/blog/dutch-pension-transfer-expats-2026/) for the broader context.

## Common BTW Mistakes That Cost Real Money

**Forgetting to issue BTW invoices to EU B2B clients with the reverse-charge note.** If you don't include "BTW verlegd / VAT reverse charged" on the invoice, the assumption is you're charging Dutch BTW — meaning you owe 21% even though you didn't collect it.

**Mixing in personal expenses.** If you reclaim BTW on personal items, an audit results in repayment plus penalties. Be ruthless about classification.

**Not opting into KOR when eligible.** If your turnover is consistently under €20K and you don't have heavy input BTW to reclaim, KOR simplifies life dramatically.

**Filing BTW returns late.** €68 minimum penalty per missed return, sometimes more. Set quarterly calendar reminders.

**Wrong BTW rate.** Educational services, medical-adjacent work, books, food — all may qualify for 0%, 9%, or be exempt. Misclassifying triggers back-payments. When in doubt, ask the Belastingdienst's BTW helpdesk or your tax advisor.

## Insurance: AOV (Disability) for Freelancers

Employees automatically have disability insurance via the Dutch social security system (WIA). Freelancers do not. If you become long-term disabled, you have no income unless you've insured yourself.

AOV (arbeidsongeschiktheidsverzekering) policies cost roughly 7–13% of your insured income. So insuring €40K of annual income costs €2,800–€5,200/year — significant. Premiums are tax-deductible.

Alternatives: broodfonds (mutual sickness fund collectives), savings buffers, partner income reliance. None replace AOV for true long-term protection.

For most freelancers earning €40K+, AOV is worth the cost. For freelancers earning under €30K with strong family support or savings, it's a calculated skip.

## When to Switch from Eenmanszaak to BV

Common conversion thresholds:

- **€80K–€100K profit.** The tax efficiency of BV-versus-eenmanszaak starts crossing over here, especially with full 30% ruling on a director salary.
- **Liability concerns.** BV provides legal liability separation that eenmanszaak doesn't.
- **Multi-shareholder situations.** BV structure is needed for partners, investors, employees with equity.

Conversion is a one-way move in practice — you can revert eenmanszaak to BV but not easily back. Plan it carefully with a [tax advisor](/blog/best-tax-advisors-expats-netherlands-2026/) who runs the projections both ways.
