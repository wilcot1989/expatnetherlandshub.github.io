## Why This Comparison Matters More in the Netherlands

The Netherlands is a great country for expats in many ways, but Dutch banks are genuinely expensive for international transfers. ING charges between €6 and €25 for a standard international wire, depending on the destination and currency. ABN AMRO is similarly priced. Rabobank, which I covered in more detail in [my ING vs Rabobank comparison](/blog/ing-vs-rabobank-expats-2026/), sits in the same range.

On top of the flat fee, Dutch banks also use their own exchange rate rather than the mid-market rate — typically 1–3% worse than what you'd see on Google. So sending €1,000 to the UK through ING might cost you a €10 flat fee plus a 1.5% spread, meaning you lose around €25 on a single transfer. Do that 12 times a year and you've paid €300 in hidden costs.

Wise and Revolut both avoid this. They use the mid-market rate — the real one, the one you see on Google or XE.com — and charge a transparent fee on top. That's the fundamental reason expats in the Netherlands flock to them.

Most of the expats I've spoken to through this site are sending money in one of three directions: to the UK (British expats are the largest group), to the US, or to India. Those are the three corridors I focused on in my testing.

## Wise: How It Actually Works

Wise (formerly TransferWise) is not a bank. It's an e-money institution, authorized by the UK Financial Conduct Authority and also registered with the [Dutch National Bank (DNB)](https://www.dnb.nl/en/payments/) for its EU operations. This is an important distinction — your money with Wise is held in ring-fenced accounts (separated from Wise's own operational funds), not covered by a standard bank deposit guarantee scheme.

The core product is simple: you hold money in multiple currencies, you can move between them at the mid-market rate, and you can send internationally. The fee structure is corridor-specific and shown upfront before you confirm any transfer. For the most common routes from the Netherlands:

- **EUR → GBP:** approximately 0.41–0.56% of the transfer amount
- **EUR → USD:** approximately 0.56–0.67%
- **EUR → INR:** approximately 0.64–0.89%

These are variable — Wise publishes them and they change slightly over time — but they've been broadly stable through 2025 and into 2026.

When you open a Wise account as a Netherlands resident, you get a multi-currency account with local account details in multiple countries. For euros, you get a **Belgian IBAN** (starting with BE). This is SEPA-compliant, meaning any standard euro transfer within the EU will work. But it is not a Dutch NL IBAN, and that creates specific problems in the Netherlands that I'll cover in detail below.

The Wise debit card is a Mastercard accepted globally. You can spend directly from any currency you hold, and Wise converts at mid-market rate if you spend in a currency you don't hold. ATM withdrawals are free up to €200 per month (maximum 2 free withdrawals), then 1.75% plus €0.50 per withdrawal above that limit.

## Revolut: How It Actually Works

Revolut started as a prepaid travel card and has grown into a full banking app. Since 2021, Revolut has held a full banking license from the Bank of Lithuania, which means deposits are protected by the Lithuanian Deposit Guarantee Scheme up to €100,000. For Netherlands residents, this is relevant because you're covered — but through a Lithuanian regulator, not a Dutch one.

The plan structure matters a lot for cost:

- **Standard (free):** Fee-free currency exchange up to €1,000/month at mid-market rate, then 0.5% above that. ATM withdrawals free up to €200/month, then 2%.
- **Plus (€3.99/month):** Higher limits, priority customer support.
- **Premium (€9.99/month):** Fee-free exchange up to €10,000/month, ATM withdrawals free up to €400/month, travel insurance, lounge access with discounts.
- **Metal (€15.99/month):** Higher ATM limits, cashback, concierge.

Since 2024, Revolut has offered a **Dutch NL IBAN opt-in** for Netherlands residents. You can switch in the app settings so your euro account displays an NL IBAN. This is genuinely useful — more on that below. The banking license and NL IBAN opt-in together make Revolut a more credible primary account option than it was two years ago.

Revolut also has stocks trading, crypto, savings vaults, and travel insurance built in. If you want one app that does many financial things, Revolut is designed for that. Wise has none of those features — it's purely a money transfer and multi-currency account tool.

**Weekend note for Revolut:** On weekdays, the numbers above hold. On weekends, Revolut adds 0.5% (EUR→GBP, EUR→USD) to 1% (more exotic pairs like EUR→INR) on top of the exchange rate. So that "free" €1,000 EUR→GBP transfer on a Saturday actually costs you around €5 — roughly the same as Wise. On weekends, Wise and Revolut Standard effectively cost the same for common corridors.

For heavy senders above €1,000/month, Revolut Premium at €9.99/month gives you €10,000 fee-free per month. If you're sending €2,000/month to the UK every month, the math looks like this:

- **Wise:** ~€9.20/month in fees (2 × €4.60)
- **Revolut Standard:** €5 in fees on the amount above €1,000
- **Revolut Premium:** €9.99/month flat, zero transfer fees up to €10k

At €2,000/month in transfers, Revolut Premium is about the same cost as Wise but gives you loads of extras. Above €2,200/month in transfers, Revolut Premium starts to win on pure transfer cost.

## The Dutch IBAN Situation — A Real Problem

This is the issue that surprises most expats who come from countries where fintech banks are treated more normally.

**Wise gives you a BE IBAN.** Your euro account looks like `BE56 9052 2314 XXXX`. This is a valid Belgian IBAN. For SEPA transfers within the EU, this works fine — you can receive money from Dutch friends, pay Dutch invoices, and make Dutch bank transfers. But in the Netherlands, several important systems check for a specifically Dutch (NL) IBAN:

- Some **Dutch employers** reject non-NL IBANs for salary payments. I've had this reported by readers at companies using older HR systems — especially Dutch multinationals with rigid payroll setup.
- The **Belastingdienst** (Dutch tax authority) can and does flag BE IBANs for certain refunds and toeslagen payments. The money usually arrives eventually, but it can trigger manual checks and delays.
- **Dutch direct debit mandates** (incasso) sometimes reject non-NL IBANs. If you're setting up automatic payments for Dutch utilities or health insurance, this can cause friction.

**Revolut now offers an NL IBAN opt-in.** Since 2024, Netherlands residents can go into Revolut settings and switch their euro account to display a Dutch NL IBAN. This helps with the Dutch employer and direct debit issues above. If you're primarily using Revolut as your main account in the Netherlands, this is a meaningful advantage over Wise.

However — and this matters — Revolut's underlying banking entity remains the Bank of Lithuania. The NL IBAN is more of a display/routing feature than a fundamental change in who holds your money or which regulatory body oversees your deposits. Some Dutch HR systems may still flag it.

If you genuinely need a Dutch NL IBAN from a Dutch-regulated bank, the cleanest solution is **Bunq** (a Dutch bank, DNB-regulated, NL IBAN from day one) or a traditional Dutch bank. ## Should You Receive Your Salary Into Wise or Revolut?

Short answer: probably not as your primary salary account, at least not yet.

The longer answer is that this depends heavily on your employer and industry. I asked a dozen expats in the Netherlands about their experience, and the breakdown was roughly:

- 4 out of 12 had salary received directly into Revolut (using NL IBAN) with no issues
- 3 had attempted it and been told by HR that they needed a "proper Dutch bank account"
- 5 hadn't tried and were using a Dutch bank by default

The problem is that if payroll rejects your Wise BE IBAN or your Revolut NL IBAN on the first of the month, you're potentially waiting days for HR to reprocess. That's not a risk worth taking for the sake of convenience.

My personal setup — and what I recommend to most expats — is to receive salary into a Dutch bank account first. ING, ABN AMRO, and Bunq all give you a clean NL IBAN from a Dutch-regulated bank with no questions. Then, on payday or shortly after, I transfer what I need to send internationally over to Wise, and keep my Revolut loaded separately for travel expenses.

This layered approach means you're never vulnerable to a payroll rejection, while still benefiting from Wise's low transfer fees and Revolut's travel features. ## ATM Withdrawals: What You Actually Get

Both services give you a free monthly ATM allowance, but the limits matter when you're traveling.

**Wise:**
- Free withdrawals: up to **€200/month**, maximum 2 withdrawals
- Above the limit: **1.75% + €0.50** per withdrawal
- No foreign transaction fees when spending from the correct currency balance

**Revolut Standard (free plan):**
- Free withdrawals: up to **€200/month**
- Above the limit: **2%** per withdrawal
- One ATM request can cover the full limit in one go

**Revolut Premium (€9.99/month):**
- Free withdrawals: up to **€400/month**
- Above the limit: **2%** per withdrawal
- Better for trips where you rely heavily on cash

For most day-to-day life in the Netherlands, you barely need cash — the Netherlands is one of the most cashless societies in Europe, and even market stalls take card now. But when traveling to countries where cash matters (Morocco, Georgia, many parts of Southeast Asia), the ATM limits become significant. Revolut Premium's €400 free allowance can easily cover a two-week trip without hitting extra fees.

## The Weekend FX Markup Problem

This caught me out in February. I transferred €800 to my mother on a Saturday morning, using Revolut because I had it open on my phone. The transfer went through fine, but when I checked the rate afterward, I noticed I'd paid approximately €6 more than I would have on a Friday.

Revolut charges a 0.5–1% markup on currency conversions that happen outside of market hours — this effectively means Saturday and Sunday, plus some public holidays. The exact percentage varies by currency pair:

- **EUR → GBP:** 0.5% weekend surcharge
- **EUR → USD:** 0.5% weekend surcharge
- **EUR → INR and other emerging markets:** up to 1%

On an €800 transfer at 0.5% surcharge, that's €4 extra. On €2,000 it's €10. If you're sending every month and you tend to do it on weekends (which many people do — it's when you have time to think about admin), this adds up.

**Wise charges no weekend surcharge.** The fee is the same on a Tuesday as on a Sunday. This is one of the reasons I now route all my regular family transfers through Wise — I send on whatever day I remember to, and I know exactly what it will cost before I confirm.

## Travel: Where Revolut Genuinely Wins

When I traveled to Colombia and Thailand last year, Revolut was the better companion app. The reasons are practical:

The Revolut app shows your spending in real time across multiple currencies, which is easier to follow when you're juggling different cash flows. The travel insurance that comes with Premium is actual travel insurance (medical, trip cancellation, delayed luggage), not just a token offering. The lounge access through LoungeKey with discounts is worth it if you're a frequent flyer.

Wise is perfectly functional for travel spending — the card works everywhere, the rate is mid-market, and there's no foreign transaction fee. But it doesn't offer the bundled travel features that Revolut Premium does. If you're spending €9.99/month on Revolut Premium and you travel three or four times a year, the travel insurance alone covers the subscription cost.

For expats in the Netherlands who make the annual trips back to their home country plus take European city breaks, Revolut Premium makes sense as a travel tool. The question is whether you want to also route your regular transfers through it (where Wise is usually cheaper on weekends) or keep the two services separate.

## Security and Regulation: What to Know

Expats sometimes ask me whether these are "real" banks or whether their money is safe. The honest answer is: both are legitimate and regulated, but with different structures.

**Wise** is an authorized e-money institution, regulated by the UK FCA and registered with the Dutch National Bank (DNB). Your money in Wise is held in ring-fenced client accounts — meaning it's separated from Wise's operating funds and protected if Wise goes insolvent. This is not the same as a deposit guarantee scheme, but it's a meaningful form of protection. The [DNB's register of payment institutions](https://www.dnb.nl/en/payments/) lists Wise as an authorized institution for EU operations.

**Revolut** holds a full banking license from the Bank of Lithuania, which means deposits up to €100,000 are covered by the Lithuanian Deposit Guarantee Scheme. This is equivalent in limit to the Dutch DGS (also €100,000) but administered by a different national authority.

For amounts I'm actively using for transfers — typically €500–2,000 at a time — I'm comfortable with either service. For large sums I'm holding long-term (an emergency fund, a house deposit), I keep that in a Dutch bank with clear DNB oversight. I wouldn't hold €50,000 in Wise or Revolut as a static savings balance. For the amounts that flow through during normal expat life, both are fine.

## What I Actually Use — And Why

After six months of tracking this properly, here's my personal setup:

**For monthly transfers to the UK:** Wise, every time. I transfer on whatever day suits me without worrying about a weekend markup. The fee shows clearly before I confirm (usually €4–6 on a €600 transfer), and the money arrives in 0–1 business days to a UK bank account. Predictable, cheap, reliable.

**For travel:** Revolut Premium. The travel insurance has paid off twice — once for a delayed flight and once for medical expenses in Thailand. The app is more comprehensive for managing travel spending across multiple currencies. The lounge access isn't something I use constantly but it's nice on longer journeys.

**For Dutch day-to-day life:** ING. Dutch NL IBAN, salary goes in, Dutch direct debits go out. It's boring but it's the right tool for the job. I don't use ING for international transfers — too expensive.

This three-tool setup might sound like overkill, but in practice it takes maybe 10 minutes a month to manage. The apps are all on my phone, the transfers are mostly automatic, and I save roughly €200–300 per year compared to routing everything through ING.

For expats just starting out, I'd say: open Wise first (free, immediate, useful for your first international transfer), get your Dutch salary account sorted (ING or Bunq — see [my first year in the Netherlands guide](/blog/first-year-netherlands-expat-pillar-2026/) for the full setup checklist), and only add Revolut if you travel more than twice a year or want the all-in-one features.
