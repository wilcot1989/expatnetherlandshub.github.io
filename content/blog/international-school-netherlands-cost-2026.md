> **Quick answer (TL;DR):** International schools in the Netherlands cost anywhere from **€6,500/year** (subsidised DIPS/DISS routes like AICS or ISH) up to **€30,000/year** at fully private schools like the American School of The Hague or British School Amsterdam. Add €500-€2,000 registration, capital levies of €1,000-€5,000, bus €1,500-€3,000, lunch €700-€1,200 and after-school care €2,000-€4,000. Under the 30% ruling, employer reimbursement of these costs is tax-free. Realistic all-in budget per child: **€10k-€35k a year**.

Last spring I sat at the kitchen table with an Australian family who had just landed in Amsterdam Zuid. Their daughter Mia, eight years old, had to start school in three weeks. They'd been quoted €22,000 at the British School of Amsterdam and €7,000 at the Amsterdam International Community School (AICS) — and they were convinced one of those numbers had to be wrong.

It wasn't. Both prices were real. They reflect two completely different funding models that almost nobody explains properly before you move. So this article is the brain-dump I wish I'd had ready for them: every fee, every levy, every hidden bus-and-lunch line item, and exactly when the 30% ruling makes the bigger schools affordable.

I help expat families compare schools roughly once a week. The numbers below are the ones I check against actual invoices in 2026 — not the brochure version.

## Why international school costs in the Netherlands are weirdly bimodal

The Dutch government runs a system most newcomers have never heard of: **DIPS** (Dutch International Primary Schools) and **DISS** (Dutch International Secondary Schools). These are international departments attached to publicly funded Dutch schools. They follow international curricula (mostly IPC, IB-PYP, IB-MYP, IGCSE, IBDP) but receive partial state funding.

Result: tuition is roughly **€5,000 - €8,500/year** in primary and **€7,500 - €12,500/year** in secondary. That's the AICS, ISH, ISA, ISE, ISH-Rijnlands tier.

Fully private international schools — British School Amsterdam, American School of The Hague, Lycée Français Vincent van Gogh, Japanese School Amsterdam, International School Eerde — receive no state funding. Their tuition is in the **€18,000 - €30,000/year** range, occasionally higher with boarding.

There is almost no middle ground. You pay €7k or you pay €22k. The middle ground only appears when you add up all the extras, which I'll do below.

Mia's parents had been quoted both numbers because they were genuinely choosing between the two systems. The €22k BSA price was for a true private British school. The €7k AICS price was for a DIPS school sitting inside a subsidised foundation. Same city, same year group — completely different model.

## The big six: what each school actually charges in 2026

I'm going to walk through the six schools families ask me about most often, with the actual 2025-2026 fees published on their websites and the 2026-2027 rates where available. Inflation has pushed nearly every school up 4-7% this year.

### 1. Amsterdam International Community School (AICS) — DIPS/DISS

- **Primary tuition:** ~€6,750/year
- **Secondary tuition (IB MYP/DP):** ~€10,500/year
- **Registration fee:** €350 (one-off, non-refundable)
- **Capital levy:** none (publicly funded foundation)
- **Lunch & bus:** not provided centrally — parents arrange
- **After-school care:** ~€280/month via Partou/external BSO partners

AICS has three campuses (South, Southeast, and a separate location in Amstelveen). Waiting lists are long. Priority goes to families with proof of international employment contracts of limited duration. Mia's family qualified because both parents were on a 4-year intra-company transfer.

### 2. British School of Amsterdam (BSA) — fully private

- **Reception - Year 6:** ~€20,500/year
- **Year 7 - Year 11:** ~€22,500/year
- **Year 12 - 13 (A-levels):** ~€24,800/year
- **Application fee:** €350
- **Acceptance/enrolment fee:** €2,500 (one-off, non-refundable)
- **Capital levy:** €1,250/year for the first 5 years
- **Lunch:** €4.50/day (optional)
- **Bus:** €2,400-€3,200/year depending on route
- **After-school clubs:** €200-€400/term

BSA is housed in a beautiful historic building in Oud-Zuid. Class sizes are smaller (max 22). It's a true UK independent school in look and feel — uniforms, houses, prefects, the lot.

### 3. International School of The Hague (ISH) — DIPS/DISS

- **Primary (Years 1-6):** ~€6,950/year
- **Secondary (Years 7-13):** ~€11,200/year
- **Registration fee:** €250
- **Capital contribution:** none
- **Lunch:** ~€5/day
- **Bus:** €1,800-€2,400/year depending on zone
- **After-school activities:** €150-€350/term

ISH sits in a purpose-built campus in Kijkduin. It's the largest international school in the country (over 2,000 pupils). The DIPS primary is genuinely affordable; the DISS secondary delivers the full IB diploma at less than half the price of the private competitors.

For families weighing The Hague against other cities, my [The Hague vs Den Bosch comparison](/blog/the-hague-vs-den-bosch-expats-2026/) covers the lifestyle trade-offs.

### 4. American School of The Hague (ASH) — fully private

- **Pre-K (half day):** ~€16,000/year
- **Kindergarten - Grade 5:** ~€25,200/year
- **Grade 6 - 8:** ~€27,800/year
- **Grade 9 - 12 (IBDP):** ~€29,900/year
- **Application fee:** €300
- **Capital fee:** €5,500 (one-off)
- **Annual building levy:** €1,800
- **Lunch:** €6.50/day
- **Bus:** €2,800-€3,400/year
- **After-school activities (ASA):** typically included in tuition; some external sports €300-€600/season

ASH is the most expensive school on this list. It's also the most "complete" — purpose-built campus in Wassenaar with two gyms, a theatre, athletics track, pool access nearby. Almost every diplomat and Shell/NATO family ends up here. The capital fee is non-refundable and pretty famous in expat circles.

### 5. Lycée Français Vincent van Gogh (The Hague & Amsterdam)

- **Maternelle (PS-GS):** ~€7,200/year
- **Élémentaire (CP-CM2):** ~€8,400/year
- **Collège (6e-3e):** ~€10,800/year
- **Lycée (2nde-Terminale):** ~€12,400/year
- **Registration fee:** €450
- **Capital fee:** none
- **CSE/contribution:** ~€300/year
- **Bus:** organised privately, ~€2,000-€2,800/year
- **Cantine:** ~€5.20/meal

The Lycée Français is partially subsidised by the AEFE (Agence pour l'Enseignement Français à l'Étranger), which is why the prices look much closer to the DIPS schools than to BSA or ASH. Excellent option if you're French-speaking or want your kids to come out trilingual.

### 6. Japanese School Amsterdam (Nichiran)

- **Primary & secondary:** ~€12,500-€14,000/year (varies by Japanese government subsidy)
- **Registration:** ~€600
- **Capital fee:** ~€1,200
- **Bus:** ~€2,200/year

Targeted at families on short-term Japanese assignments. Curriculum follows MEXT (Japanese Ministry of Education). Limited to families with at least one Japanese-speaking parent in most cases.

## The "Mia question": AICS at €7k vs BSA at €22k — which is actually better value?

When I walked Mia's parents through the breakdown, the decision wasn't actually about the money. They could afford either. It was about three softer factors that the price tags hide:

**1. Curriculum continuity.** Mia was coming from a Year 4 in a British-system school in Sydney. BSA's Year 4 is a direct match. AICS uses IPC (International Primary Curriculum) — also excellent, but a more thematic, project-based approach. For a kid who'd just been uprooted, less change was worth €15k/year of additional tuition.

**2. Length of stay.** Their assignment was 4 years with high likelihood of extension. If they were staying 2 years, AICS would have been the obvious answer — same IB pathway, two-thirds of the price. For 6+ years, BSA's continuity to GCSE and A-level becomes more valuable.

**3. The 30% ruling.** Her father's employer reimbursed school fees as a tax-free allowance under the 30% ruling extraterritorial-costs rule (more on this below). Effectively the BSA fees cost the employer maybe €13k net after tax efficiencies, vs €4k for AICS. The headline gap of €15k shrank to about €9k of actual employer cost.

They picked BSA. Mia's been there a year now, settled, doing well. The lesson: don't decide on the sticker price alone.

## Hidden costs nobody warns you about

The tuition number is the headline. The real all-in cost is 25-50% higher once you stack the extras. Here's what I tell families to budget for.

### Registration and acceptance fees

Every school charges something. Range: €250 (ISH) to €3,000+ (BSA acceptance fee plus capital). Non-refundable. If you apply to three schools as a backup strategy, expect €1,000-€2,000 in pure application costs before anyone has set foot in a classroom.

### Capital levies and building funds

The private schools all charge them. ASH €5,500 + €1,800/year. BSA €2,500 + €1,250/year for 5 years. These are "voluntary" in name but functionally mandatory. They fund building maintenance and expansion.

### Transport

Amsterdam and The Hague both have decent public transport, but international schools are often in suburbs (Wassenaar, Kijkduin, Buitenveldert) where the school bus is the only realistic option for primary kids. Budget **€1,800-€3,400/year per child** for the bus.

### Lunch

Subsidised schools (AICS, ISH) usually expect kids to bring lunch from home. Private schools offer a hot lunch programme for €4.50-€6.50/day = **€700-€1,200/year**.

### After-school care (BSO/ASA)

Dutch BSO is partially subsidised through *kinderopvangtoeslag* if you're working — but only at registered BSO providers. International schools typically have in-house after-school programmes that are *not* eligible for toeslag. Realistic cost: **€200-€400/month** for 2-3 days a week.

### Uniforms

BSA and ASH have proper uniforms. Initial kit: €400-€700. Replacement: €200-€300/year. AICS and ISH don't have uniforms.

### Extras and trips

Year-end residentials, ski trips, MUN conferences, sports tours. **€500-€2,500/year** depending on year group.

## How the 30% ruling changes the maths

This is the single biggest thing that determines whether private international school is affordable.

The 30% ruling lets qualifying expats receive up to 30% of their gross salary as a tax-free allowance (reduced to 27% from 2027, with cap of around €77,000 from 2026). Separately — and this is the bit families miss — your employer can also reimburse **extraterritorial costs** *in addition to* the 30% allowance, tax-free, if you've opted for the "actual costs" route. School fees for international (i.e. non-Dutch curriculum) schools count as eligible extraterritorial costs.

Three scenarios:

**Scenario A — 30% allowance only, school fees paid from net salary.** Your €25k tuition costs your *employer* €25k and *you* €25k of net spending power. Painful.

**Scenario B — 30% allowance + employer school-fee reimbursement on top.** Your employer pays the €25k as a tax-free reimbursement. For them, it's a deductible business expense. For you, it doesn't count as taxable salary. Effective cost to the family: zero. Effective cost to the employer: €25k minus 25.8% corporate tax saving = ~€18.5k. This is the negotiation worth having.

**Scenario C — Employer "actual extraterritorial costs" route instead of 30% flat rate.** Less common but useful for very high earners. Your employer reimburses the school fees plus other documented relocation costs at actual amounts, tax-free. The 30% flat rate is no longer applied.

If you're negotiating an expat package, the line you want in the contract is: *"School fees for [child name] at an international school will be reimbursed up to €X per child per year as a tax-free extraterritorial cost."*

## What about tax deductibility for parents?

Short answer: very limited. The Netherlands does not allow a general income-tax deduction for private school fees. The old *scholingsuitgaven* (study costs) deduction was abolished for personal income tax in 2022 and replaced by the STAP-budget subsidy, which has itself been wound down for 2026.

What still works:

- **Employer reimbursement under 30% ruling extraterritorial-costs route** — the big one (see above).
- **Childcare allowance (*kinderopvangtoeslag*)** for BSO if you use a registered provider — capped, income-tested, and does *not* apply to most in-school after-school programmes.
- **Kinderbijslag** — universal child benefit, ~€280-€400/quarter per child depending on age. Not means-tested. Worth applying for the moment you have a BSN.

If you're a freelancer/ZZP'er paying school fees yourself, the fees are not deductible against business income. Sorry.

## Application timeline — start earlier than you think

A real-world calendar based on the families I help:

- **18 months before arrival:** check eligibility for DIPS/DISS (need proof of international assignment). Get on waiting lists.
- **12 months before:** apply to private schools (BSA, ASH, Lycée). Application fees due.
- **9 months before:** assessment/interview (most schools test reading, maths, and English for non-native speakers).
- **6 months before:** acceptance letter. Capital fees and deposit due.
- **3 months before:** submit final documents — passports, vaccination records, previous school reports, BSN if available.
- **August:** start of school year.

For mid-year arrivals (January or April starts), AICS and ISH are sometimes flexible. BSA and ASH usually want you to start at the beginning of a term.

## Are there cheaper alternatives?

Yes — three of them.

### 1. Dutch state primary school

Free. Genuinely free. ~€50/year voluntary parental contribution. But: Dutch-language instruction from day one. Brilliant for kids under 7 (they'll be fluent in 6-12 months). Brutal for a 12-year-old. If you're staying 5+ years, this is the cheapest and arguably best option for younger kids.

### 2. Newcomer classes (*nieuwkomersklas / taalschool*)

Free Dutch state-school programme for kids aged 6-12 who don't yet speak Dutch. One year of intensive Dutch immersion, then they slot into a regular Dutch class. Used by many refugee and EU-mobility families. Underused by Anglophone expats.

### 3. Bilingual (TTO) Dutch secondary

State secondary schools with English-Dutch bilingual streams. ~€500-€1,500/year extra above the standard voluntary contribution. Strong academically, but you and the kids need enough Dutch to handle the half of subjects taught in Dutch. Best for families staying long-term.

## Practical decision framework

When a family sits down at my kitchen table, this is the four-question filter I run them through:

1. **How long are you staying?** Under 3 years → DIPS/DISS or stay in your home-country system if possible. 3-7 years → DIPS/DISS or Lycée. 7+ years → consider Dutch state with bilingual stream, or commit to a single private school for continuity.
2. **What does the employer reimburse?** If they cover private fees as a tax-free extraterritorial cost: private is functionally free. If not: do the maths on €25k of post-tax spending — that's a €45k+ pre-tax salary requirement.
3. **What curriculum was the kid in?** Match if you can. Switching IPC ↔ National Curriculum ↔ American Common Core is doable but stressful in the same year as the move itself.
4. **Where are you actually going to live?** Wassenaar/The Hague area → ASH or ISH. Amsterdam Zuid/Amstelveen → BSA or AICS. Don't pick a school that needs a 45-minute daily bus ride if you can avoid it.

Mia's parents went through this filter in about an hour. Most families do.

## FAQ

- q: 'Are international school fees in the Netherlands tax-deductible for parents?'
  a: 'Not as a personal income-tax deduction. The old scholingsuitgaven deduction was abolished in 2022. However, your employer can reimburse international school fees tax-free under the extraterritorial-costs provision alongside the 30% ruling. This is the single most important thing to negotiate in your expat package.'
- q: 'What is the difference between DIPS/DISS schools and fully private international schools?'
  a: 'DIPS (Dutch International Primary Schools) and DISS (Dutch International Secondary Schools) are international departments attached to publicly funded Dutch foundations. They receive partial state funding so tuition is roughly €6,500-€12,500/year. Fully private schools like BSA and ASH receive no state funding and charge €20,000-€30,000/year.'
- q: 'Can my child attend a DIPS school if we are not on a temporary contract?'
  a: 'Priority is given to families with proof of an international employment assignment of limited duration (usually 2-7 years). Other applicants can still apply but face longer waiting lists. EU citizens settling permanently may be redirected toward Dutch state schools with bilingual streams.'
- q: 'How long are the waiting lists for international schools in Amsterdam and The Hague?'
  a: 'AICS and BSA can have waiting lists of 6-18 months for popular year groups. ISH and ASH tend to absorb new students faster but still expect 3-6 months notice. Register the moment you have a confirmed assignment date, even if other paperwork is pending.'
- q: 'Does the 30% ruling cover school fees automatically?'
  a: 'No. The 30% allowance and the extraterritorial-costs reimbursement for school fees are two separate mechanisms. The flat 30% covers general extra costs of living abroad. School fees can be reimbursed on top, tax-free, only if your employer explicitly includes them in your package and processes them as documented extraterritorial costs.'
- q: 'What happens if my employer does not reimburse school fees?'
  a: 'You pay them from net (after-tax) salary. At top Dutch tax rates that means €25k of tuition requires roughly €45k of gross income. Either negotiate harder, choose a DIPS/DISS school, or consider Dutch state primary plus weekend mother-tongue classes.'
- q: 'Are there scholarships at international schools in the Netherlands?'
  a: 'Limited. ASH and BSA offer a small number of needs-based bursaries, typically capped at 25-50% of tuition and prioritised for existing students whose family circumstances change. DIPS schools do not offer additional discounts beyond the already-subsidised rate.'
