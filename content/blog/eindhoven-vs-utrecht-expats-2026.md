I have lived in both Eindhoven and Utrecht. Eindhoven first, for fourteen months, while I was contracting for a Philips spinoff. Utrecht second, for two and a half years, while my partner was at a consultancy.

People often ask me which I preferred. The honest answer is that they suit different people and different life stages. The differences are not subtle. The cities have completely different rhythms, job markets, and demographics. Choosing wrongly between them — for example, taking a job in Eindhoven and trying to live in Utrecht because it sounds nicer — can be a quietly miserable mistake.

This is the comparison I wish someone had given me before I made my own decision. I will tell you what each city is genuinely like to live in as an expat, where the trade-offs land, and which city suits which kind of person.

## The big picture

Eindhoven is in North Brabant, in the south. It is the fifth-largest city in the Netherlands at around 245,000 people in the city itself and around 800,000 in the metropolitan region (Brainport Eindhoven). It is dominated by tech and engineering — ASML, Philips legacy, NXP, Signify — and has earned the nickname "the Dutch Silicon Valley", which is partially deserved.

Utrecht is in the centre, around 30 minutes south-east of Amsterdam by train. It is the fourth-largest city at around 360,000 people and sits at the heart of the Dutch railway network. Utrecht has a 2,000-year history (Roman fort), a famous cathedral, the country's largest university, and a vibrant cultural scene.

These two facts shape almost everything else:

- **Eindhoven feels like a workshop town**: pragmatic, engineering-driven, low-key, growing fast.
- **Utrecht feels like a charming small capital**: historic, dense, walkable, intellectually dense.

If you are choosing between them based purely on aesthetics, Utrecht wins almost every comparison. But aesthetics are only one factor.

## Cost of living

Eindhoven is clearly cheaper. The differential is mostly in housing.

| Category | Eindhoven (2026) | Utrecht (2026) |
|---|---|---|
| 1-bed apartment, city centre | €1,100 to €1,500 | €1,400 to €1,900 |
| 2-bed apartment, city centre | €1,400 to €1,800 | €1,800 to €2,400 |
| 3-bed apartment, family area | €1,800 to €2,400 | €2,200 to €3,200 |
| Groceries (couple, monthly) | €450 to €600 | €500 to €650 |
| Eating out (mid-range, person) | €25 to €40 | €30 to €50 |
| Public transport (monthly) | €60 to €90 | €70 to €110 |
| Coffee at a cafe | €3.50 to €4.50 | €3.80 to €5.00 |

A typical dual-income expat couple I know can live comfortably in Eindhoven on €4,500 to €5,500 net per month including rent, savings, and reasonable lifestyle. The same lifestyle in Utrecht costs €5,500 to €7,000 net per month. The €1,500 monthly difference compounds — over five years it is enough to make a serious downpayment on a Dutch property.

## Jobs and work culture

Eindhoven and Utrecht offer fundamentally different job markets.

### Eindhoven job market

Dominated by:
- **High-tech manufacturing and semiconductors**: ASML (in nearby Veldhoven), NXP, Philips, Signify, Prodrive, FEI/Thermo Fisher
- **Embedded systems and software**: hundreds of SMEs supplying the high-tech ecosystem
- **Design**: Design Academy Eindhoven feeds the local design economy
- **Logistics and distribution**: proximity to Belgium and Germany

Working language: predominantly English at international employers. ASML, in particular, has a workforce that is 40+ percent international. Speaking Dutch is rarely a requirement for technical roles.

Salaries: senior technical roles at ASML and Philips pay €80,000 to €130,000 base. Mid-level engineers earn €55,000 to €80,000. The 30% ruling applies to most international hires.

Commute culture: most Eindhoven employers are within 15 minutes by car or 25 minutes by bicycle. The High Tech Campus is in Eindhoven itself; ASML is in Veldhoven; Philips is spread across the city. The "live close to work" model works.

### Utrecht job market

Dominated by:
- **Consulting**: Big Four (Deloitte, PwC, KPMG, EY), Bain, McKinsey have major Utrecht offices
- **Finance**: Rabobank headquarters, ABN AMRO regional, large insurance companies
- **Life sciences**: UMC Utrecht (university hospital), Genmab, Galapagos, biotech cluster
- **Government and policy**: many ministries with Utrecht offices, semi-public organisations
- **Tech**: not Eindhoven-scale but growing — Booking.com nearby in Amsterdam pulls workers via train

Working language: mixed. Big Four and life sciences are largely English. Government and Dutch-owned consultancies often require Dutch.

Salaries: consultants at Big Four start €45,000 to €60,000 base, scaling to €120,000+ at manager level. Tech roles at Utrecht-based companies pay €60,000 to €110,000.

Commute culture: Utrecht works as a hub. Many residents commute 20 to 60 minutes by train to Amsterdam, Den Bosch, Hilversum, or even Eindhoven. The "live in Utrecht, work somewhere else" model is common.

## English-friendliness in daily life

Both cities are highly English-friendly. The differences are subtle.

**Eindhoven**: Service staff speak English well. The international tech workforce means restaurants, gyms, and shops near the centre routinely operate in English. The university (TU/e) is heavily international. Outside the centre, in residential areas like Stratum or Tongelre, more Dutch is needed.

**Utrecht**: Service staff speak English very well, sometimes with mild reluctance. Utrecht University attracts a large international student population, and the historic centre is full of tourists in summer. Outside the centre, especially in family neighbourhoods like Wilhelminapark or Lombok, you will hear and need more Dutch.

For daily life as an English speaker, both cities are perfectly viable. Learning Dutch will accelerate integration in either, and I cover the options in [best Dutch language schools online for expats](/blog/best-dutch-language-schools-online-2026/).

## Public transport and connectivity

This is where Utrecht has a clear advantage.

**Utrecht Centraal** is the busiest train station in the Netherlands. Direct trains to:
- Amsterdam: 27 minutes (every 5 to 10 minutes)
- Den Haag: 35 minutes
- Rotterdam: 40 minutes
- Eindhoven: 60 minutes
- Schiphol Airport: 30 minutes

If you frequently travel within the country — for work, for visiting friends, for weekends in different cities — Utrecht is unbeatable.

**Eindhoven Centraal** is a regional hub:
- Amsterdam: 80 to 90 minutes
- Den Haag: 100 minutes
- Rotterdam: 75 minutes
- Maastricht: 60 minutes
- Düsseldorf (DE): 90 minutes
- Antwerp (BE): 60 minutes

Eindhoven is well-connected to the south and to neighbouring countries but trips to the Randstad consume real time.

**Local transport**: Both cities have good bus networks. Utrecht has a small tram line; Eindhoven has none. Both are highly cyclable, with Utrecht having the more extensive cycling infrastructure of the two (and arguably the world).

## Schools and family life

For families, school options matter.

**Eindhoven**:
- International School Eindhoven (IB curriculum, primary and secondary)
- Regional International School (Eersel, 20 minutes from Eindhoven)
- Several Dutch primary schools with English streams
- Strong family-friendly suburbs (Strijp-S, Stratum, Tongelre)

**Utrecht**:
- International School Utrecht (IB curriculum, primary and secondary)
- Several other international options within 30 minutes (Hilversum, Bilthoven, Amsterdam)
- Many Dutch primary schools with English streams or international sections
- Family-friendly neighbourhoods (Wilhelminapark, Tuindorp, Lunetten)

For most expat families both cities work. Utrecht has slightly more choice but Eindhoven's options are well-funded and well-regarded. Waiting lists exist in both cities for international schools — apply 12 to 18 months ahead if possible.

## Lifestyle and culture

This is where the cities diverge most.

### Eindhoven lifestyle

Eindhoven feels modern and pragmatic. The city was heavily bombed in WWII so the architecture is mostly post-war and contemporary. Strijp-S, the redeveloped Philips factory district, is full of design studios, breweries, and concept restaurants.

What stands out:
- **Dutch Design Week** in October — one of the biggest design events in Europe
- **Glow Festival** in November — light installations across the city
- **High-tech meetups** several nights a week
- **Strong startup scene** for hardware and embedded software
- **Brabant warmth**: Brabanders have a reputation for being more outgoing and friendly than Dutch people from the Randstad

What is missing:
- Historic centre (very limited compared to Utrecht)
- Major museums (besides the excellent Van Abbemuseum)
- Diverse food scene (improving but smaller than Utrecht)
- Nightlife scale (decent but quieter than Utrecht)

### Utrecht lifestyle

Utrecht has the second-best preserved medieval centre in the Netherlands after Maastricht. The Domtoren cathedral tower dominates the skyline. The canals (with their unique two-level wharves and water-side cafes) are the most photogenic in the country.

What stands out:
- **Historic centre**: walkable, dense, full of independent shops
- **Cultural depth**: Centraal Museum, Dom Cathedral, TivoliVredenburg (one of the best concert halls in Europe)
- **Food scene**: more diverse and developed than Eindhoven, though more expensive
- **Festivals**: Le Guess Who?, Utrecht Early Music Festival, hundreds of student events
- **Cycling culture**: the world capital — the city station has 22,000+ bicycle parking spaces

What is missing:
- Eindhoven's startup energy
- The same density of high-tech jobs
- Cheaper rents
- Easy access to the design and engineering industries of Brainport

## Healthcare and amenities

Both cities have excellent healthcare. Utrecht has the larger university hospital (UMC Utrecht), which is one of the top research hospitals in Europe. Eindhoven has Catharina Ziekenhuis and Maxima Medisch Centrum, both well-regarded but smaller.

For routine healthcare, both cities have plenty of English-speaking GPs and dentists. For specialist care, Utrecht's UMC pulls patients from across the country.

## Who should choose which city

After living in both, here is my honest take.

**Choose Eindhoven if**:
- You work in tech, engineering, or design
- Your employer is in or near Eindhoven (especially ASML, Philips, NXP, Signify)
- You want significantly lower rent and a higher savings rate
- You like a calm, modern, pragmatic city
- You are fine with travelling to the Randstad less frequently

**Choose Utrecht if**:
- You work in consulting, finance, life sciences, government, or for a Randstad employer
- You travel a lot within the Netherlands
- You value historic architecture and a developed cultural scene
- You want easy weekend trips to Amsterdam, Rotterdam, and beyond
- You are willing to pay a 25 to 35 percent premium for housing

**Avoid the wrong choice if**:
- You take a job in Eindhoven and try to live in Utrecht (the commute is brutal)
- You take a Randstad job and live in Eindhoven (90+ minutes each way is unsustainable)
- You move with kids to a city without checking school waiting lists

## My personal verdict

For my work and lifestyle, Utrecht edged it. The cultural depth, the trains, the centre — once I was settled, I did not want to leave.

For my partner's work in tech, Eindhoven would have been the better choice. We compromised because we are not in the most extreme version of either career. Many couples cannot.

If your work is in Brainport, live in Eindhoven and embrace its strengths. The Dutch Design Week alone is worth the move. The savings rate alone is worth the move. The lower stress of a smaller, less crowded city is worth the move.

If your work is in the Randstad or you are a generalist who could take a job anywhere, Utrecht is the safer bet. Five years in Utrecht teaches you the Netherlands in a way no other city does.
