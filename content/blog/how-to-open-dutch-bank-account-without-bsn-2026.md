## Why You Don't Have a BSN Yet (And How Long That Takes)

Before we get to the solutions, it helps to understand why getting a BSN takes so long. This matters because the wait time determines how urgently you need an interim banking solution.

The BSN — Burgerservicenummer — is the Netherlands' universal citizen identification number. It's issued by your local municipality (gemeente) when you register as a resident. The Belastingdienst (Dutch tax authority) uses it to track your taxes. Every Dutch institution that matters — banks, employers, health insurers, the housing authority — uses it to identify you.

For **EU/EEA citizens**, the process works like this: you move to the Netherlands, find an address (temporary or permanent), book an appointment at your gemeente, and register. The BSN is often issued the same day or within a week. Sounds fast — except that in Amsterdam, Rotterdam, and The Hague, appointment slots can be four to eight weeks out. In smaller cities, it might be one to two weeks.

For **non-EU expats** — knowledge migrants, highly skilled migrants, people sponsored by employers — the process runs through the [IND (Immigratie- en Naturalisatiedienst)](https://ind.nl), the Dutch immigration authority. Your employer or sponsor typically handles the residence permit application, and you receive your BSN once that permit is approved and you've registered at your gemeente. This process takes anywhere from two weeks to two months, depending on the IND's current caseload and whether you came through the fast-track knowledge migrant procedure.

The practical result: many new expats spend their first four to six weeks in the Netherlands without a BSN. And without a BSN, traditional Dutch banks will not open an account for you. Full stop.

## Why ING, ABN AMRO, and Rabobank All Require a BSN

I know it feels arbitrary when you're standing there with a valid EU passport and a signed lease, but the BSN requirement at traditional Dutch banks is not policy they can waive. It's a legal compliance matter.

Under Dutch financial law and the guidelines set by De Nederlandsche Bank (DNB), all regulated financial institutions must perform Know Your Customer (KYC) checks before opening accounts. For residents of the Netherlands, this KYC process requires a BSN as a mandatory identifier. The banks aren't being difficult — they are legally prohibited from opening a full current account without it.

ABN AMRO, ING, and Rabobank have all tried at various points to create "expat onboarding" products, but as of 2026, none offer a workaround to the BSN requirement for current accounts. There is no magic branch to visit, no exception form to fill out, no workaround that involves pleading with a manager. I've read every expat forum thread on this topic, and the answer is consistent: the big three Dutch banks require a BSN.

### What Wise Actually Is

Wise is not a bank. This matters and I'll explain why. Wise is an Electronic Money Institution (EMI) regulated by the Belgian National Bank (Nationale Bank van België). It holds a European licence to issue payment accounts and cards, and it passports that licence across the EU — including to the Netherlands. Because it's not a Dutch bank, it doesn't fall under De Nederlandsche Bank's KYC requirements in the same way, which is why it can open an account without a BSN.

The IBAN you receive from Wise is Belgian — it starts with BE, not NL. This is important for one specific Dutch reason: iDEAL. The Dutch online payment system iDEAL only works with Dutch (NL) IBANs, so if you want to pay for things through Dutch webshops, you'll hit a wall. More on this in a moment.

Your money in Wise is not covered by the Dutch Deposit Guarantee Scheme (DGS), which protects up to €100,000 per person at Dutch licensed banks. However, Wise is legally required to hold 100% of your funds in safeguarded accounts at regulated institutions, kept entirely separate from Wise's own operating capital. In practice, your money is safe — but the legal protection structure is different from a traditional bank.

## What the BSN Application Process Actually Looks Like

I won't write the full guide here — that lives at [/blog/expat-bsn-application-guide-2026/](/blog/expat-bsn-application-guide-2026/) — but the basic shape of the process is:

1. **Find accommodation**: You need a registered Dutch address before you can register. If you're in temporary housing, some municipalities allow registration with a letter from the landlord or a rental contract.
2. **Book your gemeente appointment**: Do this the day you arrive or even before you move. Appointment slots go fast in major cities. The municipality's website usually has an online booking system.
3. **Bring your documents**: Valid passport or EU ID, proof of address (rental contract, landlord declaration, or employer letter), and — for non-EU expats — your residence permit or IND sticker.
4. **Register**: The municipality registers you in the Basisregistratie Personen (BRP), the Dutch personal records database. Your BSN is either issued the same day or sent to you by post within a week.
5. **Inform your bank**: Once you have your BSN, add it to your Bunq account in the app, and you're fully compliant.

For non-EU expats going through the [IND](https://ind.nl) process, your employer or sponsor typically guides you through the residence permit steps. The BSN usually comes after your permit is approved and you complete the gemeente registration — so the timeline is longer, but the same interim banking solutions apply.
