Switching took me about 30 minutes online and saved roughly EUR 220 a year. The new connection was, predictably, faster.

Most expats in the Netherlands are paying more than they need to for internet. The market is competitive, switching is straightforward, and a 20-minute comparison usually finds savings. This guide covers the practicalities.

## The Dutch Internet Market in 2026

There are three large national providers and a growing number of regional fibre alt-nets:

### National Providers

**KPN** — historic incumbent, primarily fibre and DSL. Best customer service in independent surveys. <a href="https://go.expatnetherlandshub.com/kpn" target="_blank" rel="nofollow sponsored noopener">Their direct welcome offers</a> are competitive but often beaten by aggregator-routed alternatives.

**Ziggo** (VodafoneZiggo) — primarily cable (DOCSIS 3.1, sometimes 4.0 in upgraded areas). Strong TV bundle market. <a href="https://go.expatnetherlandshub.com/ziggo" target="_blank" rel="nofollow sponsored noopener">Their welcome offers</a> are aggressive on price but their out-of-contract pricing is high — switching at end-of-contract is essential.

**Odido** (formerly T-Mobile NL after merger) — mobile plus fixed converged offers. Strong if you are bundling mobile and home.

### Alt-Net Fibre Providers

In 2026 the alt-net landscape has consolidated. Major players:

- **Delta Fiber** — significant rollout in regional areas, competitive pricing
- **Open Dutch Fiber** — wholesale fibre operator, multiple resellers (T-Mobile / Odido, KPN, Online.nl) sell over their network
- **Glaspoort** — joint venture, residential fibre rollout
- **Caiway** — south-east NL focus, some bundles

If your address is on alt-net fibre, you typically have a choice of resellers offering service over the same physical fibre — sometimes 5 to 10 options. This is where switching saves the most.

## The Three Things That Determine Your Best Switch

### 1. What Connection Type Is Available

Run a postcode check at any provider's website (or use Independer's tool). You will see one or more of:

- **DSL (KPN)**: copper to your home from a cabinet. Speeds typically 50 to 200 Mbps. Reasonable but the slowest of the modern options
- **Cable (Ziggo)**: coaxial cable. Speeds 100 Mbps to 1 Gbps. Asymmetric — much higher download than upload
- **Fibre to the home (FTTH)**: optical fibre to your apartment. Speeds 200 Mbps to 8 Gbps. Symmetric. Best in 2026

If you have all three available, fibre is typically the best choice. If you only have cable, Ziggo or competitors using their network. If you only have DSL, KPN or one of its DSL resellers (XS4ALL, Online.nl).

### 2. Your Current Contract Status

Two key dates:

- **End date (einddatum)**: when your fixed contract expires
- **Notice period (opzegtermijn)**: usually 30 days, sometimes 60

If you are within 60 days of your end date, switch now (the switch will be timed to start at the end date). If you are deep in a fixed contract with 6+ months remaining, the exit fee usually outweighs the saving.

### 3. Your Real Usage

Most expats overpay because they over-spec. Real usage profiles:

- **Solo or couple with streaming and remote work**: 200 to 500 Mbps is plenty. Plans cost EUR 35 to EUR 50 per month.
- **Family with multiple devices and 4K streaming**: 500 Mbps to 1 Gbps. Plans EUR 45 to EUR 65.
- **Gamers, creators, multiple heavy users**: 1 Gbps fibre. Plans EUR 50 to EUR 75.
- **Power users, home server, NAS**: multi-gigabit fibre. Plans EUR 65 to EUR 100.

Most homes do not need more than 500 Mbps. If you are paying EUR 60+ per month for a 1 Gbps cable plan and only using 100 Mbps consistently, you are over-spec and overpaying.

## The Switch Process: Step by Step

### Step 1: Check Your Current Contract

Log into your existing provider's customer portal. Find:

- Contract end date
- Current monthly cost
- Speed tier
- TV / phone components (if bundled)
- Equipment loan (modem, decoder)

Note all of these. They determine your switching strategy.

### Step 2: Compare Available Options

Use Independer's [internet comparison](/blog/best-broadband-internet-providers-expats-netherlands-2026/) or directly check 2-3 providers' postcode tools. Filter by:

- Speed (download, upload)
- Contract length (12 / 24 months — avoid 24 unless price is meaningfully better)
- Welcome discount terms
- TV / phone bundle if needed

### Step 3: Order the New Service

Sign up directly with the new provider. You will need:

- Address
- Current provider name (so they can handle cancellation)
- Bank details for direct debit (incasso)
- BSN
- Choice of installation date (typically 5 to 14 days out)

The new provider initiates cancellation of your old contract. You do not contact your old provider.

### Step 4: Receive Equipment

The new provider ships:

- A modem / router (modem-router combo, sometimes called an "experiabox" at KPN)
- Cables and instructions
- Often a temporary 4G hotspot for the switching day if downtime is expected

### Step 5: Switch Day

On the agreed switch date:

- The old service is disconnected (typically morning)
- The new service is activated (typically same day, sometimes 1-2 hours later)
- You install the new modem (5 to 15 minutes — plug-and-play in most cases)
- Optional: the technician visits if a new line installation is needed

Total downtime is typically 1 to 4 hours. Critical work (video calls, gaming) — schedule around this.

### Step 6: Return Old Equipment

You must return your old modem within 30 days. The old provider sends a return label. Failure to return results in EUR 50 to EUR 200 charges.

### Step 7: Final Bill

Your old provider sends a final bill (eindafrekening) within 6 weeks. This reconciles your last month plus any partial-month adjustments and refunds any overpaid direct debits. Read it carefully.

## Switching at the Right Time

The optimal switch timing:

- 30 to 60 days before your contract end date — initiate the switch
- 60+ days into a new contract — if you can find a meaningfully better offer with welcome discount, the early-cancel cost may be worth it
- Mid-contract (deep) — usually wait

I switch when I see two simultaneous signals:

1. My contract end is within 60 days
2. A genuinely better offer (15+ percent saving for equivalent service) exists

If only one signal is present, I usually wait.

## Bundle vs Internet-Only

Bundles in NL typically combine:

- Internet
- TV (Dutch and basic international channels)
- Fixed phone line (rarely useful in 2026)

Pricing: Internet-only at EUR 35 to EUR 50 vs internet + TV bundle at EUR 50 to EUR 70. The TV component costs EUR 12 to EUR 25 incremental.

Worth bundling if:
- You watch live Dutch TV (news, football, public broadcasting)
- You want sport or premium channels (Ziggo Sport, Eurosport)
- You have older family members at home

Skip the bundle if:
- You stream everything (Netflix, Disney+, HBO Max, Apple TV+)
- You watch sport via specific streaming services
- Live TV is not a habit

## Internet Plus Mobile Bundle

Convergent pricing — internet plus mobile via the same provider — has become more aggressive in 2026 with Odido pushing combined offers. KPN and Ziggo have similar.

Typical saving: EUR 5 to EUR 15 per month versus separate. Worth it if both contracts end around the same time and you do not mind the lock-in. Less worth it if you actively want SIM-only flexibility — see my [best SIM-only plans](/blog/best-sim-only-plans-expats-netherlands-2026/).

## Common Switching Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Forgetting to Cancel

If you signed up to a new provider but did not specify "switch from X" or did not give enough info, your old contract may continue and you pay both. Always confirm with the new provider in writing that they have initiated the cancellation. Check your bank account two months later — if both providers are still debiting, escalate.

### Mistake 2: Booking Switch Day on a Big Work Day

Downtime is 1 to 4 hours. Avoid switch days when you have important calls or deadlines. Tuesday or Wednesday morning works well for most home workers — avoid Mondays and Fridays.

### Mistake 3: Not Returning Old Equipment

The EUR 50 to EUR 200 charge is automatic. Use the return label, get a tracking receipt, keep it for 90 days.

### Mistake 4: Signing 24-Month Contracts for Marginal Discounts

A 24-month contract typically saves EUR 1 to EUR 3 per month versus a 12-month one. The flexibility loss is much higher than the saving. I default to 12-month contracts and re-evaluate every year.

### Mistake 5: Not Using Aggregators

The aggregator (Independer, Bencom, Internetvergelijken) shows live deals across all providers. Going direct to one provider's website misses 80 percent of available offers. Always start with a comparison.

### Mistake 6: Sticking With ISP Email

Switching ISPs while using @kpnmail.nl or @ziggo.nl as your primary email creates lock-in. Migrate to Gmail / ProtonMail / your own domain before switching.

### Mistake 7: Not Documenting Speeds

Run a speed test (speedtest.net or fast.com) on your current connection before switching, and again after. If the new provider promised 1 Gbps and you are getting 200 Mbps consistently, you have grounds for complaint. Document with screenshots.

## What to Do If the Switch Goes Wrong

The most common failure: the switch happens but the new connection does not work.

Steps:

1. Contact the new provider's technical support (always English-available at major providers in 2026)
2. Document the issue: time, error messages, speed test results
3. Request escalation to a technician visit
4. If unresolved within 5 working days, file a formal complaint
5. If still unresolved, escalate to ACM (Authority for Consumers and Markets) and / or seek a full contract termination

The legal right to a working connection is clear. Persistent failures justify cancellation without penalty.

## What If I Move Address?

You typically transfer (verhuizen) your contract to the new address. The process:

1. Notify your provider 30+ days before moving
2. They schedule transfer to the new address
3. Pay the verhuiskosten (transfer fee), typically EUR 35 to EUR 75
4. Service activates at new address on agreed date

If your new address is outside your provider's coverage, you can cancel without penalty.

If moving abroad, you can always cancel — provide proof of de-registration from the gemeente (uitschrijving) and the contract ends.

## Special Situations

### Short-Term Renters

If you are renting for under 12 months, traditional contracts may not fit. Options:

- 4G / 5G home internet (Odido, KPN both offer): no long contract, plug-in router, EUR 30 to EUR 50 per month, speeds 50 to 300 Mbps
- Inheriting the landlord's contract: works in some furnished short-term rentals
- Mobile hotspot: workable for very short stays

For short-stay context see [short-stay furnished housing in NL](/blog/short-stay-furnished-housing-netherlands-2026/).

### Co-Living and Shared Houses

Many shared houses include internet in the rent. Quality varies. If yours is poor, ask the landlord to upgrade — many will if you frame it as essential to remote work.

### Home Office Heavy Users

For full-time remote workers, prioritise upload speed and latency over headline download speed. 500 Mbps symmetric fibre beats 1 Gbps asymmetric cable for video calls. See [remote work in NL](/guides/work/remote-work-netherlands-expats-2026/) for the broader setup.

## Final Thought

Internet is one of the few Dutch admin items that genuinely rewards regular review. EUR 100 to EUR 250 per year savings, available with 30 minutes of effort, every 12 to 24 months. Set a calendar reminder for 60 days before your contract end and run a comparison.

Most expats either overpay by sitting on default contracts or under-spec by chasing the cheapest. The right approach is matching real usage to a competitively-priced contract with a 12-month term, then doing it again next year. Boring, predictable, profitable.
