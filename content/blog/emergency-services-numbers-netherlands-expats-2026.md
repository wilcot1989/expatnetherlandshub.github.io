The Sunday I cut my hand badly chopping vegetables in my Hague apartment, I did not know what to do. I did not need an ambulance. I did need stitches. It was 4pm on a Sunday so my GP was closed. I sat on the kitchen floor for ten minutes pressing a tea towel against the cut, googling "where do you go for stitches in the Netherlands on a Sunday" before my partner arrived home and called the huisartsenpost for me.

That is how most expats learn the Dutch emergency system: in the moment, badly, while bleeding or panicking. This guide is what I wish I had known going in.

## When to Call 112

112 is the universal European emergency number. Call it for:

- A medical emergency where someone's life is at risk (chest pain, severe bleeding, breathing difficulty, suspected stroke, unconsciousness)
- A crime in progress (violence, break-in happening now, witnessing assault)
- A fire (any size — call immediately)
- A serious traffic accident with injuries
- A drowning or near-drowning
- An immediate threat to your safety

Do NOT call 112 for:

- A cut that is not life-threatening (call huisartsenpost)
- A theft that has already happened (call 0900-8844 or report online)
- A noise complaint (call 0900-8844 or use online forms)
- General medical questions out of hours (call huisartsenpost)
- Lost items including passports (online or 0900-8844)

Misusing 112 ties up emergency operators and is, in serious cases, prosecutable. Use it correctly.

## What to Say When You Call 112

The operator will ask, in this order:

1. **What service do you need?** (Police, ambulance, fire — politie, ambulance, brandweer)
2. **Where are you?** (Exact address — street, number, city, sometimes nearby landmark)
3. **What is happening?** (Brief description)
4. **Are you in danger now?** (For police calls)
5. **Are there injuries?** (For medical / accident calls)
6. **How many people are involved?** (For larger incidents)

Address is the single most important piece. If you are not at home, look at the nearest street sign or building number before you call. If you do not know exactly where you are (e.g., on a bike path or in a park), describe the nearest identifiable landmark and your direction of approach.

You can speak English. You can speak German, French, Spanish, and several other languages — operators have access to translation if needed. If you are calmer, ask "do you speak English?" first; if you are panicked, just speak English from the start.

Do NOT hang up until the operator says you can. They may keep you on the line for additional details or to guide you through immediate first aid.

## The Huisartsenpost — Out-of-Hours GP Service

This is the most under-explained part of the Dutch emergency system for expats.

Your regular GP (huisarts) operates business hours: typically Monday to Friday, 8:00 to 17:00. Outside those hours — evenings, nights, weekends, public holidays — the huisartsenpost takes over. Every region has one (sometimes more). It is a single shared after-hours service for all GPs in that area.

The huisartsenpost handles:

- Acute illness that needs same-day attention but is not life-threatening
- Cuts needing stitches
- Severe pain
- Suspected infection (UTI, ear infection, throat infection)
- Allergic reactions (non-life-threatening)
- Children's illnesses out of hours
- Mental health concerns that need same-day support

The process:

1. Call the huisartsenpost number (NOT 112)
2. A triage nurse asks about your symptoms in English
3. They either give phone advice, book an appointment at the post for that evening, send a GP to your home, or direct you to the emergency department

You cannot walk in without calling first — you must be triaged by phone.

### Finding Your Huisartsenpost Number

- Search "[your city] huisartsenpost" on Google
- Check your regular GP's website — they list the after-hours number
- Use the national huisartsenposten.nl directory
- Save it in your phone now, while you are calm

In Amsterdam, three huisartsenposts cover the city by region. The Hague has one. Rotterdam has two. Utrecht has one. Smaller cities typically have one shared with surrounding villages.

The service is covered by your basisverzekering (Dutch basic health insurance) — see [Dutch health insurance plans](/blog/best-dutch-health-insurance-plans-2026/). ## The Spoedeisende Hulp (Emergency Department)

Hospital emergency departments in the Netherlands operate differently than in the UK or US. You generally do not walk in.

The standard Dutch flow is:

1. Call the huisartsenpost or 112
2. Triage decides whether you need the SEH (spoedeisende hulp / emergency department)
3. If yes, you are sent there with a referral
4. If you walk in without referral, you may be triaged and sent to the huisartsenpost

The exception: if you are clearly in life-threatening condition (massive trauma, suspected heart attack, severe burns), go to or call for the SEH directly. The hospital will not turn away a genuine emergency.

The Dutch system is gatekeeper-based. The GP / huisartsenpost is the front door. The hospital is for cases that pass triage. This is more efficient than walk-in emergency systems but feels alien to American expats used to ER-direct culture.

## Police: Emergency vs Non-Emergency

### When to Call 112 for Police

- Crime in progress (burglary, assault, robbery happening now)
- Immediate threat to your safety
- Witnessing serious violence
- Domestic violence in progress
- Major traffic accident with injuries
- Suspected terrorist activity (rare but real)

### When to Use 0900-8844 (Non-Emergency)

- Reporting a theft or burglary that already happened
- Suspicious activity that is not urgent
- Traffic incident without injuries
- Lost property (in addition to online report)
- Noise complaints (after attempting direct neighbour contact)
- General police questions

### When to Use politie.nl (Online)

For most non-violent past crimes, online reporting is the fastest option:

- Bicycle theft (very common in NL)
- Phone or laptop theft
- Wallet theft
- Lost passport (always also report to your embassy)
- Vandalism
- Online fraud
- Identity theft

The site is in English, fillable in 10 to 20 minutes, and generates the aangifte (police report) you may need for insurance claims. Bicycle theft especially: report it online, get the aangifte number, and submit to your bicycle insurance for reimbursement (if covered) — see my [cycling insurance guide](/blog/cycling-netherlands-expat-insurance-theft-2026/) for details.

## Mental Health Emergencies

The Dutch system separates urgent mental health support into several layers.

### Immediate Crisis (Suicide / Severe Distress)

- **113 Zelfmoordpreventie**: 113 (free) — national suicide prevention line, 24/7, English-speaking operators available, both phone and chat
- **112**: if there is immediate physical danger (e.g., active suicide attempt)

### Acute Mental Health Crisis (Not Imminent Danger)

Call your huisartsenpost. They can arrange same-evening psychiatric assessment via the regional GGZ crisis service. Many GGZ services have English-speaking psychiatrists on call.

### Persistent Mental Health Concerns

Start with your GP for a referral to GGZ (general mental health services) or to a private psychologist. The GGZ pathway is covered by basisverzekering after the eigen risico is met.

## Fire and Carbon Monoxide

For fire of any size: call 112. Even a small kitchen fire warrants a 112 call if you cannot extinguish it within 30 seconds. Dutch buildings often have shared walls and stairwells; what looks small can spread quickly.

Smoke detectors (rookmelders) have been mandatory in all Dutch homes since July 2022 — both rentals and owned properties. If yours is missing or non-functional, your landlord (for rentals) is legally required to provide one. Carbon monoxide detectors are not yet legally mandatory but are strongly recommended for any home with gas appliances; they cost EUR 25 to EUR 60 and have a 7 to 10 year service life.

## Domestic Violence and Safety

**Veilig Thuis** is the national domestic violence and child abuse helpline: 0800-2000, free, 24/7, anonymous, available in multiple languages (Dutch, English, Arabic, Turkish, Polish among others). They handle:

- Partner violence
- Child abuse or neglect
- Elder abuse
- Honour-based violence
- Witnessing violence in your community

Calls are confidential. They can advise, refer to safe accommodation, contact police on your behalf (with your consent or in immediate danger), and connect to victim support services.

## Lost Bank Cards and Identity

Time matters. Most fraud happens within hours of card loss.

- Call your bank's 24h blocking line (in your banking app or on the bank's website)
- Block the card immediately via app if your bank supports it (most do)
- File a police aangifte if there are unauthorised transactions or you suspect theft
- Contact your bank fraud department to dispute transactions
- Replace the card via your bank app or branch (typically 3-5 working days)

## Roadside and Vehicle Emergencies

For traffic accidents with injuries: 112.

For breakdowns without injuries:

- ANWB members: 088-2692222 (24h)
- Non-members: ANWB Wegenwacht via app or 088-2692222 (paid service)
- On highways: emergency phones every 2km, free
- Heavy goods or HGV: specific operators

Carry a hi-vis vest (mandatory in NL) and a warning triangle in your car. If you breakdown on a highway, exit the vehicle to the safe side of the barrier and stand back.

## Common Expat Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Calling 112 for Non-Emergencies

Most common: calling 112 because the GP is closed and you have a non-urgent question. Use the huisartsenpost. Misusing 112 is taken seriously and operators will redirect you, occupying resources during the call.

### Mistake 2: Walking into the Hospital ED Without Calling

You may be triaged out and sent home or to the huisartsenpost. Always call first unless the situation is clearly life-threatening.

### Mistake 3: Not Knowing Their Address in Dutch

If you have just moved to the Netherlands, practise saying your address out loud. The street name pronunciation matters when speaking quickly to an operator. If your street name is challenging to pronounce, have it written somewhere visible — phone case, fridge, by the front door — for emergencies.

### Mistake 4: Not Reporting Bike Theft

Roughly half of bike theft victims I have spoken with did not report. The aangifte takes 15 minutes online, costs nothing, and is required for insurance claims. If you have bicycle theft cover (often included in inboedelverzekering), the aangifte is your evidence.

### Mistake 5: Assuming English Service Is Universal

It largely is — but the elderly receptionist at a small-town huisartsenpost may struggle. Have a Dutch-speaking friend or partner you can call to translate if the language barrier becomes a real issue. In genuine emergencies, 112 always provides English support; for less urgent layers of the system, English fluency varies.

## Building Your Personal Emergency Card

I recommend every expat have a small card or note in their wallet (and saved on phone lock screen) with:

- Your full Dutch address
- Your BSN
- Health insurance company and policy number
- Allergies and medications
- Emergency contact name and phone
- Embassy or consulate phone for your home country

In a true emergency where you are unconscious, this information is what enables responders to act fast. The Dutch system is excellent but it depends on identifying you correctly.

## Final Thought

You probably will not need 112 in your first year in the Netherlands. You will probably use the huisartsenpost once. You may use the police non-emergency line for a stolen bike. The Dutch system is generally calm, organised, and English-functional once you understand the structure.

Save the numbers now. Practise saying your address. Know which line is for which problem. The five minutes spent setting this up before you need it is worth more than I can tell you.
