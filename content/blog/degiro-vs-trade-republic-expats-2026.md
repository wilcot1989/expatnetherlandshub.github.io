## What DEGIRO and Trade Republic Actually Are

**DEGIRO** is a Dutch online broker founded in 2013 in Amsterdam. It was acquired by flatexDEGIRO Bank in 2020 and now operates as a full bank-backed broker across most of Europe. Customer base is around 2.7 million as of 2026, heavily skewed toward DIY investors.

**Trade Republic** is a Berlin-based broker-bank founded in 2015. It has grown rapidly across Europe to roughly 8 million customers and added a banking license and EU IBAN in 2023, plus a 4% interest savings account on cash balances (the rate has fluctuated — check current rate before banking on it).

The simplest framing: DEGIRO is a serious DIY investing platform with a desktop-first heritage. Trade Republic is a mobile-first investing app with banking attached.

## Account Opening for Expats

Both accept expats with a Dutch address and a valid ID document.

**DEGIRO** requires a Dutch IBAN account for funding. You need your BSN for the account opening. Verification is largely automated and takes 1–3 business days.

**Trade Republic** accepts EU IBANs, including Dutch, German, French, etc. Verification is via video call or app-based ID scan, typically completed in 30 minutes. You don't strictly need a Dutch BSN to open the account, but you'll need one for Dutch tax filing later.

If you've just arrived in the Netherlands and don't yet have your BSN, Trade Republic is technically easier to open. But you'll need the BSN within a few months to comply with Dutch tax residency rules anyway, so this is rarely a real differentiator. See my [BSN application guide](/blog/expat-bsn-application-guide-2026/) for that step.

## Fee Structures, Side by Side

This is where most people overcomplicate the decision. The actual cost difference for a typical expat investor comes down to a few line items.

### DEGIRO Costs in 2026

- **Connectivity fee:** €2.50 per year per non-Euronext exchange you trade on. Stay on Euronext Amsterdam and Euronext Paris (where most accumulating UCITS ETFs are listed) and this is zero.
- **ETF transactions:** Around 200 ETFs are commission-free on the "Core Selection" list (one free trade per month per ETF, then €1 each). Off-list ETFs cost €3.90 + 0.05%.
- **Stocks:** €2.00 + 0.04% per US stock trade, €1.00 + 0.04% per Euronext trade, similar for major European exchanges.
- **Currency conversion:** 0.25% on all non-EUR trades (auto-FX) or €10 + 0.25% for manual FX.

For a typical "monthly ETF buy" expat investor sticking to the Core Selection on Euronext, the running cost is essentially €0/year.

### Trade Republic Costs in 2026

- **Stock and ETF trades:** €1 per trade flat (called an "external charge").
- **Savings plans:** Free, with no minimum amount. You can set up automatic monthly buys of stocks or ETFs for €1+ per execution.
- **Currency conversion:** 0.25% on non-EUR trades, similar to DEGIRO.
- **No connectivity fees** on any exchange.
- **Cash interest:** Up to 4% annually on uninvested cash balances (subject to change; check current rate).

For very small monthly investments (€25–€200), Trade Republic's free savings plans win. For larger or less frequent trades, the two brokers are roughly equivalent.

## ETF and Stock Selection

**DEGIRO** offers around 50 stock exchanges globally — Euronext, Xetra, NYSE, NASDAQ, LSE, Tokyo, Hong Kong, Sydney, etc. ETF selection is wide. The Core Selection covers the major iShares and Vanguard UCITS ETFs that 90% of expats actually want.

**Trade Republic** offers fewer exchanges (Xetra primarily, plus a curated list of US, European, and a few Asian stocks). ETF selection has grown to roughly 2,400 ETFs in 2026, including all the major UCITS index trackers. Savings plans are available on most.

If you want exotic exchanges (Tokyo, Hong Kong direct, smaller European exchanges) DEGIRO wins. If you want the standard expat-investor menu of accumulating world ETFs and major US/European stocks, Trade Republic covers it fully.

## Savings Plans (Sparpläne)

This is Trade Republic's killer feature for expats. You set up an automatic monthly purchase of an ETF or stock (or a basket of them), and it executes automatically — for free, on any amount from €1 upward. You can pause, change amount, or cancel anytime in the app.

DEGIRO doesn't offer scheduled automatic purchases. You log in each month and manually place the trade.

For new investors who benefit from systematic dollar-cost averaging without behavioral interference, Trade Republic's savings plans are genuinely better.

## User Experience

**DEGIRO** has a clean web interface and a functional mobile app. It feels like a serious broker — informative, slightly clinical, with full order types (market, limit, stop, stop-limit, etc.) and detailed reports.

**Trade Republic** is mobile-first and minimal. The web platform launched in 2024 but is still less developed than the app. The interface prioritizes simplicity over information density. Power users find it limiting; beginners find it less intimidating.

After three years using both, I prefer DEGIRO when I want to research and place a deliberate trade, and Trade Republic when I'm setting up automation or wanting to glance at performance.

## Cash Management and Interest

Trade Republic's cash interest rate has been a major draw — up to 4% APY on uninvested EUR cash, paid monthly, on balances up to €50,000. Above €50,000, the rate drops or doesn't apply (rules have changed; verify current terms).

DEGIRO pays no interest on cash. The platform's traditional default has been to invest cash in money market funds, which had their own structure changes in 2023–2024. Most users now hold cash on DEGIRO at zero interest — fine for working capital between trades, terrible as a savings account.

If you keep €5,000–€20,000 of buffer cash within your investing platform, Trade Republic's interest can earn you €200–€800/year more. That said, you'd usually be better off with that cash in [Bunq's savings account](/blog/bunq-review-expats-netherlands-2026/) or another high-interest Dutch savings option.

## Tax Reporting for Dutch Residents

Both platforms produce annual tax statements (jaaroverzicht) that align with Dutch Box 3 reporting requirements.

**DEGIRO** auto-pre-fills your DEGIRO holdings into the Dutch Belastingdienst portal in some cases — this is convenient but check it carefully against your own statement. Their statement is usually available in Dutch by mid-February.

**Trade Republic** issues a "Tax Statement Netherlands" PDF in English that gives you the values needed for Box 3 (assets on 1 January at fair value). This is straightforward to copy into your Dutch tax return manually. Trade Republic does not (yet) auto-pre-fill into the Belastingdienst portal.

For dividend tax, both broker statements show withholding tax credits where applicable.

If you have a [tax advisor](/blog/best-tax-advisors-expats-netherlands-2026/), either platform's statement is straightforward for them to work with.

**DEGIRO** funding takes 1–3 business days from a Dutch bank. Withdrawals to your linked bank account are free and take 1–2 days.

**Trade Republic** has its own EU IBAN, which means funding can be instant from another EU bank if your bank uses SEPA Instant. Withdrawals to your linked bank account are also fast.

## Security and Regulation

Both are properly regulated EU brokers.

**DEGIRO** is regulated by the Dutch AFM and DNB. It's part of flatexDEGIRO Bank AG, a German bank. Cash deposits are protected by the German deposit guarantee scheme up to €100,000.

**Trade Republic** has held its own German banking license since 2023 (BaFin-supervised). Cash deposits are protected up to €100,000 under the German deposit guarantee. Securities are held in custody at Citibank or HSBC, segregated from Trade Republic's own assets.

I wouldn't lose sleep over either.

## ETF Strategy Considerations for Dutch Tax Residents

Beyond which broker, which ETFs you choose makes a bigger difference to long-term outcomes than broker selection. Three things matter for Dutch tax residents:

**Accumulating vs. distributing.** Accumulating ETFs reinvest dividends internally — no annual dividend tax friction, more efficient compounding. Distributing ETFs pay dividends to your broker account, which then sit as cash until reinvested. For Dutch Box 3 taxpayers, accumulating is generally more efficient, especially for buy-and-hold strategies.

**UCITS-domiciled (Ireland or Luxembourg).** UCITS structure provides better treaty withholding tax treatment on US dividends than non-UCITS US-listed ETFs. Ireland-domiciled (the most common) gives you a 15% treaty rate on US dividend income vs. the 30% you'd face on a US-listed ETF.

**Currency:** While the underlying assets matter more than the trading currency (a EUR-listed S&P 500 ETF still owns USD-denominated stocks), trading in EUR avoids per-transaction FX fees on both DEGIRO and Trade Republic. The Vanguard FTSE All-World UCITS ETF (ticker VWCE) and iShares Core MSCI World UCITS ETF (IWDA / SWDA) are the two most-popular accumulating world ETFs for Dutch retail investors.

Both DEGIRO (Core Selection) and Trade Republic (savings plans) support both. So broker selection doesn't constrain ETF choice for the standard expat investor.

## What If You Want to Migrate Between Them

Migration paths in 2026:

**DEGIRO → Trade Republic:** Trade Republic accepts incoming portfolio transfers (free for receiving). You'll pay DEGIRO €10 per ISIN to transfer out. For 10 positions: €100 fee. Worth it only if Trade Republic offers materially better long-term economics.

**Trade Republic → DEGIRO:** No direct transfer pathway in 2026. You'd have to sell positions on Trade Republic and re-buy on DEGIRO, which creates a Box 3 reporting moment (cash on certain dates) and potential capital gains realization (though Box 3 is asset-based, so not capital-gains in the US sense — still complicates timing).

Practically: pick your primary platform deliberately and stay there. Migration friction means switching is rarely worth chasing small fee differences.

## Box 3 Reform and What It Means for Both Brokers

The Dutch Box 3 system is undergoing reform — the move from "presumed yield on assets" to "actual return-based" taxation. Implementation has been delayed multiple times; current target is 2027–2028 for full transition.

Implications for DEGIRO vs Trade Republic users:

- Both brokers will need to provide more detailed tax statements covering actual realized gains, dividends, and interest received (not just year-end balance).
- Trade Republic's English statements are likely to remain easier for non-Dutch-speaking expats.
- DEGIRO's Belastingdienst pre-fill integration may extend to actual-return reporting first.

For ongoing tax planning, it's worth holding broker accounts that produce clear, audit-friendly reports. Both currently meet that bar, but neither is optimized for the future "actual return" Box 3 regime yet.
