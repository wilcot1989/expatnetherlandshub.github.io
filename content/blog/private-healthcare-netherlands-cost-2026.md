A friend of mine — let's call her Diane — flew in from Boston last spring on a two-year executive assignment for a semiconductor company in Eindhoven. Her HR package included "international private health insurance, fully covered." She arrived, signed her rental contract, registered at the gemeente, and three weeks later got a letter from CAK telling her she was uninsured and owed a fine. She was furious. She had a Cigna Global policy on her laptop screen. How could she be uninsured?

That conversation — over very strong coffee at a café near Strijp-S — is why I'm writing this article. Because Diane is not the only one. Every month I get emails from highly skilled migrants, US executives on rotation, and trailing spouses who assume that paying €380 a month to Cigna or Aetna means they're done. They aren't. And the rules around private healthcare in the Netherlands are genuinely confusing, even for people who've lived here a decade.

## TL;DR — Quick answer

- **If you have a BSN and live in the Netherlands long-term, Dutch basisverzekering is mandatory.** Private international insurance like Cigna or Aetna does *not* replace it. You need both, or you risk fines from CAK (€496+).
- **Private international insurance makes sense as a top-up** for: private rooms, faster specialist access, global coverage when you travel, dental and vision packages, and English-language support.
- **Private-only is legal** if you're on assignment under 1 year, don't have a BSN yet, or qualify for a specific exemption (military, diplomatic, or posted-worker status).
- **Realistic 2026 monthly costs:**
  - Cigna Global: €180–€350/month (single, age 30–45)
  - Aetna International: €200–€400/month
  - Allianz Care: €150–€380/month
  - On top of Dutch basis (€155/month) if you're a resident
- **Zorgtoeslag (healthcare allowance)** covers up to €127/month of your *basisverzekering* costs in 2026 — but only on the Dutch policy, never on Cigna/Aetna.

If that already answers your question, skip to the [FAQ](#faq). If you want to actually understand what you're paying for, keep reading.

## Why this is more complicated than your HR rep made it sound

The Dutch system is built on the Zorgverzekeringswet (Zvw) of 2006. The rule is simple on paper: anyone who lives or works in the Netherlands and pays Dutch payroll tax (loonbelasting) is automatically subject to mandatory health insurance. You have to take out a Dutch basisverzekering within four months of registering at your gemeente — otherwise CAK starts the fining process.

That word "automatically" is what trips people up. Diane's Cigna policy was beautiful: €4 million lifetime cover, evacuation rights, private rooms in 190 countries. None of it satisfies the Zvw. Why? Because Dutch law requires *Dutch-licensed* insurers to provide the *basisverzekering*. Cigna Global is licensed in Belgium and the UK, not the Netherlands. It's not on the approved list.

There are three legal ways out:

1. **You're not a Dutch resident yet.** No BSN, no gemeente registration, no Dutch payroll — no obligation. SafetyWing or Cigna alone is fine during this gap. (Read [our guide on the first 30 days](/blog/mvv-visa-family-reunification-netherlands-2026/) for the timeline.)
2. **You're posted from outside the EU for under 12 months** and your employer maintains your home-country social security via a Certificate of Coverage (CoC) under a bilateral treaty. Diane's company *could* have filed this. They didn't.
3. **You qualify for the military or diplomatic exemption.** Rare. If you're reading this, it doesn't apply to you.

Everyone else needs Dutch basis. Then private becomes an *add-on*.

## What Dutch basisverzekering actually covers (and what it doesn't)

In 2026 the basic Dutch package costs around €155/month with a yearly own-risk (eigen risico) of €405. It covers:

- GP visits (huisarts) — no own-risk applied
- Hospital stays (shared rooms only — usually 4–6 beds)
- Most specialist care via referral
- Prescription medicines on the formulary
- Maternity care (basic — see [our baby cost guide](/blog/cost-of-having-a-baby-netherlands-2026/) for the gory details)
- Mental health (limited sessions)
- Emergency care worldwide up to Dutch tariff rates

What it *doesn't* cover and what most expats discover the hard way:

- Dental care above age 18 (you pay out of pocket)
- Physiotherapy beyond a strict list of chronic indications (see [our physio guide](/blog/physiotherapy-netherlands-expats-2026/))
- Glasses and contact lenses
- Private hospital rooms
- Alternative medicine (homeopathy, acupuncture)
- Cosmetic procedures
- Most fertility treatment beyond three IVF cycles
- Coverage when you're traveling longer than 12 months

This is the gap private international insurance fills. The question is whether it's worth €200–€400/month to close it.

## Aetna International — the corporate-package favourite

Aetna shows up most often when HR has negotiated a group plan. Their "Pioneer" and "Summit" products are typical for highly skilled migrants at multinationals (ASML, Booking.com, Philips, Shell).

Realistic 2026 monthly prices for an individual:

- **Pioneer 750** — €200–€240/month. Inpatient + day surgery, €750K cover.
- **Pioneer 5000** — €270–€320/month. Adds outpatient with €5K cap.
- **Summit** — €330–€400/month. Unlimited outpatient, full maternity, dental, vision.

Aetna's strengths:
- Excellent global network — 1.4 million providers, more than Cigna
- Direct billing with Dutch private clinics is smooth
- Mental health coverage is genuinely good (up to 30 sessions on Summit)

Where I see complaints:
- Slower claims processing than Cigna (4–6 weeks vs 2–3)
- Pre-authorisation requirements are strict — if you skip the call before a procedure, they can refuse
- Customer service quality varies wildly depending on which regional hub you're routed to

If your employer offers Aetna as part of your package and pays for it: take it. If you're buying it yourself, compare against Cigna and Allianz — Aetna is rarely the cheapest on the individual market.

## Allianz Care — the European-flavoured option

Allianz Care (formerly Allianz Worldwide Care) is underwritten from Ireland. It's the closest to a "European" feel — less American customer-service energy, more measured, slightly cheaper at entry tiers.

2026 pricing for individuals:

- **Care Plan / Inpatient** — €150–€190/month. Bare-bones, mostly catastrophic cover.
- **Care Plan + Outpatient** — €220–€280/month. Adds €5K outpatient.
- **Premium / Prestige** — €310–€380/month. Comprehensive, with dental and maternity (12-month wait).

Allianz strengths:
- Cheapest entry tier of the three
- Strong reputation in Europe — Dutch hospitals recognize them instantly
- Family plans are well-priced (often 15–20% cheaper than Cigna for spouse + 2 kids)
- Genuinely flexible deductible options (€0 to €5,000) to tune your monthly premium

Allianz weaknesses:
- The mid-tier outpatient cap fills up faster than competitors
- No US coverage by default — needs a pricey add-on
- App and member portal feel a generation behind Cigna's

If you have a family and want to keep monthly costs predictable, Allianz often wins the spreadsheet. For singles flying back and forth to the US, Cigna usually wins.

## When private actually makes sense (my honest take)

After three years of writing about this, here's my no-nonsense list of who *should* pay for private on top of basis:

1. **Frequent international travelers.** If you're back in the US, UK, or Asia more than 6 weeks a year, private gives you proper cover. Dutch basis caps at Dutch tariff rates abroad — a hospitalization in Boston could leave you with a €40,000 bill.
2. **You want a private room when admitted.** Dutch hospitals default to 4–6 bed wards. Private gets you a single room with a window, every time.
3. **You want fast specialist access.** Dutch GP referrals can take 6–12 weeks for non-urgent specialists. Private skips the queue via clinics like Bergman.
4. **You want comprehensive dental.** Dutch basis covers nothing above age 18. A private add-on saves you from €1,500+ in unexpected dental bills. See [our health insurance add-ons guide](/blog/best-health-insurance-add-ons-netherlands-2026/).
5. **You're under 12 months in the country, no BSN.** Use SafetyWing or Cigna alone — it's the cleanest legal option.

Who *probably should not* pay for private:

- You plan to stay 5+ years and integrate fully — Dutch basis + a strong aanvullende (supplementary) package from VGZ or Menzis covers 95% of needs cheaper. (See [VGZ review](/blog/vgz-health-insurance-review-expats-2026/) and [Menzis review](/blog/menzis-health-insurance-review-expats-2026/).)
- You're on a tight budget — that €300/month is better invested elsewhere.
- You're young, healthy, and don't travel much — the math rarely works.

## Zorgtoeslag — the part nobody tells expats

If your household income is under €38,520 (single) or €48,224 (couple) in 2026, you can apply for zorgtoeslag — a healthcare allowance from the Belastingdienst worth up to €127/month for singles and €243/month for couples.

Two crucial details:

1. It only applies to your *Dutch basisverzekering* premium. Cigna, Aetna, Allianz — none of that counts.
2. You apply via Mijn Toeslagen on belastingdienst.nl. You need your BSN and DigiD.

For a lot of trailing spouses on highly skilled migrant visas with no own income, this is real money — €1,524/year. I've seen people miss it for two years before someone tells them. Apply.

## The "I just arrived, what now" decision tree

I get this question weekly. Here's the order of operations:

1. **Arriving with a job offer + BSN coming within 4 months?**
   Buy SafetyWing or a 90-day Cigna policy for the gap. Once your BSN lands, take out Dutch basis from a Dutch insurer (Zilveren Kruis, VGZ, Menzis, CZ — see our reviews). Optionally layer private on top.
2. **Short assignment under 12 months, paid via home payroll?**
   Get a Certificate of Coverage from your home country's social security agency. Stick with international private only. Save your CoC for any CAK letter.
3. **Family joining via [MVV / family reunification](/blog/mvv-visa-family-reunification-netherlands-2026/)?**
   Each family member needs their own Dutch basis once they have a BSN. Children under 18 are free on Dutch basis (but not on Cigna — they're €80–€150/month each on private).
4. **Hiring an au pair?**
   The host family is legally responsible for the au pair's Dutch basisverzekering. See [our au pair cost guide](/blog/au-pair-netherlands-cost-2026/) — it's about €155/month extra on your budget.
5. **Kids going to international school?**
   School fees (often €18K–€28K/year — see [our international school guide](/blog/international-school-netherlands-cost-2026/)) are not insured by anyone. Don't confuse education benefits with health benefits.

## FAQ

- q: 'Can I use Cigna or Aetna instead of Dutch basisverzekering?'
  a: 'Only if you are not a Dutch resident (no BSN, no gemeente registration), or you have a Certificate of Coverage from your home country under a bilateral social security treaty. Otherwise Dutch basisverzekering is mandatory and private international insurance is layered on top.'

- q: 'How much does private healthcare cost in the Netherlands in 2026?'
  a: 'Realistic monthly costs for international private insurance are €150-€450 depending on insurer, tier, and age. Cigna ranges €180-€350, Aetna €200-€400, Allianz €150-€380. This is in addition to Dutch basisverzekering (around €155/month) if you are a resident.'

- q: 'Is zorgtoeslag available if I have private international insurance?'
  a: 'Zorgtoeslag only applies to your Dutch basisverzekering premium, not to Cigna, Aetna, or Allianz. You can claim up to €127/month (single) or €243/month (couple) in 2026 if your household income is below the threshold and you have a Dutch policy.'

- q: 'What happens if I do not take out Dutch insurance as a resident?'
  a: 'CAK (the Dutch healthcare administration office) sends warning letters after four months. If you still do not register, they impose a fine of around €496 and can enroll you automatically in a default policy at a higher rate. The fine is real and not negotiable.'

- q: 'Can my employer pay for private international insurance instead of Dutch basis?'
  a: 'Many employers do reimburse international policies as a benefit, but legally you still need Dutch basisverzekering if you are a Dutch resident with a BSN. The reimbursement typically covers the international top-up, not a replacement.'

- q: 'Which is best for families - Cigna, Aetna, or Allianz?'
  a: 'For families, Allianz Care often offers the best value because of structured family discounts (15-20% cheaper than Cigna for a spouse plus two kids). Cigna wins if you travel to the US frequently. Aetna is most common when offered through corporate group plans.'

- q: 'Do I need private insurance for pregnancy and childbirth?'
  a: 'No - Dutch basisverzekering covers maternity well, including kraamzorg (postnatal home care). Private insurance can add private room access and faster scans, but is not necessary. Read our full breakdown in our cost-of-having-a-baby guide for the actual numbers.'
