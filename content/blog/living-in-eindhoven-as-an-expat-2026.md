> **Quick answer:** Eindhoven is the most realistic Dutch city for tech expats in 2026. Rent is 25-35% lower than Amsterdam, English is the default working language at ASML, Philips, and most High Tech Campus companies, and you can cycle across the entire city in 25 minutes. The trade-off: smaller historic center, fewer weekend tourists, and a nightlife that wraps up earlier than you'd expect.

## Why expats choose Eindhoven in 2026

Eindhoven punches far above its weight. The city itself has about 245,000 inhabitants, but the Brainport region around it employs more than 165,000 people in technology, design, and advanced manufacturing. ASML alone has grown to over 23,000 employees in Veldhoven (the neighboring municipality, 15 minutes by bus from the center), and roughly 40% of its workforce is international.

The result is a city that feels much more globally connected than its size suggests. On a normal Wednesday morning in the coffee shop near my friend's apartment in Strijp-S, you'll hear English, Mandarin, Hindi, Polish, Italian, and Turkish before you hear Dutch. Restaurants have English menus by default. Supermarkets stock paneer, gochujang, and proper tortillas without you having to hunt.

The municipality has leaned into this. The [Expat Center South](https://www.eindhoven.nl/) on Vestdijk handles BSN registration, 30% ruling intake, and IND appointments in one building, usually in English, often within two weeks of arrival. If you've ever tried to register in Amsterdam, you'll appreciate what a difference that makes. For the full registration walkthrough, see my [expat BSN application guide for 2026](/blog/expat-bsn-application-guide-2026/).

The other quiet advantage is space. Eindhoven was bombed heavily in World War II and rebuilt with wider streets, more parks, and more parking than the canal cities. You can have a real garden here without earning a Randstad salary.

## The neighborhoods, ranked by who they're actually for

This is the section most generic city guides get wrong. They list every district with the same flat description. Here's how I actually think about Eindhoven's neighborhoods after recommending them to dozens of expats.

### Strijp-S: the obvious choice that's usually correct

Strijp-S is the redeveloped Philips industrial area northwest of the center. Old factory halls have been turned into lofts, a food hall (Strijp-S Food Tower), design studios, and a Saturday market. It's where most ASML and Philips engineers between 25 and 38 end up, especially singles and couples without kids.

The downside is that Strijp-S has become its own bubble. Rent has risen sharply — a new-build one-bedroom now goes for €1,500-€1,750 — and the area can feel quiet on a Sunday because most residents are out of town. The upside is the easiest social landing in the city. If you don't know anyone, move to Strijp-S for the first year and you will know people by month three.

### Centrum: convenience over character

The actual city center, around Markt and 18 Septemberplein, is the most convenient option if you don't have a bike yet and want to walk to everything. It's also the only part of Eindhoven that still has a real nightlife, mostly along Stratumseind (the longest pub street in the Netherlands).

I usually recommend Centrum for short stays of six months or less, or for people who genuinely love living above bars and shops. For a longer stay, Centrum's apartments tend to be older, smaller, and noisier than what you get for the same money in Strijp-S or Tongelre.

### Woensel: where the families go

Woensel is the big northern district, split into Woensel-Zuid (closer to the center, denser, more affordable) and Woensel-Noord (suburban, family-friendly). This is where expats with kids most often end up, because the housing stock includes proper terraced houses with gardens at prices that would be impossible in the Randstad.

Woensel-Zuid in particular has gentrified quietly. The area around Kruisstraat has become a Turkish-Moroccan food street that's genuinely good — better cheap food than anywhere in Amsterdam, in my opinion.

### Stratum: the underrated middle ground

Stratum sits south of the center and combines older brick houses with leafy streets and easy access to both the High Tech Campus and the train station. It's where I'd live if I were moving to Eindhoven tomorrow as a couple in our early thirties. Less hyped than Strijp-S, more characterful than Centrum, and the bike ride to the Campus is under 15 minutes.

### Tongelre: quiet, green, and forgotten by foreigners

East of the train tracks, Tongelre is a residential neighborhood with a village feel. Lots of 1930s brick houses, the Dommel river running through it, and excellent primary schools. Almost no expats live here, which can be a feature or a bug depending on your goals. If you want to actually integrate into a Dutch neighborhood and learn the language properly, Tongelre is perfect. If you want an instant expat social circle, it's not.

### Gestel: the family suburb with a tech tilt

Southwest of the center, Gestel is where you end up if you work at the High Tech Campus and have school-age kids. It's a 10-minute cycle to the Campus and a 12-minute cycle to the International School. Larger houses, calmer streets, and a noticeable jump in price compared to Woensel.

## High Tech Campus, ASML, and the Brainport ecosystem

The reason this city exists in its current form, economically, is the cluster of companies in the southern part of town. Here's how the geography actually works.

**High Tech Campus Eindhoven (HTCE)** is a 250-acre fenced research park about 6 km south of the city center. It hosts roughly 280 companies — Philips Research, NXP, IBM, Signify, Bosch, Lumileds, plus hundreds of startups in the Strip building. About 12,000 researchers work there. The Campus has its own restaurants, gyms, a kindergarten, and a hotel. The vibe is intentionally academic-meets-Silicon-Valley.

**ASML** is technically in Veldhoven, a separate municipality 6 km west of Eindhoven center. The main campus there is enormous and still growing — a new factory hall opens roughly every 18 months. ASML runs free shuttle buses from Eindhoven Centraal to Veldhoven, which most international employees use until they get a car or a proper bike.

**Brainport Industries Campus** in the northwest of the city hosts the supply chain companies that build for ASML and aerospace — Anteryon, KMWE, and dozens more. Less visible, equally important.

If your job is on the High Tech Campus, the practical advice is: live in Stratum, Gestel, or the southern edge of Centrum, and cycle. The Campus has 4,000 bike parking spots for a reason. If your job is at ASML in Veldhoven, you have two options: take the shuttle from Eindhoven Centraal and live in the city, or live in one of the smaller villages between Eindhoven and Veldhoven (Aalst, Waalre) for a quieter life and a shorter commute.

One thing the recruiters won't always tell you: ASML and many High Tech Campus companies expect engineers to be physically on site most of the week. Hybrid work in the Brainport region typically means 3-4 days in the office, not the 1-2 days that's normal in Amsterdam's finance sector. Factor that into your housing decision.

## Trains, commutes, and getting around the country

Eindhoven Centraal is the country's fifth-busiest station and has direct Intercity Direct connections to Amsterdam (1h22), Schiphol (1h15), Rotterdam (1h22 via Breda), and The Hague (1h45). Belgian border via Antwerp is 1 hour 30 minutes. Düsseldorf in Germany is 1 hour 50 minutes via the international train.

For commuting within the Netherlands, the OV-chipkaart system works the same as everywhere else. I cover the practical details in my [Dutch trains guide for expats](/blog/dutch-trains-guide-expats-2026/), including how to get an NS Flex subscription and which discounts actually save money.

Within Eindhoven, the bus network (run by Bravo) is decent but not great. Most locals just cycle. The flat terrain, wide bike lanes, and short distances mean a bike is the fastest way across town for almost any trip under 5 km.

## Cycling: the everyday miracle

If you've never lived in the Netherlands before, prepare for cycling to become 80% of your life. In Eindhoven this is even more true than in Amsterdam, because the bike infrastructure is newer and the city is small enough that everything is genuinely cycleable.

Buy a second-hand bike from a reputable shop (avoid Marktplaats unless you know what you're doing — stolen bikes are a real industry). Expect to pay €180-€280 for a solid second-hand city bike, or €450-€700 for a refurbished one with a warranty.

Get bike insurance. I cannot stress this enough. Bike theft in Eindhoven is lower than in Amsterdam, but it absolutely still happens, especially around the train station. My full breakdown is in [cycling Netherlands expat insurance and theft](/blog/cycling-netherlands-expat-insurance-theft-2026/), but the short version is: buy theft insurance from your home contents insurer for €4-€8 per month, and always use two locks.

## Schools, daycare, and education for expat families

The big one for families is the **International School Eindhoven (ISE)**, located in the southwest near Gestel. It runs primary and secondary programs in English following the International Baccalaureate framework. Fees in 2026 are roughly €6,500 per year for primary and €10,000-€12,500 for secondary, which is heavily subsidized compared to true international schools in other countries.

Waiting lists at ISE are real. Apply the moment you have a signed job offer, ideally six months before your move. If ISE is full, the backup options are the Dutch International Stream schools (taught bilingually in Dutch and English in mainstream Dutch schools), which work well for kids under 10 who'll pick up Dutch fast.

For daycare (kinderopvang), expect €9-€11 per hour in 2026, with significant tax-funded reimbursement (kinderopvangtoeslag) through the Belastingdienst if both parents work. Daycare centers near the High Tech Campus have long waiting lists; register the moment you have a positive pregnancy test, if you're already in the country.

Higher education in Eindhoven is anchored by **TU Eindhoven (TU/e)**, one of the top technical universities in Europe, plus **Fontys University of Applied Sciences** and the **Design Academy Eindhoven**. The student population gives the city more energy than its size would suggest.

## Healthcare: surprisingly good for a mid-size city

Eindhoven has two large hospitals: **Catharina Ziekenhuis** (specialized in cardiology) and **Máxima Medisch Centrum** (with locations in Eindhoven and Veldhoven). Both have English-speaking staff in most departments, though it's worth confirming in advance for specialist appointments.

Finding a huisarts (GP) who accepts new patients can be the hardest part of arrival. Some practices have closed lists, especially in popular neighborhoods. Ask your employer's HR or relocation team for recommendations; many High Tech Campus companies have arrangements with specific practices.

## Food, culture, and what actually happens on weekends

Eindhoven isn't going to wow you with historic architecture — the war saw to that. What it has instead is an unusually vibrant design and food culture for a city its size.

**Dutch Design Week** in October is the city's biggest cultural moment. For nine days, every empty industrial space becomes a gallery. Hotels book out months in advance.

**GLOW** in November turns the entire city center into a light art installation. Free, outdoor, and genuinely impressive.

**Stadswandelpark** and **Genneper Parken** are the two big green spaces. Genneper has a swimming pool, an ice rink, a sports complex, and a stretch of forest. Surprisingly good for the middle of a city.

Food-wise, the standouts are the Strijp-S food hall, the Kruisstraat in Woensel (Turkish, Moroccan, Surinamese), and a small but solid cluster of fine-dining restaurants near Wilhelminaplein. Coffee culture is strong, helped by the design crowd. Vegan options have multiplied in the past three years.

Nightlife is concentrated on Stratumseind (loud, young, and definitely Dutch), with quieter cocktail bars scattered through the center and Strijp-S. The honest truth is that if you're in your late twenties and used to Berlin or London nightlife, Eindhoven will feel modest. If you're in your thirties and want a glass of wine on a terrace, you'll be happy.

## The honest trade-offs versus Amsterdam, Rotterdam, and Utrecht

This is the section my friend Priya wishes she'd had two years ago. Let me be direct about what you give up by choosing Eindhoven.

**You lose:** The dense historic beauty of Amsterdam, the world-class museum density of the Randstad, the bigger international airport experience (Eindhoven Airport is small and mostly budget), the weekend tourist energy, and a deeper bench of high-end restaurants and bars. Dating, if you're single and looking for variety, is also a smaller pool.

**You gain:** Real money saved on rent — €500-€800 per month for an equivalent apartment, which over five years is a down payment on a house. A more international and less touristic vibe (Eindhoven is international but not a tourist city, which is a different and arguably better thing). Easier and faster gemeente appointments. Genuinely livable cycling without the canal-bridge chaos. More space, more parks, and a much shorter commute to almost any tech employer.

For the Amsterdam-versus-Rotterdam side of that question, my [cost of living Amsterdam vs Rotterdam](/blog/cost-of-living-amsterdam-vs-rotterdam-2026/) breakdown has the numbers.

My honest take: if your job is in tech and you'd be working at ASML, the High Tech Campus, NXP, or any Brainport company, Eindhoven is almost always the right call. If your job is in finance, media, or government, you'll be happier in Amsterdam, Rotterdam, or The Hague.

## Expat community and how to actually meet people

The community here is real but slightly hidden. The big nodes:

- **Eindhoven Expat Spouses Initiative (EESI):** if your partner moved for work and you're trying to land socially, start here.
- **Internations Eindhoven:** monthly events, mostly 30+, professional crowd.
- **The Hub Eindhoven:** coworking space that doubles as a community hub for entrepreneurs.
- **Strijp-S Food Tower:** turns into a Friday social spot for the Philips and ASML crowd around 5pm.
- **Meetup groups:** language exchange, board games, hiking, running. More active than you'd expect.

For ASML specifically, the internal social calendar is extensive. Don't skip the welcome events; that's where you meet the people who'll become your weekend cycling group.

Learning Dutch is optional for work and necessary for friendship with locals. I always tell people to start in month two with a serious A1 course (Berlitz, Regina Coeli, or the TU/e language center), then commit to one Dutch-only social activity per week from month six onward. Two years in, you'll be conversational.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is Eindhoven a good city for expats?**

Yes. Eindhoven has one of the highest concentrations of international workers in the Netherlands thanks to ASML, Philips, and the High Tech Campus. English is widely spoken at work, in supermarkets, and at most cafés, and rent is roughly 25-35% cheaper than Amsterdam for a comparable apartment.

**How much does it cost to rent in Eindhoven?**

In 2026, a one-bedroom apartment in central Eindhoven typically costs €1,250-€1,650 per month including service charges. A two-bedroom apartment in a family neighborhood like Tongelre or Woensel runs €1,500-€2,100. Furnished short-stay units add roughly €300-€500 on top.

**What is the High Tech Campus Eindhoven?**

The High Tech Campus is a 250-acre research park in the south of Eindhoven that hosts more than 280 tech companies and around 12,000 researchers. ASML, NXP, Philips Research, and IBM all have offices there, and it's often called "the smartest square kilometer in Europe."

**Is English widely spoken in Eindhoven?**

Yes, more so than in most Dutch cities outside Amsterdam. Roughly 17% of Eindhoven's population is international, and you can comfortably live, work, and socialize in English. Learning some Dutch still helps for paperwork at the gemeente and for building local friendships.

**How far is Eindhoven from Amsterdam?**

Eindhoven Centraal to Amsterdam Centraal is 1 hour 22 minutes by Intercity Direct, with trains running every 15-30 minutes during the day. By car it's about 1 hour 45 minutes outside rush hour. Schiphol Airport is reachable in 1 hour 15 minutes by train.
