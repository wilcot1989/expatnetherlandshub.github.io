## Can expats actually invest in the Netherlands?

Yes — and this was the first myth I had to unlearn. You do not need to be a Dutch citizen. You do not need a permanent residence permit. You do not even need to speak Dutch.

What you do need:

- A valid Dutch residence permit (any type)
- A registered Dutch address (in the BRP — municipality register)
- A Dutch or EU bank account
- A BSN (citizen service number)

That's it. Once you have those four things, you can open an investment account with any regulated platform in the Netherlands. Most platforms are fully available in English. The sign-up process is online and takes about fifteen minutes.

If you're still sorting out your first bank account, check my guide on [best banking options for expats in the Netherlands](/blog/best-banking-expats-netherlands-2026/) — you'll need that sorted before you can open an investment account.

## Box 3 tax: what expats actually need to know

This is where most of the confusion lies. The Netherlands does not tax your investment gains or dividends in the conventional sense. Instead, it taxes your wealth.

Here's how it works in 2026:

Every year, on 1 January, the Belastingdienst (Dutch tax authority) looks at your total net assets — savings accounts, investment portfolios, second properties, crypto holdings, minus debts. This is your Box 3 base.

The tax-free threshold in 2026 is **€57,000 per person** (€114,000 for fiscal partners — i.e. registered couples). If your total net assets are below that threshold, you pay zero Box 3 tax on your investments. Full stop.

If you're above the threshold, a deemed (fictional) return is applied to the excess. For 2026, the deemed return rate for investments is approximately 5.88%. That fictional return is then taxed at 36%.

So: if you hold €80,000 in investments (€23,000 above the threshold), the deemed return is approximately €1,352 (5.88% × €23,000), and you'd pay 36% of that = roughly €487 in Box 3 tax for the year. Regardless of whether your portfolio actually returned 5.88%.

**This means:**
- If your total assets stay below €57,000, you pay nothing extra
- The tax applies to the snapshot on 1 January — not to profits realised during the year
- You must declare all assets: Dutch and foreign

When I filed my first [Dutch tax return as an expat employee](/blog/tax-return-netherlands-employee-expats-2026/), Box 3 was the section I found most confusing. It's worth reading up on it before your first return.

### A note on the ongoing Box 3 legal situation

Dutch courts have ruled multiple times that the current Box 3 system can be unfair to taxpayers whose actual returns are lower than the deemed rate. The Belastingdienst has introduced some adjustments, but the system remains in flux. New legislation is expected to move toward taxing actual returns ("werkelijk rendement") by 2027 or 2028. For now, the deemed-return system still applies for 2026.

## The 30% ruling and investments: a common misconception

Many expats with the 30% ruling assume it exempts them from Dutch investment taxes. It doesn't — at least not automatically.

The 30% ruling reduces your Box 1 income (salary, freelance income) by 30%, giving you a significant income tax saving. It has no direct effect on Box 3.

However, there is an option called **partial non-residency (partieel buitenlandse belastingplicht / keuzerecht)**. If you elect this option, you are treated as a non-resident for Box 2 and Box 3 purposes. This means foreign-held savings and investments may not be subject to Dutch Box 3 tax.

The catch: this only applies to assets held outside the Netherlands. If your investments are in DEGIRO (a Dutch platform), they are within the Dutch tax net regardless of your partial non-residency status.

Whether the keuzerecht makes sense for you depends on how much you hold outside the Netherlands and whether it's worth the administrative complexity. For more detail on recent changes to the ruling, see my [30% ruling guide](/blog/30-percent-ruling-extension-2024-changes-2026/).

## Personal investing vs. your employer pension

When you work in the Netherlands, your employer contributes to a pension fund (pillar 2). This is separate from your personal investments and is generally not something you control directly — your employer chooses the fund and you contribute automatically.

If you're a long-term expat or planning to stay permanently, this is fine. If you might leave the Netherlands, it becomes more complicated: pension pots accrued in Dutch pension funds are often not easily transferred when you move countries. You may be able to claim a payout when you reach retirement age regardless of where you live, but the rules vary by fund.

Personal investments through DEGIRO are entirely portable. The account stays in your name, you can manage it from anywhere with an internet connection, and there's no exit tax on unrealised gains when you leave the Netherlands. This is one reason I started investing personally even though I also have a Dutch employer pension.

## Mistakes I made — and how to avoid them

### Mistake 1: leaving home-country investments untouched

When I moved to the Netherlands, I had some investments from my home country that I left as-is. I assumed they were outside the Dutch tax net. They weren't. All assets — Dutch and foreign — must be declared in Box 3 on your Dutch tax return. I had to go back and amend my return.

If you have investments in your home country, check your country's tax treaty with the Netherlands. In many cases, you won't be taxed twice (the treaty prevents double taxation), but you do have to declare everything.

### Mistake 2: buying US-listed ETFs

Early on I was tempted by US-listed ETFs (ticker: VT, SPY, etc.) because they're cheaper in terms of annual fees. What I didn't understand is that European platforms like DEGIRO cannot legally sell US-listed ETFs to EU retail investors due to MiFID II regulations. More importantly, for non-American expats, US-listed ETFs can create withholding tax complications. Stick to UCITS ETFs listed on European exchanges.

### Mistake 3: not thinking about dividend withholding tax

VWRL distributes dividends quarterly. Those dividends are subject to withholding tax in the source countries. On DEGIRO, this is handled automatically, but it's worth knowing that a 15% withholding tax on dividends from US-held stocks is generally applied before you receive them. If you prefer to avoid the complexity, IWDA (accumulating version, SWDA) reinvests dividends automatically and simplifies your tax tracking.

### Mistake 4: panic-selling during volatility

Not a Dutch-specific mistake, but worth naming. In early 2024 I watched my portfolio drop about 8% over three weeks and seriously considered selling. I didn't, and within two months it had recovered. Index fund investing works over years and decades, not weeks.

If you hold crypto, it's also subject to Box 3 wealth tax in the Netherlands. The Belastingdienst requires you to declare the value of your crypto holdings as of 1 January each year, using the exchange rate on that date.

## Investor protection: what happens if something goes wrong?

This was a concern of mine when I started. Is my money actually safe with DEGIRO?

The Netherlands has investor protection through the **Beleggerscompensatiestelsel (BCS)**, which covers up to €20,000 per person per institution if an investment firm fails. This is separate from the €100,000 deposit guarantee on bank accounts.

DEGIRO also holds client assets in a separate legal entity (Stichting DEGIRO), which means your investments are segregated from the company's own assets — so even if DEGIRO went bankrupt, your ETFs should be recoverable.

The <a href="https://www.afm.nl/en/consumers/vergunningen/beleggingsondernemingen" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">AFM (Authority for the Financial Markets)</a> publishes a register of regulated investment firms. DEGIRO appears on it. It's always worth checking that any platform you use is listed there before depositing money.
