## Why Student Banking Is Different

Three things make student banking different from regular expat banking.

**You arrive with no BSN.** This blocks traditional bank account opening for the first 2–6 weeks. You need a workable account before BSN arrives, otherwise you can't pay your first rent installment or top up your OV-chipkaart easily.

**You're price-sensitive.** Even €3/month account fees compound over a four-year degree to €144 — meaningful when your monthly budget is €1,100. The "free for students" offers actually matter.

**You receive money from weird places.** Tuition refunds from home, student loan transfers from your country's system, parental top-ups from non-EU IBANs, occasional Bol.com refunds, marketplace selling income. Multi-currency support and low international transfer costs matter more than for someone on a Dutch salary.

The student-optimal stack is a fast neobank for week one, a Dutch student account for payroll and DUO once BSN lands, and Wise on the side for international top-ups from family.

## What About Getting a Credit Card as a Student?

Most Dutch banks won't issue you an unsecured credit card without a Dutch credit history and stable income. Workarounds:

- **ABN AMRO Student Visa:** available with the student package, low limit, decent for building Dutch credit history
- **Revolut Premium / Metal:** technically prepaid but functions like a credit card for online bookings and car rentals
- **Bunq Travel Card:** debit-with-credit-features, accepted for most car rentals and hotels

Real credit cards typically come after graduation and your first stable contract. Don't optimize for this as a student.

## DUO and Banking — Do You Qualify?

If you're EU/EEA, you may qualify for DUO student finance after 1 year of part-time work in the Netherlands (16 hours/week). Non-EU students generally don't qualify. DUO pays into a Dutch IBAN — Bunq, ABN AMRO, ING all work. Set this up correctly when you apply, because changing it mid-cycle is annoying. ## Common Mistakes Students Make

**Waiting for BSN before opening any account.** This is the most common mistake. By the time BSN arrives 4 weeks in, you've missed your first rent direct debit and paid €40 in international card fees. Open Bunq week one.

**Using only Revolut/N26.** Both work for most things but the IBAN friction surfaces at the worst moment — your first paycheck rejected because HR's system flagged a Lithuanian or German IBAN. Always have a Dutch IBAN as backup (Bunq or ABN AMRO).

**Using your home country bank card for everything.** International card fees of 2–3% on every Albert Heijn purchase add up to hundreds of euros over a semester. Get a Dutch debit card week one.

**Not opening Wise.** Family money transfers from outside the EU through traditional banks cost 3–5% in hidden FX margins. Wise typically costs 0.5–0.8%. Over 4 years of family support this is hundreds saved.

**Paying for Bunq Premium when Easy Bank suffices.** Easy Bank is enough for student-level use. Premium is for people running businesses or maximizing savings rates with €10K+ balances.

## Internet Banking for International Students — Practical Tips

**Set up Tikkie immediately.** Dutch P2P payment standard. Roommates split rent and shared bills via Tikkie. ING and ABN AMRO native; Bunq and Revolut work too.

**Add Apple Pay / Google Pay.** Most Dutch terminals accept it now, faster than fishing out a card.

**Enable transaction notifications.** Bunq's are best, ABN AMRO's are good. You'll catch fraud or accidental double-charges immediately.

**Don't share PINs with roommates** — even for "shared" purchases, use Tikkie or a Bunq sub-account. Splitting a bank PIN with a roommate creates legal liability problems if anything goes missing.

**Save bank correspondence as PDFs.** Dutch tax filings (yes, even students sometimes need to file) ask for end-of-year balances. Easier to grab statements as you go than to dig them out at filing time.
