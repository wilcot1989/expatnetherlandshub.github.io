## First: Which Type of Expat Are You?

The right bank depends almost entirely on why you are here and how long you plan to stay. Before comparing products, answer these four questions:

**1. Do you have a Dutch BSN yet?**
If not, your options are limited to fintechs that do not require one: Wise, N26, Revolut (without NL IBAN on free plan). Traditional Dutch banks (ABN AMRO, ING, Rabobank, KNAB) will not open an account without a BSN.

**2. How long are you staying?**
- Under 6 months (short-stay): Fintech-only strategy — Wise + Revolut, no Dutch bank needed
- 6–24 months (medium-stay): Dutch bank required (landlord, employer, DigiD)
- Permanent/settling: Full Dutch banking relationship + investment account
- Freelancer/ZZP: Business account is separate from personal — both matter

**3. Are you American?**
US citizens in the Netherlands face FBAR filing obligations and PFIC tax traps. Not all banks will accept US persons due to FATCA compliance costs. This guide flags which ones do.

**4. Do you need to send money internationally every month?**
If you regularly transfer money between the Netherlands and a home country, you need a low-cost transfer solution regardless of which Dutch bank you use. The two best options are Wise and Revolut, and I cover both in detail.

### Freelancer / ZZP Expat

You are self-employed. You need to separate business income from personal income for VAT and income tax purposes. This matters even if you are a small one-person operation.

## The BSN Problem — And How to Solve It

The BSN (Burgerservicenummer — Dutch citizen service number) is the single biggest bureaucratic pain point for newly arrived expats. You need it for:

- Opening a traditional Dutch bank account
- Registering with a Dutch GP (huisarts)
- Getting a DigiD (digital ID for government services)
- Filing Dutch tax returns
- Starting formal employment contracts

**How long does a BSN take?** After you register at your gemeente (municipality), a BSN is typically issued within 1–5 working days for residents of Amsterdam and Rotterdam. In smaller municipalities, it can take 2–3 weeks. If you are registering at the IND (Immigration and Naturalisation Service) as a non-EU citizen, the timeline can stretch further.

**The gap strategy:**
1. **Arrive** → Immediately open a Wise account with your passport. Get NL IBAN. Use this for all payments while waiting.
2. **Municipality appointment** (usually within first 2 weeks) → Register address, request BSN
3. **BSN arrives** → Apply for ABN AMRO Expat Banking or bunq (both have fast digital onboarding)
4. **Transition** → Move recurring payments (salary, rent, utilities) to Dutch bank. Keep Wise for international transfers.

**Can you work without a BSN?** Technically no — employers should not put you on payroll without a BSN. In practice, some employers pay to a Wise NL IBAN for the first month while waiting for the BSN to arrive. Have this conversation with HR before day one.

## Common Banking Mistakes Expats Make

### Mistake 1: Using Your Home Country Bank for Dutch Transactions

Foreign transaction fees (1.5–3%) and ATM withdrawal fees (€2–5) add up fast. Even if you are only here for three months, open a Wise or N26 account on day one. It takes 10 minutes and costs nothing.

### Mistake 2: Sending International Transfers Through Your Dutch Bank

ABN AMRO, ING, and Rabobank all charge €15–25 per outbound international wire, plus an unfavourable exchange rate. Wise charges 0.4–0.7% with the mid-market rate. On a €12,000/year transfer habit (€1,000/month), you save €300–400/year by using Wise instead.

### Mistake 3: Waiting for the Perfect Account Before Receiving Salary

I have seen expats delay their first payslip because they were "still sorting out the bank account." Your employer needs a bank account number by the payroll deadline — usually 3–5 days before payday. Have a Wise NL IBAN ready before your first day at work, even if your Dutch bank account is not yet open.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring the Dutch Tax Obligation on Foreign Accounts

The Belastingdienst automatically receives data from Wise, Revolut, N26, and most EU-based financial institutions. They know you have these accounts. The Box 3 reporting is not optional. Below the €57,000 threshold per person, the tax impact is typically zero — but you still need to declare the accounts exist.

### Mistake 5: Assuming Joint Accounts Work Like Back Home

Dutch joint accounts (and/en rekening vs. of/of rekening) have legal implications. An "and/and" account requires both signatures for withdrawals; an "or/or" account allows either party to act alone. Rabobank and ABN AMRO offer both. Fintech providers (especially Wise) do not offer traditional joint accounts.

### Mistake 6: Not Checking If Your Employer Accepts Your IBAN

Before your first payday, confirm with HR that they can pay to your account's IBAN. A German IBAN (N26) or Lithuanian IBAN (Revolut standard) can trigger rejections in some older payroll systems. A Dutch NL IBAN (Wise, bunq, Dutch banks) avoids this problem entirely.

## Tax Implications for Expats

### Box 3 Wealth Tax

If you are a Dutch tax resident, all your bank balances — Dutch and foreign — are included in Box 3 (wealth tax) calculation. The 2026 figures:

- **Tax-free threshold:** €57,000 per person (€114,000 for fiscal partners)
- **Deemed return rate on savings:** approximately 1.44% for savings accounts
- **Deemed return rate on investments:** approximately 5.88%
- **Box 3 tax rate:** 36%

In practice, most expats with average balances pay minimal Box 3 tax on their bank accounts. The tax-free threshold covers most people's liquid savings. The impact is larger for investments and real estate.

### The 30% Ruling and Banking

If you have the 30% ruling, you can elect partial non-resident status (partieel buitenlandse belastingplicht) for Box 2 and Box 3. This means your bank accounts and investments held outside the Netherlands may be excluded from Box 3 entirely. This is a significant benefit if you have substantial savings in your home country.

**Action:** Discuss this election with a Dutch tax advisor before your first Dutch tax filing. The choice must be made on the annual return and affects all Box 3 assets.

### American Expats — FBAR and FATCA

US citizens in the Netherlands face additional obligations regardless of the Dutch tax treaty:

- **FBAR (FinCEN 114):** Must be filed if you have more than $10,000 in foreign accounts at any point during the year — even for a single day. Applies to Wise, N26, Revolut, Dutch bank accounts, everything.
- **FATCA Form 8938:** Required if foreign financial assets exceed $50,000 on the last day of the year ($100,000 for married filing jointly) or $75,000 at any point.
- **Penalties for non-filing:** Up to $10,000/year per unfiled FBAR, rising to $100,000 or 50% of account balance per violation for willful non-filing.

**Banks that accept US persons:** Interactive Brokers accepts US persons. Most Dutch and EU fintech banks do not, due to FATCA compliance costs. ABN AMRO and ING will accept US person accounts but require additional FATCA documentation. Bunq explicitly rejects US persons.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I open a Dutch bank account without a BSN?**
Yes — but only with fintechs. Wise gives you an NL IBAN immediately with no BSN required. Traditional Dutch banks require a BSN.

**Which bank is best for expats who just arrived?**
Wise on day one, then ABN AMRO Expat Banking or bunq once you have your BSN.

**Does Revolut work in the Netherlands?**
Yes, and it is excellent for travel and currency spending. Less ideal as a primary Dutch account because the free plan gives you a non-Dutch IBAN.

**Is a Dutch bank account required to rent an apartment?**
Most landlords require a Dutch NL IBAN. Wise's NL IBAN satisfies this. Revolut's standard European IBAN often does not.

**Do I need to declare my foreign bank accounts?**
Yes. Box 3 reporting is mandatory for Dutch tax residents. US citizens also face FBAR obligations.

**Can I use Apple Pay and Google Pay with Dutch banks?**
Yes with: Wise, Revolut, N26, bunq, ABN AMRO, ING, Rabobank, Triodos. No with: ASN Bank.

**What is the cheapest way to send money to the US?**
Wise (€4–6 on €1,000) or Revolut (free within plan limits on weekdays). Never use your Dutch bank for international wires.

**What is the monthly fee for Dutch banks in 2026?**
ING €3.35, ABN AMRO €5.40, Rabobank €3.70, KNAB €5, bunq €2.99, Triodos €6, ASN €1.20.

**Which bank has the best English customer service?**
ABN AMRO Expat Banking and bunq are the clear leaders for English support.

**Can freelancers and ZZP expats open a business bank account?**
Yes — bunq Easy Money is the fastest. KNAB ZZP and ABN AMRO are solid alternatives. You typically need KVK registration first.
