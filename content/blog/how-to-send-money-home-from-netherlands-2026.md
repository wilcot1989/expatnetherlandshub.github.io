## What Is SEPA and When Should You Use It?

SEPA stands for Single Euro Payments Area. It's a payment integration initiative that makes euro-denominated bank transfers across participating countries as fast and cheap as domestic ones.

The SEPA zone currently covers:

- All 27 EU member states
- Norway, Iceland, and Liechtenstein (the EEA countries outside the EU)
- Switzerland
- The United Kingdom — yes, even after Brexit, the UK remains part of SEPA

That last one surprises people. Despite Brexit, UK banks still participate in SEPA, which means sending euros from your Dutch ING or ABN AMRO account to a euro-denominated UK account is still a SEPA transfer — fast, free, and straightforward.

SEPA transfers use the standard IBAN and BIC (SWIFT code) format. Your Dutch bank account has an IBAN that starts with NL. Your recipient's account in France, Germany, Spain, or the UK will have its own country-specific IBAN. You enter those details, confirm, and the money typically arrives the same day or the next business day.

**When to use SEPA:**

- Sending euros to a family member in France, Germany, Belgium, or another eurozone country — free and fast
- Sending euros to a recipient in Switzerland or Norway (they hold EUR accounts)
- Sending from your Dutch account to a UK account that holds euros (many UK expats keep a euro account at Starling or Monzo)

**The limitation of SEPA:** it only works in euros. If you want to send money to a UK recipient's GBP account, or to anyone in a non-SEPA country (USA, India, Philippines, Nigeria — anywhere outside the zone), you're outside SEPA territory and you need a different approach.

Most Dutch banks — ING, ABN AMRO, Rabobank — offer SEPA transfers for free or a very small flat fee (often zero). Check your bank's fee schedule, but within the SEPA zone you're unlikely to pay more than €0.50 per transfer.

## What Is SWIFT and Why Is It So Expensive?

SWIFT is a messaging network — it stands for Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication. It's not a transfer system itself. It's the system banks use to send each other messages about transfers. When your Dutch bank sends money to a recipient in the Philippines, it doesn't send euros directly to that Filipino bank. Instead, it sends a SWIFT message through a chain of correspondent banks, each of which has accounts with each other and moves money along the chain.

That chain is where the costs come from.

Each correspondent bank in the chain can deduct a fee. If your Dutch bank sends via SWIFT and the message passes through two correspondent banks before reaching the destination bank, each of those banks may take a small cut — typically $10–20 or equivalent. You might send €1,000 and your recipient gets €970. No warning. No itemised breakdown.

On top of correspondent bank fees, Dutch banks apply their own foreign exchange margin. When ABN AMRO converts your euros to US dollars or Indian rupees, they don't use the mid-market rate. They use a rate that's typically 1.5–3% worse than the real rate. That margin is profit for the bank — and they don't advertise it.

Here's the actual math on a €1,000 transfer from the Netherlands to a USD account in the USA via ABN AMRO:

- ABN AMRO flat fee: €12.50
- FX margin (approximately 2% on the EUR/USD conversion): ~€20
- Potential correspondent bank deductions: €5–15
- **Total real cost: €37.50–€47.50** on a €1,000 transfer

[De Nederlandsche Bank (DNB)](https://www.dnb.nl/) — the Dutch central bank — requires banks to provide fee transparency for payment services, but "transparency" in practice often means the flat fee is disclosed and the FX margin is buried in the terms and conditions. Most people never see it.

## How to Send Money Home via Wise: Step by Step

I've done this hundreds of times. Here's exactly how it works.

**Step 1: Create your account**

Go to wise.com and sign up with your email. Wise will ask you to verify your identity — you'll need your passport or Dutch residence permit plus a selfie. Verification usually takes a few hours but can be instant. You only do this once.

**Step 2: Add a recipient**

Click "Send money" and add your recipient's bank details. The fields vary by country:
- UK: sort code + account number
- USA: routing number + account number (ACH)
- India: IFSC code + account number
- Philippines: bank name + account number
- EU countries: IBAN + BIC

Wise will guide you through exactly what's needed for each country. Save the recipient for future transfers — you won't have to enter details again.

**Step 3: Enter the amount**

You can enter either the amount you want to send in euros or the amount you want your recipient to receive in their currency. Wise shows you both in real time as you type.

**Step 4: Review everything before confirming**

Wise shows you:
- The mid-market exchange rate being used
- The exact fee in euros
- The amount your recipient will receive
- The estimated delivery time

The rate is locked the moment you confirm. Even if the transfer takes a day to arrive, your recipient gets the rate you saw.

**Step 5: Fund via SEPA transfer**

After confirming, Wise gives you their Dutch bank account details and a reference number. You go into your ING, ABN AMRO, or Rabobank online banking, create a SEPA transfer to Wise's account with that reference number, and send. The SEPA transfer from your Dutch bank to Wise is free.

Wise receives your euros, converts at the agreed rate, and sends to your recipient.

**Step 6: Track the transfer**

You'll get email notifications at each stage: money received by Wise, conversion complete, sent to recipient. You can also track in the Wise app.

**After your first transfer:** Wise has your details and your recipient's details saved. Future transfers take about 3 minutes. The SEPA funding transfer typically arrives at Wise within 1–2 hours once you're a verified, returning customer.

If you're sending more than €10,000, Wise may ask for source of funds documentation — a payslip or recent tax return usually suffices. This is standard anti-money-laundering compliance, not specific to Wise.

## Practical Timing Tips

**Don't send on Friday afternoon.** If you initiate a SWIFT transfer at 3pm on Friday, your Dutch bank may not process it until Monday. That's three extra days your money is sitting somewhere. With Wise, you're less affected by this because you're doing a SEPA transfer to Wise (which processes on weekdays), but initiating on Friday afternoon still means your funding transfer arrives at Wise on Monday morning.

**Wise locks your rate when you confirm.** This is genuinely useful. If you confirm a transfer at 9am on Wednesday and EUR/GBP moves against you by the time the money arrives Thursday afternoon, you still get the rate you confirmed. Your recipient's amount is guaranteed.

**Revolut weekend markup:** if you need to send on Saturday, you'll pay an extra 0.5% for major currencies, more for exotic ones. On €1,000 that's €5 extra — not catastrophic, but worth knowing.

**Large transfers:** for anything over €5,000, I check both Wise and Revolut on the day. The rates are usually very similar, but Wise occasionally has a slightly better rate on specific corridors. Check both before confirming.

## Is Sending Money Home Taxable in the Netherlands?

No. Transferring your own earned money from the Netherlands to a foreign bank account is not a taxable event.

Here's the logic: your income was taxed in the Netherlands when you earned it (Box 1 income tax). Moving that post-tax money from your Dutch account to your account in another country is simply moving your own funds. The Netherlands doesn't tax you on what you do with money you've already paid tax on.

Your Dutch [tax return](/blog/tax-return-netherlands-employee-expats-2026/) doesn't have a field for "money transferred abroad" because transfers aren't taxable events. You declare income, deductions, and assets — not what you did with your spending money.

The one nuance: if you're investing abroad and receiving income from those investments, that investment income may be taxable in the Netherlands depending on your situation. But regular remittances home — supporting family, paying a mortgage abroad, moving savings — are not taxable.

Cryptocurrency is more complicated. Crypto held by Dutch residents is a Box 3 asset. If you sell crypto to fund a transfer, that sale may trigger a Box 3 recalculation. For more on this, see my guide to [crypto tax in the Netherlands for expats](/blog/crypto-tax-netherlands-expats-2026/).
