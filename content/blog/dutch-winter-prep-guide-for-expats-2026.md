My first Dutch winter nearly broke me financially, and I am not exaggerating. I moved to Utrecht in late September a few years back, fresh from a sunnier country, and I genuinely thought I knew what cold weather was. I had lived through cold snaps abroad, I had skied, I had owned a proper winter coat. What I did not understand was the specific Dutch combination of damp, wind, darkness, and a heating system that runs on gas at a price that, in my first January, gave me a bill of 487 EUR for a single month in a two-bedroom apartment. I cried at my kitchen table holding that bill. Then I called my Dutch friend Marieke, who laughed for about thirty seconds and then taught me everything she knew. This guide is the version of that conversation I wish someone had handed me on day one.

## The Dutch climate reality nobody warns you about

Let me set the scene properly. From mid-October to mid-March, the Netherlands averages about six hours of usable daylight per day, and that is on a good day. On a bad day the sun rises around 8:45 and sets at 16:30 behind a thick grey ceiling of cloud, which means you arrive at work in the dark and you cycle home in the dark. The temperature is rarely shockingly cold by Canadian or Polish standards. It hovers between 2 and 7 degrees Celsius for most of the season. What makes it brutal is the humidity and the wind.

The humidity sits around 85 to 90 percent throughout winter. That damp air carries cold straight through any jacket that is not specifically wind-resistant, and it condenses on every cold surface in your apartment including your bedroom walls. The wind comes off the North Sea and across the polders without anything to slow it down, so a 4 degree day with 25 km/h wind on the bike path feels like minus 5. This is why locals do not stop cycling in winter. They have learned to dress for wet wind rather than for cold, and once I made that mental switch my whole winter changed.

Snow is rare in the Randstad. I see real snow that sticks maybe two or three days per year in Utrecht, and most of the country is similar. Frozen canals are a national obsession but they only freeze hard enough for skating about once every five to seven years now. What you actually get is freezing rain, sleet, and the kind of mist that soaks you in ten minutes without ever feeling like proper rain. Mentally preparing for grey wet darkness rather than postcard snow is step one.

## Energy contracts: the single biggest financial decision you will make this winter

I am going to be direct here because nobody else was direct with me. Your energy contract is the largest variable cost in your Dutch winter. The difference between a good contract and a bad one is easily 1500 to 3000 EUR per year for a normal apartment. Get this right and the rest of winter feels manageable. Get it wrong and you will be eating rice and beans by February.

There are three main types of contract. A vaste contract locks your rate per kWh of electricity and per m3 of gas for a fixed period, usually one, two, or three years. A variabel contract changes every month or every quarter based on market prices. A dynamisch contract changes every hour based on the actual spot market, which only makes sense if you have solar panels, a battery, or an EV and you can shift your usage. For 99 percent of expat households reading this, the choice is between vaste and variabel.

In late 2025 and going into 2026, I have been recommending vaste contracts to almost everyone. Wholesale gas prices have stabilized but the geopolitical situation remains volatile enough that I do not trust variabel to stay friendly through a cold snap. A two-year vaste contract at roughly 0.28 to 0.32 EUR per kWh for electricity and 1.15 to 1.30 EUR per m3 for gas is the range I consider acceptable. Anything above 1.50 EUR per m3 of gas is overpriced for the current market.

### Vaste vs variabel: the mental math

The argument for variabel is that gas and electricity prices have been falling from their 2022 peak and might keep falling. The argument for vaste is that you sleep at night knowing what your bill will be. In my experience expats who are new to the country massively benefit from vaste for the first two or three winters because you do not yet have the financial buffer or the local knowledge to absorb a sudden price spike. After you have lived through a winter or two and built up some euros in savings, you can think about variabel.

There is one specific trap I want to flag. Some providers offer a very low introductory variabel rate that resets after three or six months. I had a colleague at my old job sign one of these in October, and by January his unit rate had doubled. Always read the small print on rate guarantees, and always check whether the kortingen are one-time or recurring.

## Damp and mold: the silent winter problem

This is the section I most wish someone had given me before my first winter. Dutch houses, especially anything built before the 1990s or rented out without proper renovation, have a serious damp problem. The walls were built to breathe in a different climate, the windows are often single or low-grade double glazing, and modern lifestyles with showers and cooking and drying laundry indoors put far more moisture into the air than the building can handle.

The result is condensation. You will see it on bedroom windows first, beading up overnight and dripping onto the windowsill. Then you will start to see black spots in the corners near the ceiling, behind curtains, behind the bed, and around window frames. By February if you have done nothing, you might find actual fuzzy mold growing on the walls, your clothes in the wardrobe might smell musty, and you could be developing a winter cough that you cannot shake.

### My three rules for fighting damp

First rule: ventilate every single room for at least ten minutes every day, even when it is freezing outside. Open the window wide, close the door to the room, let cold dry air replace warm wet air. Then close the window and let the heating warm the room back up. The Dutch call this luchten and they do it religiously. Yes, you lose a little heat. No, it does not significantly increase your gas bill because dry air heats much faster than wet air.

Second rule: never dry laundry indoors without a dehumidifier running. A single load of wet washing releases roughly two liters of water into the air over twelve hours. Buy a dehumidifier, ideally one with a hygrostat that switches off automatically when humidity drops below 60 percent. A decent unit costs 150 to 250 EUR and runs you about 0.20 EUR per hour in electricity. Far cheaper than mold damage to your deposit.

Third rule: never let humidity in any room exceed 65 percent for more than a few hours. Buy a cheap hygrometer for every bedroom, around 8 EUR each on bol.com or any DIY store. Check them daily for the first month. If you are consistently above 65, you need more ventilation or a dehumidifier. If you are below 45, the air is too dry for healthy sleep and you can run a humidifier.

If you are renting and you find serious mold, document it with photos, email your verhuurder in writing, and reference Dutch tenancy law which makes the landlord responsible for serious damp problems that are caused by building defects rather than tenant behavior. The huurcommissie is the body that handles disputes if your landlord refuses to act. ## Winter clothing: dress for wet wind, not arctic cold

I made every mistake possible in my first winter. I wore a huge down jacket that turned into a wet sponge in the rain. I wore cotton socks that froze on the bike. I tried to use an umbrella in 40 km/h wind. By December I had given up and was taking the tram everywhere, which cost me a fortune and made me miserable.

Then a Dutch friend took me to a real outdoor shop and explained the layering system. The bottom layer is merino wool or a synthetic base. The middle layer is fleece or a light synthetic puffer. The outer layer is a waterproof, windproof shell with proper seam sealing. This sounds expensive but a full setup for around 300 EUR will outlast most cheap coats and keep you genuinely dry and warm for years.

The specific items I cannot live without through Dutch winter: a waterproof shell jacket with a hood that fits over a helmet, gloves rated for at least 5 degrees Celsius (I use cycling-specific ones with windproof fabric on the back of the hand), a buff or neck gaiter to cover the gap between coat and helmet, waterproof shoe covers or proper waterproof boots, and merino wool socks because cotton kills feet on long bike rides. ## Sinterklaas and Kerst: surviving the feestdagen

The Dutch holiday season is its own special experience and very different from what most expats grow up with. Sinterklaas falls on 5 December and is technically the biggest gift-giving day, especially for families with children. Christmas (Kerst) is celebrated on both 25 and 26 December, with the second day called Tweede Kerstdag and treated as a full holiday. New Year (Oud en Nieuw) is firework chaos that you have to experience to understand.

For expats, the practical things to know: shops close completely on 25 and 26 December and most close early on 24 December. Supermarkets are closed on both Christmas days in 2026 except for a few small Albert Heijn To Go locations. Stock up on groceries by 23 December at the latest. Banks process transfers slowly between 24 December and 2 January, so if you have rent due on 1 January, send it by 22 December to be safe. The same applies to any large transfers in or out of NL, and my [best banking expats Netherlands 2026](/blog/best-banking-expats-netherlands-2026/) guide explains how the SEPA cutoff days work in practice.

Sinterklaas as a tradition is something I have come to genuinely love. The gezellig atmosphere of the avond on 5 December, sitting around with family or friends, reading silly poems (gedichten), and exchanging small wrapped gifts (kadootjes) with handmade wrapping (surprises) is unlike anything I grew up with. If you are invited to a Dutch family Sinterklaas, accept and prepare a gedicht for whoever you are assigned. It does not have to be good. It has to be earnest and a little funny. The Dutch will love you for trying.

Pepernoten and kruidnoten flood every shop from late September. Speculaas, banketstaaf, and chocoladeletters are everywhere. Buy them, eat them, do not feel guilty. Winter is for warm spice cookies and hot chocolate.

For Christmas itself, the Dutch tradition is much quieter than the Anglo-American version. Families gather for long meals, often a gourmet setup with a tabletop grill, and exchange small gifts if any. There is no shopping frenzy on 26 December because the shops are closed. It is genuinely a rest period and I have come to appreciate it deeply.

## Dark months survival: vitamin D and SAD

The single hardest thing about Dutch winter, harder than the cost and harder than the damp, is the darkness. From early November through mid-February you are simply not getting enough light. I started showing classic seasonal affective disorder symptoms in my second winter: low energy, oversleeping, craving carbs, struggling to concentrate at work, and a general grey mood that did not lift until March. My GP was helpful and direct about it.

The three interventions that worked for me, in order of cost effectiveness: vitamin D supplementation at 1000 to 2000 IU daily from October through April, since dark skin and indoor lifestyles make deficiency almost universal among Dutch winter residents; a 10000 lux daylight lamp used for 20 to 30 minutes within the first hour of waking, costing around 80 to 150 EUR for a decent unit; and outdoor walks at lunchtime even on grey days, because the cloudy Dutch sky still puts out far more lux than any indoor light.

If your symptoms are serious, talk to your huisarts. Dutch GPs take SAD seriously and your basic health insurance covers consultation. ## Practical winter checklist

Here is what I do every year around mid-October to prepare. First, I review my energy contract and switch if my current rate is more than 10 percent above the market. Second, I service my CV ketel if it has not been done in 18 months, around 100 EUR through any local installateur. Third, I bleed the radiators using a small key from the bouwmarkt to remove trapped air, which improves heating efficiency dramatically. Fourth, I check window seals and re-tape any gaps with foam strip. Fifth, I buy hygrometers for every bedroom if I do not already have them. Sixth, I dig out the winter wardrobe and order any replacement gloves or shoe covers before the first cold week, because everything sells out by November.

For checking whether your current energy provider is treating you fairly, the [Consumentenbond](https://www.consumentenbond.nl/energie) runs an excellent independent comparison and they consistently call out providers that overcharge. Combined with the regulator ACM, you have two trusted Dutch sources to verify any quote.
