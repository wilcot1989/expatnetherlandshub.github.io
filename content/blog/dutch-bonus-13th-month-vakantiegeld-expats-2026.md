When my first vakantiegeld arrived on my Dutch payslip in May 2018, I thought there had been a mistake. My salary was suddenly EUR 2,800 higher than usual, and the payslip itemisation looked wrong. It took a colleague's explanation over coffee — and an hour reading my CAO — to understand what I was actually being paid, why, and how it would be taxed.

Most expats arriving in the Netherlands encounter the same confusion. Dutch payroll is structured very differently from US, UK, or German equivalents. The base salary on your contract is not the full picture. Vakantiegeld, 13th month, performance bonuses, and various allowances each have their own rules, timing, and tax treatment. ## What Your Dutch "Salary" Actually Means

When a Dutch employer offers "EUR 60,000 per year", what you receive is rarely just EUR 5,000 per month for 12 months. The structure typically includes:

- **Base salary**: the EUR 60,000, paid in 12 monthly instalments
- **Vakantiegeld (holiday allowance)**: 8 percent extra, EUR 4,800, paid May or June as a lump sum
- **Possibly 13th month**: additional one month's salary, EUR 5,000, paid November or December
- **Possibly performance bonus**: variable, based on personal and company performance
- **Possibly other allowances**: travel, working from home, lunch, internet — all itemised separately

So a "EUR 60,000 salary" with full benefits may actually total EUR 72,000+ gross per year, paid across the calendar with peaks in May and December.

This is critical when negotiating salary, comparing offers, or budgeting cash flow. A "EUR 70,000 base" without 13th month can be worth less than a "EUR 65,000 base" with 13th month and performance bonus.

## Vakantiegeld: The Statutory 8 Percent

### How It Works

Dutch employment law (Wet Minimumloon en Minimumvakantiebijslag) requires every employer to pay at least 8 percent of gross annual salary as vakantiegeld. This is on top of base salary.

The accrual period is June 1 to May 31. So vakantiegeld earned during your work from June 2025 through May 2026 is paid in May or June 2026.

### When You Get It

- **Most common**: lump sum in May, often dated May 25 or month-end
- **Less common**: split between May and June
- **Sometimes**: monthly, 1/12 included in each regular salary

The lump-sum pattern dominates because it aligns with summer holiday planning. Many expats budget on this assumption.

### How Much

8 percent of your gross annual salary is the legal minimum. Most employers stick to exactly 8 percent. Some CAOs (collective labour agreements) specify higher percentages — for example healthcare CAOs sometimes have 8.33 percent.

For a EUR 60,000 base salary: EUR 4,800 gross vakantiegeld.

### Tax Treatment

This is where many expats get a smaller net than expected. Vakantiegeld is taxed at the **bijzonder tarief** — a special rate calculated on your full annualised income, not the average.

For most expats earning EUR 50,000 to EUR 90,000, the bijzonder tarief in 2026 falls in the 38 to 50 percent range. So a EUR 4,800 gross vakantiegeld might net only EUR 2,400 to EUR 3,000.

The system reconciles in your annual tax return — if too much or too little was withheld, you pay or receive the difference.

For 30 percent ruling holders: only 70 percent of gross is taxed, meaning vakantiegeld effectively benefits from the same exemption. See my [30 percent ruling extension changes](/blog/30-percent-ruling-extension-2024-changes-2026.md) for context.

### When You Leave a Job

If you leave employment mid-year, your final salary includes accrued unpaid vakantiegeld up to the leaving date. Verify this on your final payslip — it is occasionally forgotten or incorrectly calculated, particularly if you have been at the company under a year.

## The 13th Month: Optional but Common

### How It Works

A 13th month (dertiende maand) is an additional month's gross salary paid at year-end. Unlike vakantiegeld it is NOT mandatory — it is contractually agreed.

### Who Has It

Roughly 30 to 40 percent of Dutch employees in 2026. Common in:

- Banking, insurance, financial services
- Government and semi-public sector
- Large corporates (Heineken, Philips, ASML, Shell)
- Some IT consultancies
- Most CAO-bound roles in healthcare, retail, transport

Less common in:

- Tech startups (often use higher base + equity instead)
- Small businesses
- Self-employed (you set your own structure)
- Many expat-targeted roles in international firms (which sometimes flatten total comp into base)

When evaluating a job offer, always ask: "is there a 13th month or year-end bonus?" The answer materially affects total comp.

### When It Is Paid

- Most common: November (with November or December salary)
- Sometimes: December
- Rarely: split across two payments

The November timing aligns with Sinterklaas / Christmas spending — culturally, the 13th month is informally referred to as "Sinterklaasgeld" in some industries.

### How Much

Typically equal to one month's gross salary. Some CAOs specify a fixed percentage (e.g., 8.33 percent of annual salary, equivalent to one month). Some employers cap at a number that represents 13/12 of a "13-month year".

Pro-rated if you have not worked the full year.

### Tax Treatment

Same as vakantiegeld — bijzonder tarief. So a EUR 5,000 13th month nets approximately EUR 2,500 to EUR 3,100 for most expats.

This is why net 13th month often feels smaller than expected — at higher marginal rates the tax bite is significant.

## Performance Bonuses

### Common Structures

- **Annual bonus**: 5-30 percent of base salary, based on personal performance and company results, paid in February or March after prior-year close
- **Quarterly bonus**: smaller, typically sales-driven roles, paid quarterly
- **Sign-on bonus**: one-off at hire, often with claw-back if you leave within 12-24 months
- **Retention bonus**: paid for staying through specific milestones
- **Stock options or RSUs**: increasingly common in tech and finance, vesting over 3-4 years

### Sector Norms (2026)

| Sector | Typical bonus range |
| --- | --- |
| Government / public | 0-5 percent |
| Education / non-profit | 0-5 percent |

| Retail / hospitality | 0-10 percent |
| Tech (mid-market) | 5-15 percent |

| Sales (with commission) | 20-50 percent |

These are highly approximate. Negotiate based on role, level, and individual employer norms.

### Tax Treatment

Performance bonuses are taxed at bijzonder tarief, exactly like vakantiegeld and 13th month. For high earners with bonuses pushing total income above EUR 75,000+, the marginal bijzonder rate can reach 49.5 percent.

## Other Common Allowances

### Reiskostenvergoeding (Travel Allowance)

If you commute, your employer may pay a tax-free allowance up to EUR 0.23 per kilometre (2026 maximum). For a 30 km daily commute, that is EUR 6.90 per day x ~210 working days = EUR 1,449 per year tax-free.

### Thuiswerkvergoeding (Working from Home Allowance)

For days worked from home, employers can pay a tax-free EUR 2.35 per day (2026 maximum). Often combined with travel allowance for hybrid workers.

### Lunch / Maaltijdvergoeding

Some employers provide free lunch or a tax-free lunch allowance. Common in larger companies with on-site canteens.

### Internet and Phone

Tax-free reimbursement up to specific thresholds for work-related home internet and mobile phone usage. Typical EUR 25 to EUR 60 per month combined.

### Pension Contribution

Most Dutch employers contribute 10-25 percent of salary to a pension fund (collectief pensioen). This is genuine additional compensation that does not appear in your monthly payslip but is real long-term value.

## How to Read Your Loonstrook (Payslip)

Your monthly payslip itemises:

- **Brutoloon** (gross salary): your base
- **Vakantiegeld**: separately accrued, paid May (line item shown but not paid until May lump sum, unless monthly)
- **Loonheffing** (income tax + social security withholding)
- **Pensioenpremie** (pension contribution, your share)
- **Other deductions** (extras like company bike scheme, parking)
- **Nettoloon** (take-home pay)

When vakantiegeld or 13th month is paid, an additional gross amount appears, with corresponding higher tax line.

## Negotiating Salary in the Netherlands

When you receive a job offer, evaluate total annual compensation:

- Base x 12 months
- + 8 percent vakantiegeld
- + Possibly 1 month for 13th month
- + Performance bonus (typical or guaranteed?)
- + Pension employer contribution (real value)
- + Allowances (travel, work from home, lunch, internet, phone)
- + Equity if any
- + Other benefits (health insurance discount, training budget, lease car)

A "EUR 60,000 base" with 13th month, 15 percent bonus, full pension, and benefits totals EUR 78,000 to EUR 85,000 in real annual comp. The same "EUR 70,000 base" without 13th month and 5 percent bonus totals EUR 78,000 to EUR 80,000.

Always model this before negotiating. Many expats accept lower total comp because they fixated on base.

## Common Mistakes Expats Make

### Mistake 1: Spending the Net As If It Will Repeat

After your first vakantiegeld, treating it as recurring income is the most common mistake. It is annual, not monthly. Build the budget on base salary alone; treat vakantiegeld and bonuses as separately allocated.

### Mistake 2: Not Asking About 13th Month at Negotiation

If you do not ask, the offer might not include it. Some employers default to including; some do not. The question is free.

### Mistake 3: Forgetting to Reconcile in Tax Return

If your bijzonder tarief withholding was wrong, you may be owed money. File the annual tax return — it is the chance to fix it. See [Dutch tax return for employees](/blog/tax-return-netherlands-employee-expats-2026/).

### Mistake 4: Letting Bonus Sit in Current Account

A EUR 10,000 bonus parked in a 0 percent current account loses purchasing power. Move it to a savings account, investment platform, or specific goal fund within 30 days.

## Special Situations

### Part-Time Work

Vakantiegeld, 13th month, and bonuses pro-rate by FTE (full-time equivalent). A 0.8 FTE employee receives 80 percent of all components.

### Multiple Jobs

Each employer pays vakantiegeld and (if applicable) 13th month separately. Bijzonder tarief is calculated based on each employer's salary independently — meaning total tax may be under-withheld if multiple jobs combined push you to a higher bracket. Annual tax return reconciles.

### Joining Mid-Year

You receive pro-rated vakantiegeld for the months from start date to end of accrual period (May 31). The May lump sum reflects only the period you have actually worked, not a full year.

### Leaving Mid-Year

Final salary includes accrued unpaid vakantiegeld and pro-rated 13th month if applicable. Verify carefully on your final payslip.

### ZZP / Freelance

Self-employed do not have vakantiegeld or 13th month — these are employer concepts. As ZZP you build holiday savings and year-end cushions yourself. See my [ZZP registration guide](/blog/zzp-registration-netherlands-step-by-step-2026/).

## Sector-Specific Notes

### Tech

Tech roles in the Netherlands often have higher base salaries but smaller variable comp and fewer 13th month structures. Equity / RSUs increasingly common at international tech firms. Dutch-only tech firms typically follow more conventional Dutch comp structures.

### Finance / Banking

Finance roles have the most generous variable comp. Bonus caps under EU regulations (variable cannot exceed 100-200 percent of base for risk-takers) but mid-level roles routinely see 20-50 percent total comp variability.

### Government / Public

Solid base, full vakantiegeld, often 13th month or "eindejaarsuitkering" (end of year payment), modest bonuses. Predictable and stable.

### Consulting

Mid-tier 15-25 percent bonus; top-tier 25-50 percent. Long hours expected as part of total package.

### Healthcare

CAO-bound. Vakantiegeld at minimum, sometimes additional eindejaarsuitkering. Bonuses rare and small. Stability over upside.

### Startups

Highly variable. Often equity-heavy with modest base and minimal 13th month. Be careful evaluating "future value" of equity at face value.

## Final Thought

Dutch compensation structure rewards understanding it. A salary that looks "normal" on paper can be 15-25 percent better or worse in real annual cash than a similar headline figure depending on vakantiegeld treatment, 13th month presence, bonus structure, and benefits.

When evaluating offers, when negotiating, and when planning your year — model the full picture. The May lump sum and December bonus are not surprises; they are predictable, plannable income that expats who understand the system manage effectively and those who do not tend to spend reactively.
