## How the Dutch switch window actually works

The switch mechanism is the same every year and it confuses almost every expat I coach in their first winter here. There are three dates that matter.

The first is **12 November**. Around this date every insurer is legally required to publish the premium for the coming year. Some announce on Prinsjesdag in September, but the official campaign starts mid-November. From this point onwards you can compare prices for the new year.

The second is **31 December**. This is the deadline to cancel your current policy. If you do not formally cancel by midnight on 31 December, you are auto-renewed for another calendar year at whatever new premium your insurer set. There is no grace period for cancellation. I once had a client miss it by two days because she thought "before 1 January" meant "in the first week of January". It does not.

The third is **1 February**. This is the deadline to have a new policy active. In practice your new insurer will backdate coverage to 1 January if you sign up between 1 and 31 January, but you should aim to have everything signed before New Year's Eve to avoid any administrative confusion.

The clever part of the Dutch system is that you do not normally need to cancel your old policy yourself. If you take out a new policy through a comparison site or directly with a new insurer before 31 December, **the new insurer cancels the old one for you** under the opzegservice arrangement. This is invisible to most people and it is the single reason I recommend doing the switch online rather than via phone or paper letter.

## What changes for 2027

Every year the Dutch government adjusts three numbers that affect your wallet: the verplicht eigen risico, the average basispremie, and the zorgtoeslag thresholds. Here is what I expect for 2027 based on the autumn 2026 announcements and the trajectory of the past five years.

**Verplicht eigen risico.** This is the mandatory annual deductible. In 2026 it sits at €385, where it has been frozen since 2016. The current coalition has signalled it will rise modestly for 2027 — somewhere between €400 and €425 is the consensus among Dutch health economists. Whatever the number, it applies to almost all care except the huisarts (GP), maternity care, and care for children under 18. If you barely use healthcare, you can voluntarily raise your eigen risico up to €885 and shave around €25 per month off your premium. I do this myself.

**Basispremie trends.** The average monthly premium for the basisverzekering crept from €137 in 2023 to roughly €149 in 2026. For 2027 I expect the average to land around €155 to €162, depending on how insurers absorb rising hospital costs and salary agreements in the care sector. The spread between cheapest and most expensive will be about €25 per month, which over a year is a non-trivial €300.

**Zorgtoeslag 2027.** The healthcare allowance is a means-tested subsidy. In 2026 the maximum for a single person was around €127 per month with an income ceiling near €38,500. For 2027 expect both the maximum and the ceiling to rise slightly with inflation. If you earn under that ceiling and are a registered resident, apply through the Belastingdienst — most expats forget this exists. Full official information lives on [Rijksoverheid](https://www.rijksoverheid.nl/onderwerpen/zorgverzekering).

**Aanvullende verzekering.** Supplementary policies — for dentist, physio, alternative medicine, glasses — are not regulated like the basisverzekering. Insurers can refuse you, charge what they like, and change terms annually. For 2027 I expect another 5 to 8 percent increase on most aanvullende packages. Whether one is worth it depends entirely on your usage pattern, which I unpack in [my supplemental health insurance guide for expats](/blog/aanvullende-supplemental-health-insurance-expats-2026/).

## Aanvullende verzekering in 2027: who actually needs it

The aanvullende verzekering is where insurers make most of their margin, and where expats waste most of their money. Here is my honest framework.

**Skip aanvullende entirely if:** you are healthy, have good teeth, no glasses, no physio history, and no plans to start a family in the coming year. The basisverzekering covers you for everything serious. The maths usually does not work out — paying €25 a month for an aanvullende that you use for €100 of physio per year is a €200 loss.

**Buy a modest aanvullende if:** you visit the dentist regularly, wear glasses, or do an active sport that occasionally needs physio. A middle-tier package at €15 to €20 per month tends to break even or save a small amount over a year.

**Buy a comprehensive aanvullende if:** you have a chronic condition that requires physio, regular alternative medicine, or you are pregnant or planning to become pregnant. The kraampakket and maternity coverage in some packages alone justifies the cost.

## The four switch mistakes I see every January

In five years of coaching expats through their first Dutch winter, I see the same four mistakes on repeat. None are catastrophic, but together they cost the average expat €200 to €500 per year.

**Mistake one: missing the 31 December cancellation deadline.** This is the big one. If you do not have a new policy signed by 31 December or a formal cancellation letter sent to your old insurer, you are auto-renewed at the new premium for the entire year. Some clients lose €300 a year to this single oversight. Set a calendar reminder for 15 December, not 30 December — give yourself a buffer.

**Mistake two: cancelling without signing a replacement.** A small but real risk. If you cancel your current policy in writing but do not sign a new one before 31 December, you can end up with a coverage gap. The opzegservice prevents this when you sign through Independer or a new insurer first, but if you cancel manually you take the risk yourself. Always sign the new one first.

**Mistake three: comparing on premium alone.** The cheapest budgetpolis is not the best value if your local hospital is not in-network. I have had clients save €15 a month, then face a €600 out-of-pocket bill for treatment at a non-contracted clinic. Always cross-check the network.

**Mistake four: ignoring the aanvullende renewal trap.** Aanvullende verzekeringen do not auto-cancel when you switch basisverzekering. If you switch insurer for the basic policy but had aanvullende with the old one, the old aanvullende can quietly continue and bill you in parallel. Check this explicitly when you cancel.

## Zorgtoeslag 2027: do not leave money on the table

The zorgtoeslag is a healthcare subsidy from the Belastingdienst that most expats either do not know about or assume they cannot claim. The eligibility rules for 2027 are:

You must be 18 or older, registered as a Dutch resident, hold a valid basisverzekering, and have a gross annual income below the threshold (€38,500 in 2026, expected to rise to around €40,000 in 2027 for a single person, higher for couples). Your worldwide assets must be below €141,000 (single) or €178,000 (couple) — these figures are also indexed annually.

If you qualify, the monthly toeslag in 2026 was up to €127 for a single person and around €240 for a household. Over a year that is €1,500 of subsidy. You apply through Mijn Toeslagen on the Belastingdienst portal using DigiD. The application is in Dutch but the structure is straightforward.

Two practical points. First, apply as early in the year as possible — payments are made monthly in advance from the month after application. Second, if your income rises mid-year (a raise, a bonus, a partner moving in), update your forecast on Mijn Toeslagen straight away. Otherwise you will face a clawback in your annual reconciliation a year later, which is an unpleasant surprise.

The Zorginstituut Nederland keeps an authoritative consumer guide to all healthcare entitlements — see their site at [zorginstituutnederland.nl](https://www.zorginstituutnederland.nl/) for the official policy detail.
