Last spring I got a panicked WhatsApp from a coaching client I will call Raj. He had landed in Amsterdam six weeks earlier as a senior product manager at a fintech, moving from Bangalore with his wife and two kids. The message read: "Sarah, I think my boss hates me. She told me in front of the whole team that my roadmap was 'not realistic' and 'maybe I had not understood the European market yet.' I want to resign."

I called him back inside a minute. We talked for forty-five. By the end, he was laughing — nervously, but laughing. Because what had happened to Raj was not a confrontation, not a warning, not a sign he was about to be fired. What had happened to Raj was a Tuesday morning vergadering. His manager had not insulted him. She had given him, in the most Dutch way possible, the most useful feedback he would receive all quarter. The problem was not the message. The problem was that nobody had handed Raj the translation key.

That translation key is what this guide is. I have coached more than three hundred internationals through their first year in a Dutch workplace — Americans who thought every meeting was a tribunal, Brits who could not stop apologising, Indians who waited for instructions that never came, Italians who felt steamrolled by the agenda, Germans who thought the Dutch were too informal, Japanese clients who thought they were too informal in completely the opposite way. Every culture clashes with this one in its own particular way. And every single one of them, given six months and a bit of context, learns to love it.

> **Quick answer:** Dutch business culture runs on three pillars: direct communication ("zeg het maar" — just say it), the sacred agenda (every meeting, coffee, and conversation is scheduled), and consensus-driven decisions through endless meetings called polderen. Hierarchy is flat, work-life balance is real (5pm is 5pm), and English is fine but Dutch helps you belong. Show up on time, push back with reasons, do not take feedback personally, and never schedule anything for Friday afternoon — that is for borrel.

## Dutch directness is not rudeness — it is efficiency

Let us start with the thing everyone warns you about and nobody explains well.

Dutch directness is real, it is intense, and it is the single feature of the workplace that breaks the most new arrivals. In my first year coaching, I would say six out of every ten clients raised it in their first session. An American product manager told me her colleague said her presentation was "actually pretty boring" — in the meeting, in front of the CEO. An Indian engineer told me his architect responded to a design proposal with "no, this is wrong, we will not do this." A British marketer told me her copy was returned with the single word "unclear" written across the top in red pen.

Here is what each of them did not realise: that was the feedback. Not the opening to a negotiation about the feedback. Not a soft-launch before the real conversation. The feedback. Final. Delivered.

The cultural mechanic underneath is what anthropologists call low-context communication. In high-context cultures — much of Asia, the Middle East, parts of southern Europe, and (yes) much of the English-speaking professional world — meaning lives around the words. You wrap criticism in three layers of politeness, you signal disagreement by saying "interesting" with a particular intonation, you protect the other person's face. The Dutch grew up in a culture where you do the opposite. You strip the words back to the actual content, and you trust the listener to handle it. Adding the layers is seen as either patronising (you cannot handle the truth) or wasteful (we both have things to do).

What this means in practice:

- "I do not agree" is a position, not an attack.
- "This will not work" is an analysis, not a verdict on you.
- "Why?" is a request for reasoning, not an interrogation.
- "No" is a complete sentence and does not need follow-up.
- Silence after your idea is people thinking, not people judging.

I tell every new arrival the same thing in their first session: when a Dutch colleague gives you sharp feedback, count to three before reacting. By three, your brain has usually caught up with the fact that there was no insult in there, just information. Then ask "what would make it better?" — and you have just turned the most jarring moment of your week into the most useful one.

For new highly skilled migrants, this curve usually steepens around month three, when the honeymoon ends and the real work feedback starts arriving. ## The agenda obsession: nothing is ever spontaneous

I once watched two Dutch colleagues, sitting at adjacent desks, both refuse to discuss a fifteen-minute matter face to face on a Wednesday morning. Instead they opened Outlook, found a slot the following Tuesday, and scheduled "afstemming Wednesday's onderwerp" for 09:15-09:30. Then they went back to whatever they were doing. The matter could absolutely have been resolved in five minutes. But it was not in the agenda.

To outsiders this looks insane. To the Dutch it is respect.

The agenda — your calendar, in English — is the operating system of Dutch professional life. Time is finite, attention is finite, and the agenda is how you signal that you respect both of those things in someone else. When you "drop by" a Dutch colleague's desk without warning, you are not being friendly. You are interrupting a planned hour of focused work. When you say "do you have five minutes?", the honest answer is almost always no, because the five minutes belongs to something else that is already in the agenda. The polite, culturally fluent move is to plan een afspraakje — make a quick appointment.

A few rules I drill into every coaching client:

- **Coffees go in the agenda.** Yes, even with the person you sit next to.
- **Walk-ins are reserved for emergencies and onboarding.** New starters get a grace period of maybe a month. After that, expectations harden.
- **Recurring meetings are sacred.** A standing Monday 09:00 staffmeeting is a contract. Skipping without notice is genuinely rude.
- **Cancellations need a reason and ideally a reschedule in the same message.**
- **Double-booking yourself is unprofessional, not impressive.** Hustle culture does not translate here.

The reverse is also true: when you put time in someone's agenda, they will take it seriously. The Dutch keep their calendars accurate. If a Dutch colleague's calendar says they are free Thursday at 14:00, they are free. They do not block fake "focus time" or pad their schedule the way some Anglo-American workplaces do. So use the calendar honestly, and trust theirs.

## Meetings, vergaderingen, and the dark art of polderen

The vergadering — the meeting — is where Dutch culture compresses itself into its purest form. There will be an agenda circulated in advance. There will be a chair (voorzitter). There will be minutes (notulen). There will be a list of action points (actiepunten) with names attached. And there will be, in any meeting longer than thirty minutes, a discussion that goes on at least twice as long as you expected and ends in a consensus that surprises everyone.

This is polderen.

The word comes from the polder model — the centuries-old Dutch practice of getting farmers, dike-builders, and local lords around a table to agree on water management, because if one party defected the dikes broke and everyone drowned. That habit became a national operating principle. Every stakeholder gets heard. Every objection gets aired. No decision is made until something close to consensus is reached. It is slow. It is sometimes maddening. And it produces decisions that are remarkably hard to overturn afterwards, because everyone in the room owns them.

For internationals, the polderen meeting is the single biggest adjustment after directness. Common reactions I hear:

- "Why is everyone still talking? We already decided." (You did not. You proposed.)
- "The intern just contradicted the director and nothing happened." (Correct. That is the system working.)
- "We have had four meetings about this and made no decision." (You have made four meetings of progress. The fifth will land it.)

Things to do in a Dutch meeting:

1. **Read the agenda before you walk in.** If there is no agenda, ask for one — politely, but ask.
2. **Speak up early.** Silence is read as agreement, and you do not want to discover at the end that you have consented to something you hate.
3. **Disagree with reasons.** "I do not think this will work, because X" lands. "I have a slightly different perspective" disappears.
4. **Watch for actiepunten.** If your name is attached to one, it is a commitment. Treat it like a contract.
5. **Do not over-prepare slides.** Dutch meetings are conversations, not presentations. A printed one-pager beats a forty-slide deck.

If you are coming from a culture where the boss decides and the room nods, this will feel like chaos for the first three months. By month six, you will be the one pushing back on a director's strategy in front of twelve colleagues, with reasons, and walking out fine. That is the cultural shift, and it is genuinely worth it.

## Hierarchy is flat — call the CEO by their first name

In my second job in the Netherlands, I emailed our CEO with a complaint about the office coffee machine. I signed it "Sarah." He replied within an hour, used my first name, agreed the coffee was terrible, and CC'd facilities. The whole exchange took maybe four minutes. It was the most ordinary thing in the world.

That is Dutch flat hierarchy.

It does not mean there are no bosses. There are. They make decisions, they set budgets, they fire people. But the social distance between the top and the bottom of a Dutch company is one of the smallest in the developed world. CEOs ride bikes to work. Directors eat the same canteen lunch as the interns. First names are universal — calling someone "Mr van der Berg" in a meeting will get you a confused look and a correction. The only places I have heard "u" (the formal you) used in business in the last decade were one law firm and one private bank, both of which made a point of mentioning it as a deliberate brand choice.

What this means for you:

- **Disagree with your manager openly.** They expect it. They want it. The colleague who silently nods through every decision is the colleague who gets passed over for promotion.
- **You do not need to "manage up" the way you do in the US or UK.** Strategic flattery reads as inauthentic and slightly desperate.
- **Skip-level conversations are normal.** You can talk to your boss's boss without it being a coup.
- **Titles matter less than expertise.** The junior data scientist who actually understands the model will be heard over the VP who does not.

Internationals from steeper hierarchies (India, much of east Asia, parts of southern Europe and Latin America) often need the longest to recalibrate here. The instinct to wait for instructions or seek approval before acting reads, in Dutch culture, as either passivity or political game-playing. Both are negatively scored. Show initiative, ask forgiveness rather than permission for small things, and disagree out loud.

## Work-life balance is not a slogan — it is a clock

At 17:00 on a Tuesday, my Dutch colleagues stand up, close their laptops, get on their bikes, and go home. Not "wrap up in the next twenty minutes." Go home. By 17:15 the office is empty enough that the cleaners arrive. If you are still emailing at 19:00, no one will reply, and no one will be impressed tomorrow when they see the timestamp — if anything, they will be quietly concerned about your boundaries.

This is the cultural baseline:

- **Working hours are working hours.** Stretching them is a sign of bad planning, not commitment.
- **Holidays are sacred.** Twenty-five paid days plus public holidays is standard, often more. People actually take them. They do not check email.
- **Out-of-office replies are taken at face value.** "I am on holiday until 18 August and will not be reading email" means exactly that.
- **Part-time work is normal at every level.** It is completely standard for a senior director to work four days a week. Their Wednesday or Friday off is in everyone's calendar.
- **Parental leave is taken by both parents, increasingly equally.** A father taking three months off is unremarkable.

Coming from cultures with always-on expectations — US tech, UK consulting, much of Indian IT — this can feel uncomfortable at first. I have had clients confess they felt "lazy" leaving at 17:00. They were not lazy. They were finally working at a sustainable cadence, in a country whose productivity per hour is among the highest in the world precisely because nobody is exhausted.

The unspoken rule: outwork your colleagues during working hours, not after them. Use the [30% ruling tax advantages](/blog/30-percent-ruling-netherlands-2026/) to fund a better quality of life outside work, not to subsidise a workaholic habit you brought with you.

## Borrel culture: Friday afternoon is for beer

The borrel is the Dutch workplace ritual that nobody warns you about and everyone secretly loves once they get it.

A borrel is an informal drinks gathering — usually Friday, usually starting around 16:00 or 16:30, usually in the office canteen or a nearby cafe. There will be beer, wine, maybe bitterballen (deep-fried meatballs you absolutely should try), and a level of social ease you will not have seen in your colleagues all week. The CEO might be there. The intern definitely is. The conversation drifts between work and not-work, and a surprising amount of real business gets done in a relaxed register that would never fit into a 09:00 vergadering.

A few notes:

- **You do not have to drink alcohol.** Sparkling water is fine. Nobody tracks.
- **Showing up matters more than staying late.** Thirty minutes is enough. Skipping every week is noticed.
- **It is not a networking event in the LinkedIn sense.** Hustling here will read as weird. Be a human, ask about people's weekends, listen to bad jokes.
- **Do not pitch.** If a conversation drifts to a project you are passionate about, fine. If you steer every chat back to your roadmap, you will be the borrel character no one wants to stand next to.
- **Friday afternoon meetings are a war crime.** Nobody will say it, but they will all judge whoever scheduled it.

If you live outside the major cities and your office runs hybrid, find a way to get to the office on borrel days. The relationships built over a Friday beer are the ones that pull you into projects, defend you in calibrations, and recommend you for promotions.

## Lunch is a cheese sandwich at your desk (or in twenty minutes flat)

If you arrive expecting Italian-style two-hour lunches with wine, recalibrate. The Dutch business lunch is, traditionally and stubbornly, two slices of brown bread, one slice of cheese (usually old Gouda), maybe a glass of milk or buttermilk, and twenty minutes. Many people eat it at their desk. Some go to the canteen. Almost nobody goes out.

This puzzles internationals from food-centric cultures more than almost anything else. I had an Italian client ask me, sincerely, whether his colleagues were ill — "they eat like prisoners, Sarah." They were not ill. They were Dutch.

The lunch culture is changing slowly: bigger offices now have hot meals, salad bars, soup. International firms have catered lunches in some cases. But the underlying assumption stays the same: lunch is fuel, not theatre. Twenty to thirty minutes is normal. Forty-five is long. A two-hour lunch with a colleague means you are either celebrating something significant or interviewing them for another job.

What this means for you: do not pressure colleagues to come out for lunch every day. Bring a sandwich. Join the canteen table when you can. Save the long lunches for genuine occasions — closing a deal, welcoming a new hire, saying goodbye to someone leaving.

## Dress code: smart casual, with bicycle considerations

There are exceptions — the law firms in Zuidas, some of the private banks, certain client-facing consulting roles — but the default Dutch business dress code is smart casual leaning casual. Jeans are fine almost everywhere. Sneakers are fine in tech, increasingly fine elsewhere. Ties have largely vanished outside specific industries. The CEO of a major Dutch retailer once gave me a media interview in a fleece.

The reason is partly cultural (visible status displays are not a virtue here) and partly practical: most people bike to work. Try cycling four kilometres in the rain in a tailored suit and you will understand why the dress code relaxed. Backpacks are universal. So are rain trousers in winter.

Things that will mark you as overdressed: a three-piece suit on a normal Tuesday, designer logos prominently displayed, business cards with embossed lettering. Things that will mark you as underdressed: shorts in a client meeting, gym clothes, hoodies in a partner-facing role. Find the middle and you are fine.

## Email etiquette: short, direct, and signed with your first name

Dutch business email is its own minor masterpiece of efficiency. The norms:

- **First-name greetings.** "Beste Jan," or just "Hi Jan," — both fine, in Dutch or English.
- **Skip the "I hope this email finds you well."** It reads as filler. Get to the point.
- **Bullet your asks.** Three bullets beat one wall of paragraph.
- **Reply quickly or set expectations.** Twenty-four hours is the default. Out-of-office means out.
- **Sign with first name.** "Met vriendelijke groet, Sarah" or "Best, Sarah" works in either language.
- **CC sparingly.** Putting half the company on a CC line to make a point is seen as political and slightly aggressive.
- **No emoji in the first email to someone senior.** After that, read the room. Plenty of senior Dutch professionals use 👍 and 😅 freely.

If you are coming from a culture where you start with two paragraphs of pleasantries before getting to the request — common in Indian, Japanese, and some Middle Eastern business correspondence — strip them out. The Dutch reader will scroll past them looking for the point and feel mildly irritated by the time they find it.

## Dutch language at work: not required, deeply appreciated

Most international companies in the Netherlands run on English. Almost all big tech, fintech, scale-ups, and global consultancies have English as the working language. Many traditional Dutch SMEs are increasingly bilingual. So can you survive without Dutch? Yes. I know senior people who have lived here twenty years on operational English alone.

Should you learn anyway? Yes. Every coaching client who learned even survival Dutch ("goedemorgen, een koffie graag, fijne weekend, tot maandag") tells me it changed how colleagues treated them. Not in a transactional way — in the small social transactions that build belonging. The coffee-machine chit-chat. The lunch banter. The borrel jokes. These happen in Dutch even at "English-speaking" companies, and being able to follow, even badly, pulls you into the in-group.

A practical plan I give clients:

1. **Months one to three:** survival phrases only. Greetings, ordering, basic politeness.
2. **Months three to twelve:** structured A1 to A2 course, two to three hours a week. Several of my clients have used the [best Dutch language schools online](/blog/best-dutch-language-schools-online-2026/) options, which work well around full-time jobs.
3. **Year two:** B1 conversational. Now you can follow a borrel and contribute. This is the level that changes everything socially.
4. **Year three plus:** B2 if you want full meeting fluency, especially if you plan to stay long term.

Do not feel bad about being slow. Dutch is a hard language with brutal pronunciation. Every Dutch colleague has a story about a foreigner mangling "Scheveningen" or "gezellig" and laughs about it warmly. Trying is what counts.

## Negotiation style: low context, no haggling

When a Dutch employer makes you an offer, the number on the page is the number they think is fair. There is usually some movement — a few percent, a sign-on bonus, an extra holiday week — but the long, theatrical negotiations common in American or Indian hiring are not the norm. Asking for 30% above the offer with no clear justification will not be received as bold; it will be received as not having understood the market.

The same logic applies to client negotiations, supplier negotiations, internal budgeting. Opening positions are usually closer to landing positions than they would be in many other cultures. Padding everything by 50% so you can "give some back" reads as wasteful and slightly dishonest.

What works:

- **Make your case with data.** Market benchmarks, comparable roles, concrete achievements.
- **Ask for what you actually want.** Not 30% more so you can settle for 10%.
- **Be willing to walk.** A polite "this does not work for me, but thank you" is respected.
- **Use silence.** After you state your number, stop talking. Let them respond.

For salary specifically, sites like Loonwijzer and the [Rijksoverheid salary scales](https://www.rijksoverheid.nl/) give you defensible benchmarks. Bring numbers, not vibes.

## Common culture-clash scenarios I see every week

After years of coaching, the same patterns repeat. Here are the four most common, and how I work through them with clients.

**American expat, first month, panicking that everyone hates them.** The cause is almost always that their American instinct for upbeat, encouraging feedback ("great job, just a few thoughts!") has met the Dutch instinct for direct, content-first feedback ("the deck does not land — these three sections need rework"). The American hears criticism as rejection. The Dutch colleague thinks they have given a normal Tuesday note. Fix: I get the American to explicitly ask "what is working?" alongside "what is not?" — and they discover the positive feedback exists, it just was not volunteered.

**Indian engineer, second month, frustrated because nobody is telling them what to do.** The cause is that their previous workplaces gave instructions; this one expects initiative. They are waiting for direction that will never come, and starting to feel invisible. Fix: I get them to schedule a thirty-minute weekly one-on-one with their manager, propose three priorities for the week, and ask "are these the right things?" The shift from awaiting-instructions to proposing-and-validating changes how they are perceived within a month.

**British marketer, third month, exhausted by how blunt everyone is.** The cause is the constant low-level energy cost of hearing every piece of feedback without the British cushioning. They are not in trouble; they are just tired. Fix: time. By month six, the calibration completes and they tell me they cannot go back — British meetings now feel suffocatingly indirect.

**Japanese consultant, sixth month, struggling with the lack of formal hierarchy markers.** They do not know how senior anyone actually is, who they should defer to, when. Fix: we work on internal mapping — who has decision rights on what, regardless of title — and on the cultural acceptance that influence here does not flow neatly down an org chart.

Whatever cluster you fall into, two things are universal: it takes six months to find the rhythm, and it changes how you work forever after. ## Putting it all together: your first ninety days

Here is the playbook I give every new arrival:

**Week one:** Show up early, observe, take notes. Do not propose anything yet. Watch how meetings start and end, who talks, who chairs, how decisions are recorded. Learn five Dutch phrases.

**Weeks two to four:** Start speaking up in meetings, with reasons. Schedule one-on-ones with everyone you will work with regularly. Ask each of them: "What does good look like in my role here?" Their answers will not match, and that is information.

**Month two:** Ask for and give direct feedback. Try out small disagreements with your manager. Notice that the world does not end. Start going to the borrel.

**Month three:** Push back on a process you think is wrong. Propose an improvement. Notice that initiative gets you further than compliance.

**Months four to six:** The rhythm clicks. You stop translating the directness in your head. You learn to love the agenda. You start to understand why polderen takes so long and produces such durable decisions.

By month six, internationals who arrived bewildered are usually the ones explaining the culture to the next arrival. By year two, you will catch yourself sending a one-line email and signing it with your first name, and you will not remember how you used to write any other way.

Raj, the client from the opening of this piece, sent me a message ten months after that first panicked WhatsApp. He had been promoted. His CEO had specifically praised his "European business judgement." His former boss — the one he was sure hated him — had become his mentor. He attached a screenshot of an email he had just sent her, which read, in full: "Disagree. Will not work for Q3. Let us plan it in for Friday and I will show why."

He signed it Raj.

That is the goal.
