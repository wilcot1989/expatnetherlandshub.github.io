## The Three Dutch Mobile Networks

Every SIM card in the Netherlands runs on one of three physical networks:

**KPN.** The legacy national operator. Generally rated best for coverage, especially in rural areas, on trains, and inside large buildings. Owns Simyo, Telfort (now folded back into KPN), and reseller capacity to many MVNOs.

**Odido (formerly T-Mobile Netherlands and Tele2).** The merged second network, rebranded fully to Odido in 2023. Strong urban coverage, generally faster 5G speeds in cities. Sells under both T-Mobile and Odido brands plus reseller capacity to brands like Simpel.nl and Hollandsnieuwe.

**VodafoneZiggo.** Vodafone NL plus the Ziggo cable assets. Solid network performance in cities, less strong rurally than KPN. Sells under Vodafone, Ziggo Mobiel, hollandsnieuwe (also Odido), and reseller capacity.

**MVNO brands** (Mobile Virtual Network Operators) lease capacity from these three networks. They're often cheaper but may have lower priority during peak congestion (rush hour in train stations, festivals, etc.). For 95% of expats, the difference is invisible.

## Which Plan Fits Which User

### The Light User: <10 GB/month

Profile: Home Wi-Fi, work Wi-Fi, occasional maps and messaging on the go. Maybe Spotify on commutes if it's not too long.

**Best pick:** Simyo or Youfone. €9–€11/month for 8–10 GB on either KPN or Odido network. Solid coverage, plenty of data for the use case, no premium markup.

If you want better network priority, **Odido** at €15/month for 8 GB gives you direct-network reliability without paying a premium-brand rate.

### The Mid User: 15–30 GB/month

Profile: Heavy maps use, video calls on the train, podcasts, some streaming, light tethering.

**Best pick:** Simpel.nl 30 GB at €15/month. The pricing-to-volume ratio is the best in the market for this band. Network is Odido — fine in cities, generally fine on trains.

If you commute heavily on rural rail lines (Friesland, Zeeland, Drenthe), **Simyo on KPN network** at €18/month for 30 GB is worth the extra €3 for better rural coverage.

### The Heavy User / Tetherer: Unlimited

Profile: Streams Netflix on commutes, tethers laptop multiple days a week, works from cafes, lives partly without home internet.

**Best pick:** Odido Unlimited at €30/month. Direct-network plan, no MVNO priority issues during peak times, full 5G access. Their unlimited fair-use cap is high enough that normal usage doesn't trigger throttling.

KPN and Vodafone unlimited cost €3–€5 more per month for marginal differences. Choose KPN if you specifically need rural coverage.

### The New Arrival Without a BSN

Profile: Just landed, BSN application in progress, need a Dutch number now.

**Best pick:** Lebara or Lyca prepaid SIM, then switch to SIM-only once you have your BSN and Dutch IBAN. Both can be activated with a passport and a foreign IBAN.

## What Actually Differs Between Providers

Beyond price, these are the practical differences I've experienced over four years using multiple providers:

**Customer service in English.** KPN, Odido, and Vodafone all offer English-language customer service. Most MVNOs (Simyo, Ben, Simpel.nl) operate in Dutch first, with limited English support. If you're newly arrived and not comfortable doing service calls in Dutch, this matters.

**App quality.** KPN and Odido have polished apps with English settings, easy data top-ups, and call/SMS history. MVNOs vary — Simyo's app is good, Lebara's is functional, some smaller MVNOs barely have an app.

**Family bundles.** KPN, Odido, and Vodafone offer multi-line discounts that add up if you have 2–4 phones in a household. MVNOs typically don't.

**Bundles with home internet.** Major networks bundle SIM-only with broadband for €5–€10/month discount on combined bills. See our [broadband guide](#) for context — link to come once published.

**5G priority.** During peak congestion in Amsterdam Centraal or Schiphol, MVNO traffic is sometimes throttled relative to direct-network customers. In four years I've noticed this maybe twice; if you're never in stadiums or peak commute, it's irrelevant.

**Roaming policies.** All EU plans include EU roaming. Outside the EU (UK, US, Switzerland), policies vary widely. KPN typically charges €15/day for roaming outside the EU. Odido offers a €5/day "Roaming Plus" pass for non-EU countries on certain plans.

## The Contract Length Question

Most Dutch SIM-only plans come in 1-month, 12-month, or 24-month flavors.

**1-month plans** (often called "monthly cancellable" or "maandelijks opzegbaar") cost €2–€5/month more but you can cancel anytime. Useful for short-term assignments, trial periods, or if you're unsure how long you'll stay in the Netherlands.

**12-month plans** are the standard. Lock in a price for a year, get the best rates.

**24-month plans** typically save you €1–€3/month vs. 12-month and lock in for two years. Worth it only if you're settled and confident you won't move providers.

After your 12 or 24-month period ends, the contract converts to monthly — you can cancel with one month's notice. **Important:** providers do NOT automatically lower your price after your fixed term ends. They keep charging the contract rate. Always negotiate or switch when your term expires; you'll typically save €5–€10/month.

## EU Roaming and Travel

All Dutch SIM-only plans include EU roaming under "Roam Like at Home." Your data, calls, and SMS work across the EU plus Iceland, Liechtenstein, and Norway at no extra cost.

**Watch outs:**

- **Fair-use limits.** On unlimited plans, EU roaming is capped at typically 15–30 GB/month while abroad. Above that, providers can throttle or charge.
- **UK is not in the EU.** UK roaming is separate. KPN, Odido, and Vodafone offer specific UK passes (€2–€5/day or included on some premium plans).
- **Switzerland is not in the EU.** Often charged separately; check your plan terms before traveling.
- **Permanent roaming.** If you live abroad and use your Dutch SIM full-time in another EU country, providers will eventually disable EU roaming on your line. The plan is meant for travel, not for full relocation.

## How to Switch Providers

Switching is easier than most people think. The steps:

1. **Pick your new provider and plan.** Sign up online — typically takes 5–10 minutes.
2. **Request number portability.** During signup, choose "transfer my existing number" and provide your current SIM's number.
3. **Provide BSN, Dutch IBAN, ID.** New provider verifies and approves typically within 24 hours.
4. **Wait for the SIM card to arrive in the post.** Usually 2–3 business days.
5. **Activate at the time the new provider specifies.** Number transfers usually happen within 24–48 hours of activation. Old SIM stops working at the moment of port.

If your current contract still has months to run on a fixed term, you'll typically pay an early-termination fee (the remaining months × monthly rate, often discounted 25–40%). Outside fixed term, no fee — just notice period.

## eSIM vs Physical SIM in 2026

Most modern phones (iPhone 14+, Pixel 7+, Samsung S22+) support eSIM. Dutch providers' eSIM support has improved — KPN, Odido, and Vodafone all offer eSIM activation natively. MVNO support is patchier but growing.

Why eSIM matters for expats:

- **Faster activation.** No waiting for a physical SIM in the post — activate via QR code in 5 minutes.
- **Multi-line on one phone.** Hold your home-country SIM and your Dutch SIM on the same device (especially useful in transition periods).
- **Easier switching.** No physical SIM swap when changing providers.

If your phone supports eSIM and your provider does, prefer eSIM. If your phone is older or your chosen provider doesn't support eSIM yet (most MVNOs), physical SIM still works fine — just adds 2–3 days for postal delivery.

## Phone-Plus-Plan vs. SIM-Only

Dutch carriers also offer "phone with plan" bundles — typically a 24-month contract where you get a new phone (€800–€1,500 retail) for €0 upfront and pay €30–€60/month covering both phone and service.

Math reality: phone-plus-plans are usually 10–20% more expensive over the contract life vs. buying the phone outright (cash, financing, or an Apple/Samsung-direct interest-free plan) and pairing with a SIM-only plan.

Exception: aggressive promotional deals (€1 phone with high-data plan) can occasionally beat the standalone math, especially for top-tier flagship phones. Run the numbers before signing.

For most expats, **buy the phone separately and pair with SIM-only.** You save money and you keep flexibility to switch SIM-only plans annually.

## Roaming Outside the EU: A Cheat Sheet

Most expats travel beyond the EU at some point. Without preparation, roaming can cost €5–€20/MB. Approach options:

**Provider day passes.** KPN offers "WereldBundel" — €15/day for international data in major non-EU countries. Odido has similar passes. Useful for short trips where convenience trumps cost.

**eSIM travel data SIMs.** Airalo, Holafly, and Saily offer eSIM data plans for any destination. Buy a 5GB / 30-day Mexico eSIM for $15 instead of paying €15/day. My usual approach for trips longer than 3 days.

**Local SIMs.** Cheapest if you'll be in one country for 1+ weeks. Buy at airport on arrival.

**Free Wi-Fi only.** If you're disciplined, hotels and cafes Wi-Fi cover most needs. Pair with [NordVPN](#) for security on public networks.

## Family Plans and Multi-Line Discounts

For families, household economics shift. Major networks (KPN, Odido, Vodafone) offer multi-line discounts when you bundle 2+ SIMs:

- 2 lines: ~€5/month off second line
- 3 lines: ~€8–€10/month off
- 4 lines: ~€10–€15/month off

Plus internet bundle discount if you're on the same provider for home broadband — see my [broadband providers](/blog/best-broadband-internet-providers-expats-netherlands-2026/) guide.

For a family of 4 with 4 mid-tier SIMs and home broadband, an "alles-in-1" bundle at one provider can save €15–€30/month vs. piecemeal. Beware the lock-in: 24-month contracts on bundles are harder to unwind if you move providers later.

MVNOs typically don't offer multi-line discounts. So families are mostly choosing between "single MVNO line each" (cheaper per line, but no bundle savings) or "major-network family plan" (more per line, but bundle and unified billing).

I'm a single-line user so I run pure SIM-only on Simpel.nl. For families, I'd lean toward Odido or KPN bundles unless you really value the savings of independent MVNO lines.
