## What You Get with Bunq Easy Green (€17.99/month)

Bunq Easy Green is the top-tier personal account from Bunq, the Dutch neobank. At €17.99 per month — or €179.88 per year if you pay annually — it's the pricier option here. Here's what's included:

**25 IBANs in one account.** This is genuinely unusual. You can create up to 25 separate sub-accounts, each with its own Dutch NL IBAN. In practice I use five: one for salary, one for rent and fixed bills, one for variable spending, one for savings, and one for my side project income. More on why this matters for expats below.

**Metal card.** The physical card is a brushed metal Mastercard. It's heavier than a plastic card and feels noticeably premium. This is mostly aesthetic but it does generate comments at checkout.

**Unlimited ATM withdrawals worldwide.** No daily or monthly cash limit (within reason), no surcharge from Bunq regardless of how much you take out. Whatever the local ATM operator charges still applies.

**Travel insurance.** Bunq includes travel insurance via Allianz for all Easy Green subscribers. Coverage includes medical expenses abroad, trip cancellation, and luggage. I'll detail the exact limits in the travel insurance section below.

**A tree planted for every €100 you spend.** Bunq plants a tree through their own reforestation programme. It's the "Green" in Easy Green — the environmental positioning is core to this tier.

**Priority customer support.** Easy Green customers get priority in the support queue. In my experience this meant same-day responses on issues where lower tiers waited longer.

**Bunq is <a href="https://www.dnb.nl/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">licensed by De Nederlandsche Bank (DNB)</a> and covered by the Dutch Deposit Guarantee Scheme up to €100,000.** This matters if you're going to use it as your primary Dutch account. You can receive salary, set up iDEAL payments, and handle direct debits — it functions like a full Dutch bank account.

## Travel Insurance: What Each Card Actually Covers

Both accounts include travel insurance, but the details differ in ways that matter.

**Bunq Easy Green** provides travel insurance through Allianz Partners. Coverage includes:

- Medical expenses abroad — specific limits vary by policy year; check your Bunq app for your current policy document
- Trip cancellation and interruption
- Delayed or cancelled flights (compensation varies — review current policy)
- Lost, stolen, or damaged luggage
- Personal liability abroad

The insurance activates automatically for all Easy Green cardholders. You don't need to buy travel with your Bunq card to trigger coverage — you're covered on all trips as long as your account is active. This is a meaningful advantage. I once had a minor medical incident while visiting family in Germany and was able to use the Bunq Allianz coverage without any fuss over whether I'd booked the flight with my Bunq card.

**Revolut Metal** provides travel insurance with the following specific limits:

- Overseas medical insurance: up to €10 million — this is genuinely high and covers emergency evacuation
- Delayed flight compensation: €70 for every 4 hours of delay, up to a maximum of €700
- Lost or stolen baggage: up to €1,200
- Winter sports cover: included (skiing accidents, ski equipment, piste closure)
- Personal accident cover

For Revolut Metal, coverage for some benefits requires you to activate them in the app or have purchased travel using your Revolut card. Read the full policy document carefully before your trip — the activation requirements can catch people out.

The €10 million medical limit on Revolut Metal is impressive and useful if you're travelling outside Europe where healthcare costs can be catastrophic. For most Europe-based expat travel, Bunq's Allianz coverage is practical and the activation advantage (no card-purchase requirement) is real.

## ATM Withdrawals: The Real Math

This is where the numbers get interesting and where I found the biggest real-world difference between the two accounts.

**Bunq Easy Green:** Unlimited free ATM withdrawals worldwide from Bunq's side. Whatever the ATM operator charges is your only cost.

**Revolut Metal:** €800 per month free, then 2% with a minimum €1 charge per withdrawal.

Most expats I know in the Netherlands withdraw somewhere between €100 and €400 per month in cash. Amsterdam is heavily card-friendly, but cash still matters for markets, smaller cafés, splitting bills, and certain landlords or service providers. When you travel back home or to countries where cash is king, the amounts go up.

Let me walk through three scenarios:

**Light cash user — €200/month in withdrawals:**
- Bunq: €0 in withdrawal fees (€17.99 total monthly cost)
- Revolut Metal: €0 in withdrawal fees (€13.99 total monthly cost)
- Verdict: Revolut Metal is cheaper by €4

**Moderate cash user — €800/month in withdrawals:**
- Bunq: €0 in withdrawal fees (€17.99 total monthly cost)
- Revolut Metal: €0 in withdrawal fees — right at the limit (€13.99 total monthly cost)
- Verdict: Revolut Metal still cheaper by €4

**Heavy cash user — €1,400/month in withdrawals:**
- Bunq: €0 in withdrawal fees (€17.99 total monthly cost)
- Revolut Metal: €600 over limit × 2% = €12 in fees (€25.99 total monthly cost)
- Verdict: Bunq Easy Green now cheaper by €8

**Traveler scenario — one month with €2,000 in cash withdrawals (holiday in Southeast Asia or similar):**
- Bunq: €0 in withdrawal fees (€17.99 total monthly cost)
- Revolut Metal: €1,200 over limit × 2% = €24 in fees (€37.99 total monthly cost)
- Verdict: Bunq Easy Green cheaper by €20 in that month alone

My own average over six months was around €600–900 per month in ATM withdrawals. I travel enough that in two of those six months I hit well over €800. In those months, Bunq Easy Green was unambiguously cheaper despite the higher headline price.

## Cashback Reality Check

Revolut Metal's 1% cashback is the feature most prominently marketed. Let me show you what it actually delivers.

**The cap:** Cashback is capped at €13.99 per month — exactly the subscription price. So the maximum cashback you can ever earn is €13.99/month, which means the maximum net fee you pay if you max out the cashback is €0. Effectively, the account is free if you spend enough.

**To earn €13.99 in cashback at 1%, you need to spend €1,399 in a single month on weekdays.** That's about €350 per week in card purchases — every week — and only on weekdays.

**The weekday restriction matters.** Saturday and Sunday purchases earn nothing. A typical weekend grocery run, Saturday market, Sunday dinner out — all excluded. In my tracking, roughly 25–30% of my card spending happened on weekends, so effective cashback was on about 70–75% of my spending.

In practice over six months, my Revolut Metal cashback averaged €7.20 per month. Some months were higher (travel-heavy work months with lots of weekday business purchases), some were lower. My net monthly cost was roughly €6.79 after cashback.

**Bunq Perks** work differently. Instead of percentage-back on all spending, Bunq offers specific discounts at partner merchants — think percentage off at a specific restaurant chain, discounts on subscriptions, or offers at partner retailers. The value depends entirely on whether you use those partners. I used two Bunq Perks meaningfully over six months; the rest didn't apply to my spending.

Neither cashback system is transformational. Revolut Metal's is more predictable; Bunq's is more situational.

## The 25 IBANs Feature: Who Actually Uses This?

When I first saw "25 IBANs" on Bunq Easy Green, I thought it was a novelty. After six months of use, it's the feature I would miss most if I switched.

Here's how my five sub-accounts are currently structured:

1. **Salary IBAN** — my employer deposits my salary here. Nothing else goes in or out except the automatic transfers I set up.
2. **Fixed costs IBAN** — rent, utilities, health insurance, subscriptions all direct-debit from here. I transfer a fixed amount each month from salary and never worry about it.
3. **Variable spending IBAN** — groceries, restaurants, transport, random purchases. This is the card I use for everyday spending. When it runs out, it runs out.
4. **Savings IBAN** — automatic transfer from salary every month. Never touch it except for planned large purchases.
5. **Side project IBAN** — freelance income and business-related expenses go here. Makes tax time vastly simpler.

For expats, this structure is particularly useful because it mirrors the financial organisation that many of us do mentally anyway — separating "Dutch life costs" from "home country costs" from "savings." With Bunq, you can actually give each mental bucket its own real IBAN.

It also helps with [side hustles and passive income in the Netherlands](/blog/side-hustle-passive-income-netherlands-2026/) — keeping business money genuinely separate from personal money, even at a basic level, makes your [Dutch tax return](/blog/tax-return-netherlands-employee-expats-2026/) less chaotic.

Revolut Metal gives you one IBAN — a Lithuanian LT IBAN. You can create "pockets" in different currencies, but these aren't separate IBANs. For salary payments and direct debits, some Dutch employers and service providers still prefer or require a Dutch NL IBAN. In my experience, 95% of Dutch entities accept a Revolut LT IBAN without issue, but I've encountered one payroll system that flagged it.

For most expats, this is achievable in months with travel or heavy business spending, but not consistently year-round. Net cost after cashback averages €6–9/month for most expats in my experience.

**Bunq Easy Green at €17.99/month:**

No direct cashback, so break-even comes from avoided fees. The clearest calculation:

- Avoided ATM fees vs Revolut Metal: once you exceed €800/month in withdrawals, Bunq saves you 2% on every euro over the limit. At €1,200/month in withdrawals, you save €8 — nearly closing the €4 monthly price gap.
- Travel insurance value: if you'd otherwise pay for travel insurance separately (typical cost €10–20/month for a solo expat), Bunq Easy Green's included cover has real value.
- 25 IBANs value: if the alternative is paying €3–5/month for a separate savings account or a business account, that's genuine savings.

In my spreadsheet, over six months, Bunq Easy Green was net cheaper for my lifestyle by an average of €3.20/month because I withdrew cash heavily in two travel months and value the travel insurance year-round.

## The Verdict

After six months of tracking both accounts side by side, here's where I landed:

**Choose Bunq Easy Green if:**
- You withdraw cash regularly (over €600/month)
- You want a Dutch NL IBAN for salary and direct debits without friction
- You'd benefit from multiple sub-accounts with separate IBANs for financial organisation
- You value travel insurance that doesn't require you to book travel on the card
- You care about the environmental angle
- You have or plan to have a side income stream that benefits from account separation

**Choose Revolut Metal if:**
- Your cash withdrawals are consistently under €800/month
- You spend heavily on card during weekdays and want cashback to partially offset the fee
- You travel frequently through airports where Smart Delay lounge access applies
- The €10 million medical cover limit matters to you (Revolut Metal's headline insurance figure)
- You already use Revolut's ecosystem and don't need Dutch-specific banking infrastructure

**My personal outcome:** I kept Bunq Easy Green and closed Revolut Metal after month six. My cash usage and the multi-IBAN structure were the deciding factors. The four euro monthly savings from Revolut Metal were more than offset by ATM fees in two travel-heavy months, and I use the sub-account structure daily. For my lifestyle as an expat who works partly freelance and travels 4–6 times per year, Bunq Easy Green was the better tool.

That said, I know expats for whom Revolut Metal is the right call — particularly those with lighter cash usage and heavier card spending during weekdays. The cashback cap means it can genuinely be a break-even account if your spending habits align.

Neither is a bad choice. Both are genuinely good premium accounts. The difference is whether Dutch banking infrastructure and ATM freedom matter more to you, or whether cashback and travel perks edge it.
