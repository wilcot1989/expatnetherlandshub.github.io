I have switched my Dutch energy supplier three times in the eight years I have lived here. The first time I did not switch for two years and overpaid by what I later calculated was about EUR 600. The second and third switches each saved me EUR 200 to EUR 350 versus staying put. The process took about 20 minutes each time.

Most expats I work with overpay for energy in the Netherlands not because they pick the wrong supplier, but because they never switch. Default variable rates with the same supplier you signed up with on day one are almost always more expensive than a contract you would get switching today. This guide walks through the switch process, what to compare, and how to avoid the small mistakes that cost real money.

## How the Dutch Energy Market Works

The Netherlands deregulated its energy market in 2004. Since then, you have a free choice of energy supplier (leverancier) regardless of where you live. The grid operator (netbeheerder) is regional and not your choice — Stedin in much of the Randstad, Liander in the north and east, Enexis in the south. The grid operator delivers the electricity and gas; the supplier sets the price you pay for it.

This split matters because:

- Switching supplier is risk-free for service continuity. The grid operator does not change. You will not lose power for a moment during the switch.
- About 30 percent of your bill is grid fees and taxes, fixed regardless of supplier.
- The remaining 70 percent — wholesale energy cost plus supplier margin — is what you can shop on.

In 2026, the major suppliers are:

- Vattenfall (largest, formerly Nuon)
- Eneco
- Essent
- Greenchoice (smaller, fully green)
- Vrienden van Energie
- Budget Energie / Energiedirect (low-cost subsidiaries)
- Pure Energie, OM (small green providers)

## Why Switching Saves Real Money

The reason switching beats sitting tight is structural: suppliers offer better tariffs to acquire new customers than to retain existing ones. After your initial fixed contract expires, you typically roll onto a default variable tariff at significantly higher rates. Suppliers count on customer inertia.

Real numbers from 2026:

- Average annual energy cost for a 2-person apartment: EUR 1,650 to EUR 2,100
- Average annual energy cost for a 4-person family home: EUR 2,400 to EUR 3,200
- Difference between cheapest and most expensive available 12-month fixed contracts (same usage profile): typically EUR 350 to EUR 600 per year
- Welcome discounts: EUR 50 to EUR 250 for new customers

If you have not switched in three years, you are very likely overpaying by EUR 250 to EUR 500 per year. This is one of the highest-ROI 30 minutes you can spend in your Dutch life.

## Fixed vs Variable: What I Recommend in 2026

### Fixed Contracts

Lock the rate per kWh and per m3 for a defined period, typically 1, 2, or 3 years. The supplier cannot change the rate during the term.

**Pros:**
- Budget predictability
- Protection against price spikes (which the Netherlands has experienced)
- Often better welcome discounts than variable

**Cons:**
- You miss out if wholesale prices fall meaningfully
- Exit fees if you switch before the contract ends
- 3-year fixed contracts can become uncompetitive in years 2 and 3 even if cheap on day one

### Variable Contracts

The rate adjusts every month or quarter based on wholesale energy prices.

**Pros:**
- No exit fees
- Captures price falls quickly
- Switch any time

**Cons:**
- Exposure to spikes (households that stayed variable in 2022 saw bills triple)
- Harder to budget
- Generally less aggressive welcome discounts

### My 2026 Default

A 12-month fixed contract with a green or grey provider depending on your priorities, refreshed annually. This:

- Gives meaningful budget certainty
- Avoids the multi-year lock that becomes painful if the market moves
- Forces you to actively review your contract every year (a feature, not a bug)
- Lets you capture welcome discounts annually

For exposed households (large family home, electric heating, EV charging), I would consider 24-month fixed in 2026 — the larger the bill, the more painful a price spike is.

## The Switching Process: Step by Step

### Step 1: Find Your Annual Consumption

Look at your most recent annual energy statement (jaarafrekening) or log into your current supplier's portal. You need:

- Annual electricity consumption in kWh (typical: 2,200 for an apartment, 3,500 for a family home)
- Annual gas consumption in m3 (typical: 800 for an apartment, 1,400 for a family home)
- Whether you have solar panels and any feed-in (terugleververgoeding)

If you do not have a year of data yet — for example you just moved in — use estimated consumption based on home size. Independer's tool walks through this with sliders if you have no historical data.

### Step 2: Check Your Current Contract

Before you can switch without penalty, check:

- Contract end date (einddatum)
- Variable or fixed?
- Notice period (opzegtermijn) — typically 30 days
- Exit fee for early termination

If you are inside a fixed contract and the exit fee exceeds the savings from switching, wait until the end date. Set a calendar reminder for two months before the end date — this is the optimal time to start comparing.

### Step 3: Compare Tariffs

I always start at Independer's [energy comparison tool](/blog/dutch-utilities-explained-expats-2026/). Enter your postcode, household size, consumption, and preferences (green / grey / mix, fixed / variable, contract length). It returns a ranked list with annual cost estimates including welcome bonuses.

Compare also:

- Tariffsuper.nl (similar tool)
- Direct supplier websites (occasionally have promotional deals not visible on aggregators)

Things to verify:

- Is the price per kWh and m3 stated all-in (including energy tax and BTW) or excluding?
- What is the standing daily charge (vastrecht)?
- What are the welcome discount terms (often credited after 6 or 12 months)?
- Green energy: is it Dutch wind/solar (HVO Garanties van Oorsprong) or imported certificates? Greenchoice and Pure Energie are genuinely Dutch-sourced; some "green" tariffs from larger suppliers rely on imported certificates.

### Step 4: Sign Up for the New Contract

Once you have chosen, sign up directly with the new supplier (if Independer is your starting point, the form completes the switch).

You will need:

- BSN for identification
- IBAN for direct debit (incasso)
- Annual consumption estimates
- Your installation reference if requested (usually pulled automatically)

The new supplier then notifies the old one. You do not need to call your old supplier.

### Step 5: Final Bill from Old Supplier

Within 6 weeks of the switch date, your old supplier sends a final bill (eindafrekening). This reconciles your last meter readings against what you paid in monthly direct debits. If you over-prepaid (common), you get a refund. If you under-prepaid, you pay the difference.

Read this bill carefully. Errors are not common but do occur, particularly with the meter reading on the switch date.

## Common Switching Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Switching Inside a Fixed Contract Without Doing the Math

The exit fee can wipe out your savings. Always check your contract end date before switching. If you are within two months of the end date, switch now to time the new contract correctly. If you are mid-contract with high exit fees, wait.

### Mistake 2: Comparing Welcome Discounts Without Looking at Year-Two Pricing

Some suppliers offer EUR 200 welcome discount that effectively makes year one cheap, but the contract auto-renews onto a more expensive variable tariff. Always check the renewal terms. Set a calendar reminder for 60 days before the contract ends to switch again or renegotiate.

### Mistake 3: Choosing Variable in a Spike-Risk Year

If wholesale energy prices are climbing, variable contracts can turn brutal quickly. The 2022 to 2023 winter saw some variable households paying EUR 4,000 to EUR 6,000 in heating bills. In 2026 the market is more stable, but check the news before signing variable for a household with high gas use.

### Mistake 4: Forgetting to Read the Final Bill

Errors on switch-date meter readings happen. Take photos of your meter on the switch date. Compare against the eindafrekening when it arrives. Disputes are easy to win with photo evidence; harder without.

### Mistake 5: Not Updating Direct Debit if You Move

If you move address mid-contract, you do not switch supplier — you transfer the contract to the new address. But you must inform your supplier within 14 days of moving. Many expats miss this and end up paying for two addresses for a month.

## Saving Beyond Switching

Switching is the highest-impact move, but other actions help.

### Reduce Gas Use

Gas is roughly half of a typical Dutch energy bill (depending on heating). Practical reductions:

- Lower thermostat by 1°C: saves about 6 to 8 percent on heating
- Programmable thermostat (Tado, Nest, Honeywell evohome): EUR 80 to EUR 200 annually if used properly
- Draught-proofing (tochtstrip): EUR 30 to EUR 80 annually for an old building
- Bleeding radiators (ontluchten) — free, often saves 5 to 10 percent

### Reduce Electricity Use

- LED bulbs throughout: ~80 percent saving on lighting
- Unplug standby devices (5 to 10 percent of bill)
- Energy-efficient appliances when replacing
- Solar panels: see the [BSG and DTL coverage on saldering](/blog/buying-second-hand-netherlands-guide-2026/) (note: saldering rules changed in 2025; payback periods are now 10 to 14 years rather than 6 to 8)

### Use Less During Peak Hours

If you have a smart meter and a dynamic pricing contract, electricity is much cheaper during off-peak hours (typically nights and weekends). Running washing machines and EV charging at night can save 20 to 30 percent on the electricity portion. Most expats do not have dynamic pricing; switching to it is a 2026 trend worth considering for high-usage households.

Most expats arrive and inherit a default contract — either by signing the first one offered, or by the landlord transferring an existing contract. This is fine for the first month or two while you settle in. After 60 days, switch.

## When to Definitely Switch

You should switch energy supplier today if:

- You are on a default variable tariff with the same supplier for over 18 months
- Your fixed contract ends in the next 2 months
- You moved house in the last 6 months and inherited the previous tenant's contract
- You have not compared tariffs in over 12 months
- Your current monthly direct debit is over EUR 250 and you have not optimised for at least a year

## When Not to Switch

- You are within a fixed contract with high exit fees and the end date is more than 6 months away
- You have just switched in the last 6 months
- The savings are under EUR 100 per year (the friction is not worth it)
- You are about to move house in the next 60 days (wait and switch from the new address)
