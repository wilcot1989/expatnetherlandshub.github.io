Ana wasn't struggling financially. She just didn't exist yet in the Dutch credit system. No BKR record, no Dutch employer payslip history, no local proof of financial responsibility. For Dutch banks, that combination is basically a blank sheet — and they don't like blank sheets.

What saved her was a Wise debit card she'd set up before leaving Portugal, and a Bunq account she opened two weeks after arriving. By month six, she had enough of a track record to get a basic Dutch credit card from ABN AMRO.

That's the actual use case for prepaid and debit cards in the Netherlands. Not a permanent solution — more of a bridge that gets you from "just arrived with no history" to "established enough to access normal financial products."

## Who actually needs a prepaid card in the Netherlands?

Before getting into the rankings, it's worth being clear about who these cards are genuinely useful for — because "prepaid card" gets used loosely to describe several different situations.

**Expats with no Dutch credit history.** This is the biggest group. If you've just arrived, Dutch banks will often decline standard credit applications even if you're financially solid. The [Dutch Central Bank (DNB)](https://www.dnb.nl/voor-de-consument/) notes that credit assessments in the Netherlands rely heavily on local payment history and BKR data — neither of which you have in year one. A prepaid or fintech debit card fills the gap.

**Minors and younger teens.** Children under 18 can't open standard bank accounts in their own name. Revolut Junior (for ages 6–17, managed by a parent account) is purpose-built for this. For expat families, this is genuinely useful for giving older kids spending money with parental oversight built in.

**Budget control situations.** Some people use prepaid cards deliberately for spending discipline — load a fixed amount for groceries, travel, or discretionary spending and stick to it. This works well with Wise or a dedicated Revolut space.

**Short-term visitors and digital nomads.** If you're in the Netherlands for three to twelve months on a contract or assignment, opening a full Dutch bank account may not be worth the admin. A fintech card covers the period cleanly.

**People who've had banking issues.** If you have a BKR registration or previous banking problems, standard Dutch accounts may be harder to access. Wise and Revolut don't run Dutch credit checks.

**Card type:** Visa debit  
**IBAN:** Lithuanian (LT) by default — NL IBAN available via opt-in

**Monthly fee:** Free (Standard plan)

Revolut has become the most feature-rich free fintech option available in the Netherlands, and the Standard plan is genuinely competitive without paying anything. You get a Visa debit card, currency exchange up to €1,000/month at the interbank rate (after that a 1% fair usage fee applies), a fully functional app with spending analytics, savings pockets, and the option to get a Dutch NL IBAN.

That NL IBAN option is significant. Through Revolut's partnership with a Dutch banking institution, you can request a real NL IBAN directly in the app. This unlocks Dutch direct debits — meaning you can set up automatische incasso for utilities, gym memberships, and other services that require a Dutch account number. Not all users see this option immediately; it rolls out progressively, but as of 2026 it's available to most NL-based account holders.

**What Revolut Standard does well:**

- Free currency exchange up to €1,000/month — covers most expats' regular needs
- NL IBAN option unlocks direct debits
- Revolut Junior (child accounts 6–17) with spending limits, instant parental notifications, and spending category controls — the best option for expat families with kids
- Savings pockets with competitive interest rates (varies by plan)
- Instant transfers to other Revolut users

### 5. Mastercard Prepaid travel cards — Honorable mention

Several non-bank issuers sell physical prepaid Mastercard cards in the Netherlands — available at Albert Heijn, Primera, and petrol stations. These include cards from brands like Mastercard Prepaid and various travel cash card products.

These are genuinely prepaid: you load cash, spend, and there's no bank account behind it. They're useful in specific scenarios — a gift for someone who doesn't have a bank account, cash spending control for a teenager without a smartphone, or a one-time travel float — but they have significant limitations for expats:

- No IBAN — cannot receive transfers, direct debits, or salary
- Fees are typically high: card purchase fee (€5–10), loading fee (1–3%), and sometimes monthly inactivity fees
- No app for real-time management — just a balance you check via a website or phone number
- Not reloadable (in most versions) — you buy a new card when one runs out

For an expat who needs a functional payment tool for daily life in the Netherlands, these cards are a last resort, not a first choice. They work for what they are, but "what they are" is quite limited compared to Wise or Revolut.

## What about Dutch credit cards specifically?

A genuine Dutch credit card — issued by ING, ABN AMRO, Rabobank, or a credit card provider like Openbank — requires an established Dutch financial profile. That means:

- At least 3–6 months of Dutch banking history with the issuing bank
- Proof of Dutch income (payslip from a Dutch employer or self-employment income)
- No negative BKR registrations
- For some cards: Dutch residency for over a year

This is why expats in year one routinely get rejected. The cards in this article are not credit cards in that sense — they're debit or prepaid cards, meaning you spend money you've already loaded or deposited. No credit facility, no revolving balance.

That's actually fine for most expat needs. Dutch culture is largely debit-card-based anyway. The PIN (pinpas) system dominates in-store payments, and most Dutch people use iDEAL for online purchases rather than credit cards. Visa and Mastercard debit cards from Wise, Revolut, Bunq, and N26 are accepted everywhere that accepts card payments — including most online shops.

## How to build toward a real Dutch credit card

If getting a Dutch credit card is eventually on your list, here's the realistic path:

**Months 1–3:** Open a Bunq or ING account and use it actively. Regular deposits and payments build your Dutch banking history. Wise and Revolut are useful complements but don't build Dutch credit history — they're not Dutch banks.

**Months 3–6:** If you're employed in the Netherlands, make sure your salary arrives into a Dutch IBAN. The payslip history matters. Self-employed? Keep good records of income deposits.

**Months 6–12:** Apply for a basic Dutch credit card with your primary Dutch bank — ING, ABN AMRO, Rabobank, or Knab. Basic credit limits (€500–2,500) are easier to get than high-limit cards. The Openbank credit card (Spanish bank active in NL) has also been relatively accessible for newer residents.

**Year 2+:** Once you have 12+ months of clean Dutch financial history with no missed payments, standard Dutch credit cards become accessible.

The fintech cards in this article are the bridge that gets you through year one. By year two, you'll have options that weren't available when you first arrived.

## Frequently asked questions

**What is the best prepaid card for expats in the Netherlands with no credit history?**  
Wise is consistently the best option. Open with just a passport, no credit check, no Dutch banking history required. Revolut Standard (free plan) is a close second for those who want more features.

**Can minors get a prepaid card in the Netherlands?**  
Revolut Junior (ages 6–17, managed by a parent account) is the strongest option for full parental control. For teenagers approaching 18 who want their own account, Wise is available from age 18.

**Do prepaid cards work for Dutch direct debits (automatische incasso)?**  
Standard prepaid cards do not. Bunq and Revolut (with NL IBAN opt-in) do, because they function as bank accounts rather than pure prepaid cards.

**Is there a fee-free prepaid card option for the Netherlands?**  
Revolut Standard and Wise basic account are both free to open and maintain. Both charge fees for ATM withdrawals above monthly free limits, and Revolut charges above €1,000/month for currency exchange.

**Can I use a prepaid card to rent an apartment in the Netherlands?**  
Most Dutch landlords and rental agencies require a bank account with NL IBAN for rent via direct debit. Bunq or Revolut with NL IBAN works; a standard prepaid card or Wise BE IBAN typically does not.
