## Why student banking in the Netherlands is its own category

A Dutch student account is not just a normal account with a discount. It is a regulatory category. Banks treat anyone under 30 enrolled at a Dutch institution as low-risk and waive most fees. That sounds generous until you realise the banks also use it as a loss leader. They want your salary account in five years when you graduate into a job at Booking.com or ASML. So they fight hard for your eighteen-year-old self with free credit cards, free international transfers and English onboarding flows that most adult expats would kill for.

The catch is that "student" status has to be proven, and proving it requires Dutch documents. Your enrollment letter from your university (called a *bewijs van inschrijving*) is the magic paper. Without it you pay regular tariffs. With it, three of the six banks in this guide become free.

The second peculiarity is BSN. Your *burgerservicenummer* is your Dutch citizen number, issued after you register at the *gemeente* (municipality). Most students wait two to six weeks for that appointment depending on the city. Amsterdam in September is notoriously slow. Utrecht is faster. The Hague depends on which week you arrive. During that gap you still need money, food, a transit card, a phone, and ideally a deposit for your room. That gap is what Bunq and Revolut are built to fill.

For the bigger picture on Dutch banking outside the student bracket, my [full guide to the best bank account for expats](/blog/best-bank-account-expats-netherlands-2026/) covers the adult-tier options. This article zooms in on the under-30, in-education subset.

## ABN AMRO Studentenpakket — the gold standard if you have a BSN

ABN AMRO is the bank most international students end up with as their primary account, and there is a reason. The *Studentenpakket* (Student Package) is genuinely free for anyone under 30 who can prove enrollment at a Dutch educational institution. That includes universities, *hogescholen*, MBO, and even some certified language schools.

What you get for zero euros per month: a Dutch IBAN, a Maestro/Mastercard debit card, a free credit card with a EUR 1,000 limit (rises with usage), free SEPA transfers, free withdrawals from any ABN ATM, English mobile app, English internet banking, and access to the *World Card Student* which is essentially a credit card with travel insurance baked in. The credit card is the killer feature. Almost no other Dutch bank gives students a free credit card and it solves a million booking problems: car rentals, US-based hotel bookings, Amazon.com, Steam, anything that wants a credit card and rejects Maestro.

The opening process for international students is built around the BSN. You cannot open ABN AMRO Studentenpakket without one. Once you have your BSN, you complete the application online in English. You upload your passport, your *bewijs van inschrijving*, and a proof of address (your rental contract or BRP extract). Verification takes 24 to 72 hours. The debit card arrives by post within seven working days. The credit card arrives separately, usually within two weeks.

The downside of ABN is the same downside the adult ABN account has: branch service is patchy in English outside Amsterdam, the app is functional but not delightful, and customer service phone lines are Dutch-first with English available but slower. None of that matters much for a student because you will do 95% of your banking in the app.

## ING Studenten Betaalrekening — the strong runner-up

ING runs the second-largest international-student welcome flow in the Netherlands. Their *Studenten Betaalrekening* is also free under 30 with enrollment proof, includes an English app, a Maestro debit card, and free SEPA. The differentiator from ABN is the international student onboarding programme that ING markets aggressively at universities. They show up at orientation weeks. They run pop-up desks at Erasmus, TU Delft, Leiden, Groningen, and Maastricht during the first two weeks of September.

If your university partners with ING — and many do — you can sometimes open the account at an on-campus desk in 30 minutes. You walk in with your passport, enrollment letter, and BSN slip from the *gemeente*. You walk out with a temporary card and a Dutch IBAN that is live immediately. The permanent card arrives by post within a week.

Where ING falls behind ABN is the credit card. ING does not include a free credit card with the student account. You can request a credit card separately but it costs EUR 1.50 to EUR 3.00 per month depending on the tier, and approval for first-year students with no Dutch credit history is hit-and-miss. For students who do not need a credit card, ING is arguably the better choice because the app is slightly cleaner and the in-person support during freshers' week is unbeatable. For students who want a credit card included, ABN wins.

## Rabobank Direct — the cooperative option

Rabobank is the third *grootbank* and the one most associated with rural Netherlands and the *Randstad*'s old-money side. For students, the relevant product is *RaboDirect Student*, free under 27 with proof of enrollment. It is similar to ING in functionality: free debit card, free SEPA, English app, no included credit card.

The reason Rabobank lags ABN and ING for international students is the onboarding experience. Rabobank's English flow exists but it is less polished. The app is in English. The website is in English. The phone support is in English. But the welcome materials at universities are dominated by ING and ABN, so most students never encounter Rabobank during orientation. If your university is in Wageningen, Nijmegen, or anywhere in the agricultural east, Rabobank has stronger local presence and might be the natural choice. Otherwise ABN or ING is the easier path.

## Revolut — the bridge and travel account

Revolut is your second account, never your first. Here is why. Revolut gives you a Lithuanian IBAN (LT). It is technically a SEPA IBAN so it works for most euro transfers. But Dutch institutions — especially DUO for student loans, some employers for salary, occasionally landlords for rent direct debits — sometimes reject non-NL IBANs on principle even though it is technically illegal under EU IBAN discrimination law.

That said, Revolut is excellent as a bridge account. Open it from your home country before you fly. Get a physical card delivered to your home address. Use it for the first week of spending in the Netherlands while you wait for your Dutch IBAN to be useful. Use it for travel within Europe — Revolut's FX rates beat every Dutch bank's. Use it for currency conversion when your parents send GBP, USD, INR, or CNY. Use it as the card you give to subscriptions you might want to cancel quickly because Revolut's virtual disposable cards are excellent.

What you should not do is use Revolut as your only Dutch account. You will eventually hit something that rejects the LT IBAN. Pair it with Bunq day one, ABN week three, and you have all bases covered.

## N26 — skip as a primary, optional as a tertiary

N26 is a German neobank with a German (DE) IBAN. The product is fine. The app is clean. Spending in Europe is free. The metal card is nice if you like that. The problem for Dutch students is identical to Revolut's problem but worse: DUO occasionally rejects DE IBANs, some landlords are wary, and N26 has had a few years of negative press in the Netherlands about customer service response times.

If you already have an N26 account from living in Germany, Spain, or France before moving to the Netherlands, keep it for legacy reasons. Do not open one fresh as a Dutch student. There is no advantage over Bunq or Revolut.

## DUO student loans — the rule that decides your primary account

DUO is the Dutch student finance authority. If you are an EU national, an EU-equivalent (long-term residents, certain Master's programmes), or a refugee, you may qualify for DUO funding ranging from a basic *aanvullende beurs* (supplementary grant) to a full *studielening* (student loan) covering tuition and living costs.

DUO pays out to Dutch IBANs only. Officially the rules say "SEPA IBANs accepted" but in practice DUO's compliance team flags non-NL IBANs for manual review and rejects roughly 30% of them on first pass. Students appeal, win, and get paid eventually, but it takes four to eight weeks. If you are budgeting on DUO arriving on the 25th of every month, you cannot afford that gap.

The safe DUO IBANs in 2026 are: ABN AMRO, ING, Rabobank, SNS, ASN, RegioBank, Triodos, Knab, and Bunq (which transitioned from "fintech with NL IBAN" to "DUO-accepted" status in 2023 and has stayed there). Revolut, N26, Wise — not safe for DUO.

If you might apply for DUO funding at any point in your studies, open one of the DUO-safe accounts. ABN AMRO Studentenpakket is the default recommendation for this reason alone.

## Receiving tuition from outside the eurozone

Many international students come from India, China, the US, UK, Brazil, Turkey, or Nigeria. Their parents pay tuition and living costs from currencies that are not the euro. The naive approach is to ask the parent's home bank to send a SWIFT wire to the Dutch IBAN. This works but it is expensive. The home bank charges a EUR 25-50 fee. The correspondent bank in between charges another EUR 15-30. The receiving Dutch bank sometimes charges a small inbound fee. The exchange rate is the bank's marked-up rate, typically 2-4% worse than mid-market.

On a EUR 8,000 tuition transfer, the naive route costs EUR 200-400 in invisible fees.

The better route is Wise. Your parent opens a Wise account in their home country, funds it in their local currency, and sends a SEPA transfer (in euros, from a Wise EUR account) to your Dutch IBAN. The conversion happens inside Wise at the real mid-market rate plus a transparent fee that is usually 0.4-0.6% of the converted amount. On EUR 8,000, that is EUR 32-48 instead of EUR 200-400.

This is why Wise sits in the recommended stack. It is not a Dutch student account — Wise does not target students directly. It is the channel for inbound family transfers. If your parents pay your tuition or living costs from outside the eurozone, opening a Wise account on their side is the single biggest financial optimisation you can make this year.

## Comparing fees side by side

For a student under 30 enrolled at a Dutch institution, the monthly account fee picture in 2026 looks like this. ABN AMRO Studentenpakket: EUR 0. ING Studenten Betaalrekening: EUR 0. Rabobank RaboDirect Student: EUR 0. Bunq Easy Bank Student (with verified enrollment): EUR 0. Bunq Easy Bank without student verification: EUR 2.99. Revolut Standard: EUR 0. N26 Standard: EUR 0.

The fee fight is not about monthly account costs. It is about transaction costs. Non-euro card payments and ATM withdrawals abroad are where banks make money on students who travel. ABN charges 1.2% FX margin plus EUR 2.95 per non-SEPA ATM withdrawal. ING is similar. Rabobank slightly cheaper at 1.0% FX. Bunq charges 0% FX up to EUR 1,000 per month and 0.5% above. Revolut charges 0% FX on weekdays up to EUR 1,000 per month and 0.5% weekends/above. N26 charges 0% FX on the standard tier with limits.

For a student travelling around Europe on weekends — Berlin, Paris, Barcelona, Prague — the fintechs are dramatically cheaper. For a student staying mostly in the Netherlands and Belgium, the difference is small.

## English support: the realistic picture

All six banks claim full English support. The reality is graded.

Fully English by design: Bunq, Revolut, N26. The app, the website, the support chat, the email replies, the legal documents. Native English throughout.

English-first with Dutch fallback: ABN AMRO and ING. The app and main website are English. Some sub-pages, some forms, some terms and conditions documents drift into Dutch. Customer service is good in English but you sometimes get a Dutch-first agent who switches. Branch support in Amsterdam is fine in English; branch support in smaller cities can be Dutch-dominant.

English available but Dutch dominant: Rabobank. Good in English when you ask, but the cooperative branch system means you sometimes get a *lokaal bankkantoor* (local branch) where staff prefer Dutch. The app is English. Phone support exists in English but the queue is longer.

For an international student, this gradient rarely matters because you will use the app 99% of the time. But for the 1% — losing a card, disputing a charge, sorting out a frozen account because compliance flagged something — the gradient becomes very real.

## Common mistakes I see international students make

Number one: waiting for the BSN before opening any account. This is the most common mistake. Students arrive, realise the *gemeente* appointment is three weeks out, and try to live on cash and foreign cards racking up FX fees. Bunq solves this. Open it before you fly.

Number two: using Revolut or N26 for DUO. The Lithuanian or German IBAN gets flagged, payment delays four weeks, student panics. Always use a Dutch (NL) IBAN for DUO.

Number three: skipping the credit card. International students sometimes feel they do not need a credit card "because Europe is debit-friendly." Then they try to book a flight on Skyscanner that requires a credit card, or rent a car for a weekend trip to Belgium, or sign up for a US streaming service. The ABN AMRO Studentenpakket free credit card solves this with zero monthly cost. Get it.

Number four: keeping all your money in one account. Spread risk. Bunq + ABN means if one freezes for compliance review (it happens, especially in your first three months when banks watch for unusual patterns), you still have access to money in the other.

Number five: not opening Wise when your parents send money in non-EUR. Genuinely costs students hundreds of euros per year in invisible FX fees. Wise costs nothing to open and saves on every transfer.
