Daycare could not cover the early hours and a Dutch nanny quoted €27 an hour. She asked me a question I get a lot. "Sarah, what does an au pair from the Philippines actually cost us, all-in, for a year?"

I sat down at her kitchen table with a notebook and we mapped the whole thing out, line by line. That budget conversation is the one I want to recreate for you in this article, with the 2026 numbers I am working with for clients right now.

> **Quick answer:** Hiring an au pair in the Netherlands costs a host family between €10,000 and €14,000 for a full twelve-month stay in 2026. That includes €340 monthly pocket money, €1,500 to €2,500 in agency fees, the €243 IND application fee, €60 to €90 a month for mandatory insurance, roughly €300 to €500 of in-kind food and accommodation per month, plus the international flight and integration extras. For families with two or more children it is usually cheaper than daycare or a private nanny, but only if you treat the au pair as cultural exchange and not as a cheap employee.

## Why families even consider an au pair in the Netherlands

I want to be honest from the start. An au pair is not a babysitter and not a nanny. The Dutch immigration office (IND) calls the programme "cultural exchange" and that framing matters because it controls everything from the maximum hours to the type of work allowed.

The families I coach usually come to me when two things collide. First, a parent gets a job offer with international hours, often in tech, finance or healthcare. Second, they realise that Dutch daycare (kinderdagverblijf) closes at 18:30 and the BSO (buitenschoolse opvang) cannot bridge a 7:00 start. A live-in au pair solves the schedule problem in a way that no daycare ever will.

The trade-off is that you are inviting a young adult into your home for a year. If you are not ready for that you should stop reading and look at my piece on [the cost of having a baby in the Netherlands](/blog/cost-of-having-a-baby-netherlands-2026/) which covers daycare and nanny alternatives in much more detail.

## The IND rules you must know before you budget

I cannot count how many host parents have called me after signing an agency contract without reading the IND rules. The numbers that follow assume you stay inside the legal framework. If you break the rules the IND can revoke the permit, ban the family from future sponsorship and fine you. Worse, your au pair is sent home.

Here is the framework I work with in 2026.

- The au pair must be between 18 and 30 years old at the start of the stay.
- She or he cannot have lived in the Netherlands before on any other permit.
- Maximum 8 hours a day and 30 hours a week of light household tasks and childcare.
- Two full days off per week are mandatory.
- The maximum stay is 12 months, no extension.
- The host family must have at least one minor child living at home.
- The family income must be at least 1.5 times the Dutch minimum wage on a sustainable basis.
- A recognised sponsor (almost always an agency) submits the application.
- The au pair must have health and accident insurance for the entire stay.
- The €340 monthly pocket money is a hard minimum, not a target.

If your family situation does not match these points, an au pair is not the right route. Have a look at [our MVV family reunification guide](/blog/mvv-visa-family-reunification-netherlands-2026/) for alternative permits when, for example, you want to bring a relative who is older than 30 to help with childcare.

- **Au Pair International** (Eindhoven): around €1,495 to €1,795. Cheapest of the four, mostly European candidates.

Cheaper than that and you are usually dealing with a marketplace that does not act as IND sponsor, which means you carry all the legal risk yourself. I do not recommend it.

### The €243 IND fee

The IND application for the au pair residence permit costs €243 in 2026. The agency pays this on your behalf and adds it to your invoice. There is no way around it and no discount. It covers the residence permit decision and the biometric appointment at the IND desk after arrival.

### Pocket money: why €340 is sacred

The €340 a month is a net minimum set by Dutch regulation. It is not a wage and it is not taxed. I always tell host families to pay it on the first of the month in cash or by SEPA transfer, never late and never partial. Some families top up the pocket money. I think that is fine as long as you are clear that the extra is a gift and not a wage. The moment you start paying for "extra hours" you cross into employment, which is exactly what the IND wants to prevent.

### Food, accommodation and the in-kind value

The agency contract will list an in-kind value for room and board, usually around €600 a month combined. That is not money that leaves your account, but it is real spending. Your grocery bill goes up by maybe €150 to €250 a month for an extra adult. Your gas and electricity go up too, especially in winter. Read my piece on [Dutch utilities for expats](/blog/dutch-utilities-explained-expats-2026/) if you want to understand how much a longer shower and an extra laptop in the house really cost.

The room itself does not need to be huge but it must be private, lockable, heated, with a window and a bed. A converted study or attic room is fine. A mattress in the living room is not.

### Insurance: do not improvise this one

The IND requires proof of health and accident insurance for the entire stay. There are two routes I see families take.

1. **Specialised au pair insurance via the agency**: roughly €60 to €75 a month. Includes liability, repatriation and dental basics. Easy and IND-approved.

For one child the kinderopvangtoeslag refund makes daycare the cheapest option in 2026, particularly because the government raised the maximum hourly rate again in January. The point is not that an au pair is "cheap". The point is that for a specific family profile, dual-income with two or more young children and irregular hours, an au pair beats every other option on flexibility per euro.

## Hidden costs nobody mentions on the agency website

I keep a list of these in my notebook because they catch every first-time host family by surprise.

### The integration tax

Most au pairs have never lived alone, never cooked for a family of five and never cycled in Dutch rain. The first month you will spend extra on takeaway because dinner does not happen, on a proper raincoat and bike lights, on a public transport top-up because she gets lost twice, and on phone credit because she will FaceTime home every evening.

Budget €300 to €500 for the first month on top of everything else. It is worth it. Joy was cooking adobo for the twins by week six.

### The Dutch course

Not mandatory, but the IND likes to see that you are giving your au pair something to take home other than memories. A basic A1 Dutch course at a community school runs €250 to €600 for the year. Many municipalities subsidise it for non-EU residents. Worth asking at your local gemeente.

### Family holidays

If you travel for two weeks and take the au pair with you, you pay her flight, accommodation and meals. If you leave her behind, you still pay pocket money and you still owe her food. There is no "pause" in the contract.

### Phone, transport, gym

Most agencies recommend you provide a Dutch SIM (around €10 a month), a bicycle, and either an OV-chipkaart with a small credit or a monthly transport allowance. Some families also offer a gym membership. None of this is mandatory but it materially improves the year for everyone.

### The rematch scenario

Sometimes it does not work. The au pair is homesick, the children do not connect, the chemistry is off. A good agency will rematch within four to eight weeks. During the gap you still pay pocket money to the original au pair until she leaves, and you may pay agency admin fees of €200 to €500 for the new search. Budget for it mentally.

## Taxes, payroll and the magic words "cultural exchange"

This is where I see Americans, in particular, get confused. In the US an au pair is technically an employee and taxes apply. In the Netherlands she is not. The legal frame is cultural exchange.

That means:

- No payroll tax (loonheffing).
- No social security contributions for the family.
- No holiday pay (vakantiegeld).
- No pension contribution.
- No 30% ruling shenanigans, even if your au pair earns above some threshold (she will not).
- No need to register her with the Belastingdienst as an employee.

The host family does not need a BSN-based payroll. The au pair gets her own BSN at the gemeente after arrival, which she uses for her insurance, her bank account and her Dutch phone contract. Most agencies arrange the gemeente appointment in the first week.

If your au pair earns money on the side, say tutoring or babysitting outside the family, she has to declare it and you should not be involved. Here is the timeline I gave Marieke, adjusted to 2026 dates.

- **Month -6 to -4**: choose an agency, sign the contract, pay first instalment.
- **Month -4 to -2**: review candidates, video interview shortlist, choose.
- **Month -3**: agency submits IND application, you pay the €243 fee.

- **Month 2 to 11**: cruise.
- **Month 12**: goodbye, certificate from the agency, flight home. No extensions.

Mark the end date in your calendar. Several families I know have been caught at month 11 with no childcare lined up because they assumed they could extend "just a few weeks". The IND does not allow it.

## When an au pair is the wrong answer

I will not sell you something you do not need. An au pair is the wrong answer if:

- You have only one child and daycare hours fit your schedule. Use [the daycare numbers in my baby cost guide](/blog/cost-of-having-a-baby-netherlands-2026/) instead.
- You need someone for more than 30 hours a week. Hire a nanny.
- You want help with cleaning or cooking for the adults. Hire a cleaner and a meal kit.
- You cannot offer a private room. The IND will reject the application.
- You expect a sibling figure who babysits every Saturday night. The two days off rule is strict.
- You will be travelling more than 4 weeks a year and cannot bring her along.

If you are pulling a child out of an [international school](/blog/international-school-netherlands-cost-2026/) because the after-school care is too expensive, an au pair can help bridge that gap, but only if the school hours are compatible with 30 hours a week of total childcare.

## FAQ

**How much does an au pair really cost a family in the Netherlands per year?**

Realistically I budget between €10,000 and €14,000 for one full year. That covers €340 pocket money each month, €300 to €500 of in-kind food and accommodation value, agency fees of €1,500 to €2,500, the IND fee of €243, mandatory insurance at €60 to €90 a month, and the flight plus extras.

**Is an au pair cheaper than Dutch daycare or a nanny?**

For one child daycare is usually cheaper after the kinderopvangtoeslag refund. For two or three children an au pair becomes the cheapest option, especially if you also need flexible early-morning or evening hours that a daycare cannot offer.

**Can I hire an au pair without going through an agency?**

Technically you can sponsor an au pair yourself if you become a recognised IND sponsor, but the paperwork, audits and fees make it impractical for almost every family I know. Using a recognised agency from the IND public register is the normal route.

**How many hours per week can my au pair work?**

The IND rules are strict. Maximum eight hours a day and thirty hours a week of light household tasks and childcare. Two full days off per week are required and the stay is capped at twelve months in total.

**Do I need to pay tax or social security for an au pair?**

No. The relationship is cultural exchange and not employment, so there is no payroll tax, no holiday pay and no pension. The €340 pocket money is net and the in-kind food and lodging are not taxed for the family.

**What insurance is mandatory and who pays?**

The host family pays. You need either a private expat health and accident package such as SafetyWing or a Dutch basisverzekering plus liability insurance. Budget €60 to €90 a month. Without proof of cover the IND will not issue the residence permit.
