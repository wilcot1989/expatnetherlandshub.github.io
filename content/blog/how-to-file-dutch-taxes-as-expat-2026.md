My first Dutch tax return was a disaster — not catastrophically, but enough to cost me money and months of stress. I filed as a partial non-resident (buitenlandse belastingplichtige) when I should have filed as a full resident (binnenlandse belastingplichtige). At the time, I genuinely thought I was doing the right thing. I'd moved to Amsterdam in September of the year before, still had my UK bank account, my parents' address was still listed somewhere. Partial non-resident seemed logical.

Eighteen months later, a correction letter (naheffingsaanslag) arrived in my mailbox. The Belastingdienst had reassessed my return, applied the correct status, and I owed an additional €840 plus interest. Not world-ending, but completely avoidable. I spent a week on the phone with a tax advisor sorting it out, paid her €380 for the privilege, and came out the other side determined to actually understand this system.

Now I file every April in about 90 minutes, usually on a Saturday morning with coffee. I get a refund most years. The system is genuinely less complicated than it looks once you understand the logic — which is what this guide is about.

## The Dutch tax year at a glance

The Dutch tax year runs from January 1 to December 31 — a full calendar year, no surprises there. But here's what trips up most expats: you file your return for the *previous* year. So in spring 2026, you're filing for the 2025 tax year.

The filing window opens on March 1. The standard deadline is April 30. Miss that? You can request an automatic extension online via the Belastingdienst that pushes your deadline to September 1. If you use a tax advisor, they can request a further extension on your behalf through a professional arrangement called *uitstelregeling*, often extending to May of the following year.

Refunds typically land in your account within six weeks of submitting. Payments due work the same way — you get a bill, and you have a few weeks to pay it.

If the Belastingdienst sends you a letter inviting you to file (*uitnodiging aangifte*), that's not optional. But even without an invitation, you can and sometimes should file voluntarily. More on that below.

For more background on the full first-year setup, including getting your BSN sorted before you can do any of this, see my [complete first year in the Netherlands guide](/blog/first-year-netherlands-expat-pillar-2026/).

## The three-box system explained

The Dutch tax system splits income into three "boxes," each taxed at different rates. This is the single most important concept to grasp.

### Box 1 — Income from work and home

Box 1 covers:
- Salary from employment
- Freelance and self-employment income
- Social security benefits (AOW pension, WW unemployment, etc.)
- Income from your own home (notional rental value, or *eigenwoningforfait*)

In 2026, Box 1 uses two rates:
- **36.97%** on income up to €75,518
- **49.50%** on income above €75,518

Most salaried expats will only hit the first bracket unless they're in a senior role. The 36.97% rate includes both income tax and national insurance contributions (volksverzekeringen), which is why it's higher than many people expect.

If you're employed, your employer already withholds *loonheffing* (payroll tax) monthly. When you file your return, the system credits those payments against what you actually owe. The difference is either a refund or an additional payment.

For employees who file their own return, I've written a dedicated guide: [tax return Netherlands for employee expats 2026](/blog/tax-return-netherlands-employee-expats-2026/).

If you're freelancing, the calculation is more involved. You deduct business expenses first, then pay Box 1 tax on your profit. There are also some freelancer-specific deductions like the *zelfstandigenaftrek* (self-employment deduction) and *MKB-winstvrijstelling* (SME profit exemption). Full details are in my [tax filing guide for freelance expats 2026](/blog/tax-filing-freelance-expat-netherlands-2026/).

### Box 2 — Income from substantial shareholding

Box 2 applies if you own 5% or more of a company's shares — a *aanmerkelijk belang*. This includes dividends paid out and capital gains when you sell those shares.

The 2026 rates:
- **24.5%** on income up to €67,000
- **31%** on income above €67,000

Most expats don't deal with Box 2 at all unless they're founders or directors with a personal holding company (*BV*). If that's you, you almost certainly need a tax advisor — the BV structure and director salary requirements alone warrant professional help.

### Box 3 — Savings and investments

Box 3 is where things get interesting, and where many expats get caught out.

Box 3 taxes your *wealth* — specifically your savings, investments, and other assets above a threshold — not the actual return you earn on them, but a *deemed return* (fictief rendement) that the government calculates.

**2026 parameters:**
- Tax-free threshold: **€57,000** per person (€114,000 for fiscal partners)
- Tax rate: **36%** on the deemed return
- The deemed return percentages vary by asset type (cash, investments, property)

If you have €80,000 in savings, only €23,000 (the amount above €57,000) is subject to the deemed return calculation. The actual tax you pay is typically much less than it sounds — but it does apply.

**Critical point for expats:** your foreign savings and investments count too. Your UK savings account, your pension pot from back home (in some cases), shares you hold with a foreign broker — these all need to be declared in Box 3 if the combined total with your Dutch assets exceeds the threshold. The Belastingdienst has data-sharing agreements with tax authorities across the EU and beyond. Omitting foreign assets is not a clever strategy; it's how you end up with a correction letter like mine.

Speaking of foreign investments: if you're using a Dutch broker like [DeGiro](/blog/degiro-review-expats-netherlands-2026/), they provide an annual tax statement that makes your Box 3 declaration straightforward. Foreign brokers usually don't, so you'll need to pull the year-end statement yourself.

For crypto specifically, the rules are particular — read my separate [crypto tax Netherlands expats guide](/blog/crypto-tax-netherlands-expats-2026/) before assuming your holdings work the same as savings.

## Step-by-step: how to file your Dutch tax return

### Step 1: Get your DigiD

Before you can access anything in the Dutch government's online systems, you need a DigiD — the national digital identity credential. If you've been in the Netherlands for a while, you probably already have one. If you don't, go to [digid.nl](https://www.digid.nl) and apply.

The standard route sends a verification letter (with a code) to your registered home address. That takes 5-7 days. If you have a Dutch phone number, you can often verify faster via the DigiD app using document scanning. The app route has improved a lot — I'd recommend it.

One thing: the DigiD process requires a BSN. If you're still sorting your BSN registration, do that first. My [BSN application guide](/blog/expat-bsn-application-guide-2026/) walks through the whole process.

### Step 2: Log into Mijn Belastingdienst

Go to [belastingdienst.nl](https://www.belastingdienst.nl/wps/wcm/connect/nl/aangifte-en-betalen/aangifte-en-betalen) and find the *Aangifte inkomstenbelasting* (income tax return) section. Log in with your DigiD.

The portal opens on March 1 for the previous year's return. The interface is in Dutch only, which is annoying. If you can read basic Dutch, you can absolutely handle it. If official letters from the government are still a mystery to you, consider spending a few weeks with a Dutch tutor on platforms like <a href="https://go.expatnetherlandshub.com/preply" target="_blank" rel="nofollow sponsored noopener">Preply</a> — understanding the terminology pays for itself quickly when you're not misreading a crucial question on a tax form.

### Step 3: Review the pre-filled data

Here's the pleasant surprise: the Belastingdienst pre-fills a lot. Your employer has submitted your annual salary statement (*jaaropgave*) electronically, so Box 1 employment income should already be there. Your bank may have submitted your end-of-year balance for Box 3. Some other data points — like AOW entitlements, child benefit — also come through automatically.

Do not just accept the pre-filled data and click submit. Check everything. Common errors:
- Old address still listed
- Employer submitted incorrect figures (it happens)
- Missing or wrong loonheffing withheld
- Partner data not correctly linked

If you had the 30% ruling in 2025, verify that it's correctly reflected. The ruling should reduce your taxable income by 30% — if that's missing from the pre-filled data, it's a significant error. The [30% ruling guide](/blog/30-percent-ruling-extension-2024-changes-2026/) explains what to look for.

### Step 4: Add your Box 3 assets

This is the step most expats skip or underestimate. You need to add:

- Dutch bank account balances (as of January 1 of the tax year)
- Dutch investment accounts (value on January 1)
- Foreign bank accounts (value on January 1, converted to EUR at the exchange rate on that date)
- Foreign investment accounts
- Other assets: a second property, crypto holdings, loans you've extended to third parties

The date that matters for Box 3 is January 1 of the tax year — not December 31, not the date you file. So for your 2025 return, it's January 1, 2025.

The threshold of €57,000 applies per person. If you have a fiscal partner (your registered partner or spouse in the Netherlands), you can split Box 3 assets between you however you want — this can reduce the combined tax significantly.

If you're using <a href="https://go.expatnetherlandshub.com/wise" target="_blank" rel="nofollow sponsored noopener">Wise</a> for your international money transfers or hold a balance there, that Wise balance also counts as a foreign financial asset for Box 3 purposes. Wise makes it easy to download year-end statements, so at least the admin is painless.

### Step 5: Add your deductions

More on specific deductions below, but in this step you're entering anything that reduces your taxable income. The most common ones:

- Mortgage interest (*hypotheekrenteaftrek*)
- Certified charitable donations (*giftenaftrek*)
- Work-related study costs (*studiekosten* — limited since 2022 but some situations still qualify)
- Healthcare costs above the income-dependent threshold (*aftrek specifieke zorgkosten*)

### Step 6: Check your residency status

This is where I went wrong. Make sure the form reflects your actual status:

- **Binnenlandse belastingplichtige** (resident taxpayer): you lived in the Netherlands for the full year, or for the part of the year after you arrived. You're taxed on your worldwide income.
- **Buitenlandse belastingplichtige** (non-resident taxpayer): you lived outside the Netherlands but had Dutch income. Limited taxation, fewer deductions available.

If you arrived partway through the year, the system handles this through the *M form* (migrant form) — a paper form that covers the period you were and weren't resident. This is more complex, and honestly the first year with a mid-year arrival is the one I'd suggest getting professional help with, just to establish the correct baseline.

### Step 7: Submit and track

Once everything looks right, submit. The system gives you a confirmation and a reference number. You can track the status in Mijn Belastingdienst. Refunds come through within six weeks in most cases. If you owe money, you'll receive a payment reference with a due date.

## Deductions expats commonly miss

### Mortgage interest (hypotheekrenteaftrek)

If you own your home in the Netherlands and have a mortgage, the interest you pay is deductible from Box 1 income. This is one of the most valuable deductions in the Dutch system — worth thousands of euros per year for many homeowners.

The deduction isn't unlimited (it's capped and phased down over 30 years of mortgage), but in the early years of a Dutch mortgage it's substantial. Your mortgage lender sends you an annual statement with the interest paid — enter that figure in the relevant section.

Note that alongside the deduction, you also add back a small *eigenwoningforfait* (notional rent) to your Box 1 income. The deduction is almost always larger than this addition, so it still comes out in your favour.

### Gifts to ANBI-registered charities (giftenaftrek)

Donations to charities registered with the Dutch government as *ANBI* (Algemeen Nut Beogende Instelling) are deductible. There's a threshold — only the portion above 1% of your income (minimum €60) is deductible, up to 10% of income.

If you have a standing annual gift arrangement (*periodieke gift*) committed for at least 5 years, there's no threshold — 100% is deductible. This is worth setting up if you regularly donate significant amounts.

### Healthcare costs (specifieke zorgkosten)

Specific medical costs above an income-dependent threshold can be deducted. This includes costs not covered by your basic insurance (*basisverzekering*) — things like prescribed aids, some therapy, transport to medical appointments. The threshold is fairly high, so this only helps if you had significant unreimbursed medical expenses.

### Work-related study costs (studiekosten)

The old *scholingsaftrek* was abolished in 2022, replaced by a system of study allowances (STAP budget, which itself was cancelled in 2024). The situation as of 2026 is that very limited situations still allow a study cost deduction — essentially transition rules from the old system. If you had significant work-related study costs, check with a tax advisor whether any of it qualifies.

## Foreign asset declaration: don't skip this

I want to emphasise this again because it catches so many expats.

From the day you become a Dutch resident, you're required to declare your worldwide income in Box 1 and your worldwide assets in Box 3. That includes:

- Bank accounts in your home country
- Investment accounts abroad
- Foreign property you own (not your Dutch home — that's in Box 1)
- Pension pots from previous employers in other countries (rules vary by treaty)
- Savings in your parents' country, if the account is in your name

The Belastingdienst participates in the Common Reporting Standard (CRS), a global automatic exchange of financial information. Banks in most countries send data about non-resident account holders to their local tax authority, which then shares with the Netherlands. They often know about your foreign accounts before you've told them.

For pension matters specifically, the rules differ depending on tax treaties with your home country. UK, US, and many EU country pension arrangements have specific treaty provisions. This is where the M form gets complicated. See my guide on [Dutch pension transfer and foreign pensions](/blog/dutch-pension-transfer-expats-2026/) for more detail.

## The 30% ruling and your tax return

If you have the 30% ruling, your taxable salary is already reduced at source — your employer applies it monthly. But you still need to check a few things on your tax return:

1. The ruling period is correctly reflected (it has a start and end date)
2. The partial non-residency option (*keuzerecht*) — whether to use it — is actively considered, not just defaulted

The keuzerecht is complex. Electing partial non-residency means your Box 3 wealth is only taxed on Dutch assets, not your foreign savings. That sounds great, but it also limits some deductions (like mortgage interest). Whether it helps you depends entirely on your balance sheet.

This is a genuine calculation exercise, not an obvious default. The [30% ruling changes guide](/blog/30-percent-ruling-extension-2024-changes-2026/) covers the 2024 changes that affected many expat holders, and what that means for your 2025 filing.

## What about vakantiegeld and bonuses?

Many expats are surprised to discover that holiday allowance (*vakantiegeld*, typically 8% of annual salary paid in May) and year-end bonuses are taxed as regular Box 1 income. Your employer withholds loonheffing on these payments when they're made, usually at a higher rate because the payment pushes you into a different bracket temporarily.

When you file your return, these amounts are reconciled with your actual annual income and the correct rate. If too much was withheld, it comes back as a refund. ## DIY vs. hiring a tax advisor

Here's my honest assessment after several years of doing this myself and having done it with a professional:

**File yourself if:**
- You're a salaried employee with one employer
- You don't own property or have a simple mortgage situation
- Your savings and investments are modest and straightforward
- You don't have significant foreign assets
- You don't have the 30% ruling (or have it but don't want to optimise the keuzerecht)
- Your partner, if applicable, has a similarly simple situation

**Hire a specialist if:**
- You arrived or left mid-year (M form)
- You want to optimise the 30% ruling keuzerecht calculation
- You have significant foreign savings, investments, or property
- You're self-employed or have a BV
- You earn income in multiple countries
- You have a complex pension situation (US, UK, or country with unusual treaty terms)
- The Belastingdienst sent you a questionnaire or correction notice

**What does it cost?** A straightforward expat tax return via services like Blue Umbrella costs around €199. More complex situations through firms like Expat Tax, PwC, or Deloitte's private client division run €350–€700+. For genuinely complex international situations, don't be surprised by higher fees.

DIY is free. The Belastingdienst portal is usable. If you speak enough Dutch to navigate it (or are willing to use browser translation carefully), you can absolutely do your own return in a straight employee situation.

**Before you file, gather:**
- [ ] DigiD working and app installed

## My recommendation

If your situation is standard — Dutch employer, one country, no complex investments — do it yourself. The Belastingdienst portal is not elegant, but it works. Block two hours on a Saturday morning in late March, have your jaaropgave ready, and go through it section by section. You'll probably get a refund.

If anything is genuinely complex — especially mid-year arrival, 30% ruling optimisation, or significant foreign assets — budget for a specialist for your first year, then decide whether to continue or take it over yourself once you understand your specific situation.

The worst outcome is what happened to me: filing with incomplete information and finding out 18 months later. The system will catch errors. Much better to file correctly, even slowly, than to rush and guess.
