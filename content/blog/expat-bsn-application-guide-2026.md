## What Is the BSN and Why Does It Matter So Much?

The BSN (Burgerservicenummer, or citizen service number) is a unique nine-digit number assigned to every person registered in the Dutch municipal records (BRP — Basisregistratie Personen). It is the single identifier that links you to the Dutch government, healthcare system, tax authority, banking system, and employer payroll.

Without a BSN you cannot:
- Open a current account at ABN AMRO, ING, Rabobank, or any traditional Dutch bank
- Register with a Dutch GP (huisarts)
- Receive salary through Dutch payroll (legally — some employers work around this temporarily)
- File a Dutch income tax return
- Access government services through DigiD
- Sign up for Dutch health insurance (basisverzekering)
- Apply for zorgtoeslag (healthcare allowance) or other benefits

The BSN is also required for many rental contracts, childcare registrations, and any formal interaction with Dutch government agencies. It is, in short, the foundation of your administrative life in the Netherlands.

The good news: getting a BSN is straightforward if you follow the right sequence. The bad news: the sequence is not obvious, and mistakes at any step create delays that compound.

## The BSN Application Process: Overview

The BSN is issued automatically when you register in the BRP (the Dutch Population Register) at your local gemeente (municipality). You do not apply for a BSN separately — it is generated by the registration itself.

The process has four steps:

1. Find your municipality and its burgerzaken (civic affairs) department
2. Book an appointment (inschrijving eerste vestiging or eerste inschrijving)
3. Attend the appointment with the correct documents
4. Wait for confirmation and your BSN

That is it. The complexity lies entirely in the details of steps 2 and 3.

---

## Step 1: Identify Your Municipality

The Netherlands has 342 municipalities. You must register at the gemeente where you actually live — not where you work, not where you are staying temporarily. If you move municipalities later, you notify your new gemeente and the records transfer automatically.

For most expats, the relevant municipality is one of the major cities:

- **Amsterdam** — gemeente.amsterdam.nl/burgerzaken
- **Rotterdam** — rotterdam.nl/burgerzaken
- **The Hague (Den Haag)** — denhaag.nl/burgerzaken
- **Utrecht** — utrecht.nl/burgerzaken
- **Eindhoven** — eindhoven.nl/burgerzaken
- **Groningen** — groningen.nl/burgerzaken
- **Breda, Tilburg, Nijmegen** — each has its own gemeente website

All gemeente websites have an English-language section for first registration. If you cannot find it, search "[city name] + first registration + BSN" or call the gemeente's main number and ask for the expat or international desk.

## Step 2: Book Your Appointment — Do This on Day One

This is the single most important thing I can tell you: **book your appointment on the day you arrive in the Netherlands, or the day before if possible.**

In larger cities — especially Amsterdam, Rotterdam, and The Hague — appointment slots for eerste inschrijving (first registration) fill up 2–4 weeks in advance. If you arrive on a Monday and think "I will book an appointment this week," you may find the next available slot is in three weeks. Meanwhile you have no BSN, no bank account, and potentially payroll issues with your employer.

### How to Book

Go to your gemeente's website and search for "eerste inschrijving" or "inschrijven als nieuwe inwoner." Most municipalities use an online booking system. You will need to:

1. Select the type of appointment (first registration / eerste vestiging)
2. Choose a date and time
3. Enter your name, date of birth, and contact details
4. Receive a confirmation email with your appointment details

Some municipalities — particularly smaller ones — allow walk-in appointments. Do not rely on this in any city with more than 100,000 residents.

### Employer-Facilitated Registration

If your employer has a relocation agency or uses a large employer's HR service, they may have a dedicated channel with the IND or the municipality that allows faster registration. Ask your HR department explicitly: "Can you arrange a BSN registration appointment for me, or do you work with a relocation company that can?" For highly skilled migrants (kennismigranten), some employers in Amsterdam use the Expat Center Amsterdam, which offers same-day or next-day BSN registration slots that are not available to the general public.

## Step 3: Gather Your Documents

This is where most delays happen. Missing or incorrect documents mean a failed appointment — and rebooking can add another 2–4 weeks.

### Documents Required for All Expats

1. **Valid passport or national ID card** — original document, not a copy. If your passport expires within 6 months, renew before applying (some countries require this; Dutch municipalities do not, but it causes complications later).

2. **Proof of address in the Netherlands** — one of the following:
   - Rental contract (huurcontract) in your name, showing the full address
   - Property purchase deed (koopakte)
   - A signed statement from the person at whose address you are staying (toestemmingsverklaring), confirming they consent to you registering there

3. **Appointment confirmation** — bring the email or QR code from your booking

### Additional Documents for Non-EU Expats

Non-EU expats must also bring:

4. **Valid residence permit or MVV entry visa** — your verblijfsvergunning (residence permit sticker in your passport or a separate card) must be valid. If you are in the Netherlands on an MVV (Machtiging tot Voorlopig Verblijf), that is sufficient while your residence permit application is pending.

5. **Birth certificate** — required in some municipalities, not all. It must be:
   - Original or certified copy
   - Legalised with an apostille (for Hague Convention countries) or double legalisation (for non-Hague countries)
   - Translated into Dutch, English, French, or German by a sworn (beëdigd) translator

6. **Marriage certificate or divorce decree** (if applicable) — same legalisation and translation requirements as the birth certificate

### Documents for EU Citizens

EU citizens (EEA nationals) only need documents 1–3 from the main list. Some municipalities may ask for a birth certificate, but it is not universally required for EU nationals.

### A Note on Address Proof

The most common problem I see is expats arriving with no acceptable address proof. Common situations:

- **Staying in a hotel or temporary accommodation:** A hotel booking is not accepted. You need a letter from the hotel or serviced apartment confirming you are a resident (some do this, most do not). Alternatively, register at a friend's address as a briefadres if they allow it.
- **Sharing a flat:** If your name is not on the main rental contract, you need a written permission letter (onderhuurcontract or toestemmingsverklaring) from the main tenant or the landlord.
- **Company housing:** If your employer provides housing, they should supply an official letter on company letterhead confirming your address.
- **No address yet:** Register as a briefadres at a trusted person's address. They must sign the declaration form and bring their ID when you register.

## Step 4: The Appointment Itself

Gemeente appointments for eerste inschrijving typically last 20–40 minutes. The desk officer will:

1. Verify your identity documents
2. Verify your proof of address
3. Enter your details into the BRP
4. Confirm the registration

You will **not** receive your BSN on the spot. The registration is confirmed in the system, and your BSN is generated and communicated to you within 3–5 working days, usually by letter sent to the address you registered. Some municipalities now also send it by email.

### What If Documents Are Questioned?

If the desk officer raises an issue with your documents, ask them to specify exactly what is missing or needs to be corrected. Get this in writing if possible (ask for a written summary or take notes). Do not leave the appointment without understanding exactly what you need to fix and the process for rebooking.

## EU vs. Non-EU Expat Paths: Key Differences

### EU Expat Path

- Simpler document requirements (passport + address proof)
- No residence permit needed
- No legalised documents usually required
- Same appointment process, same waiting time for BSN
- Typical total time from arrival to BSN: 1–2 weeks (depending on appointment availability)

### Non-EU Expat Path

- Must have IND-approved residence permit or MVV visa before registering
- Birth certificate and possibly marriage certificate required, with apostille + sworn translation
- If on a highly skilled migrant (kennismigrant) permit: employer has usually handled IND notification, which unlocks faster registration channels
- If on a student visa: university's international office often has a direct channel to the gemeente
- If on a family reunification visa: the sponsoring partner handles most of the paperwork
- Typical total time from arrival to BSN: 2–4 weeks (longer if documents need translation)

### The IND–Municipality Sequence

For non-EU expats, the sequence matters: the IND (Immigratie en Naturalisatiedienst) must approve your residence before you can register with the gemeente. If you arrived on an MVV, you have a 90-day window to convert it to a full verblijfsvergunning at an IND office. You can register with the gemeente during this window using your MVV sticker as proof of legal residence.

### 2. Apply for DigiD

DigiD is the Dutch government's digital authentication system. You need it to log into the Belastingdienst (tax authority), MijnOverheid, your health insurer's portal, and dozens of other services. Apply at digid.nl — you need your BSN, your Dutch mobile number, and a Dutch address. Activation takes 3–5 days by post.

### 3. Register with a GP (Huisarts)

Health insurance is mandatory in the Netherlands (unless covered by an employer's collective scheme). Register for basisverzekering at any health insurer within 4 months of registering as a resident. You will need your BSN. After that, register with a local GP — most practices accept new patients online or by phone with your BSN and insurance policy number.

### 4. Notify Your Employer

Send your BSN to your HR or payroll department immediately. Dutch employers are required to record your BSN for payroll tax purposes. Without it, they must apply the highest tax rate (loonheffing zonder loonheffingskorting), which means you will pay too much tax and need to reclaim it via your annual tax return.

### 5. Belastingdienst (Tax Authority)

The Belastingdienst automatically receives your BSN from the BRP. You do not need to "register" with the tax authority separately as an employee. However, if you are self-employed (ZZP), you need to register with the KvK (Chamber of Commerce) which triggers a BTW (VAT) number and automatic tax registration.

## Common Mistakes That Cause 4–6 Week Delays

### Mistake 1: Not Booking Immediately on Arrival

Already covered above, but worth repeating. In Amsterdam, Rotterdam, and The Hague, appointment slots fill up 2–4 weeks in advance. Book on day one.

### Mistake 2: Bringing a Rental Contract That Is Not in Your Name

A rental contract in your partner's name, flatmate's name, or employer's name does not prove your residence. You need a subletting agreement, a permission letter from the main tenant or landlord, or a new contract in your name.

### Mistake 3: Non-Apostilled Foreign Documents

Birth certificates and marriage certificates from non-EU countries that have not been apostilled are rejected by Dutch municipalities. The apostille is a standardised government seal issued by your home country's competent authority (varies by country). Allow 2–6 weeks to obtain one if you do not have it. Countries that are not Hague Convention members (e.g., some African and Asian countries) require double legalisation, which takes longer.

### Mistake 4: Translation by Non-Sworn Translators

The Netherlands requires foreign documents to be translated by a beëdigd vertaler (sworn/certified translator) registered with the Raad voor Rechtsbijstand. Google Translate printouts, translations by bilingual colleagues, or translations by non-registered translators are not accepted. Sworn translations for a birth certificate typically cost €50–€150 depending on language pair and complexity.

### Mistake 5: Assuming Your MVV Is Enough for Everything

Your MVV (entry visa sticker) allows you to enter the Netherlands and register with the gemeente. However, you still need to convert it to a verblijfsvergunning at the IND within 90 days. Missing this deadline creates serious legal complications. Book your IND appointment as soon as you have your MVV.

### Mistake 6: Moving Without Updating Your Registration

If you move to a new address, you must notify your current gemeente within 5 days of the move. Failure to do so is a registratieplicht violation, which carries a fine. If you move to a different municipality, register at the new gemeente — they handle the transfer automatically.

## Special Cases

### Briefadres (No Fixed Address)

If you have no fixed address — living in temporary accommodation, between flats, or in a housing emergency — you can register a briefadres. This is an address where you receive official mail, not necessarily where you live. Requirements:

- Written consent from the address holder
- The address holder must be registered at that address
- You must be actively seeking a permanent address (some municipalities require evidence of this)

The briefadres gives you a BSN and allows you to use government services, but some banks and employers will not accept a briefadres as your residential address for their purposes.

### Living Abroad While Working in the Netherlands

If you work in the Netherlands but live in Germany, Belgium, or elsewhere, you are a grenswerker (border worker) and do not register with a Dutch gemeente. You receive a BSN through the Belastingdienst directly — ask your employer's payroll department to initiate this. The process is different from the gemeente registration route.

### Short-Term Assignments (Under 4 Months)

If your assignment is shorter than 4 months, you may not need to register with the gemeente at all. Short-term registrations are possible for some categories (seasonal workers, project staff), but the rules are complex. Ask the IND or your employer's relocation company.

### Students

University international offices in the Netherlands almost always handle BSN registration for new international students. Check with your university's international student services before attempting to register independently — the university channel is usually faster.
