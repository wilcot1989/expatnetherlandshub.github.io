It sits 15 minutes from Amsterdam by tram, rents run €1,500–€3,500/month, and it has the largest Japanese expat community in the country. ## Who Actually Lives in Amstelveen?

According to the [gemeente Amstelveen](https://www.amstelveen.nl/), around 16% of the municipality's population is registered as non-Dutch nationals. That's approximately 15,000 expats in a town of 92,000 — one of the highest concentrations of any Dutch municipality outside The Hague.

The international mix is genuinely diverse:

- **Japanese:** 4,000–5,000 people, the largest Japanese expat community in the Netherlands
- **Indian:** a large community, many working in IT and pharma (KPMG, Accenture, Tata Consultancy Services all have Amsterdam/Schiphol-area offices)
- **American and British:** typically corporate expats at financial services firms or consultancies based in the Amsterdam Zuidas
- **Korean:** a smaller but established community with dedicated restaurants and social clubs
- **Dutch-speaking Belgians and expats from other EU countries:** professionals relocating for Amsterdam-area jobs who want more space than central Amsterdam allows

This density of international residents has a practical effect: almost every administrative office, doctor's practice, and school in Amstelveen is used to dealing with non-Dutch speakers. The town doesn't feel like a foreign experience the way smaller Dutch municipalities can.

## Neighborhoods in Amstelveen: Which One to Choose?

Amstelveen isn't one homogeneous place. Different neighborhoods suit different expat profiles. Here's what I tell people when they're deciding.

### Elsrijk

Elsrijk is the most popular neighborhood for expat families — particularly Japanese and Indian families. It's leafy, quiet, and well-maintained, with large detached and semi-detached houses, wide pavements, and a suburban atmosphere that feels deliberately family-oriented.

Rents in Elsrijk run high: €2,500–€4,000/month for a family home with a garden is normal. If your employer provides a housing allowance (common for senior relocation packages), Elsrijk is where most of that money goes. The upside is school access — both the Japanse School Amsterdam and good Dutch primary schools are nearby, and the neighbourhood is safe for kids cycling to school independently.

### Stadshart

Stadshart is Amstelveen's commercial and social heart — a modern shopping centre with 150+ shops, restaurants, a cinema, and easy tram access. Living near Stadshart suits single expats and couples more than families. Apartments here are typically newer, smaller, and better connected to Amsterdam.

Rent near Stadshart: €1,500–€2,200/month for a one- or two-bedroom apartment. This is the zone that gives you the most "urban" feel without leaving Amstelveen.

### Bankras

Bankras is one of Amstelveen's quieter residential districts, further from the main tram lines. Rents are slightly lower here — €1,800–€3,000/month for larger properties — and the neighbourhood attracts expats who prioritise space and calm over commute convenience. Car ownership is more common in Bankras.

### Kronenburg

Kronenburg is more mixed in character — a blend of 1970s and 1980s low-rise apartments and newer builds. It's accessible by bus and is popular with expats on middle-tier budgets who want to be in Amstelveen without paying Elsrijk prices. Expect €1,500–€2,500/month depending on the property.

### Groenelaan and Westwijk

These western neighbourhoods border the Amsterdamse Bos (Amsterdam Forest), which is one of Amstelveen's genuine lifestyle advantages — 1,000 hectares of parkland accessible by bike. Families who value outdoor access and a greener setting often end up here. Rents are slightly lower than Elsrijk, which makes them an attractive alternative.

## Housing Market in Amstelveen: What to Expect in 2026

The Amstelveen housing market has tightened significantly since 2023. The combination of high international demand, limited new supply, and the general Amsterdam-region housing squeeze means that finding a rental in Amstelveen takes effort.

**Typical rent ranges in 2026:**

| Property type | Rent range per month |
|---|---|
| Studio / 1-bedroom apartment | €1,300–€1,800 |
| 2-bedroom apartment | €1,800–€2,500 |
| 3-bedroom apartment or small house | €2,500–€3,200 |
| Family home with garden (4+ beds) | €3,200–€4,500 |

**What drives up the price:**
- Proximity to Stadshart tram stops
- Garden or outdoor space
- Parking included
- Newly renovated or furnished
- Elsrijk postcode (1181 or 1182)

Unfurnished is standard for longer-term rentals. Furnished properties exist but carry a 15–20% premium. ## Public Transport: Getting to Amsterdam and Schiphol

One of Amstelveen's core advantages is its OV connection to Amsterdam. This is better than most people expect before they arrive.

### Tram lines 5 and 25

These are the backbone of Amstelveen–Amsterdam public transport:

- **Line 5:** Amstelveen Binnenhof → Amsterdam Zuid → Amsterdam Centraal. Journey time from central Amstelveen to Amsterdam Zuid: 12–18 minutes. To Amsterdam Centraal: 30–40 minutes depending on your exact boarding stop.
- **Line 25:** Runs to Amsterdam via a slightly different route, also reaching Amsterdam Zuid and connecting to the metro.

Both trams run frequently — every 7–10 minutes during peak hours, every 12 minutes off-peak. They're reliable by Dutch standards and covered by the standard OV-chipkaart system.

### Bus connections

Multiple bus lines run from Amstelveen toward:
- Amsterdam Schiphol Airport (~20 minutes by bus or car)
- Amsterdam Arena / ArenA station (useful for connections south and east)
- Hoofddorp and Haarlemmermeer (for expats working in the Schiphol business district)

### Cycling

Much of central Amstelveen is bikeable to Amsterdam within 30–45 minutes via the Amsterdamse Bos route. Many expats cycle in spring and summer and switch to tram in winter. The cycling infrastructure is good.

### OV cost

A monthly OV subscription covering Amstelveen–Amsterdam travel runs approximately €80–€120/month depending on zones. For the [best prepaid SIM card in the Netherlands](/blog/best-prepaid-sim-card-netherlands-2026/) and other practical arrival tips, see my first-year guide.

## The Japanese Expat Community: Why Amstelveen?

This section is worth its own heading because Amstelveen's Japanese community is a genuine phenomenon — something that makes the town meaningfully different from every other Amsterdam suburb.

Amstelveen has been a hub for Japanese corporate expats since the 1970s and 1980s, when large Japanese companies — Sony, Mitsubishi, Panasonic, and others — established European headquarters near Schiphol. The community created its own infrastructure, which then became self-reinforcing: more Japanese families chose Amstelveen because the infrastructure was there, which expanded the infrastructure further.

In 2026, that infrastructure includes:

**Japanse School Amsterdam (JSA):** A full Japanese-curriculum school in Amstelveen, running from elementary through junior high school level. Children follow the Japanese national curriculum in Japanese, which allows smooth re-entry into the Japanese school system on return. This is the single most important reason Japanese families choose Amstelveen over Amsterdam itself or other suburbs.

**Japanese supermarkets:** Nishiki is the best-known — a proper Japanese grocery store with fresh tofu, Japanese vegetables, a full deli section, Japanese snacks, and specialty products that you simply cannot find in a Dutch Albert Heijn. There are also smaller Asian importers in the Stadshart area.

**Japanese restaurants:** Multiple sushi restaurants, ramen shops, and Japanese-owned cafes in and around the Stadshart. Not tourist sushi — food aimed at Japanese residents who know the difference.

**Japanese-speaking healthcare:** At least one GP practice with Japanese-speaking doctors or staff. For families with young children, having a paediatrician or GP who speaks Japanese reduces an enormous amount of anxiety.

**Social networks:** The Japanese Association Netherlands (Nichiran-kai) and multiple informal community groups organise events, festivals (including an annual Japanese cultural festival that draws several thousand visitors), and social connections for new arrivals.

My friend Kenji's family was folded into this community within weeks of arriving. His wife, who spoke no Dutch and limited English, had a social life and practical support network within a month. That would not have happened as quickly anywhere else in the Netherlands.

## International Schools in and Around Amstelveen

School choice drives many family relocation decisions, and Amstelveen's school access is genuinely strong.

**Japanse School Amsterdam:** For Japanese families specifically. Full Japanese curriculum.

**Amsterdam International Community School (AICS):** Located in Amsterdam (easily accessible by tram), AICS offers the International Baccalaureate (IB) from age 4 through 18. It's one of the main choices for English-speaking expat families in the greater Amsterdam area. Waiting lists exist for popular year groups — apply early.

**British School of Amsterdam:** Another Amsterdam-based international school accessible by tram, following the English National Curriculum through to A-levels.

**Local Dutch schools with international support:** Several Amstelveen primary schools have experience with non-Dutch-speaking children and run "taalklas" (language class) integration programs. For families planning to stay 3+ years, this can be a better long-term choice than an expensive international school, as children often integrate into Dutch schools effectively within 12–18 months.

## Healthcare for Expats in Amstelveen

Dutch healthcare is excellent but requires navigation. The basics:

**You need a GP (huisarts):** All specialist care in the Netherlands goes through a GP referral first. Register with a local Amstelveen GP as soon as you have your BSN number. In Amstelveen, many practices have experience with non-Dutch-speaking patients. The Japanese-speaking practice mentioned above is one example; several others have English-speaking doctors or staff.

**Health insurance is mandatory:** Every resident in the Netherlands must have Dutch health insurance — typically starting the month you register. The base premium in 2026 runs around €135–€160/month for basic coverage. You choose your insurer and package.

**Pharmacy:** Multiple apotheken in Amstelveen, all operating on the standard Dutch system. Prescriptions from your GP are dispensed at your registered pharmacy.

## Shopping in Amstelveen

Day-to-day shopping is comfortable in Amstelveen.

**Dutch supermarkets:** Albert Heijn (multiple locations, including a large AH at Stadshart), Jumbo, Lidl, and Aldi. The larger Albert Heijn branches stock a reasonable international selection.

**Nishiki Japanese supermarket:** The essential stop for Japanese residents, and interesting to non-Japanese expats as well. Stock includes fresh Japanese produce, prepared foods, and pantry staples not found elsewhere in the Amsterdam region.

**Asian grocery stores:** Several smaller shops near Stadshart carry Korean, Chinese, and South/Southeast Asian products.

**Stadshart shopping centre:** 150+ shops including clothing (Zara, H&M, various Dutch brands), homeware, electronics, a cinema, and a good range of restaurants and cafes. It's a proper regional shopping destination, not just a local strip.

**Amsterdam for the rest:** For international fashion brands, Ikea, specialty stores, or simply a larger selection, central Amsterdam is 15–20 minutes away by tram. Most Amstelveen residents combine local shopping with regular Amsterdam trips.

## Sports, Recreation, and Green Space

Amstelveen has better recreational infrastructure than its size might suggest.

**Amsterdamse Bos:** The Amsterdam Forest — 1,000 hectares of parkland directly adjacent to Amstelveen's western edge — is the area's greatest asset. Running, cycling, rowing, swimming in the outdoor pools (Bosbad) in summer, cross-country skiing in the rare snowy winters. A 10-minute bike ride from most of Amstelveen.

**Sports clubs:** Amstelveen has active clubs for football (AFC, one of the Netherlands' oldest clubs), tennis, hockey (HC Amstelveen is one of the top Dutch hockey clubs), swimming, and martial arts. Several clubs have English-speaking members and are used to integrating expat families.

**Skating:** In cold winters (rare), the outdoor skating infrastructure along canals and in the Bos becomes active.

**Cultural life:** The Cobra Museum of Modern Art is in Amstelveen — one of the Netherlands' better modern art museums, significantly less crowded than Amsterdam options. Worth knowing if you have visiting family to entertain.

## Practical Admin: Arriving in Amstelveen

### BSN registration

Before you can do almost anything in the Netherlands — open a bank account, register with a GP, sign a lease, get health insurance — you need a BSN (Burger Service Nummer). In Amstelveen, you register at the Stadsplein gemeente office.

Book an appointment as early as possible. Wait times have increased significantly across Dutch municipalities. See my [BSN application guide](/blog/expat-bsn-application-guide-2026/) for the step-by-step process and what documents to bring.

### Banking

You'll need a Dutch bank account quickly. Most expat arrivals open an account with ABN AMRO, ING, or Rabobank in the first weeks. Some find that Bunq or Revolut bridge the gap while waiting for a traditional account. See my [best banking for expats Netherlands guide](/blog/best-banking-expats-netherlands-2026/) for current options.

### DigiD

DigiD is your digital identity for Dutch government services — tax portal, health insurance administration, municipality communications. Apply online as soon as you have your BSN. You'll need it for almost everything.

### Car insurance (if you drive)

Amstelveen has reasonable public transport, but many expat families — particularly those in Elsrijk or Bankras — own a car for school runs, weekend trips, and Schiphol access. ## Cost of Living in Amstelveen vs Amsterdam

Amstelveen is cheaper than Amsterdam, but not dramatically so. The main saving is in rent. Everything else — groceries, utilities, transport — is broadly similar.

| Category | Amsterdam (comparable property) | Amstelveen |
|---|---|---|
| 2BR apartment rent | €2,200–€2,800/month | €1,800–€2,500/month |
| Utilities | €160–€180/month | €155–€175/month |
| Groceries (2 adults) | €500–€600/month | €480–€560/month |
| OV to work in Amsterdam | — | €80–€120/month extra |
| Eating out (2x/week) | €250–€350/month | €200–€280/month |

The net saving: roughly €300–€600/month compared to a central Amsterdam equivalent, after accounting for the OV cost. For families needing a 3- or 4-bedroom property, the saving is larger — those properties simply don't exist in central Amsterdam at comparable prices.

## Is Amstelveen Right for You?

After helping a dozen or so expat families and individuals think through this decision, I've noticed a pattern.

**Amstelveen works well if:**
- You have children, especially if they're Japanese and the JSA is relevant
- Your office is near Schiphol, Amsterdam Zuidas, or in the Amsterdam metro area (not central Amsterdam)
- You want a house with a garden, not an apartment
- You want quiet residential life with easy Amsterdam access
- Your housing budget is €2,000+/month and you want to maximise space for that budget

**Amstelveen works less well if:**
- You want to be in central Amsterdam — the tram is good but it's not the same as walking distance
- You're single and want city social life; Amstelveen is quiet in the evenings
- Your budget is under €1,500/month — the cheapest options are limited and often not in great condition

**Amsterdam itself may suit you better if:**
- Your work, social life, and personal preference are all urban
- You don't need school infrastructure for children
- You can find something within budget in the city
