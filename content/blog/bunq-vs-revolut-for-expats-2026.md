## The NL IBAN Question — Why It Actually Matters

This section will decide which bank is right for most expats, so I'm going to be specific about it.

**Bunq:** Real NL IBAN, issued by a Dutch bank licensed by DNB. Accepted everywhere in the Netherlands without question.

**Revolut:** NL IBAN opt-in available for Dutch residents. You navigate to Settings → Account → IBAN and switch to NL. The IBAN you receive genuinely starts with NL. However, the institution behind it remains Revolut Bank UAB, licensed in Lithuania.

In practice, Revolut's NL IBAN works fine for most things: online shopping, card payments, peer-to-peer transfers, most Dutch direct debits. But here's where it can break down:

**Dutch employer payroll systems.** Some Dutch HR systems — particularly in larger corporates or public sector — run validation checks that go beyond just the IBAN format. They check the BIC code and the bank behind it. If the system sees Revolut Bank UAB (Lithuanian), it may flag or reject it. I've heard this from several expats who had to get their employer's HR department to manually override the system before their salary would process.

**Belastingdienst interactions.** For tax refunds or benefit payments (toeslagen), the Belastingdienst expects a Dutch or EU IBAN. The NL IBAN opt-in from Revolut satisfies this technically, but again, edge cases exist.

**Zorgverzekeraar (health insurance) direct debits.** Most major Dutch health insurers accept Revolut's NL IBAN without issue. But some still require a Dutch-bank-issued account. If you're having trouble setting up a zorgtoeslag (healthcare allowance) direct debit, this is often why.

**Mortgage and tenancy references.** If a Dutch landlord or mortgage broker runs a bank verification check, Bunq passes as a Dutch bank. Revolut may not.

None of this means Revolut's NL IBAN is useless. For most people, most of the time, it works. But if your priority is being embedded in Dutch systems without friction, Bunq is the safer choice. ## Deposit Insurance: Dutch vs Lithuanian Scheme

Both Bunq and Revolut cover deposits up to €100,000 per person under the EU Deposit Guarantee Scheme — Bunq under the Dutch DGS administered by DNB, Revolut under the Lithuanian DGS.

The €100,000 limit is identical. The EU framework is harmonised, so both schemes operate under the same rules. Practically speaking, most expats will never come close to the limit and will never need to claim.

The one practical difference: if you're based in the Netherlands and something goes wrong, claiming from the Dutch DGS through DNB is faster and involves Dutch-language processes you can navigate more easily than Lithuanian bureaucracy. The [DNB deposit guarantee scheme page](https://www.dnb.nl/en/sector-information/supervision-of-banks/deposit-guarantee-scheme/) explains what's covered and how claims work.

For amounts under €100k, I wouldn't lose sleep over either option. For larger amounts, you'd want to diversify across institutions anyway — which is good practice regardless of which bank you use.

## Opening an Account Without a BSN

Both Bunq and Revolut allow you to open an account with just your passport and a selfie. No BSN required.

This is one of the most important practical facts I share with expats in my [first-year Netherlands guide](/blog/first-year-netherlands-expat-pillar-2026/). Traditional Dutch banks — ING, ABN AMRO, Rabobank — all require a BSN to open an account. Getting a BSN takes time: you need a DigiD appointment, which can be 3–4 weeks out in busy municipalities. You can't open a traditional bank account without it.

The neobank path bypasses all of this. You arrive, open a Bunq or Revolut account the same day, and have a functioning account before your BSN arrives. Once you have your BSN, you add it to your profile in the app to complete the full KYC process.

Both banks will eventually ask for your BSN as part of regulatory requirements. But you can start using the account immediately, receive salary, and set up direct debits without waiting.

## Receiving Your Dutch Salary

For salary deposits, Bunq is the clear winner and I want to be specific about why.

Dutch payroll software — Nmbrs, Salure, ADP Netherlands, SD Worx — often validates bank account details by cross-referencing the IBAN against a registry of Dutch banking institutions. Bunq passes this check immediately because it is a Dutch bank. The BIC code (BUNQNL2A) is Dutch and maps correctly to a DNB-supervised institution.

Revolut Bank UAB has a Dutch BIC associated with its NL IBAN opt-in, but the underlying institution is Lithuanian. Larger employers with strict payroll validation — particularly in the public sector, healthcare, or multinational companies with conservative IT departments — may flag this. Several expats I've helped have had to manually override their company's HR system to accept a Revolut IBAN.

If your employer is a startup or a company with modern payroll software, Revolut's NL IBAN probably works fine. If your employer is a large Dutch corporation or public institution, I'd use Bunq for your salary account to avoid any risk of the first paycheck bouncing or being delayed.
