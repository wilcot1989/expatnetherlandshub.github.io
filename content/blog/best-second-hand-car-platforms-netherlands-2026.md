## Why the Dutch second-hand car market is different

If you are coming from the US, UK, or Australia, the Dutch used car market will feel slightly off. Cars are smaller, fuel is expensive, and APK (the Dutch MOT) is taken seriously. A car that fails APK is not driveable, not insurable, and not legally on the road.

Three things shape every used car decision here:

**APK schedule**: Cars over four years old need an APK every two years until age eight, then yearly. A "fresh APK" sticker is the single most valuable thing on a listing.

**BPM and road tax (motorrijtuigenbelasting)**: Heavy or high-CO2 cars cost a fortune to own. A 2016 BMW 530i diesel might look like a bargain at €11,000, but the road tax alone could be €1,800 a year. Always check road tax before buying — the [Belastingdienst MRB calculator](https://www.belastingdienst.nl/) is your friend.

**Mileage culture**: Dutch cars typically have higher mileage than UK or US equivalents. Do not panic at 200,000 km on a 2018 Skoda. Dutch service intervals and motorway driving mean these cars often have years left in them.

## My ranking of Dutch second-hand car platforms in 2026

After three years of buying, selling, and helping expats hunt cars, here is how I rank the main platforms.

### 1. Gaspedaal — best for searching

Gaspedaal is not a marketplace. It is a search engine that aggregates listings from Marktplaats, AutoTrack, AutoScout24, and dozens of dealer sites. I always start here.

You set filters once (year, fuel type, transmission, max price, location radius) and Gaspedaal pulls every matching listing across the Dutch market. You can save searches and get email alerts. The interface is in Dutch but Google Translate handles it perfectly.

Why it wins for expats: you do not need to know which platform a car is listed on. Gaspedaal finds it.

### 2. AutoTrack — best for dealer cars

AutoTrack is dealer-focused. Almost every listing comes with a full inspection report, photos of the underside, and the BOVAG warranty status. Listings are typically €500 to €1,500 more expensive than Marktplaats but you are paying for transparency and recourse.

If this is your first Dutch car and you do not yet have a trusted garage, AutoTrack is where I send people.

### 3. Marktplaats — biggest inventory, most risk

Marktplaats is the Dutch Craigslist. Everything ends up here eventually: private sellers, small dealers, and chancers offloading problem cars. The inventory is enormous but the noise is real.

I use Marktplaats when:
- I already know what I want and I am hunting for a specific model and year
- I have €200 to spend on a pre-purchase inspection from an independent mechanic
- I am willing to walk away from a deal that smells off

I avoid Marktplaats when:
- I am in a hurry
- I cannot bring a Dutch-speaking friend to the viewing
- The listing has fewer than five photos

### 4. AutoScout24 — pan-European, useful for imports

AutoScout24 covers Germany, Belgium, and the Netherlands. If you are open to importing — and you have done the BPM maths — this is where the deals hide. German cars often have lower mileage and meticulous service histories.

Be honest about whether you actually want to manage an import. I have done it once. It took six weeks and three trips to the RDW.

### 5. Bynco — fixed-price, online-only

Bynco is interesting. It is online-only, fixed-price, with a 14-day return policy. Cars are delivered to your door. You pay a small premium versus a dealer but the convenience is real.

I have not bought through Bynco myself but two expat friends have, and both reported smooth experiences. It works particularly well if you do not yet drive confidently in Dutch cities.

## How to actually inspect a used car in the Netherlands

Whichever platform you use, the inspection matters more than the price. Here is the checklist I now run on every viewing.

**Before you go**:
- Run the licence plate through the [RDW database](https://www.rdw.nl/) — free, instant, shows MOT history, owner count, mileage at last APK
- Check the road tax (motorrijtuigenbelasting) on the Belastingdienst calculator
- Request the service book photos before driving an hour to see the car

**At the viewing**:
- Cold start the engine — if the seller has warmed it up before you arrived, walk away
- Check all four tyre dates (the four-digit DOT code) — tyres over six years old need replacing regardless of tread
- Look under the car for oil leaks, rust on the subframe, and chewed CV boots
- Open the oil cap — milky residue means head gasket trouble
- Take a 30-minute test drive including motorway and stop-start traffic

**Before paying**:
- Ask for the tenaamstellingscode (transfer code) — without this you cannot register the car
- Verify the VIN on the chassis matches the registration document
- Confirm the seller is the registered owner (or has authorisation to sell)

For €150 to €250 you can have an independent mechanic do a pre-purchase inspection (aankoopkeuring). ANWB offers this nationwide. For any car above €5,000, this is not optional. It is insurance against making my €2,200 mistake.

## Insurance: arrange this before you collect the car

You cannot legally drive away without insurance starting that day. I have watched expats turn up at handover and discover they cannot drive their new car home.

I use Independer myself for my own car and renewals.

Three things to know:

**WA, WA+ or Allrisk**: WA is third-party only (cheapest, legally minimum). WA+ adds theft, fire, and glass. Allrisk is fully comprehensive. For a car under €4,000, WA+ is usually the right choice. For anything newer, Allrisk pays for itself the first time you scuff a bumper in a parking garage.

**No-claims bonus from abroad**: Dutch insurers will accept your home country no-claims discount but only with an official statement from your previous insurer in English or Dutch. Get this letter before you cancel your old policy.

**Excess (eigen risico)**: Standard excess is €150 to €300. You can lower it for a small premium increase, but only if you are accident-prone. Most expats are better off keeping the excess and saving the monthly cost.

## Paying for the car: avoid cash, avoid bank transfers from abroad

This is where expats lose money. A few ground rules:

**Never pay cash on the spot**. Dutch sellers know that flashing €5,000 in cash is suspicious and reputable sellers will ask for bank transfer anyway. If a seller insists on cash, walk away.

**Avoid international transfers from your home account**. Wire transfers from a US or UK account take 2 to 5 working days and the FX spread on your home bank can cost €100 to €300 on a €7,000 car. I use [Wise](https://go.expatnetherlandshub.com/wise){target="_blank" rel="nofollow sponsored noopener"} for any cross-border payment — the rate is the mid-market rate plus a small flat fee, and the transfer lands in 1 to 3 hours from euros to euros.

**Pay only after the registration is transferred**. The Dutch system uses a tenaamstellingscode that the seller hands to you at the post office. You both go in. The transfer happens in front of you. Then you pay. Reverse this order and you have no recourse if the seller disappears.

## Common scams and how to spot them

Three patterns I see repeatedly on Marktplaats:

**The "I am abroad" listing**: Seller claims they have moved to Spain and the car is at a shipping company. They want a deposit before sending it. This is always a scam. Always.

**The cloned VIN**: A car listed for 30 percent below market value with a clean APK and full service history. The VIN may have been cloned from another car. Run the plate through RDW — if the VIN does not match, walk away.

**The "warranty included" private sale**: Private sellers cannot legally offer warranty in the Netherlands. If a private seller is offering 12 months warranty, they are either a dealer pretending to be private (to avoid VAT and BOVAG obligations) or lying. Either way, do not buy.

A useful sanity check: the RDW website is free and shows you exactly how many owners a car has had and the mileage at every APK. A 2018 car with five owners and inconsistent mileage between APKs is a red flag.

## Do you actually need to buy a car?

Before I close, this is worth saying. The Netherlands has the best public transport in Europe and the best cycling infrastructure in the world. In Amsterdam, Utrecht, and Rotterdam I would not own a car. The parking costs alone (€350+ per month for a residents' permit in central Amsterdam) wipe out any benefit.

Where a car makes sense:
- You live outside the Randstad
- You commute to a non-rail location
- You have kids who need transport to multiple activities
- You travel regularly to family in nearby countries

If you are unsure, try a Greenwheels or MyWheels carshare for two months before committing. I lived in Utrecht for a year on shared cars and saved €4,000.
