## When I Moved from Berlin to Amsterdam with an N26 Account

When I moved to the Netherlands for my current job in Amsterdam, I had an N26 account I'd been using for two years while living in Berlin. I liked N26 — the app is clean, the card works everywhere, the fees are low. My plan was to keep using it in the Netherlands rather than deal with opening yet another bank account.

That plan did not last two months.

The first problem came on payday. My Dutch employer's HR system processed salary transfers using a payroll platform that expected an NL IBAN. My DE IBAN — which starts with DE, not NL — got flagged in their system. The HR coordinator had to manually override it, which she did, but the payment was delayed three days while she figured out the issue. This happened again the second month. And a third time when a new payroll manager processed the run. Each time, somebody had to get on the phone or email to explain that yes, a German IBAN is valid under EU law.

The second problem was my Dutch health insurance. When I tried to set up an automatic direct debit (automatische incasso) with my zorgverzekering provider, their online portal simply would not accept my DE IBAN in the IBAN field. The field validated only NL IBANs. I ended up setting up a manual monthly transfer instead — which I immediately forgot to do in month three and got a late payment notice.

By month two I had switched to Bunq. I've used it as my primary Dutch account ever since.

I still think N26 is a good bank. But for living in the Netherlands, the IBAN is a real, practical issue — not a theoretical one. This comparison is based on that lived experience, plus research into both products as they stand in mid-2026.

## Why the IBAN Question Matters More in the Netherlands Than Anywhere Else

The Netherlands has one of the highest rates of direct debit usage in Europe. If you live here, you will set up automatische incasso for almost everything: health insurance, energy bills, rent (in some cases), streaming subscriptions, gym memberships, phone contracts. This is not how banking works in many other countries, where you pay monthly bills manually or by card. Here, you give your IBAN to a company and they pull the payment on a fixed date.

Dutch direct debit systems are built around NL IBANs. Under SEPA Regulation (EU) No 260/2012, Dutch companies are legally required to accept all EU IBANs for payments — meaning a German DE IBAN from N26 is technically valid. The regulation is clear. The practice is messier.

Smaller Dutch companies, older automated payroll systems, and some utility providers' online portals check that the country code in the IBAN matches the country they are operating in. Some do this by design. Some do it by accident because their developer hard-coded NL as the expected prefix. Either way, when their system rejects your IBAN, you have to call customer service, explain EU SEPA rules, and wait for a human to manually process your payment. This is a friction cost that compounds every time you sign up for something new.

If you arrive in the Netherlands and your bank account has a DE IBAN, you will run into this. Maybe once, maybe five times. With a Bunq NL IBAN, you never will. You hand over your IBAN, the direct debit is set up, and the money leaves your account on time.

## The IBAN Comparison: This Is Where It Actually Matters

Let me be direct about this. Every other feature comparison between N26 and Bunq — pricing, UI design, ATM fees, travel insurance — is secondary to the IBAN question for someone living in the Netherlands. Everything else can be worked around. The IBAN is either right or it is not.

Here is how each bank stacks up against the things you will actually encounter in Dutch daily life:

**Dutch employer payroll:** Bunq wins. An NL IBAN from Bunq processes through Dutch payroll systems without flags. N26's DE IBAN may require manual intervention, especially at traditional Dutch employers using older payroll platforms. This does not affect everyone — larger multinationals with modern HR systems usually handle it fine — but it is a known point of friction.

**Belastingdienst (Dutch tax authority):** Bunq wins. The [Belastingdienst links your bank account IBAN to your BSN](https://www.belastingdienst.nl/wps/wcm/connect/bldcontentnl/belastingdienst/prive/bankrekening_en_belastingen/bankrekening_en_belastingen) in their system. Tax refunds, tax correspondence, and DigiD-linked payment processes all work more cleanly with a Dutch IBAN. A DE IBAN is legally accepted but adds an extra verification step. When you are already navigating Dutch bureaucracy as a new expat, removing unnecessary friction is worth something.

**Dutch direct debits (utilities, insurance, subscriptions):** Bunq wins. As described above, most Dutch direct debit setup processes work smoothly with NL IBANs. With a DE IBAN, you may encounter portal rejections or manual processing requirements.

**Cross-border SEPA transfers:** Equal. Both banks process SEPA transfers to any EU country without issues. If you are sending money to Germany, France, Spain, the UK (via SEPA-adjacent routes), or elsewhere in Europe, both work equally well.

**International transfers (non-EU, or multi-currency):** Neither is optimised for this. Add Wise for transfers outside the SEPA zone. More on that in a later section.

## Dutch Tax System: What Both Accounts Mean for Box 3

Both N26 and Bunq are standard current accounts and savings products. From a Dutch tax perspective, balances held in either account count toward Box 3 (income from savings and investments) if your total assets exceed the heffingsvrij vermogen threshold — currently around €57,000 per person (2026 threshold, confirm with your tax adviser as this changes annually).

The bank you use does not change your tax liability. A €10,000 balance in N26 and a €10,000 balance in Bunq are treated identically by the Belastingdienst for Box 3 purposes. What matters is that you report all foreign and Dutch bank accounts accurately in your annual aangifte.

Where the Dutch IBAN does make a practical difference is in how the Belastingdienst processes payments to you. Toeslagen (allowances), zorgtoeslag (health allowance), and tax refunds are paid to the IBAN you register with DigiD. Dutch NL IBANs process through this system more smoothly. If you register a DE IBAN, it usually works, but additional identity verification steps may be required.
