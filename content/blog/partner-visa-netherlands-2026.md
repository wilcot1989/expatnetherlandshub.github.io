## What the MVV and GVVA Actually Mean

Before anything else, let's sort out the terminology, because this is where most people get confused.

**MVV — Machtiging Voorlopig Verblijf** translates as "authorisation for temporary stay." It is, practically speaking, a long-stay entry visa: a sticker placed in your partner's passport by a Dutch embassy or consulate abroad. Without it, non-EU nationals cannot legally enter the Netherlands for the purpose of staying long-term. The MVV is what gets them on the plane and through passport control.

**GVVA — Gecombineerde Vergunning voor Verblijf en Arbeid** is the combined residence and work permit. It's what your partner receives after arriving in the Netherlands. Some family migration cases result in a GVVA, others in a standard residence permit — the practical difference for labor market access is negligible. Both give your partner the right to live in the Netherlands and the right to work without a separate work permit.

The process runs in two stages. First, the sponsor (you, the person already in the Netherlands) submits the MVV application to the IND. The IND assesses whether the conditions are met. If approved, the embassy or consulate abroad receives the green light and issues the MVV sticker. Your partner then travels to the Netherlands and, within a few days of arrival, collects the actual residence permit from the IND.

The official IND page for this route is at [ind.nl/en/residence-permits/family-and-partner](https://ind.nl/en/residence-permits/family-and-partner) — bookmark it, because that's the authoritative source and conditions do change.

## Who Can Bring a Partner Under This Route?

The family migration route is open to a range of sponsors, but the rules differ depending on your own status in the Netherlands.

**Dutch citizens** can bring a foreign partner under a specific EU-law track that has somewhat more favorable conditions. The IND processes these under the Dutch Aliens Act.

**EU/EEA/Swiss nationals** living in the Netherlands can bring non-EU partners under EU freedom of movement rules, which can be more flexible than the standard third-country national route.

**Non-EU nationals with a residence permit** — this is the category most of my readers fall into: kennismigranten, holders of a regular work permit, recognized refugees, long-term residents. You can bring your partner, but you must meet the income requirement from your own permit basis, and your own permit must be valid long enough.

For all categories, the relationship must be one of:

- **Marriage** — registered in the country of origin or in the Netherlands, with a legally recognized marriage certificate.
- **Registered partnership** — a formal registered partnership (not just cohabitation) recognized under Dutch or foreign law.
- **Durable relationship** — for couples who are not married or registered partners but can demonstrate a lasting relationship. This requires more evidence: shared accommodation, joint finances, correspondence, travel records, statutory declarations.

The minimum age for both partners is 21 years for the standard route (with limited exceptions).

## The Income Requirement: What the Sponsor Must Earn

This is the number one question I receive, so I'll be direct about it.

The sponsor must earn at least **100% of the Dutch statutory minimum wage** on a sustainable basis. In 2026, based on the January 2026 minimum wage adjustment, this is approximately **€2,317 gross per month** — but this figure is updated twice a year (January and July), so always verify the current figure at ind.nl before you rely on it.

"Sustainable" is the operative word. The IND does not simply look at your pay slip for last month. They assess:

- Whether your contract is permanent (vast contract) or fixed-term. Fixed-term contracts are accepted, but the remaining duration must be long enough to cover the expected period of the permit.
- Whether you are employed or self-employed. Self-employed income (ZZP, directeur-grootaandeelhouder) is assessed over the previous three years of tax returns.
- Whether the income includes social benefits. Benefits like unemployment insurance, social assistance, or housing benefit do not count toward the income threshold.

The 8% holiday allowance (vakantietoeslag) that Dutch employers pay on top of base salary does count toward the gross monthly figure — this often matters for people whose base salary is just below the threshold.

If you work as a highly skilled migrant on a [kennismigrant permit](/blog/highly-skilled-migrant-visa-netherlands-2026/), your salary almost certainly clears the threshold by a wide margin. If you are on a general work permit with a more modest salary, check carefully.

One practical note: freelancers and ZZP workers sometimes struggle here because IND requires three years of verified income. If you are relatively new to self-employment in the Netherlands, this can be a bottleneck. Getting advice from [an English-speaking immigration lawyer](/blog/best-english-speaking-lawyers-netherlands-2026/) early can save you from submitting a weak application.

## The Civic Integration Exam Abroad (Inburgering Basisexamen Buitenland)

This is the part of the process that surprises people most. Before the MVV can be issued, most non-EU partners must pass the **inburgering basisexamen buitenland** — the civic integration exam taken outside the Netherlands.

The exam has two components:

1. **Dutch language component:** reading comprehension (A1 level) and listening comprehension. The reading test is conducted via telephone. The listening test is also telephone-based.
2. **Knowledge of Dutch society (KNS):** a computer-based test on Dutch laws, customs, institutions, and values. This can be taken at Prometric test centers in many countries.

The KNS test costs approximately **€350** (though fees are set by DUO — Dienst Uitvoering Onderwijs — and may change). The language tests are included in this fee structure. Your partner pays these fees directly.

**Exemptions** apply to nationals from: Australia, Canada, Japan, Monaco, New Zealand, South Korea, the United States, and Vatican City. EU/EEA nationals are also exempt. Some other exemption categories exist for people who have previously passed an integration exam in the Netherlands or hold certain Dutch qualifications.

Preparation materials are available through DUO and commercial courses. The [best Dutch language schools and online courses](/blog/best-dutch-language-schools-online-2026/) can supplement the official DUO prep materials — I've seen partners use general learning platforms as a warm-up, but the official DUO practice tests are essential for the actual exam format. For app-based learning, the [best language apps for learning Dutch quickly](/blog/best-language-app-to-learn-dutch-quickly-2026/) round-up has options that work well for A1 preparation from abroad.

Exam booking can take weeks in countries with limited Prometric test center availability. In Daniela's case, she had to travel from her smaller city to São Paulo just for the KNS component. Factor this into your timeline.

## Step-by-Step: How the Application Works

Here is the process broken into stages, as it runs in practice.

### Stage 1: Sponsor Submits MVV Application to IND (Netherlands)

You, the sponsor in the Netherlands, submit the MVV application on behalf of your partner through the IND online portal or at an IND desk. You'll need:

- Proof of your identity (valid passport)
- Proof of your residence permit (or Dutch/EU citizenship)
- Recent pay slips (last 3 months minimum) or employer statement (werkgeversverklaring)
- Employment contract or employer declaration confirming contract duration and salary
- Proof of the relationship: marriage certificate (apostilled), partnership certificate, or relationship evidence package

**IND fee: approximately €243** for the standard family reunification permit in 2026 (fees change annually — verify at ind.nl). This is paid by the sponsor at the time of application.

The IND has up to **90 days** to decide on the application. In practice, decisions often come faster — 4 to 8 weeks is common for straightforward cases — but do not book flights or make firm arrangements until you have a decision.

### Stage 2: Partner Passes Civic Integration Exam (Abroad)

Simultaneously with or before the IND decision, your partner prepares for and passes the inburgering basisexamen buitenland. DUO must confirm the pass before the Dutch embassy can issue the MVV sticker. The exam result is shared digitally between DUO and the embassy/consulate system.

### Stage 3: Embassy Issues MVV

Once the IND approves the application and DUO confirms the exam pass (if required), the Dutch embassy or consulate in your partner's country contacts them to schedule a visa appointment. Your partner brings:

- Valid passport (valid for at least 6 months beyond the intended stay)
- Passport photos
- Any documents the embassy requests (varies by country)

The embassy places the MVV sticker in the passport. Validity is typically 90 days — your partner must travel to the Netherlands within that window.

### Stage 4: Partner Arrives in the Netherlands

Within 3 days of arrival, your partner must register at the municipality (gemeente) if they don't already have an address there. They then have a short window to collect their actual residence permit from the IND — they'll receive an appointment letter. The permit card (verblijfsvergunning) is usually ready within 2 weeks of arrival.

### Stage 5: BSN, Bank Account, Health Insurance

After municipality registration, your partner gets their [BSN (burgerservicenummer)](/blog/expat-bsn-application-guide-2026/) — the Dutch citizen service number that unlocks almost everything else: bank accounts, employment, health insurance, tax registration. ## Registered Partnership vs Marriage: Does It Matter?

For the purpose of the IND application, both marriage and registered partnership (geregistreerd partnerschap) are treated equivalently. Both require an official certificate that may need to be apostilled (for countries in the Hague Apostille Convention) or legalized by the Dutch embassy (for countries outside it).

If your marriage was concluded in a country where it might not be recognized under Dutch law — polygamous marriages, marriages between persons below Dutch age requirements, certain proxy marriages — the IND may decline to recognize it as a basis for family migration. In those cases, legal advice is essential. The [best English-speaking lawyers in the Netherlands](/blog/best-english-speaking-lawyers-netherlands-2026/) includes immigration specialists who can assess specific situations before you commit to an application.

For couples in a durable relationship (not formally married or registered), the IND assessment is more demanding. You'll need to demonstrate that:

- You have been together for at least 12 months in a committed relationship
- You have a lasting and exclusive relationship
- You can demonstrate shared life — cohabitation, joint finances, regular contact records

Building this evidence file takes time. I always advise couples who are not yet married to start documenting their relationship systematically well before they intend to apply: shared lease agreements, joint bank statements, travel tickets, hotel bookings showing you travel together. The more consistent and varied the evidence, the stronger the application.

## Document Checklist

Here is a practical document list. Requirements vary by country and individual circumstance — the IND and the relevant embassy may request additional items.

**Sponsor (in the Netherlands):**
- Valid passport copy
- Valid residence permit copy (or proof of nationality for Dutch/EU citizens)
- Last 3 months of pay slips
- Employer declaration (werkgeversverklaring) confirming contract type, duration, and salary
- Employment contract
- Recent bank statements confirming salary receipt

**Partner (abroad):**
- Valid passport (minimum 6 months validity beyond intended stay)
- Passport photos (embassy-format)
- Proof of relationship: apostilled marriage/partnership certificate, or relationship evidence package
- Birth certificate (apostilled, may require sworn translation)
- Proof of civic integration exam pass (if applicable) — DUO handles the digital confirmation, but keep a copy
- Any country-specific documents the embassy requests

**Translations:** Documents not in Dutch, English, German, or French typically require a sworn translation (beëdigd vertaler). Do not use machine translations — the IND and embassy require certified human translators. The Netherlands maintains a registry of sworn translators at wbtv.nl.

If your partner has foreign professional qualifications they plan to use in the Netherlands, check [diploma recognition for expats](/blog/diploma-recognition-netherlands-expats-2026/) early — some regulated professions require formal recognition before you can practice, and starting that process before arrival saves time.

## Timeline: What to Realistically Expect

Based on cases I've followed and community feedback:

| Stage | Duration |
|---|---|
| Document preparation | 2–6 weeks |
| Civic integration exam preparation | 4–12 weeks (variable) |
| Exam booking and sitting | 2–4 weeks after preparation |
| IND processing time | 4–12 weeks (up to 90 days) |
| Embassy appointment and MVV issuance | 1–4 weeks after IND approval |
| Travel to Netherlands + permit collection | 1–2 weeks after arrival |
| **Total (realistic range)** | **3–6 months** |

The IND and exam prep stages often run in parallel, which compresses the total time. Start the exam preparation the moment you decide to apply — it's always better to have the exam passed before the IND decision comes through, rather than the exam becoming the bottleneck after IND approval.

If your partner's country has limited embassy slots or Prometric test center availability, the timeline stretches further. Some countries in West Africa and parts of Asia have historically had 6+ week waits for embassy appointments alone. That's not unusual — plan for it.

## After Arrival: Labor Market Access and Next Steps

One of the most practical aspects of the partner visa outcome is how clean the labor market access is. Once your partner has their residence permit, they can work in the Netherlands without a separate work permit:

- They can take any job, with any employer
- They can switch employers without notifying the IND
- They can work as a freelancer or ZZP (self-employed)
- There is no restriction on the type of work or sector

## Common Reasons for Delays and Refusals

Understanding what goes wrong helps you avoid it.

**Income just below threshold.** The IND is strict on the minimum wage requirement. If your salary is right at the edge, get a detailed employer declaration and include holiday allowance calculations. If you're self-employed, make sure your most recent three years of tax assessments (aanslagen) are available and clearly show sufficient income.

**Relationship evidence gaps.** For unmarried partners, thin evidence is the most common reason for additional information requests (which add 4–6 weeks). Start building your documentation file early, before you think you need it.

**Document authentication issues.** A marriage certificate that wasn't apostilled, or a translation done by an uncertified translator, will be rejected and you'll have to start that document over. Verify authentication requirements per country before submitting anything.

**Exam timing mismatch.** If the IND approves the MVV but your partner hasn't yet passed the civic integration exam, the MVV cannot be issued. Don't let the exam fall behind the IND timeline — treat them as parallel tracks from day one.

**Embassy slot availability.** In high-demand locations, embassy appointment waits can stretch 4–6 weeks after the IND decision. Nothing can accelerate this — it's a capacity constraint. Account for it in your planning.

## FAQ

**How long does the partner visa process take in the Netherlands?**
The full process typically takes 3 to 6 months from the moment the MVV application is submitted. That includes IND processing time (up to 90 days), plus scheduling delays at the embassy and document preparation time. If your partner still needs to pass the civic integration exam abroad, add extra weeks or months for preparation and exam booking.

**What income does the sponsor need to earn to bring a partner to the Netherlands?**
The sponsor must earn at least 100% of the Dutch statutory minimum wage on a sustainable basis. In 2026 this is approximately €2,317 gross per month (January 2026 level — verify at ind.nl). The income must come from employment or self-employment and must not rely on social benefits.

**Does my partner need to pass a Dutch language test before arriving?**
Most non-EU partners need to pass the civic integration exam abroad (inburgering basisexamen buitenland) before the MVV can be issued. It tests basic Dutch reading and listening comprehension plus knowledge of Dutch society. Exemptions apply for EU/EEA nationals and nationals from Australia, Canada, Japan, Monaco, New Zealand, South Korea, USA, and Vatican City, among others.

**Can my partner work in the Netherlands after arrival?**
Yes. Once your partner has a valid GVVA or regular residence permit as a family member, they have free access to the Dutch labor market. No separate work permit is required. They can work for any employer, switch jobs, or work as a freelancer.

**What is the difference between MVV and GVVA?**
The MVV is the entry visa sticker placed in the passport that allows travel to the Netherlands. The GVVA is the combined residence and work permit issued after arrival. Some cases result in a standard residence permit instead of a GVVA, but the labor market access is the same.

**What relationship evidence does the IND require?**
For married couples: an apostilled marriage certificate, with sworn translation if not in Dutch/English/German/French. For registered partnerships: the partnership certificate. For unmarried couples: evidence of a durable relationship — shared lease, joint bank statements, travel records, and a statutory declaration. The IND looks for consistency and depth of evidence.
