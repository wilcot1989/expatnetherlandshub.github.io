## Why Dutch employee tax returns matter

Most newly arrived expats assume their employer's payroll handles everything. It does not.

Your monthly payslip applies a withholding (loonheffing) that approximates your annual tax. The annual return reconciles the actual tax due against what was withheld. The result is usually a refund, sometimes a small payment, occasionally a wake-up call.

Reasons employees almost always benefit from filing:

**The 30% ruling is finalised in your tax return**. If you have the ruling, your employer has applied it to your monthly payroll. The annual return confirms it and sometimes adjusts for changes mid-year.

**Mortgage interest deduction is claimed in your return**. If you bought a Dutch house, this is where your interest deduction shows up. It is often the single largest deduction Dutch homeowners claim.

**Foreign income disclosure**. The Netherlands taxes worldwide income. Your foreign rental, savings interest, or capital gains must be declared even if a tax treaty exempts them.

**Box 3 wealth tax**. If your savings, investments, or crypto exceeded the threshold (around €57,000 per person in 2026) on 1 January, you owe Box 3 tax. The annual return is where this is calculated.

**Healthcare allowance and other toeslagen**. These are calculated on your declared income. An incorrect return triggers clawbacks.

I know expats who skipped filing for two years and then received a backdated assessment with €4,000 in penalties and Box 3 underpayment. The Belastingdienst is patient but not forgetful.

## P form versus M form

Two forms you will hear about:

**P form (Particuliere aangifte)**: The standard annual return for full-year Dutch tax residents. Filed online via Mijn Belastingdienst. Pre-filled with payroll data, mortgage data, and bank interest data.

**M form (Migratieformulier)**: The return you file in the year you arrived or the year you leave. It splits the tax year into a Dutch-resident period and a non-resident period. Pre-fill is much more limited and the form is significantly longer.

If you arrived in 2025, your 2025 return is an M form. Your 2026 return is a P form.

The M form is the tax return that catches the most expats out. It cannot always be filed online (depending on your situation) and it requires you to manually allocate income between the resident and non-resident periods. For most expats I recommend a specialist tax advisor for the M form year. The €300 to €500 fee is almost always recovered through deductions you would otherwise miss.

For the standard P form years, doing it yourself in Mijn Belastingdienst is realistic for most employees.

## What you need before you start

Documentation checklist for a typical employee return:

**Identification**:
- DigiD login (the Dutch government's online identity service)
- BSN
- Bank account number (Dutch IBAN) for refunds

**Income**:
- Annual income statement (jaaropgave) from each employer — usually emailed or in your employer portal in January or February
- 30% ruling confirmation letter (if applicable)
- Statements for any side income (board memberships, freelance work, royalties)

**Foreign income**:
- Statements for foreign rental income (gross and net)
- Foreign salary if you worked partly outside the Netherlands during the tax year
- Foreign pension payments
- Foreign capital gains and dividends

**Assets (for Box 3)**:
- Bank account balances on 1 January (Dutch and foreign)
- Investment account statements on 1 January
- Crypto holdings on 1 January
- Cash equivalents above the threshold

**Home (for homeowners)**:
- Mortgage statement showing interest paid in the tax year
- WOZ value letter from the gemeente
- Notary deed if you bought during the tax year (closing costs are partially deductible)

**Other deductions**:
- Healthcare costs above the threshold (specifieke zorgkosten)
- Charitable donations (giften)
- Study costs (deductibility varies by year — check the current rules)
- Alimony payments

I keep all of these in a single folder labelled by tax year. When the aangiftebrief arrives in February, I can usually file within a week.

## How to actually file in Mijn Belastingdienst

The process for a regular P form:

**Step 1**: Log in to [Mijn Belastingdienst](https://mijn.belastingdienst.nl/) using your DigiD.

**Step 2**: Open the current year's return. Most fields are pre-filled.

**Step 3**: Verify employer data. Your jaaropgave should match the pre-filled income line exactly. Discrepancies usually mean the employer reported late.

**Step 4**: Add 30% ruling details. The system asks specifically whether you have the ruling. Tick yes and enter the period.

**Step 5**: Enter mortgage data if you own. The system asks for the WOZ value (pre-filled from gemeente) and the mortgage interest paid (pre-filled from most major Dutch banks).

**Step 6**: Enter Box 3 assets. Bank balances are partly pre-filled but foreign accounts must be entered manually. Add investments, crypto, and second properties.

**Step 7**: Enter deductions. Healthcare costs, donations, study costs.

**Step 8**: Enter any additional income. Side jobs, foreign rental, foreign pensions.

**Step 9**: Review the calculation. The system shows your refund or payment due before you submit.

**Step 10**: Submit and download a PDF copy for your records.

The pre-fill is genuinely good for employees. For a standard P form with no homeownership and no foreign income, the whole thing takes 30 to 60 minutes.

## The 30 percent ruling in your tax return

If you have the 30% ruling, your monthly payroll already reflects it. The annual return confirms it.

Three things to check:

**The ruling period must match what the Belastingdienst has on file**. If your ruling started in March, the return must reflect 10 months at the reduced base rather than 12.

**Partial non-resident election (partieel buitenlandse belastingplicht)** is selected within the return. This treats you as non-resident for Box 2 and Box 3 purposes, which can mean your foreign savings and investments escape Dutch wealth tax. The election is annual — you can choose each year. For most expats with significant foreign assets, election is the right answer.

**The 2024 cap (€233,000 base salary in 2026 figures)** limits the income that can benefit from the ruling. High earners hit this and the return calculates the cap automatically.

If your 30% ruling was extended or reduced mid-year (for example, due to the 2024 phasing rule), the return is where the year-end reconciliation happens. I covered the phasing rules in [30% ruling extension and 2024 changes for 2026](/blog/30-percent-ruling-extension-2024-changes-2026.md).

## Foreign income: what to declare

This is where expats often get nervous. The rules are clearer than they look.

**Foreign salary**: Declare. Apply the relevant tax treaty. The Netherlands typically grants a credit (verrekening) for foreign tax paid, not an exemption.

**Foreign rental income**: Declare gross rental and allowable expenses. Most tax treaties allocate the right to tax rental income to the country where the property sits, so the Netherlands usually grants a "voorkoming dubbele belasting" exemption with progression. You declare it but do not pay Dutch tax on it.

**Foreign savings interest and dividends**: Declare. Withholding tax in the source country is usually creditable.

**Foreign pension**: Declare. Allocation under the relevant treaty determines whether the Netherlands or the source country has the primary taxing right.

**Foreign capital gains**: Generally not taxed by the Netherlands (Box 3 taxes only deemed return on assets, not realised gains). Declare the assets, not the gains.

**US persons (citizens or green card holders)**: You also have US filing obligations regardless of where you live. The US-Netherlands tax treaty handles credits but not the filing requirement. Most US expats need both a US return (to the IRS) and a Dutch return.

For complex cross-border situations a tax advisor is essential. The Big Four (Deloitte, PwC, KPMG, EY) have expat tax desks; smaller specialists like BDO and TaxSavers also serve the expat market with reasonable fees.

## Box 3 wealth tax: what counts

Box 3 taxes a deemed return on your net assets above the threshold. For 2026, the threshold is approximately €57,000 per person (€114,000 for fiscal partners).

What counts:
- All bank accounts (Dutch and foreign)
- Investment accounts (DEGIRO, Trade Republic, Trading 212, Interactive Brokers)
- Crypto holdings (yes, even if held abroad)
- Second homes
- Cash above approximately €570
- Loans owed to you

What does not count:
- Your primary residence (that goes in Box 1 with mortgage interest deduction)
- Pension and lijfrente accounts
- Furniture, art, and personal possessions

The deemed return rates change each year. In 2026, savings have a deemed return of around 1.0% and investments around 5.88%. Box 3 tax is then 36% of the deemed return.

Tip: If you have the 30% ruling and elected partial non-resident status, your foreign-held assets may not be subject to Box 3. This is one of the largest financial benefits of the ruling for asset-rich expats.

## When and how the refund arrives

For digital P-form returns filed before 1 April:
- Provisional assessment (voorlopige aanslag): usually within 4 to 8 weeks
- Definitive assessment (definitieve aanslag): usually within 3 to 6 months
- Refund: paid to the IBAN on your return shortly after the definitive assessment

The Belastingdienst pays statutory interest on refunds delayed beyond 6 months from the filing date.

## Common mistakes I see expats make

**Not declaring foreign bank accounts**. Even if the balance is small, undeclared foreign accounts trigger penalties and a longer assessment window (12 years instead of 5).

**Forgetting crypto**. Holdings on Coinbase, Binance, Kraken, and self-custody wallets all count for Box 3. The Belastingdienst has been increasingly aggressive on crypto since 2023.

**Wrong WOZ year**. The mortgage interest deduction uses the WOZ value from the year before the tax year. People routinely use the current year's WOZ and get corrected by the Belastingdienst.

**Missing the M form deadline**. The M form for new arrivals has the same 1 May deadline as the P form. Many expats assume they have more time because the form is more complex. They do not.

**Claiming the 30% ruling without an active ruling**. The 30% ruling must be granted in writing by the Belastingdienst. Assuming you qualify and self-applying it on the return triggers an audit.

## My honest recommendation

For your first Dutch tax year (M form), pay for a specialist. €300 to €500 to a reputable expat tax advisor saves money, time, and worry. The advisor catches deductions you would miss and configures your file correctly with the Belastingdienst for future years.

For subsequent years (P form) with a single employer, no homeownership complications, and modest foreign assets, file yourself in Mijn Belastingdienst. The pre-fill is solid and the system in 2026 is well designed.

For complex situations (homeownership, cross-border income, US person status, freelance income alongside employment), keep working with the advisor. The fees are deductible if the advisor specialises in business and investment situations.

Most expats overpay tax in their first Dutch year because they do not file. Filing is not optional for most expats — and even when it is, it is usually worth doing.
