It was a Tuesday in my first week in Amsterdam. I had my British passport, a printed rental contract from my landlord, and what I thought was a perfectly reasonable plan: walk into the ING branch on Kalverstraat, open a bank account, and stop bleeding money on Barclays' international transaction fees.

## Why Dutch Banking Is Uniquely Hard for New Arrivals

Most EU countries let you walk into a bank with your passport and open a basic payment account within a day. The Netherlands doesn't work that way.

Dutch banks operate under strict KYC (Know Your Customer) requirements and use the BSN as the primary identity anchor. The BSN links to your tax record, your municipality registration, your healthcare policy, and your employer payroll. Without it, Dutch banks have no way to verify you exist in the Dutch system.

There's also the address requirement. Dutch banks want a BRP-registered address — that is, an address that appears in the Basisregistratie Personen, the national population register. A rental contract alone doesn't satisfy this. You need to have physically registered at your gemeente (local municipality office) and received your BRP extract.

This creates a timing problem. You can't register at the gemeente until you have a Dutch address. You can't always get a Dutch address until you have income. You can't always prove income without a Dutch bank account for payroll. The circle is maddening.

The good news: you don't have to break the circle. You can work around it entirely with the right staging. For a full breakdown of all your options at the start, see my article on [the best banking options for expats in the Netherlands in 2026](/blog/best-banking-expats-netherlands-2026/).

## Getting Your BSN: The Gemeente Registration Walkthrough

The BSN is not something you apply for separately. You get it automatically when you register at your local gemeente (municipality office). The process differs slightly depending on your nationality.

### If you're an EU/EEA citizen:

You have the right to live and work in the Netherlands without a visa. Registration at the gemeente is still mandatory if you stay longer than four months.

**Documents you need:**
- Valid passport or national ID card (EU ID is fine)
- Proof of Dutch address: rental contract, or a declaration from your landlord (a "verklaring van huurder/verhuurder" signed by both parties)
- For employees: sometimes a letter from your employer confirming your job
- For some municipalities: proof of health insurance (you have 4 months to arrange Dutch health insurance after arrival)

**Process:**
1. Book an appointment online via your gemeente's website (search "[city name] gemeente inschrijven" or "inschrijving bevolkingsregister")
2. Attend the appointment in person with your documents
3. The municipality officer verifies your documents and registers you in the BRP
4. You receive your BSN **on the spot** in most cases — it's printed on a letter they hand you at the desk. In some municipalities it arrives by post within a few days.

Amsterdam waiting times as of mid-2026: around 1–3 weeks for an appointment slot. Rotterdam and The Hague are faster, often within a week.

### If you're a non-EU citizen:

Your BSN is issued as part of your residence permit (verblijfsvergunning) process. You'll need to apply through the IND (Immigration and Naturalisation Service). More detail on the IND process at [ind.nl](https://ind.nl/en/residence-permits).

IND processing times vary significantly: 4–8 weeks for some permit types, up to 12+ weeks for others. During this entire period, Wise and Bunq are your banking tools. For a detailed walkthrough of the BSN application process, see my [expat BSN application guide](/blog/expat-bsn-application-guide-2026/).

## Stage 3: ING — The Traditional Dutch Bank Account

Once you have your BSN and a BRP-registered Dutch address, you can open a traditional Dutch bank account. ING is the most expat-friendly of the big Dutch banks — their app has an English-language option (though the desktop site is primarily Dutch), their branch staff generally speak English, and they have widespread ATM coverage.

This is the stage I reached three weeks after arriving. The second ING visit went very differently from the first.

### ING Account Opening: Step by Step

**Option A: Online application (if you have DigiD)**

Once you have a BSN, you can apply for DigiD — the Dutch government digital identity system. With DigiD, ING's online application is straightforward. Most people get DigiD 2–5 days after getting their BSN.

1. Go to ING.nl and navigate to "Rekening openen" (open account)
2. Select "Betaalrekening" (current account)
3. Log in with DigiD when prompted
4. Fill in your personal details — they pre-populate from your DigiD data
5. Accept the terms and wait for confirmation email (usually same day)
6. Your ING pas (debit card) arrives by post within 5–7 business days

**Option B: In-branch (no DigiD required)**

1. Book an appointment via ing.nl/particulier/afspraak or call 020 22 888 88
2. Attend the branch with:
   - Valid passport or EU ID
   - Your BSN (on the letter from the gemeente, or on your residence permit)
   - Proof of Dutch address (gemeente registration extract — "uittreksel BRP" — is best; a utility bill also works)
   - Proof of income if requested (employment contract or payslip)
3. The appointment takes about 20–30 minutes
4. Account is created same day; card arrives by post in 5–7 days

**ING costs:**  
- Standard Oranje Betaalrekening: free for the first year, then €0–€2.95/month depending on age and conditions
- Students under 27: free
- Seniors 60+: free

## Address Proof Workarounds

The address proof requirement is where a lot of expats get stuck, particularly in the gap between signing a rental contract and getting their BRP extract.

**What counts as valid address proof:**

| Document | ING | Bunq | Wise |
|----------|-----|------|------|
| BRP extract (uittreksel) | ✅ Best | ✅ | Not needed |
| Signed rental contract | ❌ Alone | ✅ | Not needed |
| Utility bill in your name | ✅ | ✅ | Not needed |
| Employer letter with address | Sometimes | Sometimes | Not needed |
| Hotel / temporary housing | ❌ | ❌ | ✅ Any address |

**Practical workarounds if you don't have a utility bill yet:**

1. **Employer declaration letter**: ask your HR department for a letter on company letterhead confirming your name, job start date, and Dutch residential address. Some ING branches accept this alongside your rental contract as a combined package.

2. **Landlord declaration**: your landlord can sign a formal "verklaring van huurder" (tenant declaration) confirming you live at the address. This is a standard document in the Netherlands. Your landlord likely has a template. This plus your rental contract often satisfies Bunq.

3. **BRP extract first, bank second**: the cleanest path. Book your gemeente appointment as early as possible — even before you arrive if the online booking system allows it. Then do your banking applications after registration. Waiting for the gemeente removes nearly all address proof friction downstream.

4. **Timing utilities**: if you're renting, ask your landlord to arrange the utility transfer to your name on arrival day. You'll have a utility bill within 2–3 weeks.

## Common Mistakes — and How to Avoid Them

After helping several dozen expats through this process (and making most of these mistakes myself), here's where things tend to go wrong:

**Mistake 1: Walking into ING on day one**  
I did this. Many people do. ING cannot help you without a BSN. Don't waste the trip. Open Wise first, then plan the ING visit for three to four weeks after arrival.

**Mistake 2: Not opening Wise before you leave your home country**  
The Wise verification process is usually smooth, but occasionally triggers a manual review that takes 24–48 hours. If you're already in the Netherlands with no working payment method, those 48 hours are painful. Open Wise a week before departure. Use your home country address. Switch the address later.

**Mistake 3: Assuming your Wise IBAN works for everything Dutch**  
Wise gives you a Belgian IBAN (BE prefix). Tikkie doesn't accept non-NL IBANs. Some Dutch rent collection systems block non-NL IBANs. Some employers' payroll software throws an error. Get Bunq for the NL IBAN as soon as you have a Dutch address.

**Mistake 4: Delaying the gemeente registration**  
Some expats put off gemeente registration for weeks because they're busy settling in. Every week you wait is a week longer without a BSN, which delays ING access, DigiD access, healthcare registration, and more. Book the appointment your first or second day in the country — even if it's three weeks out, at least you're in the queue.

**Mistake 5: Not knowing which Bunq plan you need**  
The free Easy Bank plan is sufficient for most purposes. Don't automatically upgrade. Upgrade to Easy Bank Pro only if you need multiple sub-accounts (useful for budgeting) or higher ATM limits. Easy Money makes sense only if you're keeping large balances and want the savings interest.

**Mistake 6: Using your home bank card for more than a few days**  
I bled about €85 in fees during my two weeks of using Barclays in Amsterdam. €3 per ATM withdrawal plus 2.99% on every card payment adds up faster than you think. Wise eliminates this almost entirely.

## When Should You Switch — and Should You Keep All Three?

This is the question I get most often. The short answer: it depends on your financial life.

**Keep Wise permanently if:**
- You regularly send money between countries (salary in GBP, rent in EUR, family in USD)
- You travel frequently and want mid-market exchange rates
- You want to keep a multi-currency buffer

**Keep Bunq as your Dutch daily driver if:**
- You want iDEAL, Tikkie, and Dutch payment compatibility without a full traditional bank
- You like the app experience and don't need branch access
- The subscription cost (if you go Pro) is justified by features you use

**Add ING (or another traditional bank) if:**
- Your employer requires a traditional Dutch bank IBAN
- You want access to cash mortgages, savings accounts, or loans in the Netherlands
- You deal with older systems that still sometimes reject neobank IBANs (this is increasingly rare but still happens in some municipal and government contexts)

Most expats I know end up with Wise + one Dutch account (either Bunq or ING). The three-account setup is useful for the first three months, then you consolidate based on what actually gets used.
