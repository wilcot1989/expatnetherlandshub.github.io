## What "Online Dutch Course" Actually Means

There are three categories of online Dutch learning, and they solve different problems.

**Category 1: Habit apps.** Babbel, Mondly, Duolingo, Pimsleur. €5–€15/month. You spend 10–20 minutes per day on guided exercises — vocabulary, grammar drills, listening. Best for building daily habit, terrible for reaching real conversational fluency.

**Category 2: Tutor marketplaces.** Preply, iTalki. €10–€35/hour for one-on-one Dutch tutors. You book lessons, the tutor adapts to your level, you actually speak. Best for moving from passive understanding to active conversation. Required to reach B1+ in a reasonable timeframe.

**Category 3: Structured online courses.** NedLes, Direct Dutch online, UvA Talen online, KlasseTaal. €300–€2,000 for a course. You follow a curriculum, do homework, get group classes via Zoom. Best for exam prep (NT2, inburgering) and for learners who need structure.

Most expats benefit from **a habit app plus a tutor marketplace**. Structured courses make sense if you have a specific exam to pass.

## The Stack I Recommend

For most working expats trying to learn Dutch alongside a job:

**Months 1–3 (A0 to A1):**
- Babbel: €12/month (15 min/day)
- Duolingo: free (10 min/day)
- Bart de Pau YouTube: free (1 video/week)

**Months 4–9 (A1 to B1):**
- Preply: 1 lesson/week with a B1-focused tutor (~€20/lesson, €80/month)
- Babbel continued: €12/month
- Watch one Dutch TV show per week with subtitles

**Months 10+ (B1 to B2):**
- Preply: 2 lessons/week (€160/month) OR
- iTalki cheaper community tutors for conversation: €60/month
- Read a Dutch news source daily (NRC.nl, NOS.nl)
- Switch your Netflix/podcast to Dutch
- If exam-bound: NedLes or Direct Dutch course on top

Total budget over 18 months to reach solid B1: €1,000–€1,800. Compare against in-person language courses costing €2,500–€5,000 for the same outcome.

## Common Mistakes Expats Make Learning Dutch Online

**Trying to do it all on apps.** Babbel and Mondly will plateau you at A2. Beyond that you must speak with humans. The transition to tutors at month 4–6 is non-negotiable.

**Switching tutors constantly.** Some platform pressure pushes you to "try different tutors." It's better to find one you click with and stay 6+ months — they learn your weaknesses.

**Skipping listening practice.** Reading Dutch is much easier than understanding spoken Dutch. Make NPO podcasts, NOS Met Het Oog op Morgen, and Dutch Netflix daily habits early. Apps neglect this.

**Avoiding speaking until you "feel ready."** You won't feel ready. Schedule a Preply trial lesson in week 4, even if you only know 50 words. Speaking unlocks the next level.

**Buying a 2-year app subscription.** Apps lose their value at A2. Annual subscriptions only — pivot to tutors when ready.

**Ignoring NT2 / inburgering structure.** If you're going for citizenship or staying long-term, the NT2 exam matters. NedLes and Direct Dutch are designed around it; apps and Preply tutors aren't.

**Not setting Dutch as your phone language.** Most overlooked free trick. Switch your phone, then your laptop, then your Netflix. Passive immersion adds 30% to active learning effectiveness.

## Which Should You Actually Pick

**You're an absolute beginner with no time pressure:**
Babbel (€12/month) for 4 months. Then add Preply (1 lesson/week) at month 5.

**You're inburgeringsplichtig with an exam in 12 months:**
NedLes or Direct Dutch structured course (€800–€1,500), plus 1 Preply tutor lesson per week for speaking practice.

**You're already at A2, need B1 quickly for work:**

**You want maximum flexibility and minimum cost:**
Duolingo (free) + iTalki community tutor (€10–€15/hour, 2x/week). About €100–€120/month.

**You're an audio learner:**
Pimsleur for 6 months. Add Preply at month 4 for active speaking.
