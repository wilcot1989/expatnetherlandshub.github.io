> **Quick answer:** Having a baby in the Netherlands as an expat costs realistically between €8,000 and €15,000 net in the first year. Basic zorgverzekering covers the bulk of pregnancy and birth, but expect a kraamzorg co-pay of around €230, a hospital co-pay near €690 if the birth is not medically indicated, and dental costs of €15–€30 per month on top. Kinderbijslag (€280/quarter) and kinderopvangtoeslag offset daycare, but the real budget killers are the things nobody warns you about: a stroller that fits Dutch trams, the deposit on a daycare spot eighteen months in advance, and the first winter of paracetamol drops.

My friend Mei called me at 11pm on a Tuesday in November, six months pregnant, panicking because the kraamzorg agency had just sent her an invoice she did not understand. She had moved from Singapore to Utrecht eight months earlier, her Dutch was still at "twee koffie alstublieft" level, and her insurance app was telling her she owed €230 for something called "eigen bijdrage". She thought she had done something wrong.

She had not. That €230 is just how it works here. But nobody had explained it to her, because the Dutch system assumes you grew up inside it.

I spent the next ninety minutes on the phone walking her through every line item she would face between then and her daughter's first birthday. By the end of the call she was laughing, not crying. The numbers are actually fine once you see them laid out. It is the not-knowing that breaks people.

This is the breakdown I wish I had been able to send her in a single link.

## The headline number

If you are healthy, your pregnancy is uncomplicated, you give birth at home or in a birth centre, and you take a standard kraamzorg package, your total out-of-pocket cost from positive test to delivery day is roughly **€400 to €800**. That is the entire pregnancy and birth, not a typo.

The Dutch system covers almost everything. What you pay for is the co-pays, the dental, and whatever lifestyle choices you make on top — a private room, a doula, a fancy stroller.

Where the budget actually grows is the first year *after* birth. Daycare, formula, baby gear, a bigger apartment, lost income during parental leave. That is where you go from €800 to €8,000–€15,000.

Let me break it down piece by piece.

## Prenatal care: almost entirely free

From the moment you confirm your pregnancy, you call a verloskundige (midwife practice) in your neighbourhood. They handle nearly all prenatal care for healthy pregnancies. No GP referral needed.

What is covered by basic zorgverzekering:

- All midwife appointments (typically 10–14 visits)
- The 13-week and 20-week ultrasounds
- The NIPT prenatal test (a €175 co-pay applies in 2026 — this is one of the few prenatal items you pay for)
- Blood work and standard screenings
- Referral to a gynaecologist if a medical indication arises

What you pay extra for:

- Extra ultrasounds for non-medical reasons (so-called "pretecho's" — €50–€90 each)
- Private prenatal yoga or hypnobirthing courses (€150–€350 per course)
- Acupuncture, chiropractor, or other complementary care (often partially covered by aanvullende verzekering — see [best health insurance add-ons](/blog/best-health-insurance-add-ons-netherlands-2026/))

Realistic prenatal out-of-pocket: **€175 NIPT + €100–€300 extras = €275–€475 total**.

## The birth itself: home, birth centre, or hospital

This is where the Dutch system surprises most expats. The default assumption is that you give birth at home, attended by your midwife, with a kraamverzorgende (maternity nurse) present from the moment labour gets serious. About 13 percent of births still happen at home, another 15 percent in a birth centre (geboortecentrum), and the rest in hospital.

**Home birth:** Fully covered. You pay nothing for the birth itself. You will need to buy a kraampakket (a sterile birth kit) for around €30–€50, but most insurers send one for free if you ask.

**Birth centre:** Fully covered if you have a medical indication or your midwife refers you. If you choose it without medical reason, you pay a co-pay of roughly €230–€350 depending on the centre.

**Hospital birth without medical indication ("poliklinische bevalling"):** This is the famous one. Co-pay around **€690** in 2026. The number changes slightly each year. If your midwife refers you for medical reasons, this drops to zero.

**Hospital birth with medical indication:** Fully covered by basic insurance, minus your eigen risico (deductible) of €395 for the year if you haven't used it yet.

Mei chose a hospital birth in Utrecht because she felt safer there as a first-time mother who did not speak Dutch fluently. No medical indication. She paid €690 for that choice and considered it money well spent. Other friends of mine have given birth at home and described it as the best experience of their lives. There is no wrong answer, but the price tag varies.

## Kraamzorg: the thing nobody warned you about

This is the line that confused Mei. Let me explain it carefully because almost every expat I know has been blindsided by it.

Kraamzorg is postnatal home care. A trained nurse (kraamverzorgende) comes to your house for 4–8 hours a day, every day, for the first 8–10 days after birth. She checks on you and the baby, helps with breastfeeding, does light housework, teaches you how to bathe the baby, monitors for postpartum issues, and basically keeps you alive while you figure out parenthood.

It is genuinely one of the best things about the Dutch healthcare system. Friends who have given birth in the US or UK weep when I describe it.

**Cost:** A standard package is 49 hours over 8 days. Basic insurance covers it almost entirely, but there is a mandatory co-pay (eigen bijdrage) of **€4.70 per hour** in 2026.

49 hours × €4.70 = **€230.30** out of pocket.

If you take a bigger package (some agencies offer up to 80 hours), the co-pay scales linearly. If you need extra hours for medical reasons (twins, C-section recovery, baby in NICU), the co-pay is often waived.

You can pay this in instalments. Mei set up a payment plan of €40/month for six months with her kraamzorg agency. They were extremely chill about it.

> **Tip:** Book your kraamzorg agency around week 12 of pregnancy. The good ones in Amsterdam, Utrecht, and Rotterdam fill up fast, and English-speaking kraamverzorgenden are in short supply.

## Insurance: basic, add-on, or private?

This is the question I get asked most often. Let me lay out the three main paths.

### Path 1: Standard Dutch zorgverzekering (what most expats do)

Basic insurance costs around €165–€185 per month per adult in 2026. The baby is added to the policy after birth and is free until age 18. You will probably want an aanvullende verzekering (add-on) for things basic does not cover:

- Dental: €15–€30/month for adult cover. Critical during pregnancy because of gum sensitivity.
- Physio: €10–€20/month — useful for pelvic floor recovery.
- Extra kraamzorg hours, lactation consultant visits, sometimes a maternity package: €5–€15/month.

Total monthly insurance for an expecting couple: roughly **€400–€500/month**.

### Path 2: International expat insurance (Cigna, Allianz Care, Aetna)

If you want English-speaking care across the board, private hospital rooms, more choice of provider, or you are only in the Netherlands temporarily and not enrolled in Dutch basic insurance, an international policy can make sense.

Premiums for a couple with maternity coverage typically run **€350–€700 per month**, and maternity is usually only included after a 10–12 month waiting period. That last point catches a lot of people out. If you get pregnant before the waiting period ends, the pregnancy is excluded.

## After the birth: registration and admin

Within three working days of birth, you (or your partner) must register the baby at the gemeente. You get a BSN number on the spot, free of charge.

Other admin to budget for:

- Birth certificate copy in English (apostille): €15–€30 if you need one for your home country embassy
- Adding the baby to your zorgverzekering: free, must be done within four months
- Registering with the consultatiebureau (free baby health checks until age 4): free
- Choosing a huisarts for the baby: free

If you are on a residence permit pathway, the baby's status follows yours. If your partner is joining via family reunification, see [MVV visa family reunification](/blog/mvv-visa-family-reunification-netherlands-2026/) for the full process.

## Kinderbijslag: the quarterly cheque from the state

Every child living in the Netherlands receives kinderbijslag, paid by the SVB. You apply once after birth and it lands in your account every three months until the child turns 18.

2026 amounts (per quarter, per child):

- Age 0–5: roughly **€280**
- Age 6–11: roughly **€340**
- Age 12–17: roughly **€400**

For a newborn, that is around €1,120 per year. Not life-changing, but it covers nappies and formula with money to spare.

To receive it cleanly, you need a Dutch IBAN. ## Kinderopvangtoeslag: the real money saver

Daycare in the Netherlands is expensive on paper. A full-time spot (5 days, 10 hours per day) costs €1,800–€2,400 per month in the big cities in 2026. That is the sticker price.

But kinderopvangtoeslag refunds a large portion based on household income. The Belastingdienst pays it monthly, in advance, directly into your account.

Rough net costs after toeslag for a full-time spot (per child, per month):

- Household income €40,000: net cost around €120–€250
- Household income €70,000: net cost around €350–€550
- Household income €100,000: net cost around €600–€800
- Household income €150,000+: net cost around €900–€1,200

Two important catches:

1. The daycare must be a registered, listed centre. Informal arrangements do not qualify. Au pairs are different — see [au pair Netherlands cost](/blog/au-pair-netherlands-cost-2026/).
2. The waiting lists are insane. Sign up at three or four daycares the moment you see two lines on the test. Some of my friends in Amsterdam-Zuid signed up before they even told their parents.

Some expats prefer an au pair (€500–€650/month including pocket money and room/board, no daycare allowance, more flexibility) or a nanny (€2,000–€3,500/month, no allowance, maximum flexibility). The maths depends on how many children you have and how often you need childcare.

## Parental leave: the income hit

This is the elephant in the room and the part most expats underestimate.

**Mother:** Sixteen weeks fully paid maternity leave (zwangerschapsverlof + bevallingsverlof) at 100% of salary up to the daily max, paid by the UWV. For most salaries this is genuinely 100%. Beyond 16 weeks, you can take additional unpaid parental leave (ouderschapsverlof) of up to 26 times your weekly hours per parent. The first 9 weeks of that are paid at 70% of your daily wage by UWV (max around €2,800/month).

**Partner:** One week fully paid birth leave immediately after birth, plus five additional weeks at 70% of daily wage. Then 26 weeks of parental leave available, first 9 paid at 70%.

So a couple where both work, on average Dutch professional salaries, can expect to lose roughly **€2,000–€6,000 of net household income** during the leave period beyond paid weeks, depending on how much unpaid leave they take.

This is the single biggest line item for most families, and it almost never appears in "cost of having a baby" articles. Budget for it explicitly.

## How to bring the number down

Things that actually work, in order of impact:

1. **Home birth or birth centre instead of hospital** — saves €690 instantly if no medical indication.
2. **Three days of daycare instead of five** — the single largest variable cost. One stay-at-home day per parent plus three daycare days is common.
3. **Secondhand baby gear** — Marktplaats and Facebook expat groups in Amsterdam, Utrecht, Rotterdam, The Hague have constant baby gear circulation. Saves €400–€1,000.
4. **Breastfeed if possible** — saves €1,200–€1,800 in formula, plus reduces sickness in the first year.
5. **Apply for kinderopvangtoeslag the day daycare starts** — backdating is limited and you can lose months of payments.
6. **Add dental BEFORE conceiving** — most add-ons have waiting periods and pre-existing exclusions.
7. **Compare zorgverzekering every November** — switching deadline is 31 December. Premiums vary by €200–€400/year between insurers for identical cover.

## A note on schools (because everyone asks)

I know you are not thinking about it yet, but expats reading this often want to plan three years ahead. Dutch public primary school starts at age 4 and is free. International schools cost €5,000–€25,000 per year and have long waiting lists. If you think you might want the international route, get on lists early. See [international school Netherlands cost](/blog/international-school-netherlands-cost-2026/) for the full picture.

## FAQ

**Does basic Dutch health insurance cover the full cost of having a baby?**
Mostly yes. Prenatal care from a midwife, the birth itself (if at home or in a birth centre with a medical indication), and the first eight days of kraamzorg are covered by basic zorgverzekering. You will still pay the kraamzorg co-pay of €4.70 per hour and a hospital co-pay of roughly €690 if you choose a hospital birth without a medical indication.

**How much does kraamzorg actually cost out of pocket?**
For a standard package of 49 hours over eight days you pay around €230 in co-pay (49 × €4.70). If you extend the package or your baby needs extra care, the hourly co-pay still applies. The rest is reimbursed by your insurer.

**Is dental care for the mother included during pregnancy?**
No. Adult dental is not part of basic zorgverzekering, even during pregnancy. A dental add-on costs €15 to €30 per month and is worth taking out before you conceive if possible, since hormonal changes often cause gum issues.

**Can I claim kinderbijslag if I have just moved to the Netherlands?**
Yes, as long as you are insured under Dutch social security (most employees and self-employed residents are). You apply through the SVB after registering your baby with the gemeente. The benefit is paid quarterly and is roughly €280 per quarter for a child under six in 2026.

**What is kinderopvangtoeslag and how much can I get back?**
It is the Dutch daycare allowance from the Belastingdienst. Depending on your household income, you can get back between 33 percent and 96 percent of registered daycare costs, up to a maximum hourly rate set each year. For most middle-income expat families this means a net daycare cost of €400 to €900 per month per child.

**Should I take out Cigna or SafetyWing if I already have Dutch basic insurance?**
Most expats do not need both. Add Cigna or SafetyWing only if you specifically want English-speaking private maternity care, a private hospital room, or coverage during a transition period before your Dutch insurance starts. Otherwise an aanvullende verzekering on top of your Dutch policy is far cheaper.

**What if I am self-employed (ZZP) — do I still get paid maternity leave?**
Yes. Self-employed mothers receive ZEZ (Zelfstandig en Zwanger), a UWV benefit of at least 16 weeks at minimum-wage level. Self-employed partners do not receive paid partner leave.

**Is the eigen risico of €395 charged for the birth itself?**
Not for an uncomplicated birth attended by a midwife. It is charged if you use hospital care under a medical indication. Most pregnancies trigger at least some eigen risico through ultrasounds, blood work, or specialist consults.
