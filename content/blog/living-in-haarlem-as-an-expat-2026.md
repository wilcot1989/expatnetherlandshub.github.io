My friend Nadia moved from Amsterdam-Oost to Haarlem in February 2024. She had lived in Amsterdam for four years, loved the city, and had zero plans to leave. Then her landlord raised the rent on her two-bedroom apartment from €1,950 to €2,600 — citing market conditions — and that was that. She found a slightly larger flat in Haarlem's Centrum for €1,750, packed her bike, and took the train to see what the fuss was about.

Eight months later, she called me and said something I've now heard from about a dozen expats who made the same move: "I wish I'd done this two years ago."

I've since spent a lot of time in Haarlem — visiting Nadia, meeting expats who settled there, and getting to know the city properly. What follows is what I've actually learned, not a tourist brochure.

## Why Expats Are Choosing Haarlem Over Amsterdam

Amsterdam is still Amsterdam — globally connected, full of culture, the beating heart of the Dutch economy. But for expats actually living there rather than just visiting, the housing market has made the city increasingly punishing. Available apartments are scarce, rents have climbed sharply even in outer neighborhoods, and competition for anything under €2,000 is intense.

Haarlem sits just west of Amsterdam, 21 km door to door, and it solves the housing problem without solving it by moving somewhere remote or dull. The city has a genuinely beautiful historic center — the Grote Markt, the Grote Kerk, the Spaarne river, the old hofjes (almshouses) hidden behind wooden doors on narrow streets. It's a real city, not a suburb.

The intercity train between Haarlem Centraal and Amsterdam Centraal runs several times per hour and takes 17-20 minutes. That's shorter than many Amsterdam residents' tram commute across the city. With an OV-chipkaart, the journey costs around €4.50 each way. Monthly commuters tend to buy NS subscription passes — more on that below. The [NS website](https://www.ns.nl/) has a reisplanner (journey planner) and full subscription options worth bookmarking once you decide on Haarlem.

I've covered the full OV-chipkaart system and NS subscriptions in my [Dutch trains guide for expats](/blog/dutch-trains-guide-expats-2026/).

The population of Haarlem is around 165,000 — large enough to have everything you need (diverse restaurants, proper supermarkets, hospitals, specialty shops) but small enough that you're never fighting a crowd just to do groceries.

## Haarlem's Neighborhoods: Where to Actually Live

Haarlem is compact — you can cycle across it in 20 minutes — but the neighborhoods have distinct characters. Here's an honest breakdown.

### Centrum (City Center)

The area surrounding the Grote Markt and the Spaarne river is what people picture when they imagine Haarlem. Historic canal houses, independent boutiques, excellent cafes and restaurants, weekly markets (Saturday's Botermarkt is the main one). The streets are beautiful and genuinely pleasant to walk.

Rent is highest here, but still lower than equivalent Amsterdam canal house neighborhoods. You're also walking distance from Haarlem Centraal, which simplifies the Amsterdam commute considerably.

One honest downside: parking is a non-issue if you bike everywhere (as you should), but if you have a car, the centrum is a maze of paid parking and permit zones. Most expats I know who live here gave up on car ownership within six months and have been fine.

### Haarlem-Noord

North of the Spaarne river, Haarlem-Noord has a different feel — more residential, somewhat grittier in places, with a mix of prewar worker housing and newer apartment buildings. Rents are noticeably lower than the centrum. There's a growing café and small restaurant scene around the Ripperda quarter.

For expats who want more space for the budget, or who are less focused on the historic aesthetic, Haarlem-Noord is worth a serious look. The neighborhood connects easily to the centrum by bike — crossing the bridge takes about five minutes.

### Haarlemmerhout and the Amsterdamse Buurt

Southeast of the city center, the Haarlemmerhout area runs along the large park of the same name. This is where you find some of the larger homes — late 19th and early 20th century villas and townhouses, good-sized gardens, quieter streets. Families with children tend to cluster here, and the schools nearby are well-regarded.

It's the area Nadia recommended when I asked where she'd live if budget were less of a factor. Haarlemmerhout itself — 160 hectares of old beech and oak woodland — is larger than Amsterdam's Vondelpark and considerably more peaceful.

### Schalkwijk

Schalkwijk is Haarlem's postwar residential district — apartment blocks, wider streets designed around cars, less of the canal-house charm. It has a reputation among locals for being the less desirable end of Haarlem, which is partly fair (the architecture is uninspiring) and partly unfair (rents are lower, it's well-served by bus, and it's a perfectly functional place to live).

For expats on tighter budgets, or those who care more about apartment size than neighborhood aesthetics, Schalkwijk offers the most affordable Haarlem options. It's not where I'd start looking, but it's far from a bad choice.

## The Train Commute: What It's Actually Like

The 17-20 minute train journey sounds simple, and it mostly is. Haarlem Centraal is a proper intercity station — not a tiny village stop — with multiple platforms and frequent service. Trains to Amsterdam Centraal run at least four times per hour during peak hours.

The commute has a few practical realities worth knowing.

**Peak hour crowding.** The 8:15 to 8:45 trains are genuinely packed. If you have any flexibility in your start time, leaving Haarlem at 7:45 or 9:15 makes a meaningful difference to your comfort. The carriages are double-decker Intercity Direct trains, but Amsterdam-bound services at peak are still busy.

**Bikes on trains.** You can take your bike on the train but need a dagkaart fiets (day ticket for bikes, around €7.50) and bikes aren't allowed during peak hours (06:30-09:00 and 16:00-18:30 on weekdays). Most Haarlem commuters leave a second bike locked at Amsterdam Centraal or use the OV-fiets rental bike system. OV-fiets is excellent and worth setting up — you get access to thousands of rental bikes at Dutch stations with a linked OV-chipkaart subscription.

**NS subscription options.** NS offers several passes including the Dal Voordeel (40% off-peak discount) and fixed-route trajectory subscriptions. If you commute five days a week, the monthly traject abonnement (route subscription) is typically most cost-effective. Check [ns.nl](https://www.ns.nl/) for current pricing.

## Cycling Culture in Haarlem

Haarlem is one of those Dutch cities where cycling infrastructure is genuinely excellent rather than just adequate. The city has invested heavily in separated bike lanes, and the compact size means you can reach almost everything by bike in under 15 minutes from a central location.

The route to the beach at Zandvoort is one of the better recreational cycling paths in the region. It goes through the Kennemer Duinen national park — rolling dunes, pine forest, occasional red deer — and takes about 35-45 minutes at a relaxed pace. On a sunny summer Saturday, you'll share this path with half of Haarlem, which is a feature rather than a bug.

Bike theft is a real issue in Haarlem, as it is across the Netherlands. Never rely on just the frame lock; always add a chain or U-lock through the wheel and to a fixed object. ## Schools and Education

Education is often the deciding factor for expat families, and Haarlem has reasonable options at multiple levels.

**International School Haarlem (ISH)** provides the International Primary Curriculum (IPC) for primary-age children. Class sizes are manageable and the school has a solid reputation among the expat families I've spoken with. For secondary education, students often transition to international schools in Amsterdam or the broader region.

**British School of Amsterdam** and **International School Amsterdam (ISA)** in Amstelveen are both reachable from Haarlem. ISA is in Amstelveen, about 35-40 minutes by public transport — manageable with an early routine. For more on the Amstelveen expat setup, read my [Expats in Amstelveen guide](/blog/expats-in-amstelveen-guide-2026/).

**Dutch public schools with bilingual tracks (tweetalig onderwijs, or TTO)** are worth exploring if your children have some Dutch exposure, or if you plan to stay longer-term. Several Haarlem secondary schools offer TTO — equal English and Dutch — from age 12. These are fully state-funded.

[Gemeente Haarlem](https://www.haarlem.nl/) maintains an overview of education options including childcare registration and school enrollment procedures for new residents.

## Healthcare and Health Insurance

Dutch health insurance is mandatory from the moment you register at the gemeente. You have four months to arrange coverage, but sorting it immediately upon registering is the sensible approach.

The Dutch system requires a basic insurance package (basisverzekering) — every insurer must offer this at a fixed minimum level of coverage. Supplementary (aanvullende) packages add dental, physiotherapy, glasses, and other extras. The difference between insurers is primarily price, supplementary coverage scope, and customer service quality.

## Daily Life and Practical Logistics

**Supermarkets.** All main Dutch chains are well-represented — Albert Heijn, Jumbo, Lidl, Dirk. The Jumbo at Haarlem Centraal is the standard "pick something up before the train home" option. For international groceries, several Asian supermarkets and a well-stocked Ekoplaza (organic and international) operate in the centrum.

## Restaurants, Cafes, and the Food Scene

Haarlem's restaurant scene is genuinely good — not Amsterdam good in sheer volume, but much better than you'd expect for a city of 165,000. The Grote Markt and surrounding streets have the main concentration, with a mix of Dutch brown cafes (bruine kroegen), Italian trattorias, Indonesian restaurants (a Dutch staple since colonial ties), and more contemporary spots.

A few places Nadia — now a proper Haarlem local — pointed me toward:

The **Jopenkerk** is the most memorable venue in the city: a church converted into a multi-floor brewery and restaurant, with Jopen's own beers brewed on-site in tanks you can see from the dining balcony. Order the Mooie Nel IPA and stay for dinner. It's the kind of place that makes you understand why people like living in Haarlem rather than just visiting.

The **Saturday market** on the Botermarkt and Grote Markt is worth giving up a morning. Fresh stroopwafels, Dutch cheese stalls, bread, seasonal vegetables. This is where locals actually shop, not a tourist market.

For coffee, Haarlem has developed a small but decent specialty coffee scene. The usual Dutch chains are there, but you can find proper third-wave espresso within a five-minute walk of Haarlem Centraal.

## The Beach: Zandvoort and Bloemendaal aan Zee

Living 10-12 km from the North Sea coast is not a minor detail. Haarlem residents treat beach access as a genuine quality-of-life benefit, and it is.

**Zandvoort aan Zee** is the closer and more developed option — a proper beach town with a long stretch of sand, beach clubs, seafood restaurants, and a Formula 1 circuit (Circuit Zandvoort hosts the Dutch Grand Prix, which creates one completely chaotic weekend per year to either embrace or avoid). The direct train from Haarlem Centraal takes about 15 minutes in summer, with extra services added on warm days.

**Bloemendaal aan Zee** is the less commercial option — narrower access paths through the dunes, fewer beach clubs, significantly more space. It's a 40-minute bike ride from central Haarlem through the Kennemer Duinen national park. On a clear September day, cycling through the dunes and arriving at an uncrowded beach is the kind of thing that makes you feel genuinely fortunate to live in this particular corner of the Netherlands.

Both beaches are swimmable in summer (June through September), though the North Sea is reliably cold. Dutch people do not seem to find this a problem, and you will eventually adapt.

## Registering with Gemeente Haarlem

If you're moving to Haarlem, registering at [Gemeente Haarlem](https://www.haarlem.nl/) is your first official task. You must do this within five days of taking up residence at your new address. What to bring:

- Valid passport or identity document
- Rental contract or a letter from your landlord confirming your address
- For non-EU citizens: your residence permit

Registration happens in person at the Stadhuis (town hall) — appointments are available via haarlem.nl. After registration, your BSN is issued and you're in the Dutch administrative system. Read my [BSN Application Guide](/blog/expat-bsn-application-guide-2026/) for what happens next.

## Haarlem vs Amsterdam: The Honest Trade-offs

I want to be fair about what you give up by choosing Haarlem.

**Nightlife and cultural events.** Amsterdam's scale means more happening, more often. World-class museums, major concerts, a broader international community. Haarlem has cultural life — the Frans Hals Museum is genuinely excellent, there are good smaller theaters and music venues — but it's a different scale. If frequent evening culture in the city is central to your lifestyle, you'll be taking the train back from Amsterdam regularly. That works fine, but it's worth factoring in.

**Language.** Haarlem has an international community, but smaller than Amsterdam's. English is universally spoken in shops and restaurants, but you're more likely to find yourself in Dutch-only situations in day-to-day life here. If picking up basic Dutch is on your list, Haarlem might actually accelerate that.

**Job market.** Most international employers are in Amsterdam, Schiphol, or the broader Amsterdam metropolitan area. If your job is in Rotterdam or Den Haag, Haarlem becomes a much longer commute. It specifically works for the Amsterdam commuter.

**Daily costs beyond rent.** Groceries, restaurants, and everyday expenses are broadly similar to Amsterdam — maybe marginally cheaper at independent cafes where Haarlem venues have lower overheads. Don't move expecting to save on everything; the rent saving is the primary financial benefit, and it's substantial.

## The Expat Community in Haarlem

Haarlem has a growing international community. The same housing economics that pushed Nadia out of Amsterdam have pushed out hundreds of other expats over the past three years, and many of them stayed. There are Facebook groups specifically for expats in Haarlem, a reasonable Internations presence, and enough English-speaking residents that finding your people is feasible without being trivially easy.

The trajectory I've observed: expats arrive, miss Amsterdam for a month, discover their neighborhood café and the Saturday market and the bike path to the beach, and stop missing Amsterdam. Nadia described this as "the adaptation arc" and said it took her about six weeks.

One practical thing that makes Haarlem easier to settle into than some expat cities: the Dutch residents here tend to engage with newcomers without too much awkwardness. The stereotype of Dutch reserve is real, but Haarlem's scale — small enough that you recognize faces in your neighborhood — creates the kind of low-level social familiarity that Amsterdam's anonymity doesn't. I've had multiple expats tell me that they know their neighbors' names in Haarlem, something they never managed in two years in Amsterdam.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is Haarlem a good place to live as an expat?

Yes. Haarlem offers a high quality of life, a compact and very cyclable city center, international schools, and a growing expat community — all at rents 20-35% lower than comparable Amsterdam areas. The 20-minute train connection to Amsterdam Centraal makes it one of the most practical Amsterdam-adjacent cities for expats who work in the capital.

### How long does the train from Haarlem to Amsterdam take?

The intercity train from Haarlem Centraal to Amsterdam Centraal takes approximately 17-20 minutes and runs several times per hour. With an OV-chipkaart, the single journey costs around €4.50. Monthly commuters typically buy a monthly or annual NS subscription pass, which reduces the per-journey cost significantly.

### What are average rent prices in Haarlem in 2026?

As of mid-2026, a furnished one-bedroom apartment in Haarlem centrum costs roughly €1,400-1,800/month. A two-bedroom is typically €1,800-2,400/month. These figures are 20-35% lower than comparable Amsterdam apartments. Haarlem-Noord and Schalkwijk are noticeably cheaper than the city center.

### Are there international schools near Haarlem?

Yes. The International School Haarlem (ISH) offers the International Primary Curriculum and is located in the city. For secondary education, the British School of Amsterdam and the International School Amsterdam (ISA) in Amstelveen are both reachable within 30-40 minutes. Several Dutch schools also offer bilingual education tracks (tweetalig onderwijs).

### How far is Haarlem from the beach?

Haarlem is approximately 10-12 km from both Zandvoort aan Zee and Bloemendaal aan Zee. By bike it takes around 35-45 minutes through the dunes. Direct trains also run from Haarlem Centraal to Zandvoort station in about 15 minutes during summer, with extra services added on warm days.
