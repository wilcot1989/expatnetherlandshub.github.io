Two weeks after my second daughter was born, I sat at the kitchen table with a half-cold coffee and a stack of insurance quotes. My husband was upstairs trying to get the baby to nap. The mortgage broker had said it almost casually during our refinance: "You probably want to think about overlijdensrisicoverzekering now you have two kids." Term life insurance. The thing nobody wants to research at 11pm but everyone with dependants eventually does.

I had quotes from four insurers on the table. Two of them — Aegon and Interpolis — kept coming back as the names everyone mentioned. My Dutch colleague swore by Interpolis ("Rabobank, just easy"). The mortgage broker pushed Aegon because the premium for a 25-year level-term was twelve euros cheaper per month. Twelve euros over twenty-five years is 3.600 euros. Not nothing.

I spent that weekend reading policy documents in Dutch with Google Translate open in another tab, calling both insurers, and trying to figure out which one actually wanted my business as an expat with a Polish passport, a Dutch BSN, and a partner from Spain. This article is what I learned — expanded to cover not just term life but the full range where Aegon and Interpolis compete: pension and lijfrente, banksparen, home insurance (inboedel + opstal), liability (aansprakelijkheid), and the messy reality of getting English service from companies whose entire infrastructure assumes you speak Dutch.

## TL;DR Quick answer

- **Aegon** (now legally a.s.r. after the 2023 sale, but brand still active) — strongest for pension, lijfrente, banksparen, and standalone term life. Best if you are building retirement savings or want a long-term life policy at the lowest premium. English support is patchy but better than Interpolis.
- **Interpolis** — strongest for bundled household insurance (inboedel + opstal + aansprakelijkheid) especially if you bank with Rabobank. Easiest "alles bij één partij" experience. Weak on pension and English service.
- **For most expats:** Aegon for pension/life, Interpolis for home/liability if you are a Rabo customer. Splitting is fine and often cheaper than bundling everything with one provider.

I went with Aegon for term life and stayed with my existing inboedel provider rather than switching to Interpolis. Reasons below.

## The corporate context: who actually owns what in 2026

This matters because the answer to "is Aegon still a thing in the Netherlands" is genuinely confusing.

Aegon was a Dutch insurance giant for over a century. In **June 2023** it sold its entire Dutch life, pension, and non-life business to **a.s.r. Nederland** for around EUR 2,5 billion plus shares. The deal closed in July 2023. Since then:

- The legal entity behind your Aegon policy is **ASR Levensverzekering N.V.** or **ASR Schadeverzekering N.V.**
- The **Aegon brand** is still used on pension, lijfrente, and banksparen products under a licence — you will see "Aegon (a.s.r.)" on documents
- Service moved partly to ASR systems but the Aegon customer portal still exists
- Aegon the global group is now headquartered in Bermuda with main operations in the US (Transamerica) and UK

Interpolis is simpler. It is a wholly-owned subsidiary of **Rabobank**, founded in 1969, and has always focused on Dutch retail insurance. Their tagline "Glashelder" (crystal clear) reflects a deliberate strategy of plain-language policies — at least, plain Dutch.

For an expat this means: when you buy "Aegon" in 2026 you are really buying ASR with an Aegon sticker, and when you buy Interpolis you are buying a Rabobank product. Both are solvent, both are regulated by **De Nederlandsche Bank** and **AFM**, and both will still exist in 20 years when your term life policy expires.

## Pension and lijfrente: where Aegon dominates

This is the single biggest reason to choose Aegon over Interpolis.

### What is lijfrente?

Lijfrente is a tax-advantaged retirement product. Within your **jaarruimte** (annual deduction room), you can contribute pre-tax euros to a lijfrente account, defer income tax until withdrawal in retirement, and avoid Box 3 wealth tax on the balance. For expats with the 30% ruling who later transition off it, lijfrente becomes one of the few remaining tax shelters.

Aegon offers:

- **Aegon Lijfrente Sparen** (banksparen) — savings account variant, low risk, ~2-3% interest in 2026
- **Aegon Lijfrente Beleggen** (investment variant) — invests in funds, higher return potential, market risk
- **Aegon Direct Ingaand Lijfrente** — annuity that pays out monthly from a chosen retirement date

Interpolis offers a more limited pension product range and routes most pension advice through Rabobank's wealth management arm. If you want a self-directed lijfrente, Aegon is the practical choice between these two.

I cover the broader retirement landscape in [retiring in the Netherlands: an expat guide](/blog/retiring-in-netherlands-expat-guide-2026/), and if you would rather invest your jaarruimte through low-cost ETFs you can compare brokers in [ETF investing platforms Netherlands for expats](/blog/etf-investing-platforms-netherlands-expats-2026/).

### Costs

Aegon banksparen lijfrente has **no annual fee** on the savings variant and roughly **0,2-0,5% annual fund costs** on the investment variant (plus underlying fund TER, typically 0,2-0,4% for index funds). Total cost for the investment variant is in the 0,4-0,9% range — not the cheapest in the market (Brand New Day and DEGIRO are cheaper) but acceptable for a one-stop pension product.

Interpolis lijfrente, when offered, is priced through Rabobank advisory channels and typically lands at 1,0-1,5% all-in. Worse value for self-directed expats.

### English support for pension

Both fail here. Pension documents are dense Dutch with terms like *uitkeringsduur*, *fiscaal aftrekbaar*, *banksparen*, *afkoopwaarde*. Aegon has more English written material on their corporate site because Aegon's global group operates in 20+ countries. Interpolis is Dutch-only in practice. For complex pension choices I would budget for one session with an independent **financieel adviseur** regardless of which insurer you pick — see my notes on [renting vs buying in the Netherlands](/blog/renting-vs-buying-netherlands-expat-2026/) for similar advice on mortgage advisers.

## Term life insurance (overlijdensrisicoverzekering)

This is where I spent my kitchen-table weekend. Term life — pure protection, no savings component, pays out a fixed sum if you die during the policy term.

### Why expats need it

If you have a Dutch mortgage, your lender may require **overlijdensrisicoverzekering** to cover the mortgage balance. Even if not required, dependants left with a mortgage in a country whose social safety net they do not fully understand are in a vulnerable position. The Dutch state will pay **Anw** (surviving partner pension) only if the surviving partner has dependent children under 18 or is more than 45% disabled. Otherwise — nothing from the state.

For a deeper view on this, my piece on [life insurance for expats in the Netherlands](/blog/life-insurance-expats-netherlands-2026/) walks through the whole calculation: how much cover, how long, level vs decreasing.

### My actual quotes (35-year-old female non-smoker, EUR 250.000, 25-year level term)

- **Aegon:** EUR 14,80/month
- **Interpolis:** EUR 17,20/month
- **TAF (white-label):** EUR 13,10/month
- **Reaal:** EUR 15,50/month

Aegon won on price by a meaningful margin against Interpolis but lost to TAF, which underwrites for several brokers and consistently undercuts named insurers. The reason I still chose Aegon over TAF was the **klachteninstituut Kifid** complaint resolution record — Aegon and Interpolis both have established large-scale complaint handling, TAF is smaller. For a 25-year promise I valued institutional weight slightly above the EUR 1,70/month saving.

### Application process for expats

Both insurers require:

- BSN
- Dutch address (residential, not PO box)
- Medical questionnaire in Dutch (Aegon has an English summary on request — I had to email and wait three days)
- Sometimes a doctor's report for cover above EUR 300.000 or if you disclose conditions

Aegon accepted my application online, no broker required. Interpolis pushed me toward Rabobank advisory which would have added a EUR 300-500 advice fee. That was the dealbreaker for me — paying nearly two years of premium difference in advisory fees made no sense for a straightforward policy.

## Home insurance: inboedel and opstal

Here Interpolis becomes the stronger candidate.

### What you actually need

- **Inboedel** — covers contents (furniture, electronics, clothes, bikes inside the home)
- **Opstal** — covers the building itself (only relevant if you own; renters skip this)
- **Aansprakelijkheid** (AVP) — personal liability, often EUR 1,25 million cover, costs EUR 50-80/year, considered essential

### Pricing for a typical Amsterdam 75m2 apartment, EUR 50.000 inboedel cover

- **Interpolis Alles in één Polis:** EUR 22/month (inboedel + opstal + AVP bundled)
- **Aegon (ASR) Verzekeringspakket:** EUR 26/month
- **Centraal Beheer:** EUR 19/month
- **Univé:** EUR 21/month

Interpolis wins on bundled price if you are a Rabobank customer (additional ~5% discount for *Rabo Bonus*). It loses to Centraal Beheer if you do not need Rabo integration. My recommendation: if you already bank with Rabobank and want one less thing to think about, Interpolis bundle is fine. If you bank with ING or are using a fintech, compare Centraal Beheer first — see also [ING vs Rabobank for expats](/blog/ing-vs-rabobank-expats-2026/) on the banking choice that often drives the insurance choice.

### Claims experience

Anecdotally, both Interpolis and Aegon score well on claim payouts in **Consumentenbond** surveys. Interpolis has slightly better consumer scores for bike theft claims (a major Dutch claim category) because they include bikes under inboedel by default; Aegon requires a separate bike rider for theft outside the home.

### English support for claims

This is where the brand differences disappear. Both insurers handle claims in Dutch. You upload photos, fill in a Dutch form, and a Dutch claims handler calls you back. If your Dutch is below B2, budget for hiring help with the claim or call them with a Dutch-speaking friend on the line. I had a EUR 2.000 bike theft claim with a different insurer two years ago and the form had a field — *toedracht van het schadegeval* — that Google Translate rendered as "the run-up of the damage event," which is technically correct and entirely useless.

## Aansprakelijkheidsverzekering (personal liability)

The cheapest important insurance you will ever buy. Costs EUR 50-90/year and pays out up to EUR 1,25 million if you accidentally injure someone or damage their property. Mandatory in practice if you cycle in city centres.

Aegon AVP: ~EUR 65/year standalone, included in bundles. Interpolis AVP: ~EUR 58/year standalone, included in *Alles in één Polis*. Functionally identical cover. If you take nothing else from this article, **buy AVP from somebody** — the cheapest non-negotiable for any expat living in the Netherlands.

For other insurance basics including [car insurance for expats](/blog/car-insurance-netherlands-expats-2026/) the same logic applies: shop on price among solvent providers, do not overpay for "name brand" peace of mind on commodity products.

## English support compared side by side

I called both insurers twice during this comparison. Notes:

**Aegon (a.s.r.) English phone call** — answered in Dutch, asked for English, transferred after ~90 seconds to an agent who handled the conversation in fluent English. Could answer product questions but said anything contractual would be in Dutch and I should "ask a friend or your mortgage broker to translate." Hours 09:00-17:00 weekdays.

**Interpolis English phone call** — answered in Dutch, asked for English, was told they would "try to find someone." Five-minute hold. Agent who came on spoke functional but limited English, struggled with the term "level term" (I had to translate to "gelijkblijvende premie"). Hours 08:00-21:00 weekdays plus Saturday morning — much longer hours than Aegon, which is a point in their favour.

**Written communication** — both send all policy documents in Dutch. Both have Dutch-only customer portals. Aegon has more English explanatory material on their corporate site; Interpolis has essentially none.

## Health insurance note

Neither Aegon nor Interpolis is a major player in Dutch **basisverzekering** (mandatory health insurance). Aegon exited basic health insurance years ago and Interpolis only resells via Zilveren Kruis. For health you will pick from CZ, VGZ, Zilveren Kruis, Menzis, or DSW — see my separate guide. ## How I decided (and what I would tell a friend)

After the kitchen-table weekend I split my insurances:

- **Aegon** for term life (EUR 14,80/month, 25-year level term, EUR 250.000 cover)
- **Centraal Beheer** for inboedel + opstal + AVP bundle (EUR 19/month) — Interpolis lost by EUR 3/month and I was not a Rabobank customer
- **Aegon lijfrente banksparen** for retirement savings within jaarruimte (no annual fee, 2,8% interest in 2026)
- Health insurance: CZ basisverzekering (separate decision)

If I were a Rabobank customer I would probably have gone Interpolis for the household bundle. The Rabo Bonus discount and single-app integration are worth something. But for term life and pension, Aegon was clearly stronger.

**The framework I would give a friend:**

1. Get three quotes for term life — Aegon, TAF, one Dutch broker. Take the cheapest with a Kifid-registered insurer.
2. For household insurance, decide first whether you want a bank-bundled experience. If yes and you are Rabo, Interpolis. If no, compare Centraal Beheer, Univé, and ASR/Aegon on price.
3. AVP is non-negotiable — buy it from whoever bundles it cheapest.
4. Pension is a separate decision. Aegon banksparen is fine for set-and-forget; ETF brokers are better if you want lower fees and willingness to manage it yourself.

## Pricing reality check 2026

Premiums have risen 6-12% across the Dutch insurance market in 2025-2026, driven by claims inflation (especially weather-related opstal claims) and reinsurance costs. Both Aegon and Interpolis raised opstal premiums 8-10% in their January renewal cycle. Term life premiums for new policies stayed roughly flat because mortality assumptions did not change materially. Lijfrente interest rates rose with ECB policy and Aegon banksparen is now competitive with savings accounts — see the broader context in [Dutch utilities explained for expats](/blog/dutch-utilities-explained-expats-2026/) where I cover similar inflation dynamics in household budgets.

## What can go wrong (the boring but important part)

- **Mid-policy address change** — both insurers handle international moves badly. If you leave the Netherlands mid-policy, you usually have to cancel term life and pension; lijfrente has tax consequences if withdrawn before age 67.
- **Claim disputes** — Kifid (independent ombudsman) is your friend. Both Aegon and Interpolis are signatories and will accept Kifid mediation.
- **Language ambiguity** — if you signed in English-translated documents that differ from the Dutch contract, the Dutch text prevails. Always.
- **Aegon brand confusion** — when calling customer service, sometimes the agent does not realise you have an "old Aegon" policy now serviced by ASR. Have your policy number ready.

## Final verdict

Aegon (a.s.r.) is the stronger choice for **expats focused on long-term wealth and protection** — pension, lijfrente, term life. The pricing on term life beats Interpolis by 10-20% and the lijfrente product range is broader.

Interpolis is the stronger choice for **expats who bank with Rabobank and want simple bundled household insurance**. The *Alles in één Polis* is well-designed and the customer experience is smoother than Aegon's post-acquisition systems.

For most expats the right answer is: split. Use Aegon for what Aegon does best, use a separate provider (Interpolis, Centraal Beheer, Univé) for household. Loyalty discounts in the Dutch insurance market are small and rarely justify suboptimal product choices in a single bundle.

## FAQ

**Is Aegon still active in the Netherlands after the ASR sale?**
Yes. In 2023 Aegon sold its Dutch life and pension business to a.s.r. The Aegon brand still appears on pension, lijfrente, and banksparen products, but the legal carrier is now ASR. Service quality stayed broadly stable, but contact channels moved.

**Does Interpolis offer English-language service?**
Partially. Policy documents are Dutch. Phone support has English-capable agents but availability is inconsistent — you may need a Dutch-speaking friend or translator for claims. Online portal is Dutch-only.

**Which is cheaper for inboedel and opstal insurance?**
Interpolis tends to be 5-15% cheaper for bundled household (inboedel + opstal + aansprakelijkheid) if you are a Rabobank customer. Aegon/ASR is competitive on standalone policies but rarely undercuts Interpolis bundles.

**Can I get a lijfrente (annuity) as a non-Dutch resident?**
You need to be a Dutch tax resident to benefit from the lijfrente deduction (jaarruimte). Aegon accepts expat customers with a BSN and Dutch address. Interpolis offers fewer pension products and routes most clients through Rabobank advice.

**Is term life insurance (overlijdensrisicoverzekering) worth it for expats?**
If you have a mortgage or dependants, yes. A 20-year level-term policy of EUR 250.000 costs around EUR 12-25 per month for a healthy 35-year-old non-smoker. Both Aegon and Interpolis offer it; Aegon usually wins on price for longer terms.

**Do I need a Dutch bank account to pay premiums?**
Both insurers require SEPA direct debit from a euro IBAN. You can use Wise or Revolut euro accounts if you do not yet have ING or Rabobank, though some processes still expect a Dutch IBAN.

**What happens to my policy if I leave the Netherlands?**
Term life can usually continue if you keep paying SEPA — confirm with the insurer. Lijfrente withdrawal before age 67 triggers income tax plus 20% revisierente. Household insurance ends when you no longer have a Dutch address.
